## Executive recommendation

**Adopt the big bet now, not after submission.**

Three reasons converging:

1. The curatorial record has been building toward an asymmetric damage theory for years. The symmetric *sorting* framing is borrowed scaffolding from comparative welfare-state vocabulary; it is not what the research programme is actually doing. (See Part 6 of the intellectual portrait.)
2. The empirical result is asymmetric. MEMORY.md records "Accept as genuine asymmetry" as a decision already made. The v4 paper rhetorically softens what the working notes have already conceded.
3. The Danish registry thesis is theoretically tighter under the asymmetric framing than under the symmetric one. The asymmetric theory predicts that conditionality reforms produce damage signatures *and that decommodification expansions do not produce mirror-image protection signatures of equivalent strength*. That is a sharper empirical claim than "welfare reforms shift the sorting pattern." The thesis design benefits from the bigger commitment now.

Risks of adopting the big bet are real but smaller than the risks of submitting the medium bet:

- Loss of "sorts into solidarity or scapegoating" rhetoric — yes, but the rhetoric was concealing the finding rather than describing it.
- Need to engage the cultural-backlash camp more seriously — yes, but the v4 draft was going to face that referee question regardless, and the asymmetric framing actually answers the question better than the symmetric one.
- More work in six weeks — yes, but the work is bounded (5–6 sessions per the v5 file's recommended path), and most of the additions you would make under the medium bet (Why Welfare paragraph, aggregation paragraph, attitudes-vs-votes paragraph) are already drafted in the v5 §III and can be adapted.

If you reject this recommendation, the medium-bet draft (now in `paper_draft_v4_final.md` with the three additions implemented) is genuinely submission-ready. This is not a recommendation made under pressure. It is a recommendation that the published version of the paper should match the version of the argument the apparatus actually produces.

## Empirical extensions — what the pipeline can support

The seminar paper STATUS file identifies several "nice-to-have" analyses skipped in the polish phase. Two of them are theoretically more important than their "nice-to-have" status suggests:

### CWED sub-components (unemployment / sickness / pensions)

The current CWED finding is a *composite* across three programme types. The asymmetric theory makes a sharper prediction than the symmetric one: the damage channel should run through the *unemployment* sub-component most strongly (because that is where vulnerable workers actually encounter conditional welfare), more weakly through *sickness*, and weakly or null through *pensions* (because pensions are typically less judgement-laden in their administration).

If unemployment-CWED carries the bulk of the cross-national correlation, the asymmetric mechanism gets a clean within-measure validation. If pensions carry surprising weight, the theory needs to accommodate that.

**Recommendation:** run this analysis (1 hour per STATUS estimate). Under the big bet it is theoretically central; under the medium bet it is a robustness footnote in §VI.D.

### `gvctax` two-item composite as supplementary solidarity outcome

A second outcome variable on the solidarity side. If the redistribution null in Model 5 is an artefact of the single `gincdif` item, `gvctax` would surface that. If both nulls in the same direction, the asymmetry is more robust.

**Recommendation:** run this (20 min). Either result strengthens the paper — null confirms the asymmetric finding, positive uplift complicates it productively.

### CWED reform-timing within-country tests (the thesis design seed)

Per STATUS, CWED waves 1971–2011 show meaningful within-country variation for AT, DE, NL, GB. A within-country fixed-effects test on these countries would not solve identification, but would show whether the cross-sectional CWED–slope correlation is consistent with within-country temporal variation. If the cross-section is real, within-country shifts in CWED should be associated with within-country shifts in the RTI–exclusion slope.

**Recommendation:** if time permits, draft this as Appendix D. It is the methodological bridge between the seminar paper and the thesis — and it pre-empts a referee asking why you have not used the within-country variation already in the data.

## Thesis trajectory implications

Under either path, the thesis design described in `projects/msc_thesis/STATUS.md` is correct. The question is what theoretical claim the thesis is testing.

**Under medium bet (or Path C):** Thesis tests whether welfare reform changes the cross-sectional sorting pattern over time. Acceptable but vague — "what should we expect to see?"

**Under big bet:** Thesis tests two distinct predictions — (a) conditionality reforms in countries with previously dignity-preserving regimes should produce *new* damage signatures (clear positive prediction), and (b) decommodification expansions in countries with previously stigmatising regimes should produce *no* mirror-image protective signatures, or much weaker ones (clear null prediction). The asymmetric theory is testable in a way the symmetric theory is not.

**Recommendation regardless of path chosen:** the thesis is materially stronger under the asymmetric framing. Even if you stay with the medium bet for the seminar submission, draft the thesis prospectus around the asymmetric prediction set.

The Danish 2003, 2006, 2013 activation reforms are the empirical handles. The 2003 reform (Foss-Frederiksen) introduced contact requirements and active job-search verification. The 2006 reform tightened benefit duration. The 2013 reform (Disability Pension Reform) tightened eligibility for early retirement. These are conditionality-introducing reforms, exactly the kind your asymmetric theory predicts should produce damage. There is no symmetric expansion-of-decommodification reform of comparable magnitude in the same period — which is itself consistent with the asymmetry.
