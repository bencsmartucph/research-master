## Why each one might be the right choice

### `v_false_dichotomy` is right if:

- You want the piece to make an actual argument and risk something
- You're OK with the contested-thesis frame ("most AI-and-learning discourse is about a kind of student I am not")
- You want to dismantle the cheating-vs-productivity binary explicitly
- LinkedIn audience: yes, but a thoughtful subset
- The two confessional sections (admitting you used Claude for parts of methodology design AND admitting the supervisor implication is real) are the moments that make this version land harder than the others. They are also the moments most likely to draw pushback. Both are intentional.

### `v_building_is_learning` is right if:

- You want the most LinkedIn-friendly version
- You're more interested in the pedagogy-future angle than the personal angle
- You want a piece that finishes optimistic rather than confessional
- The implicit policy claim ("apprenticeship has just become affordable") does intellectual work without making the supervisor implication the headline
- This is the safest version. It will travel well, draw little pushback, and probably get the most engagement on LinkedIn. It is also the version that contributes the least new thing to the discourse, because "build to learn" is a familiar idea presented in fresher framing.

### `v_discovery_story` is right if:

- You want the piece that is most likely to actually be remembered
- You believe the story does the argumentative work better than any explicit argument could
- You're willing to publish at the longer length on Substack (probably not LinkedIn-shaped)
- You want the version that demonstrates the practice rather than describing it
- This is the version most likely to feel like writing rather than content. It is also the version that takes the most craft risk: if the story doesn't land for the reader, there's no fallback thesis to carry them.

### `v_curious_confession` is right if:

- You want to publish something fast and short
- You want the piece to feel like writing first, argument second
- You're comfortable with the fragmented, lyrical form (it's a real risk on LinkedIn; it's natural on Substack)
- You want to leave the door open for longer essays later by establishing voice first
- This is the version closest to the "intelligent best friend, slightly aloof, ironic, too-cool-for-school" voice you described. It is also the most personal and the most vulnerable, which means it is the version most likely to get a "this stayed with me" comment, and also the most likely to be misread as not making an argument.

## How to choose tomorrow morning

Read each opening paragraph in order. After all four, ask yourself: which one do you wish you had written? That answer is the one to publish. Whichever one it is, the other three become source material for future pieces — a long-form Compile Error essay, a pedagogy follow-up, an Atlantic-style narrative if you ever decide to push there.

If you cannot decide, the practical recommendation is `v_building_is_learning` first (lowest risk, broadest reach, sets up the audience for the riskier ones later) and `v_curious_confession` immediately after on Substack (signals voice, builds the kind of subscriber base that wants the longer essays).

If you can decide, ignore the practical recommendation and follow the answer to the "which one do you wish you had written" question. That is what authentic writing under your name is supposed to feel like.
