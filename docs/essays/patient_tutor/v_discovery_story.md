# The Tuesday Session

*Version: pure narrative. ~1600 words. Best for Substack long-form. No section numbers, no overt thesis statement. The story carries the argument; the reader extracts the meaning. Anchors marked [RETYPE].*

---

[RETYPE — opening from memory]

It was just past nine in the morning on a Tuesday in late April, and I had been stuck on the same problem for six days. My supervisor was on leave; the methods textbook I needed was queued for inter-library loan; the lme4 documentation was making sense in isolation and refusing to make sense together, which is the way lme4 documentation tends to behave. The problem was small. Buried in the empirical section of my master's thesis was a multilevel regression with a random-slopes specification — written, in lme4 syntax, as `(1 + RTI | country-wave)` — and I could not tell, from the documentation alone, whether the line was doing what my paper required or whether I had been misreading the syntax for six months. The right answer mattered. If I had been misreading the syntax, the standard errors in my regression were wrong, and the p-value I had been quoting in the paper was somewhere between misleading and meaningless. I needed to know which.

I opened a fresh chat with Claude — not the GPT one, not the LinkedIn one, not the version everyone is talking about, but the one I had spent a year configuring with a few markdown files, a handful of skills, and a habit of refusing answers I had not earned. I had built a small system around the model the way you build a small system around a tool you intend to use for a long time. I typed out the problem in the kind of detail you only use when you are stuck. I included the formula. I included the data structure. I included what I thought the specification was doing and what I thought it was supposed to be doing. I asked the model not to answer the question but to walk me through random-effects structure by Socratic method, refusing the conclusion until I had constructed it.

This is, I should say, an embarrassing thing to ask an AI to do. The whole point of the AI is that it can give you the answer. Asking it to refuse is asking the gas station to make you walk to your destination. There is a kind of pretentiousness in the request that I want to acknowledge before I keep going. I asked anyway. The reason I asked was that I had been in this place before, with this same paper, and I had learned the hard way that an answer I had not built was an answer I could not defend, and that a paper full of answers I could not defend was a paper that was going to fall apart the first time anyone serious looked at it. So: walk me through it.

The conversation went the way these conversations go. I produced a partially-correct interpretation of what `(1 + RTI | country-wave)` was specifying. The model asked me what I thought the consequence would be if I were wrong about which part. I gave a half-right answer. The model picked the wrong half and asked me to defend it. I went back to the data and looked. I came back with a corrected version. We went around like this for the better part of an hour. By the end of it I could explain the random-slopes specification to a colleague, I had caught a misspecification I had been carrying for six months, and I had a small notebook of worked examples I would be able to come back to in three weeks when I had inevitably forgotten the syntax again.

This was already, by any honest measure, a successful session. What turned it into something else was the question the model asked next, which I had not expected and which I have been thinking about for two weeks.

The question was: which slope-extraction method had actually produced the correlation reported in §V.D of my paper?

I should explain the stakes. §V.D contains the central empirical finding of the thesis, the one the entire theoretical argument hangs on. The finding is a correlation: between a country-level measure of welfare generosity (CWED) and the within-country slope of routine-task-intensity on anti-immigration attitudes. The correlation is r = −0.848 on fifteen countries, p < 0.001. It accounts for seventy-two per cent of the variance the cross-country comparison is trying to explain. It is the number on which I had built four months of writing.

The question — *which slope-extraction method produced this correlation* — was a question with two possible answers. Either it was the bivariate per-country OLS the §V.D text appears to describe, or it was something else. I had assumed for fourteen months that it was the bivariate OLS, because that was what the section read like, and because the older code path in the analysis pipeline implemented exactly that.

I went and looked.

The answer was no, and the answer was wrong. The published correlation did not come from the bivariate OLS. It came from BLUPs — best linear unbiased predictors — extracted from a random-slopes mixed model with individual-level controls, which lived in a different module entirely and which the §V.D text did not mention. The bivariate version, the one the text described, gave a correlation of r = −0.625 on the same fifteen countries. The published version gave r = −0.848. The two methods agreed on the direction of the effect and disagreed by twenty-three percentage points on the strength of it. My paper had been describing one method while reporting the result of the other. This had been true since the first draft. I had circulated four drafts to colleagues. None of them had noticed. I had not noticed. The model had not noticed for the first hour of our conversation, and only noticed because the conversation had got far enough into the specification that the inconsistency surfaced.

I sat with this for a few minutes. The discovery was not catastrophic — both methodologies were defensible, the qualitative finding survived the choice between them, the substantive argument of the paper was still correct in every important sense. But the paper had been wrong about a specific factual claim for fourteen months and the only reason it was about to stop being wrong was that I had asked an AI tutor to walk me through random-effects syntax on a Tuesday morning in April. The supervisor I did not have would not have caught this. The textbook I had been waiting for would not have caught this. The colleagues who had read four drafts of the paper had not caught this. The thing that caught it was not the model's intelligence, exactly; it was the conversation having lasted long enough, at a low enough level of abstraction, that the two pieces of inconsistent information were both on the table at the same time. The model noticed because it had no choice but to notice once both pieces were in front of it. I noticed because I had asked the model to keep going past the point where I would normally have stopped.

I want to write something about what happened next, but I am genuinely not sure what it was. The closest I can come is this: the discovery changed the shape of what I had been doing all year. For twelve months I had been telling myself a slightly nervous story about my AI use — that I was leaning on it more than I should, that I was outsourcing parts of my craft, that the paper would have been more authentically mine without it. That story had been comfortable in the way slightly nervous stories often are. It made me feel like I was getting away with something, and feeling like I was getting away with something kept me alert. After the Tuesday session it was no longer the right story. The model had not eroded my paper. It had caught me, on a small but consequential point, being wrong about my own paper. It had asked the question a careful supervisor would have asked. The discomfort I had been carrying about my use of these tools turned out to be discomfort about the wrong thing. I had not been cheating. I had been, slowly and somewhat by accident, building the only piece of intellectual infrastructure I had access to that was capable of catching me when I got something wrong.

I opened a new file and began to write.

I will not pretend the rest of this story is dramatic. I corrected the paper text. I sent a note to my supervisor. I went for a walk. I spent the rest of the week working through the rest of §V the same way I had worked through the random-slopes specification — slowly, conversationally, with a model that had been configured to refuse answers I had not earned. I caught a few smaller things and resolved a few questions that had been sitting in the margin for months. I did not catch anything else as consequential as the BLUPs error, which is fine; one of those per Tuesday is a good rate.

[RETYPE — closing paragraph from memory]

The supervisor problem in academia is older than the AI debate and will outlast it. Most graduate students do not have a careful supervisor, not because supervisors are careless but because supervisors are scarce. What changed for me on that Tuesday morning was not that the supervisor problem went away. It was that the consequence of the supervisor problem — the small, slow accumulation of errors in a paper that no one has time to catch — became, for the first time in my education, something I could do something about. The Tuesday session is not what most people mean when they talk about AI tutoring. It is what tutoring used to mean before it became a thing you mostly bought and stopped meaning much. I am going to keep the Tuesday session. I do not know what to call it. It is what I have got.
