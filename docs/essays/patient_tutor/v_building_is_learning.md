# Building Is Learning

*Version: optimistic pedagogy essay. ~1400 words. Best for LinkedIn or Substack. Audience: students, educators, anyone bullish on the future of how we learn. Less confessional, more philosophical. Anchors marked [RETYPE].*

---

[RETYPE — opening from memory]

I have learned more econometrics in the last six weeks than in the previous two years of an MSc, and the most consequential thing I have learned is that I should have been building all along. The maxim "if you want to learn to code, just start building" was true the entire time. The reason it did not generalise to fields outside coding is that, outside coding, building things you cared about was prohibitively expensive. You needed a lab; you needed clean data; you needed someone patient enough to walk you through the parts you were going to get wrong. Without those resources, the only learning available to most students was reading about other people's buildings — also known as "doing the readings" — and reading about a building, it turns out, is to building what reading about swimming is to swimming. You can become impressively articulate about either while remaining unable to do them.

What is happening to me right now, and to a small but growing number of people in the same position, is that the cost of building has fallen far enough that build-to-learn is becoming available across fields where it has not been available before. The implication for what undergraduate education looks like in five years is significant, and I want to write about it before I forget what it felt like to discover, in real time, that the model I had of how learning works was wrong about the part that matters most.

The build-to-learn principle has a long and undertheorised history. Programming culture has known it forever — the canonical pedagogy of "build a side project, get stuck, debug, repeat" is what every working programmer credits as their actual education, regardless of whether they hold a CS degree. Construction trades have known it for centuries — the apprentice builds a house under the master's eye, the master corrects what the apprentice gets wrong, the house gets built and the apprentice becomes a builder. Medical education has known it imperfectly — the residency is the build-to-learn principle bolted onto an academic credential, and the parts of medical training that work best are reliably the residency parts. Outside these examples, build-to-learn has not been the dominant educational mode, and the reason is the same in every case: building things you can learn from is expensive. You need materials, time, supervision, and the willingness to discard what you build wrong. Most fields could not afford to give every student all four.

Econometrics is one of the fields that could not afford it. The build in econometrics is "do the analysis on real data, get the wrong answer, figure out why, do it again." The materials (clean data, working code, a question worth answering) are non-trivial to assemble. The time is significant. The supervision required to catch the wrong answer and explain why it was wrong is what graduate supervisors are theoretically for and what they realistically have only fragments of available to give. The result is that most econometrics is taught by reading and exam — by encountering other people's analyses second-hand, memorising the specifications they used, and demonstrating recall on a written test. Most graduates of econometrics master's programmes can produce a regression and report its coefficient. Many cannot, when challenged, defend the choice of specification or explain what would have happened if they had chosen differently. This is not a personal failure of those graduates; it is what the curriculum is designed to produce, given the resources the curriculum has to work with.

The thing the AI tools are quietly changing is the cost structure underneath this. Building things in econometrics is no longer expensive in the way it was. The materials assemble themselves: replication datasets are findable, working code is generatable, the question worth answering is whatever you brought into the session. The supervision required to catch the wrong answer is available on demand, with the proviso that the supervisor itself sometimes gets things wrong and has to be verified. The time is still significant — building takes the time it takes — but the dead time around the building (the hours spent looking up syntax, the days spent waiting for inter-library loan, the weeks spent stuck on something a passing comment from a supervisor would have fixed in five minutes) is approaching zero. What is left is the building itself, and the building itself is what produces the learning.

Two short examples from my own work, because abstract claims about the cost structure of building need to be cashed out in something concrete.

I spent six years bouncing off mixed models because I could not see clearly what a random-slopes specification was actually doing. The lme4 documentation, like most documentation, is correct in a sense that has been carefully separated from being useful. The textbook treatments fall into the same gap from the other direction — they specify the math precisely without ever showing the math doing the thing it is supposed to be doing. What unstuck me, finally, was building a notebook in which I fitted the same model six different ways, looked at what each version implied for my own data, and asked Claude to walk me through what was different between them. By the end of the afternoon I could explain the specification to a colleague. None of this was reading. All of it was building.

The other example is the one that matters more. While I was building, the same notebook caught a discrepancy between what my paper text said about the analysis and what the analysis was actually computing. Same dataset, same fifteen countries, two slope-extraction methods, two different headline correlations. The text described one method; the analysis used the other; the gap between them was twenty-three percentage points of variance, and it had sat in the paper for fourteen months. Nobody — not me, not any reader, not any of the colleagues I had circulated drafts to — had noticed. The notebook noticed because the notebook was a build, and a build forces you to specify what you are doing precisely enough that the contradiction surfaces. The reading I had done on the topic, no matter how careful, would never have surfaced it.

What this implies for undergraduate education in the next five years is the obvious thing: the curriculum should look more like an apprenticeship and less like a reading list. Specifically: every course should culminate in a project the student builds, with a fifteen-minute oral defence at the end where an examiner picks three of the student's choices to push back on. AI tools are explicitly allowed in the building phase; what is assessed is the student's ability to defend what they built. This is the only assessment regime that survives the AI-can-do-the-output era without collapsing into either grade inflation or a nostalgic return to closed-book exams. It is also the regime that builds the kind of learning the build-to-learn principle has always promised, and that most curricula until now have lacked the resources to deliver.

Three honest acknowledgments before I close.

The first is that none of this is novel. I am describing, in 2026 language, what apprenticeship has always been. The interesting claim is not "apprenticeship works" — every educational tradition that takes itself seriously already knows that — but "apprenticeship has just become affordable for fields that previously could not run it." That affordability claim is testable and contestable. I am betting on it; reasonable people will disagree.

The second is that the tools alone do not produce the learning. They reduce the cost of building to the point where the student's own desire to build becomes the binding constraint. If the desire is absent, the tools just produce faster outputs the student does not understand. If the desire is present, the tools produce learning at a rate the older curriculum could not match. The discourse about AI in education is mostly about the first kind of student. The interesting story is about the second.

The third is the honest limit on the audit version of the practice. The fact that my own build caught a methodological error in my own paper is evidence that build-to-learn produces understanding the reading-only path missed. It is also evidence that the paper had errors no human reader had caught in fourteen months. I do not know which of those readings is more important. I suspect they are the same reading.

[RETYPE — closing line from memory]

The next generation of students will look back at the curriculum I sat through and ask why so much of it was reading. The honest answer is going to be that we could not afford anything else. That answer is no longer true. We should stop using it.
