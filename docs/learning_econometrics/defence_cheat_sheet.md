# Defence Cheat Sheet — What the Paper Shows and What It Doesn't

For tomorrow's seminar. Not theory. Not pedagogy. Just the sentences that hold under pressure and the things to refuse to claim.

The trap to avoid: when someone asks a clean question, *answer it cleanly*. The Q&A failure mode for you is reaching for the rich theoretical context — the dignity thread, the cascade, what welfare communicates — when the questioner asked something narrower. Save the theory for the question that actually invites it. Don't import caveats the questioner didn't ask for; they read as defensiveness even when they're scholarly.

When in doubt, the strongest move is: **state the result cleanly, name one limit, stop talking.** Three sentences. The questioner can follow up if they want more.

---

## What the paper shows

These are the claims you can make confidently. Each is grounded in a specific result in the paper. When asked, lead with one of these — not with theoretical framing.

**1. RTI predicts anti-immigration attitudes across welfare regimes.**  
"In every welfare regime in our 34-country sample, workers in more routine-task-intensive occupations report more anti-immigration attitudes, controlling for age, gender, education, income, and urban residence." (Model 1, β = 0.168, p < 0.001.)

**2. The strength of that relationship varies systematically with welfare context.**  
"The slope of RTI on anti-immigration attitudes is significantly steeper in Liberal welfare regimes than Nordic ones — about 0.13 scale points steeper per standard deviation of RTI, after controls." (Model 2, RTI × Liberal = 0.127, p = 0.003.)

**3. The cross-regime variation tracks decommodification, not spending.**  
"Comparing two measures of welfare on the same 15-country sample: ALMP spending shows essentially no correlation with country-level RTI slopes (r = 0.01), while CWED decommodification shows a strong negative correlation (r = −0.85). It's not how much welfare states spend; it's whether they let workers sustain themselves without market dependence." (Country-level scatter; individual-level confirmation in Model 3, RTI × CWED = −0.059, p = 0.015.)

**4. The asymmetric pattern: damage detectable, solidarity not.**  
"The same model on redistribution support returns a null — RTI predicts slightly more redistribution demand everywhere, but welfare context doesn't moderate that pathway. We replicate this null on ISSP 2006 with a different sample and outcome. Same data, same method, same power — moderation on exclusion, no detectable moderation on solidarity." (Model 5, β = +0.011, p = 0.285; ISSP β = +0.010, p = 0.55.)

**5. The pattern is robust to the obvious confounders we can test.**  
"The interaction survives adding GDP growth and Gini as macro controls. It survives single-country exclusion in the country-level scatter. The individual-level test on 82,000 individuals gives the same direction and significance as the country-level scatter on 15 countries."

That's the empirical core. Five sentences. Practise saying each one cleanly, in your own voice, without reaching for footnotes.

## What the paper doesn't show

These are the limits. State them only when asked, or when honesty about scope strengthens your case — never as pre-emptive hedging.

**1. It's not causal.** Cross-sectional, cross-national. CWED correlates with social trust, union density, electoral systems, ethnic homogeneity, and a dozen other country-level features. Any of those could be doing the work. The within-country test belongs to the thesis design (Danish registry, welfare reform timing).

**2. It doesn't demonstrate the cascade.** The theoretical chapter argues for a multi-step mechanism — identity switching, misattribution, defensive othering. The data shows the endpoint correlation, consistent with the cascade but not identifying any specific step. The mechanism is theorised; the pattern is consistent.

**3. The mechanism could be felt vulnerability or structural occupational position — the data can't distinguish.** RTI is structural. Subjective economic insecurity isn't in the current models. So when we say "automation exposure converts into exclusion," we don't know whether the channel is "I feel afraid" or "people in my occupational position become this way." Both are consistent with the result.

**4. The CWED interaction can't fully isolate decommodification from other welfare-design features.** ALMP is a noisy comparator — it aggregates enabling and punitive programmes that may have opposite political effects. The cleaner claim is "*specific* unemployment-benefit decommodification matters more than aggregate spending effort," not "decommodification, full stop."

**5. The solidarity null is a null in this data, not a proof of impossibility.** What we can say: in the cross-national variation we observe, welfare design doesn't detectably moderate the vulnerability-to-redistribution pathway. What we can't say: solidarity cannot be produced by welfare. Supply-side political conditions (party systems, mobilising organisations, electoral rules) might be necessary additions that this design doesn't capture.

**6. CWED is a paper-entitlement measure, not an encounter-quality measure.** Decommodification scores capture replacement rates, durations, and qualifying conditions — formal features. The dignity mechanism is about what it feels like to encounter the welfare state. CWED is the closest available proxy; it isn't the construct itself.

## Likely Q&A objections — and the cleanest response to each

For each: a one-sentence honest answer, then a one-sentence redirect to what the paper *does* establish. Don't skip the redirect — it's how you keep the conversation on the work's actual ground.

**"How can you say it's the welfare state and not [trust / unions / electoral systems / homogeneity]?"**  
"You can't, fully — the cross-section can't isolate causality. What the ALMP/CWED contrast does is narrow the set: if it were just 'Nordic countries are different,' generic spending should also predict the slope, and it doesn't. The within-country thesis design is what closes the causal gap."

**"Doesn't your robustness narrative depend on a country-level correlation with N=15?"**  
"It does, and the country-level scatter is best read as illustration. The individual-level Model 3 cross-level interaction on 82,000 people gives the same direction and significance and is the more defensible test."

**"Isn't the solidarity null just underpowered?"**  
"The same data, same estimator, same sample size detects the exclusion moderation cleanly. The point estimate on solidarity is in the wrong direction for the symmetric account, and a separate test on ISSP returns the same null on a different sample. Power isn't the right reading."

**"Why CWED rather than total welfare spending?"**  
"Total spending aggregates programmes that operate on different logics — universal income replacement vs. punitive activation. CWED isolates the dimension the theory predicts should matter: whether workers can sustain themselves without market dependence. The contrast with ALMP, where spending is null, is what makes that distinction empirically visible."

**"Why RTI? Why not trade exposure, or perceived insecurity?"**  
"RTI is the canonical structural-occupational-exposure measure in this literature, which lets the result connect to existing comparative work. Trade exposure and subjective measures are robustness checks I'd value next — and the panel-data extensions in the thesis can adjudicate which is the operative channel."

**"What about the voting models that go the other direction?"**  
"That's the supply-side translation point — Liberal regimes in our sample have first-past-the-post electoral systems and weak or absent radical-right parties. The attitudinal mechanism is at the demand side; whether attitudes become votes depends on institutions the paper doesn't claim to model. The voting models in §V.F are descriptive of that distinction, not tests of the asymmetric mechanism."

**"What does this paper add over Gingrich, Stutzmann, Burgoon & Schakel?"**  
"They establish that compensation often fails. This paper specifies *which dimension* of welfare design does the work — decommodification rather than spending — and identifies the asymmetric pattern: welfare context moderates damage but not solidarity. That's a sharper claim about mechanism, not just an additional null on compensation."

## The single sentence that captures the paper

If you have one sentence to leave the room with — for instance, when wrapping up or when someone asks "so what's your contribution":

> "Welfare design shapes how economic disruption converts into exclusion, but doesn't equivalently shape how it converts into solidarity. The dimension that does the work is decommodification, not spending — what the welfare state lets workers sustain themselves through, not how much it costs to provide. The asymmetry is what the data show, and it's harder for the symmetric buffering account to accommodate than for the dignity-based account."

Memorise it. It's three sentences and it's the spine of the paper.

## Things you do not need to do tomorrow

You do not need to defend things the paper doesn't claim. If someone asks about Danish registry data, the cascade mechanism, conditionality vs. generosity, or alternative measures, the right answer is *"that's the thesis"* or *"that's a limit of this design — here's what the design does establish"*. You don't owe the questioner a full account of what your full research programme will eventually do. You owe them clarity about what *this paper* shows.

You do not need to bring up confounders or limits before being asked. Pre-emptive hedging signals weakness. Wait for the question; then answer it cleanly.

You do not need to relitigate the framing. The asymmetric framing is the commitment. If someone challenges it, defend it. Don't volunteer the alternatives you considered.

## Final note

Your strongest move tomorrow is calm, specific, short answers. The numbers are robust. The framing is right. The audit found nothing that breaks the substantive argument. What can break tomorrow is *you* — by overexplaining, by reaching for theoretical context the questioner didn't ask for, by offering caveats unprompted, by getting interesting in directions that take you away from the paper's actual claim.

Trust the work. Speak short sentences. Stop talking when you've answered the question.
