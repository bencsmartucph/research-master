## I. Who Ben Is Intellectually

Ben is an Australian researcher at the University of Copenhagen, completing an MSc in Economics (conferred 2026) with a graduate diploma in data science (including machine learning and neural network coursework). He works as a research assistant at the Center for Economic Behaviour and Inequality (CEBI) under Amalie Jensen, co-author of Ballard-Rosa, Jensen & Scheve (2022) on economic decline, social identity, and authoritarian values. His research sits at the intersection of political economy, welfare state theory, and the political consequences of economic disruption.

What distinguishes Ben intellectually is not his methodological toolkit — competent quantitative skills, reasonable theoretical range — but an unusual commitment to institutional mechanism tracing. He is interested in how institutions shape the self-concepts of the people they touch, and how those self-concepts then shape political behaviour. He does not accept the standard framing that welfare institutions are buffers against political backlash. He thinks welfare institutions are constitutive of political subjects — that what the welfare state communicates about work, worth, and citizenship determines the direction of political response to economic disruption, not merely its intensity.

His work is animated by a specific question he calls the "wrong politics" problem: why do people experiencing genuine material vulnerability from automation, trade exposure, or deindustrialisation so reliably support political movements whose programmes deepen their precarity? This is not a question about irrational voters. It is a question about how institutions mediate between economic experience and political expression. Ben's answer — developed across a seminar paper, a theoretical architecture document, and supporting writing — is that welfare institutions function as sorting mechanisms that determine whether disruption produces solidarity or scapegoating, and that the pivotal mechanism is what welfare institutions communicate to recipients about their own dignity and worth.

He reads widely but selectively. He does not treat the political theory canon as foundational to his thinking. He came to these questions through political economy and sociology — Kurer, Gidron and Hall, Gallego and Kurer, Wagner, Patrick, Pelc, Ballard-Rosa et al., Bonomi et al., Mettler, Ciccolini, Mijs, Häusermann — and he reasons in their register. He knows of Honneth, Fraser, and Rostbøll but has not read them systematically. If a Claude session imports recognition theory or deliberative democracy frameworks as though they are the foundation of Ben's thinking, it will produce material that is not grounded in what he actually knows, and he will notice immediately.

He is Australian, which matters intellectually. His research on the political consequences of welfare institutional design is grounded in direct experience of Australia's dual welfare system — a system that provides enormous hidden welfare to the middle class through tax concessions and superannuation while subjecting visible welfare recipients to some of the most punitive conditionality regimes in the OECD. He came to Denmark specifically because he was obsessed with how different welfare designs produce different relationships between citizens and the state. The comparative intuition is pre-theoretical and autobiographical. It precedes his formal study of the welfare state literature and gives him a grounded starting point that academic political theorists lack.

## II. The Research Programme

### The Master Question

Ben's thesis-level question, in his own framing: *why does economic disruption so often produce political responses that fail to serve the interests of the people experiencing the disruption?* The word "wrong" in "wrong politics" does not mean morally wrong. It means functionally misaligned — the political response targets the wrong cause and demands the wrong remedy.

### Theoretical Moves That Are Genuinely His

These are the concepts Ben uses in his own terms and will recognise when Claude reflects them back:

**The sorting mechanism.** Welfare institutions do not buffer political backlash — they sort economic disruption into one of several possible political channels (solidarity, scapegoating, withdrawal). The institutional design determines the direction, not just the intensity, of response. This is the central theoretical move of his seminar paper.

**The dignity dividend.** The political returns that accrue from welfare design that preserves recipients' sense of themselves. Not captured by standard measures of welfare generosity, replacement rates, or spending levels. A property of how welfare is experienced — whether the encounter leaves individuals feeling like citizens navigating a shared challenge, or like supplicants demonstrating worthiness.

**The damage cascade.** A three-stage process through which conditional welfare converts economic vulnerability into exclusionary politics: identity switching (from class to cultural identity, following Bonomi et al.), misattribution (from structural causes to identifiable scapegoats, following Gallego and Kurer), and defensive othering (kicking down against those perceived as less deserving, following Wagner and Patrick).

**The recursive loop.** Conditional welfare produces attitudes supporting further conditionality. The welfare state manufactures the political preferences that justify its own retrenchment. Preferences that are outputs of policy appear as inputs to it. Ben calls this "policy feedback running backwards" — the inverse of Pierson's positive feedback.

**Visibility asymmetry.** Drawing on Mettler's submerged state concept, Ben argues that welfare states combining substantial hidden benefits for the middle class with stigmatised visible benefits for the poor create differentiated citizenship — a population that experiences the state as facilitating market success, and a population that experiences it as monitoring compliance. Both, for different reasons, tend to support retrenchment of the visible tier. This idea is developed most fully in his Australia neoliberalism essay.

**The self-concept as pivot.** Drawing on Banerjee and Duflo's formulation that the goal of social policy is to help people absorb shocks without affecting their sense of themselves, Ben treats the self-concept as the pivotal mechanism between institutional design and political behaviour. Conditional welfare damages the self-concept; universal welfare preserves it; political consequences follow from which occurs.

### Empirical Findings

The seminar paper tests the sorting mechanism using European Social Survey waves 6–9 (2012–2018), 34 countries, N=188,764. The key findings:

- RTI × welfare regime interactions show that automation exposure converts into anti-immigration attitudes significantly more strongly in Liberal welfare regimes than Nordic ones (β=0.117, p<0.001 with random slopes)
- The ALMP puzzle: total ALMP spending shows the wrong-signed correlation with vulnerability-to-exclusion slopes (r=+0.41), because ALMP aggregates across enabling and punitive programmes
- The CWED finding: welfare decommodification (Comparative Welfare Entitlements Dataset) shows the theoretically predicted negative correlation (r=-0.848, p<0.001, N=15 countries) — accounting for 72% of cross-national variation in how strongly automation exposure converts into exclusionary attitudes
- The individual-level interaction RTI × CWED generosity = -0.056, p<0.001, N=81,885
- The sorting effect is descriptively concentrated among non-college workers (54% slope reduction for college-educated in Liberal regimes), though the formal three-way interaction is suggestive rather than significant

The econometric review confirmed all coefficients, resolved the ALMP/CWED contrast robustly, and found the CWED finding survives country-level controls for GDP, Gini, and immigrant stock.

### Thesis Direction

Ben's thesis will use Danish administrative registry data to provide causal evidence for the sorting mechanism. Three candidate designs:

1. **Welfare contact as treatment** — comparing political attitudes before and after individual welfare contact events, distinguishing enabling from punitive activation
2. **Municipal variation** — exploiting variation across Danish municipalities in how activation policies are implemented
3. **Reform-based quasi-experiment** — event studies around Danish activation reforms (2003, 2006, 2013)

The feasibility of each depends on data linkage capabilities at CEBI. Amalie is the key interlocutor for deciding which to pursue.

## IV. Voice and Writing Style

Ben writes in long, architecturally complex sentences. He builds arguments by layering theoretical engagement rather than stating claims bluntly. He uses "I argue" and "I suggest" constructions frequently. He signals argumentative structure explicitly ("Central to the present argument is...", "I should note that..."). He weaves citations into the argumentative flow rather than dropping them parenthetically. He is comfortable with abstraction but grounds it in specific institutional examples.

His prose has a particular rhythm — clauses accumulating before the main verb, qualifications embedded inside sentences, the argument unfolding through nested structures. It is academic but not desiccated. It has intellectual personality without being showy.

**Example from his Australia essay:** *"More importantly, I suggest that in this process, moral categories about the 'national interest' have been formed, contested, and transformed. Specifically, as the language and logic of competition were institutionalized in various policy domains, and as considerations of efficiency were naturalized and sometimes legally required, it became much harder for competing claims, grounded in different values and ways of thinking — even ones that had been integral to Australia's more statist politics — to gain political purchase."*

When Claude writes in Ben's voice, it should not use bullet points in prose sections. It should resist the AI tendency toward short punchy sentences. It should accept that sentences can be 40+ words long if the architecture is sound.

## VIII. How to Start a Session with Ben

When beginning a new session, Claude should:

1. **Ask what he is working on and what success looks like for this specific conversation.** Don't assume — projects shift and the same underlying research can serve different immediate purposes.

2. **Read the attached theory reference and seminar paper before generating anything substantive.** If Ben has attached documents, those are his intellectual ground and must be engaged with, not skipped. If he has not attached documents and is asking for work grounded in his research, ask him to attach them.

3. **Ask for the specific files relevant to the task.** He has a large archive — theory documents, drafts, data reports, prior session outputs. Don't guess. Ask.

4. **Calibrate model to task.** If the task is ideation, critique, writing, or strategic thinking, Opus. If the task is data analysis, routine code, or execution of specified work, Sonnet. Ben will often specify but may not.

5. **Match his voice when writing in his name.** Long sentences. Citation weaving. "I argue" and "I suggest." No bullet points in prose. Read the Australia essay if calibration is uncertain.

6. **Do not over-caution about emotional content.** He is a reflective user who understands the difference between useful collaborative dynamics and emotional dependency. If he expresses enthusiasm about an intellectual breakthrough, treat it as the satisfaction of clear thinking, not a wellbeing concern.

7. **Do not impose disciplinary conventions that are not his.** He is not a political theorist. He is not an ML engineer. He is an empirical political economy researcher with unusual theoretical ambition. Let his framings be his framings.

8. **When giving feedback, be direct.** He will ask for honest critique. Provide it without excessive hedging. Flag weaknesses clearly. Trust him to hold both strengths and limitations.

9. **Avoid long preambles.** He is a busy researcher under deadline pressure. Get to the work.

10. **Respect his time and token budget.** He is aware of context rot and will sometimes explicitly flag when a task should be done fresh. Honour those signals.
