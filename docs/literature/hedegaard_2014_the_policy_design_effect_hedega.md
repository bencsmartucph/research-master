# ⭐(Hedegaard, 2014) The Policy Design Effect Hedegaard argues that welfare policy designs (universal, contribution-based, or selective) affect public support through proximity mechanisms at the micro-level. The central thesis is that being "proximate" to recipients of welfare benefits positively influences attitudes toward spending on those benefits, but this effect varies systematically based on policy design type. Specifically, proximity effects are strongest for selective policies, moderate for contribution-based policies, and weakest for universal policies.

The paper assumes that policy feedback effects operate not just at macro levels but through individual experiences and relationships. An implicit assumption is that proximity creates both self-interest (incentive effects) and interpretive effects (sympathy, altered perceptions) that shape attitudes. The argument is compelling as it provides a micro-level explanation for observed patterns in welfare support that macro theories alone cannot account for.

## Primary Theories Used

### 1. Policy Feedback Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Policy feedback theory posits that policies, once implemented, reshape politics by altering resources, incentives, and interpretations available to citizens and officials. Hedegaard builds on Pierson's (1993) distinction between incentive effects and interpretive effects. Incentive effects lead citizens to act in self-interest when forming attitudes, resulting in positive views toward benefits they directly or potentially gain from. Interpretive effects shape how benefits and recipients are perceived, affecting attitudes through changing frameworks. The theory suggests that policies create their own constituencies and interpretive frameworks that influence subsequent political developments, creating path dependencies that shape future policy options. In welfare contexts, this means different policy designs generate different patterns of support based on how they structure citizen experiences and interpretations.

**Direct Quotes**:

- "Pierson (1993) was the first to theorise the idea of feedback from welfare policies to the general public as an explanation for welfare attitudes. This policy feedback, Pierson argues, affects attitudes through incentive effects and interpretive effects" (p.368)
- "The interpretive effect shapes how social benefits are perceived and thus informs attitudes towards them. Pierson (1993, 619) is less specific on the interpretive effect, but does argue that the design of a policy might affect attitudes: 'The specific design of programs may heighten the visibility of some social and political connections while obscuring others.'" (p.369)

**Operationalization and Application**: Hedegaard operationalizes policy feedback by categorizing Danish welfare policies into three design types (selective, contribution-based, and universal) and measuring attitude differences across these types. He then tests whether proximity to beneficiaries (personal experience, family ties, or friendship ties) influences attitudes differently across policy types. The effectiveness of this operationalization lies in its ability to test both incentive effects (self-interest) and interpretive effects (perceptions) in a single study through multiple orders of proximity.

**Relevance to My Thesis**: The application of policy feedback theory at the micro-level offers a valuable framework for my research on how welfare institutions mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics. The dual pathways of incentive and interpretive effects parallel my interest in status stabilization and moral framing, suggesting that welfare institutions shape both material interests and moral frameworks. This micro-level approach helps explain cross-national variations in populist responses to similar economic pressures.

### 2. Self-Interest Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Self-interest theory suggests that welfare attitudes are primarily guided by personal, material interests. Hedegaard discusses both narrow and broader definitions of self-interest. The narrow definition focuses on "personal, short-term and material interests" (p.367), while broader interpretations incorporate potential gains from welfare protection or indirect benefits to family members. The theory proposes that individuals adopt social ideologies most congruent with their life experiences and material interests. A key insight is that self-interest effects are more likely to be found at the policy level, where connections between specific benefits and personal gains are more direct, rather than at abstract ideological levels. This theory provides a rationalistic explanation for welfare attitudes, suggesting support comes from potential beneficiaries and opposition from those who bear costs without benefits.

**Direct Quotes**:

- "According to Hasenfeld and Rafferty (1989, 1031), this shapes attitudes though a process whereby 'people adopt social ideologies that best explain and are most congruent with their life experiences'" (p.367)
- "The explanatory power of self-interest has, however, been poor: 'Short-term, material self-interest, it turns out in study after study, has remarkably little to do with public opinion on a wide range of political issues'" (p.367)
- "Kumlin (2007) argues that there is no natural connection between receiving a single benefit and attitude change towards abstract and ideologically laden issues like the role of government or redistribution. Therefore a self-interest effect is more likely to be found at a policy level, where the connection between spending on a social benefit and the personal gains is much more direct." (p.368)

**Operationalization and Application**: The author operationalizes self-interest through proximity measures, particularly the 1st order (direct recipients) and 2nd order (family members of recipients) proximity categories, which capture direct and indirect self-interest respectively. By comparing these groups with the 3rd order proximity (friends of recipients, who have no material self-interest), the study isolates self-interest effects from purely interpretive effects.

**Relevance to My Thesis**: This nuanced approach to self-interest helps my thesis examine how welfare states might mitigate economic vulnerability's translation into populist politics. The distinction between direct material benefits and interpretive effects aligns with my dual pathway model, where status stabilization (material) and moral framing (interpretive) operate simultaneously. The varying effectiveness of welfare regimes in preventing populist voting may depend on how they satisfy both material needs and status/identity concerns.

### 3. Deservingness Heuristics Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Deservingness heuristics theory addresses how individuals use mental shortcuts to judge whether welfare recipients deserve government assistance. According to Van Oorschot's framework, people apply five criteria (control, need, identity, attitude, reciprocity) when making deservingness judgments, with control over one's situation and identity/proximity to the recipients being most significant. These heuristics are especially influential when individuals form opinions about unfamiliar topics. The deservingness framework explains systematic differences in public support for different beneficiary groups (e.g., elderly typically seen as more deserving than unemployed). It helps explain why policies targeting similar needs (e.g., unemployment benefits vs. social assistance) receive differing levels of support based on how they frame recipients' deservingness.

**Direct Quotes**:

- "Studies show that the mental shortcuts are most influential when individuals form opinions about something that they have little or no personal experience or knowledge of (Petersen et al. 2011)" (p.368)
- "Studies by Van Oorschot (2000; 2006) argue that individuals apply five criteria (control, need, identity, attitude, reciprocity) when making judgments of deservingness, of which the recipients' control of their situation and identification with the recipients are the most important." (p.368)
- "In Van Oorschot's argument for the identity criteria, the line between the deserving and undeserving groups are drawn between ethnic minorities, asylum-seekers and illegal foreigners, on the one side, and the general population, on the other." (p.373)

**Operationalization and Application**: Hedegaard examines Van Oorschot's identity criterion to connect proximity with deservingness perceptions. He extends the identity concept to include multiple levels of identification (family, friends, fellow citizens) and tests whether closer identification increases support for benefits. The author shows that proximity effects are strongest for selective policies, suggesting deservingness perceptions are most influential when policies single out specific target groups.

**Relevance to My Thesis**: Deservingness theory provides an important framework for understanding how welfare institutions shape moral judgments about economic vulnerability. The ways different welfare regimes structure deservingness criteria could explain why economic grievances translate into populist politics in some contexts but not others. The identity dimension of deservingness particularly connects to my interest in how welfare institutions affect status perceptions and in-group/out-group dynamics in populist contexts.

## Overview of Data Source and Empirical Methodology

**Detailed Description**: The study uses survey data collected by Analyse Danmark from January 23-27, 2012, with 1,134 respondents in Denmark. The data was weighted to match national averages on sex, age, urbanization, and voting preferences. The dependent variable measures attitudes toward spending on five social benefits (social assistance, incapacity benefit, unemployment benefit, state education grant, and state pension), asking whether spending is "too generous," "suitable," or "too low."

The key independent variable measures proximity to recipients in four "orders": 1st order (direct recipients), 2nd order (family members of recipients), 3rd order (close friends of recipients), and 4th order (no close relation to recipients). Control variables include sex, personal income (six categories), education (years of education), ideology (left-wing vs. right-wing voting), and age (five categorical groups).

The analytical methodology includes:

1. Bivariate analysis using "opinion balances" (percentage answering "too generous" subtracted from percentage answering "too low")
2. Logistic regression comparing respondents who answered "too low" to those who answered "suitable," controlling for demographic factors

**Reproducibility and Adaptability**: The methodological description is reasonably transparent, detailing variable construction, sample size, and analytical approaches. However, some aspects could be more detailed for full reproducibility, such as specific survey question wording for proximity measures and complete regression specifications.

The methodology is adaptable for my thesis research on welfare state effects on populist politics. I could use a similar proximity-based approach to examine how different types of welfare experiences affect populist attitudes. The ordering of proximity (direct experience, family, friends, none) provides a valuable framework for examining how welfare institutions create different feedback effects. ## Findings and Evidence

**Summary of Key Findings**:

1. Universal policies (state education grant, state pension) show minimal or no effect of proximity on support for spending
2. Selective policies (social assistance, incapacity benefit) show strong proximity effects, with recipients and their close contacts expressing much more support for spending than those without proximity
3. Contribution-based policy (unemployment benefit) shows a moderate proximity effect, mainly for direct recipients
4. These patterns persist after controlling for demographic factors and ideology

**Supporting Evidence**:

- For selective social assistance: 1st order proximity shows an odds ratio of 3.2 (p<0.10), 2nd order 3.0 (p<0.05), and 3rd order 2.8 (p<0.05) compared to reference group
- For selective incapacity benefit: 1st order proximity shows odds ratio of 6.2 (p<0.01), 2nd order 1.9 (p<0.05), and 3rd order 1.6 (p<0.10)
- For contribution-based unemployment benefit: only 1st order proximity shows significant effect with odds ratio of 2.2 (p<0.01)
- For universal benefits (education grant and state pension): no statistically significant effects of proximity
- Opinion balance scores show dramatic differences: social assistance (-20), incapacity benefit (14), unemployment benefit (16), state education grant (26), and state pension (58)

**Implications for My Thesis**: These findings strongly support my thesis's dual pathway model. The differential effects of proximity across policy types suggest that welfare institutions structure both material interests and interpretive frameworks. Universal welfare appears to create broad solidarity that transcends proximity, while selective welfare reinforces in-group/out-group divisions unless mitigated by personal contact. This helps explain why universal welfare regimes may better prevent economic grievances from translating into populist politics—they avoid creating the status threats and moral boundary-drawing that selective systems generate. The strong effect for 3rd order proximity (friends) with selective policies is particularly relevant, as it shows how welfare design affects moral perceptions beyond self-interest.

## Discussion

**Interpretation of Findings**: Hedegaard interprets the differential impact of proximity across policy types as evidence that welfare design shapes both incentive effects (self-interest) and interpretive effects (perceptions of recipients). Universal policies generate broad support regardless of proximity because they avoid creating distinctions between deserving and undeserving groups. Selective policies create stronger proximity effects because they generate both material interests for beneficiaries and negative perceptions among those without proximity. The contribution-based policy shows moderate effects, consistent with its hybrid nature. These findings explain the "policy design effect" at the micro level, demonstrating how individual experiences and relationships mediate policy feedback effects.

**Connections to Other Research**: The author connects findings to several research strands. The results align with research by Soss (1999) and Kumlin and Rothstein (2005) showing that interactions with universal institutions build social capital while selective institutions erode it. The findings extend Van Oorschot's (2000; 2006) deservingness framework by specifying how proximity affects the identity criterion at multiple levels. The paper also relates to Rothstein's (1998) procedural justice theory, suggesting selective policies generate perceptions of arbitrary judgment while universal policies create perceptions of fair inclusion.

**Relevance to My Research**: Hedegaard's micro-level explanation of the policy design effect provides a crucial mechanism for my thesis. It helps explain how welfare institutions mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics by showing that institutional design affects both material interests and moral frameworks. The finding that proximity effects are strongest for selective policies suggests that welfare chauvinism and in-group/out-group distinctions—central to populist rhetoric—may be partly institutional products rather than just cultural attitudes. This supports my hypothesis that universal welfare regimes provide greater resilience against populism during economic downturns by avoiding the status threats and moral boundary-drawing that selective regimes generate.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Welfare design creates different feedback patterns**: The policy design effect operates through differential impacts of proximity on attitudes. Universal policies show minimal proximity effects, suggesting they avoid creating in-group/out-group distinctions that feed populist politics. This is directly relevant to my thesis's focus on how welfare regimes mediate economic vulnerability's translation into populism.
2. **Proximity operates through dual pathways**: Being close to welfare recipients affects attitudes through both incentive effects (self-interest) and interpretive effects (perceptions, sympathy). This mirrors my thesis's dual pathway model of status stabilization and moral framing, providing empirical support for how welfare institutions shape both material interests and moral frameworks.
3. **Interpretive effects exist beyond self-interest**: The significant effect of 3rd order proximity (friends) for selective policies demonstrates that welfare design shapes moral perceptions beyond material interests. This supports my argument that welfare institutions affect moral framing of economic outcomes, not just material compensation.
4. **Policy design can override target group perceptions**: The similar attitudes toward unemployment benefit and incapacity benefit, despite differences in perceived deservingness of target groups, suggests institutional design can shape public perceptions more powerfully than pre-existing group stereotypes. This offers hope that well-designed welfare institutions can mitigate cultural backlash against vulnerable groups.
5. **Proximity as a linking mechanism**: As Hedegaard concludes, "proximity could be one of these mechanisms" linking norms, interests, and institutional arrangements in welfare attitudes. This provides a theoretical bridge between macro-institutional theories and individual-level political behavior that my thesis on populism can build upon.
