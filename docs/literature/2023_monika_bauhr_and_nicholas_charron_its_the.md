## Executive Summary

This research paper byexamines how the quality of government (QoG) affects support for populist radical right (PRR) parties, specifically analyzing Marine Le Pen's electoral support in the 2017 and 2022 French presidential elections. The authors demonstrate that regions with lower QoG show higher support for Le Pen, and importantly, that decreases in QoG between 2017 and 2022 correlate with increased support for her candidacy. Using both regional-level data and individual-level survey responses, the authors provide compelling evidence that perceptions of government quality—particularly regarding public service delivery—significantly predict far-right voting patterns, even when controlling for traditional explanatory factors like economic conditions and immigration.

The study contributes to understanding the rise of far-right populism by highlighting the importance of "output legitimacy" (how citizens experience public services) rather than just "input legitimacy" (representation and political participation) or traditional economic explanations. The authors introduce the concept of "welfare insecurity" as a key driver of far-right support, suggesting that dysfunctional public services may fuel perceptions of scarcity, competition, and relative deprivation that make citizens more receptive to nativist messaging.

## Motivation and Research Contribution

The authors position their research in the context of the rapid expansion of populist radical right parties across Europe, noting this represents "one of the most important transformations of Western, democratic politics in recent decades" (p.1). While existing literature has explored economic and sociocultural factors behind this rise, the authors identify a critical gap: insufficient attention to how the quality of government affects populist support.

Their core contention focuses on how low-quality public service delivery creates welfare insecurity that drives voters toward the far right:

> "This study suggests that declining quality of government increases support for populist radical right parties. QoG allows citizens to gain access to well-functioning public services but also reduces the extent to which certain individuals and groups gain preferential treatment in their interaction with services such as schools, hospitals, or the police." (p.1)
> 

The authors differentiate their work from prior research by emphasizing "output legitimacy" over "input legitimacy":

> "While supply-side explanations tend to focus on factors such as the role of money in politics, the growth of technocracy, electoral rules manipulated by elites and the accountability deficits produced by European integration (Tucker 2019; Berman 2021). In other words, they tend to focus on perceptions of 'input legitimacy', while insufficient attention has been granted to the importance of 'output legitimacy' (e.g. Scharpf, 1988), or how citizens perceive public services in their day-to-day interaction with service providers." (p.2)
> 

This focus on public service quality represents a meaningful contribution to understanding populist support mechanisms:

> "A focus on welfare insecurity and the role of quality of government would thereby contribute to complement and enrich current research that tend to focus on the importance of economic insecurity (Guriev and Papaioannou 2022) or sociocultural grievances (Norris and Inglehart 2019)." (p.2)
> 

## Theoretical Framework and Literature Review

### Conceptualizing Populist Radical Right Parties

The authors frame PRR parties as characterized by nativism, anti-immigration sentiment, and a conception of "the people" that stands in opposition to "the elite." Drawing on established literature, they explain:

> "These parties derive their appeal from particular policy concerns, and in particular an anti-immigrant agenda (Eatwell, 1998; Backlund and Jungar 2019; Abts et al., 2021), but also from a general promise to bridge the gap between the political establishment and the people, and give voice to those who are not heard (Mudde and Kaltwasser, 2012; Mudde 2004; Van Kessel 2015)." (p.1)
> 

They note that membership in the PRR conception of "the people" is "typically viewed as highly restricted to natives" (p.2), underscoring how ethnic-based favoritism aligns with PRR ideology.

### Quality of Government and Its Dimensions

The paper defines quality of government (QoG) as comprising three interconnected dimensions:

1. Quality: The effectiveness and performance of public services
2. Impartiality: Equal treatment regardless of personal characteristics
3. Corruption: Absence of exploitation of public office for private gain

The authors operationalize QoG as "impartial (Rothstein and Teorell, 2008), low corrupt and high-quality public service provision" (p.2). This multi-dimensional conceptualization allows them to analyze which aspects of government quality most significantly impact far-right support.

### Mechanisms Connecting QoG and Far-Right Support

The authors propose three interrelated causal mechanisms:

1. **Welfare insecurity**: Low QoG creates perceptions of scarcity and competition for public services:

> "Low quality of government may lead to a sense of scarcity, competition and relative deprivation, or welfare insecurity. This may fuel perceptions of welfare abusers or citizens that are not part of the people (immigrants) gaining access to services that they are not entitled to at the expense of 'the people'." (p.2)
> 
1. **Elite mistrust**: Poor public services undermine trust in government:

> "Perceptions and experiences of deficiencies in essential public services may fuel elite mistrust (Klašnja et al., 2016; De Blok and Kumlin 2022; Rothstein 2009) and support for nativist and exclusionary policy alternatives (Agerberg 2017; Hanley and Sikk, 2016), while programmatic and well-functioning public services may contribute to reduce the divide between insiders and outsiders (Bauhr and Charron 2018), and undermine demand for ethnic favoritism and even welfare chauvinism (Ennser-Jedenastik 2018)." (p.2)
> 
1. **Party agency**: The strategic reorientation of the French far-right toward redistributive policies:

> "The link may also partly be explained by party agency, in this case the decision of the radical right party (Rassemblement Nationale, 'RN') and its front figure, Marine Le Pen, to move the party's manifesto away from the free market toward redistributive policies (Ivaldi 2015; Espagno and François, 2015)." (p.2)
> 

### Bridging Economic and Cultural Explanations

The authors position their theory as complementary to existing demand-side explanations, offering a more comprehensive understanding of far-right support:

> "Demand-side explanations tend to focus in particular on economic and socioeconomic changes that drive economic insecurity, such as i.e. globalization, competition from low wage countries, technological progress or economic crises (Mughan 2007: Margalit 2019; Rodrik 2018; Guriev and Papaioannou, 2022). Yet, insecurity or perceptions of relative deprivation, inequality and scarcity may equally be driven by deficient basic public services, what we term 'welfare insecurity'" (p.2)
> 

This bridges economic and cultural explanations, suggesting that quality of service delivery affects both material well-being and perceptions of fairness and community.

## Research Design and Methodology

### Case Selection and Justification

The authors leverage a unique research opportunity in the French presidential elections:

> "Our sample relies on data from the 2017 and 2022 French presidential elections, where Emmanuelle Macron and Marie le Pen were the top two candidates of the first rounds of each election...The two candidates met again in 2022, with the primary difference being that Macron was the incumbent in 2022." (p.2-3)
> 

This case selection provides significant methodological advantages:

1. The same candidates competed in both elections, controlling for candidate-specific effects
2. France exhibits substantial within-country variation in government quality
3. Le Pen's party explicitly shifted toward redistribution between elections
4. The availability of regional-level QoG data and individual-level survey responses

### Data Sources and Measurements

The study employs multiple data sources:

1. **European Quality of Government Index (EQI)**: Survey data from 2017 and 2021 covering all NUTS 2 regions in France, with over 20,800 respondents
2. **Official French election results**: Second-round vote shares for Le Pen in 2017 and 2022
3. **Socioeconomic controls**: Data on economic development, unemployment, population size, and foreign-born population at the regional level

The authors construct several key variables:

- **QoG perceptions index**: Composite measure combining quality, impartiality, and corruption perceptions
- **QoG change**: First-difference in QoG perceptions between 2017 and 2021
- **Vote share change**: First-difference in Le Pen's vote share between 2017 and 2022
- **Individual-level party support**: Self-reported voting intentions from EQI surveys

### Analytical Strategy and Models

The paper employs four complementary analytical approaches:

1. **Cross-sectional regional analysis**: OLS regressions examining how QoG affects Le Pen's 2022 vote share
2. **Regional change models**: First-difference models analyzing how changes in QoG predict changes in Le Pen support
3. **Pillar-specific analysis**: Separate models for each QoG dimension (quality, impartiality, corruption)
4. **Individual-level logit models**: Testing how personal QoG perceptions affect likelihood of supporting Le Pen

The authors incorporate extensive controls and robustness checks, including:

- Controls for capital region and overseas territories
- Alternative specifications using first-round results
- Comparison of results at different administrative levels (NUTS 2 and NUTS 3)
- Testing individual QoG dimensions separately

## Key Findings

### Regional Patterns of QoG and Far-Right Support

The authors' analysis reveals a strong negative relationship between QoG and support for Le Pen:

> "We observe that the perceptions of QoG are the most prominent predictor of the share of Le Pen vote in the second round across French Regions in 2022, and account for more variation than levels of economic development, unemployment rates, share of foreign-born citizens, and population size, inter alia." (p.1-2)
> 

Quantifying this relationship, they note:

> "Going from the lowest EQI score to the highest is estimated to result in a roughly 26.1% lower vote share for Le Pen (model 2), which is nearly the difference between the vote share in the high and low NUTS 2 regions (Bretagne, 33% versus Guyane, 61%)." (p.3)
> 

The bivariate correlation is particularly strong:

> "In both cases, we find a strong bivariate relationship in the direction expected (r = -0.75), particularly when we remove the residual outlier, the capital region of Il de France, where the correlation is -0.87 (p < .001)." (p.3)
> 

### Temporal Changes in QoG and Electoral Support

The authors find compelling evidence for their second hypothesis regarding changes over time:

> "Interestingly, while we find that all change variables are in the expected direction, in the full model, we find that only changes in the collective perceptions of institutional quality matter in explaining significant changes in the Le Pen vote over time among Regions." (p.4)
> 

This finding is particularly important as it suggests a causal relationship rather than just correlation:

> "We find that where the collective perceptions of the quality of public services in particular, increased (decreased) the support for Le Pen decreased (increased) between the two elections." (p.4)
> 

### Differential Impact of QoG Dimensions

When decomposing QoG into its constituent dimensions, the quality of services emerges as the strongest predictor:

> "Comparing these total effects coefficients across models 3-5 demonstrates that the 'quality' pillar explains a slightly greater amount of variance in the 2022 le Pen vote share, ceteris paribus." (p.3)
> 

> "In this case, we find that there is a negative coefficient for each, as expected, yet only for the changes in quality do we observe a significant effect (p = .005), holding constant past levels of the dependent variable and each respective EQI pillar from 2017 along with the capital and overseas regions." (p.4)
> 

This finding suggests that citizens' direct experiences with service quality may be more important than perceptions of corruption or impartiality.

### Individual-Level Confirmation

The individual-level analysis strongly supports the regional findings:

> "Indeed, we find that higher corruption perceptions, and lower perceptions of impartiality and public service quality, as well as recent changes in these collective attitudes, are significantly related with the likelihood of supporting the far right in France, even accounting for socio-demographic and economic factors." (p.2)
> 

> "We observe that people living in regions with positive changes in regional QoG attitudes toward public services from 2017 to 2021 are significantly less likely to vote far right in 2021, all things being equal." (p.5)
> 

These individual-level results reinforce the regional patterns and suggest the relationship is not merely ecological.

## Theoretical Implications

### Reframing the "Welfare Chauvinism" Paradox

The findings address an apparent paradox in far-right politics: why would low government quality drive support for parties advocating ethnic-based favoritism in service delivery? The authors explain:

> "From this perspective it is perhaps somewhat surprising that low quality of government would drive the right-wing populist vote, since at least ethnic-based favoritism in public service delivery could be seen as a desirable end as long as it benefits the native population." (p.2)
> 

Their explanation centers on how poor services create perceptions of scarcity that increase demand for in-group favoritism:

> "Populist radical right voters may perceive that non-deserving immigrants gain preferential treatment in access to public services, depleting resources that could be used to provide better services for more deserving citizens." (p.2)
> 

This transforms governmental inefficiency into a perceived zero-sum competition between natives and immigrants.

### The Importance of "Output Legitimacy"

A central theoretical contribution is highlighting the role of service delivery experiences in shaping political preferences:

> "While much of the research on support for far-right parties and candidates has focused on the supply of what could be characterized as 'input legitimacy', or the extent to which systems are geared towards being responsive to citizens concern as a result of political participation (see i.e. Berman 2021), far less attention has been granted to the importance of quality of government (Agerberg 2017), or 'output legitimacy' more broadly." (p.6)
> 

The authors argue that both dimensions must be considered:

> "Thus, while general preferences for nativism and ethnic based favoritism partly defines populist radical right parties' political agenda on the 'input' side of politics (Ennser-Jedenastik 2018; Abts et al., 2021), such demand may be stifled by providing high quality and impartial public service delivery on the 'output' side of politics, i.e. in citizens encounter with public services." (p.1)
> 

### Welfare Insecurity as a Distinct Explanatory Factor

The concept of "welfare insecurity" represents a meaningful theoretical innovation:

> "Yet, insecurity or perceptions of relative deprivation, inequality and scarcity may equally be driven by deficient basic public services, what we term 'welfare insecurity' (cf Van Hootegem et al., 2021; Vlandas and Halikiopoulou, 2022; Ennser-Jedenastik 2018; Swank, 2003)." (p.2)
> 

This concept provides a bridge between economic and cultural explanations of far-right support:

> "A focus on welfare insecurity and the role of quality of government would thereby contribute to complement and enrich current research that tend to focus on the importance of economic insecurity (Guriev and Papaioannou 2022) or sociocultural grievances (Norris and Inglehart 2019)." (p.2)
> 

## Methodology Critique and Limitations

### Endogeneity Concerns

While the first-difference design helps address some causal concerns, potential endogeneity issues remain:

> "As models 1-3 introduce concerns of endogeneity, we then test whether the macro-level change variables from Table 2 can account for intended vote choice in the 2021 survey, controlling also for people residing in the capital and overseas regions." (p.5)
> 

The authors acknowledge these limitations:

> "While admittedly not causal evidence, we consider the findings in Table 4 as an empirical implication of our main hypotheses." (p.5)
> 

The paper would be strengthened by more explicit discussions of reverse causality possibilities, where support for far-right parties might affect QoG perceptions.

### Measurement Validity

The study relies heavily on perceptions of QoG rather than objective measures. While perceptions are certainly relevant for voting behavior, the authors might have complemented this with objective service quality indicators.

The authors acknowledge potential measurement issues regarding the overseas territories:

> "We acknowledge that a dummy variable may not account for all possible heterogeneity between mainland and overseas regions, in particular the observe differences in the negative slopes observed in Fig. 1." (p.3, footnote 5)
> 

### Alternative Explanations

While the authors control for several confounding factors, they might have more extensively addressed:

1. The role of media consumption in shaping both QoG perceptions and far-right support
2. Regional cultural and historical differences that might influence both variables
3. The specific impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on service delivery perceptions

## Policy Implications

The findings suggest several important policy implications, though the authors do not extensively develop these:

1. **Improving public service quality as a counter-populism strategy**: Focusing on service delivery effectiveness could help reduce far-right support.
2. **Addressing perceptions versus reality**: Since perceptions of service quality matter, governments should attend to both objective improvements and public communication about services.
3. **Targeting specific service domains**: Given that quality emerged as most significant among QoG dimensions, policymakers might prioritize visible service improvements over anti-corruption or impartiality measures.
4. **Regional targeting**: Resources could be directed toward regions showing declining QoG perceptions to mitigate far-right electoral gains.

## Research Connections and Future Directions

The paper connects to several important research streams:

1. **Welfare chauvinism literature**: Building on Ennser-Jedenastik's (2018) work on welfare chauvinism in PRR platforms.
2. **Institutional trust research**: Extending work by Klašnja et al. (2016) and Rothstein (2009) on how service quality affects political trust.
3. **Party strategy studies**: Complementing analyses of the RN's strategic shift toward redistributive policies (Ivaldi 2015).

Future research directions might include:

1. **Cross-national comparisons**: Testing whether QoG effects on far-right support generalize across European countries.
2. **Domain-specific analysis**: Identifying which public services most strongly influence political preferences.
3. **Experimental designs**: Using experimental methods to establish causality more definitively.
4. **Intervention studies**: Evaluating whether targeted service improvements reduce far-right support.

The authors conclude with a call for incorporating QoG in future populism research:

> "We encourage future research to investigate this relationship in additional contexts and times and to include quality of government as a key correlate in studies seeking to explain support for PRR parties." (p.6)
> 

## Conclusion

Bauhr and Charron's paper makes a significant contribution to understanding far-right support by highlighting how declining government quality—particularly public service delivery—fuels populist voting. Their mixed-methods approach, combining regional and individual-level analyses across two presidential elections, provides compelling evidence for this relationship.

The concept of "welfare insecurity" offers a valuable theoretical bridge between economic and cultural explanations for populism. It suggests that effective public services may reduce support for nativist, exclusionary politics by diminishing perceptions of scarcity and competition that make citizens receptive to populist messaging.

By emphasizing "output legitimacy" alongside more traditional explanatory factors, the authors enrich our understanding of populism's electoral success and suggest potential policy interventions to address its root causes. Their findings indicate that investments in public service quality may be as important as economic policies or cultural interventions in addressing the populist challenge to liberal democracy.
