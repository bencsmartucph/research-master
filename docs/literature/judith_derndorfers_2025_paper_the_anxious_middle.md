## Executive Summary

## Introduction and Theoretical Framework

Derndorfer situates her research within the ongoing debate about the rise of populism in Western democracies, focusing specifically on the middle class and radical right parties. The author frames her work against two competing explanations for RRP support:

1. **Cultural backlash thesis**: Voters support RRPs because they oppose progressive cultural values.
2. **Economic insecurity thesis**: Voters turn to RRPs due to economic factors.

> "The recent decline of the middle class in many Western European countries has been portrayed as a threat to democracy. One reason for this is that radical right parties (RRP), whose ideology often stands in contrast to liberal democracy (Mudde, 2007), allure to voters dissatisfied with the established mainstream parties. With a shrinking middle class, RRP may gain support from discontent voters who face status anxiety." (p. 1)
> 

The author identifies several economic explanations for political backlash:

> "First, the most prominent economic reason for the political backlash is attributed to globalisation shocks, such as increased immigration and refugees, job displacement due to trade, and distributional struggles between the winners and losers of globalisation (Rodrik, 2018). Second, scholars discuss the role of increased labour market polarisation and automation leading to the job losses for certain occupational groups (e.g., routine jobs) and middle-educated workers (Im et al., 2019; Kurer & Palier, 2019). Finally, and more recently, scholars point towards the fact that rising income inequality increases the threat of social decline, which then prompts voters to express their discontent by voting for populist parties (Engler & Weisstanner, 2021)." (p. 2)
> 

Derndorfer argues that the fear of social decline is particularly relevant for middle-class voters due to loss aversion:

> "As people are generally loss averse (Kahneman & Tversky, 1984), losing one's position and moving down the social ladder has more far-reaching consequences than maintaining or improving one's standing in society." (p. 2)
> 

The theoretical contribution of this paper is to focus on the latent economic insecurity of the middle class specifically, developing a novel measure that extends beyond unemployment risk to include broader economic insecurity. As Derndorfer notes:

> "The concept applied is thus more encompassing than focusing solely on unemployment risk, which is one important dimension of EI, but by no means the only one." (p. 2)
> 

### Theoretical Connections to Prior Research

The paper builds on and extends several research streams:

1. **Middle class studies**: Draws on Banerjee & Duflo (2008), López-Calva & Ortiz-Juarez (2014), and Derndorfer & Kranzinger (2021) to conceptualize the middle class and its decline.
2. **Economic insecurity research**: Builds on Western et al. (2012), Rohde & Tang (2018), and Bossert & D'Ambrosio (2013) to develop a comprehensive understanding of economic insecurity.
3. **Electoral behavior studies**: Connects to Abou-Chadi & Kurer (2021), Burgoon et al. (2019), and Engler & Weisstanner (2021) to investigate how economic factors influence voting.

## Conceptualization of Economic Insecurity

Derndorfer distinguishes between two forms of economic insecurity:

1. **Manifest economic insecurity**: Actual economic hardship, such as low income or unemployment.
2. **Latent economic insecurity**: The risk of future economic loss.

> "Manifest EI is concerned with economic hardship, such as low income, unemployment or having difficulties to pay the bills, while latent EI can be understood as the 'risk of economic loss faced by workers and households as they encounter the unpredictable events of social life' (Western et al., 2012, p. 342)." (p. 3)
> 

The paper focuses on latent economic insecurity, with particular emphasis on household-level analysis:

> "It is important to stress that the household level is of great importance for this research for two reasons: First, household income is the most relevant level when talking about the middle class, as individual income is typically shared with other household members. Thus, a person who is currently not in paid employment due to care responsibilities may still enjoy a middle-class lifestyle depending on the income of the other members of the household (usually the spouse or partner). Second, it is important to integrate the economic risk at the household level when studying voting behaviour, given that individual political preferences are not formed in isolation but are embedded in the social context of the voter." (pp. 2-3)
> 

### Middle Class Conceptualization

Derndorfer defines the middle class primarily in economic terms:

> "I define the middle-income class as individuals living in households with an equivalised disposable income between 75% and 150% of the national median income. The lower-income class and the upper-income class have incomes below 75% and above 150% of the national median income." (p. 10)
> 

This definition aligns with economic approaches to class analysis while acknowledging the importance of economic stability to middle-class identity:

> "In addition to income, economic stability and a secure lifestyle can be seen as one of the main characteristics of the middle class. As Banerjee & Duflo (2008, p. 26) put it, 'nothing seems more middle class than the fact of having a steady well-paying job.'" (p. 4)
> 

## Mechanisms Linking Economic Insecurity to RRP Support

The paper identifies several theoretical mechanisms that might explain the connection between economic insecurity and RRP support:

1. **Pessimism and declinism**: Economic insecurity promotes general pessimism about society.

> "The literature has shown that voters with pessimistic views are more inclined to support RRP (Gest et al., 2018; Mayer, 1993; Steenvoorden & Harteveld, 2018). Pessimism or declinism (i.e., feeling negative about the direction that society is going) is not necessarily rooted in personal experience, but takes into account social change in general (Elchardus & Spruyt, 2016)." (p. 5)
> 
1. **Distrust in mainstream parties**: Economic insecurity breeds distrust in established political institutions.

> "In a similar vein, the threat of downward mobility can fuel distrust of mainstream parties. In a recent study, Rohde (2023) shows that rising EI leads to increased institutional and electoral distrust. Supporting RRP can partly be attributed to the expression of loss of trust and disillusionment with national and European politics." (p. 6)
> 
1. **Status anxiety and authoritarian values**: Economic decline affects social identity, promoting authoritarian tendencies.

> "Two recent studies (Ballard-Rosa et al., 2021, 2022) on the electoral success of Donald Trump in the United States and the Brexit referendum in the United Kingdom argue that sustained economic decline induced by globalisation (specifically Chinese import competition) adversely impacts the social identity of historically dominant groups. In order to cope with the anxiety of negative economic shocks, individuals adopt more authoritarian values, which in turn are closely linked to RRP." (p. 6)
> 
1. **Welfare chauvinism**: Economic insecurity promotes opposition to sharing welfare benefits with immigrants.

> "Another important strand of literature deals with the fierce anti-immigration policies of RRP and their appeal to voters, who believe that immigrants should not be entitled to the same welfare benefits and services as native citizens. This opposition is referred to in the literature as welfare chauvinism (Kitschelt, 1997)." (p. 6)
> 

## Methodology and Data

### Data Sources

The study combines two main data sources:

1. **EU-SILC longitudinal data (2005-2018)**: Used to compute the risk of downward mobility and economic insecurity index.
2. **European Social Survey (ESS rounds 4-9)**: Used to analyze voting behavior and RRP support.

### Sample and Case Selection

The analysis covers 13 Western European countries: Austria, Belgium, Denmark, Finland, France, Greece, Italy, Netherlands, Norway, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, and the United Kingdom.

### Variables and Operationalization

### Independent Variables: Measures of Economic Insecurity

1. **Risk of Downward Mobility**: Calculated as the probability of middle-class households falling into the lower-income class based on occupational class.

> "To compute the risk of downward mobility, I make use of the EU-SILC longitudinal dataset for the years 2005–2018, which follows each household and its members for four consecutive years. [...] Having assigned each household and its respective members to one of the three income classes for the first 3 years and the last year in the panel data, I compute transition matrices." (pp. 9-10)
> 
1. **Economic Insecurity Index**: A novel measure that accounts for both downward and upward mobility, as well as changes in the middle-class size.

> "Economic insecurity EIc,t,o for country c at time t for occupational class o is measured as,
(1) EIc,t,o = λdDc,t,o − λuUc,t,o − λmΔMc,t,o,
where D indicates the rate of downward mobility, as explained in Section 3.1; U refers to the rate of upward mobility, calculated analogously to the rate of downward mobility. Last, ΔM measures the change in the size of the middle class by calculating the average size of the middle class in the first 3 years of the examined time period and comparing it with the size of the middle class in the last period." (p. 12)
> 

The weights in this index reflect loss aversion principles, with downward mobility weighted more heavily than upward mobility:

> "To reflect the loss aversion of individuals (i.e., to derive greater disutility from loss than utility from gain), the risk of downward mobility is given more weight than upward mobility, λu <1. Furthermore, the EI index is constructed such that an increase in the middle class is also given less weight compared with the rate of downward mobility, λm <1." (p. 12)
> 

### Dependent Variable

**RRP Support**: A binary variable indicating whether a respondent voted for an RRP in the last election or feels close to an RRP.

> "The ESS asks respondents whether they voted in the last election and, if so, for which party. The survey also asks respondents whether they feel close to a particular party. I combine these two items to measure RRP support. If the respondent has voted for an RRP in the last election or feels close to an RRP, the dummy variable measuring RRP support is one and zero otherwise." (pp. 14-15)
> 

### Control Variables

The analysis includes several control variables:

- Age
- Gender
- Education level
- Current unemployment
- Recent unemployment within the last 5 years
- Subjective assessment of current household income
- Foreign background
- Religiosity
- Urban/rural residence

### Analytical Strategy

The empirical analysis employs a logit model to test whether latent economic insecurity increases the likelihood of supporting RRP:

> "The empirical analysis is based on a logit model to test whether latent EI increases the likelihood of supporting RRP.
(2) P(Yi = 1|X) = G(α + β1EIi + β2Xi + γc + θs),
where Y is binary variable of supporting RRP for individual i, and G(z) is the logistic cumulative distribution function, which takes on values between zero and one for all real numbers z." (p. 16)
> 

The analysis includes country and ESS-round fixed effects, with standard errors clustered by country-round.

## Key Findings

### Middle Class Changes (2005-2018)

1. **Overall middle class size**: About 50% of the population in each country lives in middle-class households.
2. **Occupational differences**: The middle class is largest among the lower-grade service class (around 62%), followed by skilled workers (around 55%) and higher-grade service class (around 48%). The middle class is smaller among business owners and low-skilled workers (around 43% each).
3. **Time trends**: The middle class declined slightly from 2005 to 2014-2015 before recovering somewhat by 2018, but not reaching 2005 levels.
4. **Compositional changes**: The middle class experienced different trajectories by occupational class:

> "For the higher-grade service class, there is a marked increase of 5 percentage points in households belonging to the middle class. While the middle class remains relatively stable between 2005 and 2018 for the lower-grade service class and business owners, it decreases most notably for low-skilled workers (−8.9 percentage points) and skilled workers (−6 percentage points)." (p. 17)
> 

### Risk of Downward Mobility

The paper reveals substantial variations in downward mobility risk:

1. **By occupational class**: Lowest risk for higher-grade service class (5.17%), followed by lower-grade service class (6.83%), skilled workers (11.68%), low-skilled workers (17.98%), and business owners (19.02%).
2. **By welfare regime**: Lower risk in Social-democratic welfare states, higher risk in Mediterranean and Liberal welfare states, with Corporatist welfare states in between.

> "Based on the literature on welfare regimes (i.e., Esping-Andersen, 1989; Ferrera, 1996), the risk of downward mobility can be clustered by different types of welfare regimes. While Mediterranean and Liberal welfare states exhibit the highest risks of falling out of the middle class, people living in middle-class households in the Social-democratic welfare state face a much lower risk." (p. 19)
> 
1. **Time trends**: Derndorfer observes different patterns of change in downward mobility risk over time:

> "While the risk of downward mobility is relatively stable in most countries for the higher- and lower-grade service class, it increases in some countries (e.g., Greece, Norway, Sweden and the United Kingdom) and decreases in others (e.g., Spain and France). Business owners face a relatively high risk of downward mobility with great volatility. In most countries, an increasing instability among middle-class households belonging to the group of skilled workers is notable (e.g., Norway, Sweden and the United Kingdom). Interestingly, the opposite is true for low-skilled workers, where the risk of downward mobility out of the middle class has decreased over time in most countries." (p. 19)
> 

### Economic Insecurity and RRP Support

The analysis shows a significant positive relationship between economic insecurity and RRP support:

1. **Risk of downward mobility**: One standard deviation increase in downward mobility risk increases the probability of supporting RRP by 1.9 percentage points.

> "The results of Model 1 show that the risk of falling out of the middle class is significantly correlated with a rise in the probability of supporting RRP. I find that one standard deviation in the risk of downward mobility increases the probability of supporting RRP by 1.9 percentage points." (p. 21)
> 
1. **Economic insecurity index**: One standard deviation increase in the economic insecurity index increases the probability of supporting RRP by 2.4 percentage points.

> "The effect for the EI index reported by Model 2 is slightly larger, with one standard-deviation changing the probability of supporting RRP by 2.4 percentage points." (p. 22)
> 
1. **Interpretation**: Given the relatively low baseline probability of RRP support, these effects are substantial.

> "Overall, given that the probability of RRP support is rather low, the size of the marginal effects of the two EI measures on RRP support are considerable." (p. 22)
> 
1. **Other factors**: The analysis confirms expected patterns for control variables, with RRP supporters more likely to be male, native-born, less educated, and rural residents.

### Robustness Checks and Extensions

Derndorfer conducted several robustness checks:

1. **Attitudinal variables**: Including left-right self-placement, redistribution support, and immigration attitudes reduces the magnitude of economic insecurity effects but they remain significant.
2. **Alternative outcome**: Economic insecurity is also positively associated with electoral abstention.
3. **Alternative specifications**: Results hold when excluding Spain, matching economic insecurity to election years rather than survey years, using lagged measures (t-1), broadening the middle-class definition, and excluding subjective economic insecurity measures.

## Welfare State Implications

Although not the primary focus, the paper provides insights into how welfare states might moderate economic insecurity and RRP support:

1. **Welfare regime differences**: The observed pattern of lower economic insecurity in Social-democratic welfare states suggests that generous welfare provisions may mitigate economic insecurity.

> "For instance, the average risk of falling out of the middle class for the higher-grade service class is about three times higher in Greece than in Sweden. The risk of downward mobility in Corporatist welfare states is mostly between the high- and low-risk welfare regimes." (p. 19)
> 
1. **Social protection**: The paper acknowledges the role of welfare states in addressing economic insecurity:

> "Families or individuals can protect themselves from economic hardship through wealth (Bossert & D'Ambrosio, 2013; Hacker et al., 2013, 2014; Weller & Logan, 2009), but the strength of the welfare state also plays a crucial role in determining feelings of insecurity about the future (Mau et al., 2012)." (p. 3)
> 
1. **Health care systems**: The welfare state's role in providing healthcare is specifically noted:

> "As shown by Osberg & Sharpe (2014), the insecurity level of financial risk due to illness is much higher in the United States than in comparison with Canada, Denmark and Germany. Hence, in welfare states with high-quality and affordable health care, EI is less likely to stem from the worry about costly out-of-pocket medical expenses." (pp. 3-4)
> 

## Theoretical and Policy Implications

### Theoretical Contributions

The paper makes several theoretical contributions:

1. **Novel economic insecurity measure**: Develops a comprehensive index that goes beyond unemployment risk.
2. **Household focus**: Emphasizes the importance of household-level analysis for understanding both middle-class status and voting behavior.
3. **Integration of economic and status concerns**: Bridges economic insecurity and social status perspectives on RRP support.

### Policy Implications

While not explicitly detailed, several policy implications can be derived:

1. **Welfare state design**: Stronger welfare states with more generous provisions might reduce economic insecurity and, consequently, RRP support.
2. **Labor market policies**: Policies addressing job security, especially for skilled and low-skilled workers, could mitigate economic insecurity.
3. **Middle class stabilization**: Preserving the size and stability of the middle class appears important for democratic stability.

## Limitations and Future Research

The author acknowledges several limitations:

1. **Measurement challenges**: Combining different datasets introduces potential measurement errors.

> "Combining different datasets comes with the possibility of introducing measurement errors. Typically, the household head is defined as the member with the highest personal income. Owing to the lack of personal income data in the ESS, I define the household head as the member with the highest occupational class in both datasets." (p. 14)
> 
1. **Data collection differences**: Income data collection varies between sources.

> "Another limitation of matching the ESS with EU-SILC data is that the collection of income data differs between data sources. The ESS relies on self-reporting of total household income and is not adjusted for household size." (p. 14)
> 

For future research, Derndorfer suggests exploring:

1. **Welfare state policies**: Investigating which specific policies prevent middle-class downward mobility.

> "Further work is needed to gain more knowledge about which policies prevent middle-class households from falling into the lower-income class and whether these policies (e.g., social benefits such as unemployment replacement rates or family benefits) in turn have an impact on RRP support." (p. 26)
> 
1. **Wealth effects**: Examining how wealth mediates the relationship between downward mobility risk and RRP support.
2. **Subjective experiences**: Further exploring how risk of downward mobility affects subjective social status, political discontent, and attitudes toward out-groups.

## Conclusion

Derndorfer's study makes a significant contribution to understanding the relationship between economic insecurity and radical right support among middle-class voters. By developing novel measures of economic insecurity and demonstrating their association with RRP support, the paper adds nuance to debates about the economic versus cultural drivers of populism.

The finding that economic insecurity varies systematically by occupational class and welfare regime suggests that both labor market structures and welfare state policies play important roles in shaping political behavior. The lower economic insecurity observed in Social-democratic welfare states indicates that generous social policies might mitigate the economic anxieties that fuel RRP support.

This research strengthens the economic insecurity thesis while acknowledging the interconnections between economic and cultural factors in driving political behavior. It suggests that preserving middle-class stability may be important not just for economic reasons but also for democratic stability in Western Europe.

The paper examines the relationship between economic insecurity of the middle class and support for radical right parties (RRP) in 13 Western European countries. Economic insecurity is conceptualised as the occupational risk of downward mobility. To capture the potential mitigating effects of upward mobility prospects and a growing middle class on economic insecurity, the author computes a novel index that incorporates the other two dynamics in addition to the risk of downward mobility. The findings show that economic insecurity differs across occupational groups and countries, and both measures of insecurity are positively correlated with RRP support, highlighting that the decline of the middle class has implications for the political sphere.
