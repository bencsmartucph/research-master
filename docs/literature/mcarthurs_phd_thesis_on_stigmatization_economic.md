## Paper Summary

Daniel McArthur's PhD thesis investigates the relationship between economic position, economic context, and stigmatizing stereotypes about the poor and welfare recipients. Through three empirical chapters using quantitative analysis of survey data from the UK and Europe, McArthur examines: (1) why higher education correlates with more sympathetic attitudes toward welfare recipients, finding that authoritarian values and social status play key roles; (2) how income inequality affects beliefs about poverty's causes, finding that higher inequality correlates with more individualistic beliefs, especially among less advantaged individuals; and (3) whether exposure to local unemployment affects attitudes toward the unemployed, finding minimal evidence that neighborhood context shapes these attitudes. The thesis advances our understanding of how economic structures shape welfare attitudes through mechanisms like status anxiety and social distance, with important implications for understanding the self-reinforcing dynamics of inequality and welfare stigma.

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### 1. Status Anxiety Theory

McArthur draws extensively on status anxiety theory to explain how economic position and context influence welfare attitudes. Status anxiety refers to negative social emotions experienced when individuals feel they are failing to meet shared social ideals of esteem and respect.

**Explanation:** Status anxiety theory suggests that individuals who experience low or threatened social status often respond by othering stigmatized groups to protect their own self-image and relative position. This dynamic applies particularly to attitudes toward welfare recipients, as lower-status individuals may stigmatize those perceived as beneath them on the social hierarchy.

**Key quotes:**

> "Status anxiety is a negative social emotion experienced by people who feel that they have failed to live up to shared ideals of esteem and respect" (p. 22)
> 

> "Status anxiety plays an important role in linking individual economic position with attitudes towards outgroups. This PhD is the first to apply such ideas to the relationship between economic position, income inequality, and stigmatising stereotypes about welfare recipients." (p. 23)
> 

> "There is a wide range of evidence that individuals who experience a loss of, or threat to, their social status often respond by becoming less tolerant towards minorities or other stigmatised groups, and identifying with status enhancing but potentially exclusionary groups such as their nation." (p. 95)
> 

McArthur operationalizes status anxiety primarily through indirect measures, using education levels as a proxy for social status and examining how contextual factors like inequality amplify status concerns. He builds on work by Wilkinson and Pickett (2009), Sennett and Cobb (1972), and recent studies by Gidron and Hall (2017) and Wetts and Willer (2018).

**Application to welfare attitudes:**

> "Poor or low status individuals may express stigmatising views about others in poverty to shore up their own threatened social status – either as someone who has worked hard to support their family, is genuinely deserving of welfare, or simply not the poorest." (p. 95)
> 

**Innovation:** McArthur extends status anxiety theory by applying it specifically to welfare attitudes and connecting it to both individual-level factors (education) and macro-level factors (inequality). This creates a multi-level theoretical framework that helps explain seemingly contradictory findings about economic position and welfare attitudes.

### 2. Social Distance Theory

The social distance theory examines how inequality affects attitudes by transforming social structures, particularly through increased segregation between different economic groups.

**Explanation:** According to this theory, when inequality increases, the physical and social distance between income groups grows through mechanisms like residential segregation, different consumption patterns, and divergent lifestyles. This increased distance makes it harder for affluent individuals to empathize with or understand the poor, leading to less sympathy and more stigmatizing attitudes.

**Key quotes:**

> "According to the social distance theory, inequality affects individual attitudes because of the way it transforms the structure of society: when inequality is higher, individuals at the top and bottom of the income distribution are less likely to interact with one another, and have divergent lifestyles and concerns." (p. 94)
> 

> "As a result, when income inequality is higher, more affluent individuals should have less contact with poorer individuals, and their lifestyles should diverge more. This increase in social distance between income groups should lead the affluent to be less sympathetic towards the poor." (p. 94)
> 

McArthur operationalizes social distance by examining how inequality affects attitudes differently across income and education groups, and through a direct test of neighborhood exposure effects in Chapter 4.

**Historical context:** The theory draws on work by Lupu and Pontusson (2011), Mijs (2019), and research on residential segregation by income (Musterd et al., 2017; Reardon and Bischoff, 2011).

**Theoretical innovation:** McArthur tests competing predictions from social distance theory against status anxiety theory. His findings challenge the social distance explanation:

> "My results thus challenge the extent to which influential theories about the attitudinal consequences of economic inequality in sociology and comparative political economy can be applied to attitudes towards welfare recipients and the poor." (p. 41)
> 

> "My finding that increases in unemployment do not seem to have a strong or straightforward effect on attitudes towards the unemployed conflicts with several bodies of evidence... One way of reconciling my findings with these literatures is to note that the receipt of unemployment benefits is a social status that is hard to identify by casual observation in public space." (p. 152)
> 

### 3. Othering and Identification Theory

McArthur draws heavily on cultural class analysis to develop a framework around identification and othering processes that shape welfare attitudes.

**Explanation:** Othering is a process whereby individuals distance themselves from stigmatized identities by emphasizing differences with those who possess these identities. Identification, conversely, involves recognizing similarities with others in similar structural positions. These mechanisms help explain how economic position affects attitudes toward welfare recipients.

**Key quotes:**

> "Othering starts from the idea that individuals respond to possessing a low social status or stigmatised identity by disidentifying with that identity. Othering or stigmatising those who also possess it may enable them to protect their own compromised self-image." (p. 33)
> 

> "It is far more commonly reported in the cultural class analysis literature that people in poverty and welfare recipients other those in a similar situation to themselves, rather than identify with them." (p. 33)
> 

McArthur operationalizes these concepts by examining how economic disadvantage relates to welfare attitudes, and how this relationship is moderated by education and values.

**Historical context:** The theory is grounded in qualitative cultural class analysis by scholars like Skeggs (1997), Tyler (2008, 2013), Chase and Walker (2012), and Shildrick and MacDonald (2013).

**Theoretical innovation:** McArthur applies these primarily qualitative concepts to quantitative analysis:

> "The concepts of othering and identification are central to this thesis because they suggest ways in which an individual's economic position could affect attitudes towards the poor and welfare recipients... This PhD is one of the first to apply ideas from the cultural class analysis literature to quantitative welfare state research." (p. 34, 36)
> 

### 4. Authoritarian Values and Moral Deviance Framework

McArthur develops an innovative theoretical approach connecting authoritarian values to welfare attitudes through perceptions of moral deviance in non-economic domains.

**Explanation:** This framework suggests that perceptions of welfare recipients are shaped by broader values regarding hierarchy, authority, and intolerance of diversity. Individuals who hold authoritarian values are more likely to stigmatize welfare recipients because such recipients are often presented as morally deviant across multiple domains beyond economic behavior.

**Key quotes:**

> "If welfare recipients and the poor are presented in the media and popular culture as morally deviant in non-economic domains, then individuals who are more authoritarian and thus less tolerant of diversity and difference are likely to hold negative views about them." (p. 52)
> 

> "Assuming it can be replicated with other measures of authoritarianism, [this finding] suggests that attitudes towards welfare recipients have more to do with intolerance for diversity in non-economic domains than is often recognised." (p. 47)
> 

McArthur operationalizes this theory using established measures of libertarian-authoritarian values and examines their relationship with negative stereotypes about welfare recipients.

**Theoretical innovation:** This represents a significant departure from economic self-interest models that dominate welfare attitudes research:

> "My research breaks with this focus on economic self-interest. In Chapter 2 I set out an account of the relationship between economic position and negative stereotypes about welfare recipients that does not use the concept at all." (p. 29)
> 

> "The strong association between authoritarian attitudes and negative stereotypes about welfare recipients is rather surprising given that welfare attitudes are usually thought to be closely associated with economic attitudes towards inequality, redistribution, and the role of the state." (p. 83)
> 

## Findings & Discussion

### Finding 1: Higher education is associated with lower levels of negative stereotypes about welfare recipients, but not because of economic advantage

McArthur finds that education's association with more sympathetic welfare attitudes cannot be explained by economic advantage. Instead, this relationship is largely mediated by authoritarian values and potentially social status.

**Evidence:**

> "Controlling for any or all of the other three measures of economic position does not change the size of the association [between education and negative stereotypes] in any substantively meaningful manner." (p. 68-69)
> 

> "The strength of the association between education and negative stereotypes is dramatically reduced by controls for authoritarian values. All else equal, an individual with no qualifications would be expected to have 0.7 standard deviations higher levels of negative stereotypes than a degree holder with no value controls, but only 0.25 standard deviations higher negative stereotypes when authoritarian values are controlled." (p. 72)
> 

**Relevance to my research:** This finding highlights the importance of considering non-economic values and status concerns when examining welfare attitudes and support for redistribution. It challenges economic self-interest models and suggests that welfare/social policies might influence political attitudes through non-economic pathways like social status and recognition. This provides a potential mechanism for how social investment policies could affect support for far-right parties by addressing status anxiety rather than purely economic concerns.

### Finding 2: Higher inequality is associated with more individualistic beliefs about the causes of poverty

McArthur finds that individuals living in contexts with higher income inequality are more likely to believe poverty is caused by individual laziness rather than social injustice.

**Evidence:**

> "The main finding of this chapter is that individualistic beliefs about the causes of poverty are more common in contexts with higher levels of income inequality. A 10 point increase in the Gini coefficient is associated with a 6% increase in the probability of believing that laziness rather than injustice is the cause of poverty." (p. 120)
> 

> "This association is observed within countries over time, increasing my confidence that it is not confounded by unobserved cross-sectional differences between countries." (p. 110)
> 

**Relevance to my research:** This finding suggests that inequality may create a self-reinforcing cycle where higher inequality reduces sympathy for the poor, potentially decreasing support for redistributive policies. This is highly relevant to understanding how welfare regimes might influence far-right support, as it indicates that the structural economic context shapes baseline attitudes toward potential welfare beneficiaries. If welfare policies reduce inequality, they may indirectly foster more collective attributions for poverty, potentially reducing support for parties that scapegoat marginalized groups.

### Finding 3: Lower-status individuals respond more strongly to inequality than higher-status individuals

An important finding is that the relationship between inequality and individualistic beliefs about poverty is stronger among low-income and low-education individuals.

**Evidence:**

> "I find that the association between income inequality and the probability of holding individualistic beliefs about the causes of poverty is greater among low income and low education individuals." (p. 41)
> 

> "The probability of high income and high education individuals holding individualistic beliefs increases with inequality either at the same rate or a lower rate than among low education or income individuals." (p. 121)
> 

**Relevance to my research:** This finding directly connects to mechanisms through which welfare policies might moderate far-right support. If low-status individuals are more responsive to inequality in forming attitudes toward welfare recipients, then policies that specifically address their status concerns or economic insecurity might be particularly effective in reducing support for far-right parties that capitalize on these anxieties.

### Finding 4: Local exposure to unemployment has minimal effects on attitudes toward the unemployed

Contrary to both social distance and contact theories, McArthur finds little evidence that living in areas with higher unemployment significantly shapes attitudes toward the unemployed.

**Evidence:**

> "There is very little evidence that changes in area level unemployment benefits claim rates are associated with changes in individual attitudes towards the unemployed." (p. 152)
> 

> "While the coefficient estimates are in the direction predicted by the contact hypothesis, the estimates are both small in magnitude and not robust to controls for time-fixed effects." (p. 145)
> 

**Relevance to my research:** This finding challenges a key assumption of the social distance theory and suggests that the micro-mechanisms linking welfare policies to political attitudes may operate through channels other than direct exposure to beneficiaries. For research on welfare policies and far-right support, this implies that national-level discourse and framing of policies may be more important than local implementation experiences in shaping attitudes.

## Key Takeaways

### 1. Status anxiety better explains welfare attitudes than social distance

McArthur's evidence consistently supports status anxiety over social distance as an explanation for how economic context shapes welfare attitudes.

> "While the results presented in this section are consistent with social status playing such a role, the next section more directly investigates this question by examining how the relationship between education and negative stereotypes depends on aggregate levels of education." (p. 74)
> 

> "This pattern is inconsistent with the social distance explanation of the association between inequality and individualistic beliefs, which predicts that advantaged individuals will be less sympathetic towards the poor in higher inequality contexts." (p. 121)
> 

This suggests that welfare policies might influence far-right support primarily by addressing status concerns rather than merely facilitating cross-class contact.

### 2. Authoritarian values are strongly linked to welfare attitudes

The strong connection between authoritarian values and welfare attitudes represents a significant theoretical advancement.

> "Authoritarian values are strongly associated with negative stereotypes about welfare recipients, much more so than right wing values. This provides evidence that attitudes towards welfare recipients are related to a desire for hierarchy and intolerance of diversity, which is heavily influenced by a low or threatened social status." (p. 76)
> 

This connection offers a direct pathway between welfare attitudes and far-right politics, which often mobilize authoritarian values.

### 3. Rising inequality may create self-reinforcing cycles of welfare stigma

McArthur's findings suggest that inequality can create vicious cycles where higher inequality increases individualistic attributions for poverty.

> "If my results do represent a causal effect of inequality on individualistic beliefs about the causes of poverty then they present a depressing prospect for those who support increased redistribution in response to high economic inequality." (p. 128)
> 

> "The association between income inequality and individualistic beliefs about the causes of poverty implies that individuals become less sympathetic towards the poor in high inequality contexts." (p. 128)
> 

This suggests that welfare policies that reduce inequality might create virtuous cycles where reduced inequality increases support for further redistribution.

### 4. Non-economic bases of welfare attitudes deserve greater attention

Throughout the thesis, McArthur demonstrates that welfare attitudes are not primarily shaped by economic self-interest.

> "There is a clear link between high levels of education and low levels of authoritarian values, and there is a large literature in sociology and social psychology that explores it in more detail. I draw on this literature to understand how education is a source of social status, and why a low social status might lead individuals to other welfare recipients." (p. 49)
> 

This suggests that research on welfare policies and far-right support should consider how policies address non-economic concerns like social status and recognition.
