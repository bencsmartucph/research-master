### Theoretical Background

- Postwar expansion created unprecedented upward mobility but rates of upward mobility began decreasing toward end of 20th century
- Declining mobility attributed to postindustrial occupational change (deindustrialization, job polarization)
    - *"Deindustrialization, job polarization, and the recent slowdown in the growth of relative demand for high-skilled workers...have impaired the social escalator, eliminating decent, middle-skill jobs while at the same time gradually reducing the value of a college degree."* (p. 1340)

### Methodological Approach

- **Data Source**: German Socio-Economic Panel (SOEP 2019)
- **Sample**: German household panel data, focusing on the 2017 federal election
- **Predictive Methodology**: Used random forest algorithm to predict expected occupational status based on father's socioeconomic status and respondent's childhood characteristics
- **Status Measurement**: International Socio-Economic Index (ISEI) used to measure occupational status
    - *"ISEI is a continuous, unidimensional measure, created by optimally scaling occupation as an intervening variable between education and income."* (p. 1345)
- **Calculation of Status Discordance**: Difference between predicted and actual occupational status (ISDi = ŷi - yi)
- **Variables included in model**:
    - Father's characteristics: occupational status, major occupational group, education level, year of birth
    - Respondent's pretreatment characteristics: age, gender, migration background, citizenship, region, urban/rural childhood location, school grades at age 15

## Key Findings: Psychological Mechanisms Driving Political Alienation

### Impact of Status Discordance on Political Behavior

- Status-discordant voters show:
    1. Higher rates of abstention
    2. Lower identification with political parties
    3. Less support for mainstream parties
    4. Higher support for radical parties
- *"The analysis reveals that political dissatisfaction is widespread among voters who fall short of intergenerational status expectations. We show that such dissatisfaction is associated with higher abstention rates, less mainstream party support, and more radical voting."* (p. 1340)

### Quantitative Evidence

- A one-standard-deviation increase in negative status discordance associated with:
    - 1-2 percentage point increase in abstention
    - 1-2 percentage point increase in support for radical parties
    - 3 percentage point decrease in support for mainstream parties
- *"In terms of magnitude, the coefficient of status discordance indicates a larger effect than that of log income and age. This favorable comparison to important and well-known predictors of radical-right voting underscores the importance of expectations in shaping voter behavior."* (p. 1350)

### The Psychology of Disappointed Expectations

- Disappointment occurs when individuals cannot meet parental standards
- Status discordance reflects "a sense among certain individuals that they were denied the opportunity to make the status achievements that they observe among socioeconomic peers from childhood" (p. 1346)
- Voters blame mainstream political establishment for lack of opportunities:
    - *"When disappointed voters lose faith in both standard political responses, whether the government-based policies of the center-left or the market-based solutions of the center-right, it is the political establishment as a whole that has failed."* (p. 1342)

## Variation in Responses to Status Discordance

### Gender Differences

- Status discordance has weaker effects on women's political behavior
    - *"The marginal effect of status discordance is indeed much weaker among women. It is no longer statistically different from zero with respect to the radical right and is virtually zero with respect to the radical left."* (p. 1351)
- Two explanations for this gendered pattern:
    1. Women experience negative status discordance less frequently than men
    2. Women's socioeconomic gains and broader gender equality movements may moderate perceived status loss
    - *"The empowering and emancipatory experience of increasingly equal access to paid employment may moderate political reactions among the individual women who do experience status loss as conceptualized in this article."* (p. 1342)

### Socioeconomic Background Variation

- Status-discordant voters from different socioeconomic backgrounds choose different political responses:
    - **Weaker socioeconomic backgrounds** → support radical-right parties
    - **Stronger socioeconomic backgrounds** → support radical-left parties
- *"Respondents who experience downward mobility relative to fathers who worked middle- and, especially, low-skill jobs are significantly more likely to support radical-right parties, whereas we do not find robust evidence of radical-right support among those from high-status origins."* (p. 1352)
- *"Support for the radical left is in fact confined to voters who experience downward mobility relative to fathers at higher positions in the occupational hierarchy."* (p. 1352)

### Education Level Differences

- College education strongly decreases likelihood of supporting radical-right parties among those experiencing status discordance
- *"Although its positive effect on the likelihood to vote for a radical-left party is weaker and imprecisely estimated, this analysis nonetheless supports our hypothesis that socioeconomic background influences party choice."* (p. 1352)

## Why Economic Policies May Fail to Address Status Politics

### The Limits of Traditional Economic Redistribution

- Political reaction to status discordance not simply about material compensation
- Status-discordant voters attribute blame to the political establishment and seek more radical change
    - *"It is not only that status-discordant voters are dissatisfied with mainstream incumbents but also that they consider these parties as the source of their current hardships."* (p. 1342)

### Status Politics and Party Appeal

- Radical right appeals to those with weaker socioeconomic backgrounds by:
    - *"...promising to bring back the conditions that prompted postwar growth and created economic opportunities for workers without college degrees."* (p. 1352)
    - *"Radical-right parties are attractive for these voters because they advocate for a return to the times in which jobs not requiring a college education still had integrity and carried great prospects for upward mobility."* (p. 1353)
- Radical left appeals to those with privileged backgrounds by:
    - *"...criticizing elite financial institutions and calling for a sweeping reform of capitalist economies."* (p. 1352)
    - *"Radical-left parties appeal to these voters not by calling for a return to earlier times but instead by calling for a radically different economic system to combat growing inequality and to economically reward those with goals other than profit growth, be they environmental, sociocultural, or humanitarian."* (p. 1343)

## Implications for Policy

### Need for Status-Focused Policies

- Neither traditional left nor right mainstream economic policies address status concerns
- *"The experience of negative status discordance thus heightens the likelihood that these voters envision more radical change than that which is on the policy agendas of mainstream parties."* (p. 1342)

### Status Anxiety and Political Instability

- *"Upward mobility was an undervalued cornerstone of postwar politics. As increasing shares of citizens face declining opportunities for mobility, disappointed expectations are on the rise, affecting voter behavior and electoral outcomes."* (p. 1354)
- *"Importantly, the disappointment we capture is distinct from the usual democratic dissatisfaction in that it cannot be addressed through short-term measures or replacement of party leaders. It instead reflects a sense among a growing group of voters that the political solutions prevailing in recent decades are out of touch with the encroaching reality of downward mobility across postindustrial societies."* (p. 1354)

## Long-Term Perspective

### Potential Future Stabilization

- The authors suggest the current political disruption may be transitional:
    - *"More recent birth cohorts who were already born into societies with less optimistic or stagnant mobility prospects likely adjust their socioeconomic expectations to this new reality. As a result, we might see fewer instances where expectation and realized outcome deviate to an extent that creates politically relevant grievances."* (p. 1354)
- However, they also note continuing challenges:
    - *"Given that ongoing occupational and educational trends seem likely to continue, as the task of social and economic inclusion looms, party politics in advanced democracies may never be the same."* (p. 1354)

## Conclusion

- Status discordance from declining mobility explains rising political alienation and radical voting
- Traditional economic policies insufficient to address status concerns
- Status politics require policies that maintain dignity and mitigate status anxiety
- Future political landscapes will need to confront intergenerational mobility challenges to restore political stability
