## Primary Theories Used

## Paper Summary

Ciccolini's article investigates the relationship between economic status loss and support for populist radical right (PRR) parties in Western Europe. Using a novel measure of "positional income" to capture economic status, the author demonstrates that social classes experiencing economic status loss (relative decline in position compared to both the poorest and richest segments of society) show greater support for PRR parties. Drawing on data from the European Social Survey and EU Statistics on Income and Living Conditions across 23 elections in 11 Western European countries (2008-2019), the research establishes that this effect is distinct from absolute economic decline. Importantly, the study reveals that economic status loss increases cultural conservatism and, counterintuitively, anti-redistribution preferences, explaining why voters facing status decline turn to the PRR rather than left-wing parties. This research bridges cultural and economic explanations of PRR voting, highlighting how economic phenomena are culturally interpreted through the lens of status.

## Primary Theoretical Frameworks

### 1. Economic Status Loss and Relative Deprivation Theory

Ciccolini builds upon relative deprivation theory, which posits that individuals' political reactions are shaped not just by absolute economic conditions but by their perceived position relative to others. The key innovation is the development of a "positional income" measure to capture a social class's relative economic standing.

> "Regardless of actual material loss, citizens may perceive themselves as facing a relative economic deterioration if the rest of society is improving its condition relative to them (Burgoon et al. 2019; Kurer and van Staalduinen 2022; Rooduijn and Burgoon 2018)." (p. 2)
> 

The author distinguishes between economic resources and economic status, arguing that the latter is structured as a zero-sum game:

> "Because a subject's economic status depends on others' resources, the scale of economic status in a society is virtually inexpansible (Hirsch 1976; Milner 1994). In laypeople's terms, it is not possible for everyone to get richer and simultaneously acquire more economic status. This implies that economic status is structured as a zero-sum game: Someone's gain in status coincides with someone else's loss." (p. 4)
> 

This theoretical framework builds on a long tradition in economics and sociology, drawing from:

- Veblen's (1899) concept of positional goods
- Hirsch's (1976) work on social limits to growth
- Bourdieu's (1974, 1984) notion of group trajectory or slope
- Lipset's (1959, 1960) analysis of economic status anxieties and fascism

The author operationalizes this theory through a novel "positional income" measure that quantifies a social class's distance from both the richest and poorest in society. This approach allows for distinguishing between economic status loss and absolute economic decline, showing how the former can occur even when material conditions improve.

For my research on welfare policies and far-right support, this framework provides crucial insights: policies focused solely on improving absolute economic conditions might fail to address status concerns. Effective interventions might need to consider the relative positioning of different social groups and how policies affect perceived status hierarchies.

### 2. Class-Based Group Deprivation Theory

Ciccolini argues that economic status loss matters more politically when experienced as a group phenomenon rather than an individual one. This approach emphasizes social class as a critical unit of analysis for understanding political behavior.

> "Research in social and political psychology demonstrates that loss matters more politically when it is lived as a group experience (for a review, see Smith and Ortiz 2001). This is because individuals tend to blame themselves for misfortunes that are confined to their own lives but criticize the system and react politically when facing group deprivation (Miller et al. 1981; Verba and Nie 1972)." (p. 4)
> 

The author draws on both classical and contemporary research showing that class remains politically relevant:

> "My class-based approach is also grounded in recent evidence—which finds confirmation in my data presented later—that rising economic disparity between social classes is a critical factor driving current trends in income inequality (Albertini, Ballarino, and De Luca 2020; Mouw and Kalleberg 2010; Tomaskovic-Devey et al. 2020; Weeden et al. 2007; Wodtke 2016)." (p. 4)
> 

This theoretical approach connects to my research by suggesting that welfare policies might be more effective when they target class-based disadvantage rather than focusing exclusively on individual-level assistance. The collective experience of status loss among social classes appears to drive political reactions more powerfully than individual circumstances.

### 3. System Justification Theory

Ciccolini introduces a system justification perspective to explain why status loss leads to PRR support rather than left-wing voting:

> "Individuals tend to think of the status quo—that is, the current distribution of resources among society and the corresponding hierarchy—as justified (for a review, see Jost, Banaji, and Nosek 2004). This implies that changes in the extant hierarchy represent a deviation from a legitimate order and therefore are seen as unfair, thus spurring a feeling of resentment that is electorally consequential." (p. 4)
> 

This theoretical lens helps explain the paradoxical finding that economic status loss leads to anti-redistribution preferences:

> "Voters facing status loss do not demand more redistribution (which the left champions) but instead the opposite." (p. 12)
> 

> "This is a novel insight, because the opposite is true for those experiencing financial loss, which extant research has focused on. I suggest this may be because, when economic resources are portrayed as a zero-sum game, those facing relative loss would be further harmed by governments bringing relief to the have-nots: This would shift the balance in favor of the latter, further exacerbating voters' fear of ending up in last place (Kuziemko et al. 2014)." (p. 12)
> 

This framework is particularly important for my research on welfare policies, as it suggests that certain types of redistributive programs might paradoxically increase support for the radical right among those experiencing status loss, if those policies are perceived as benefiting others at their expense.

### 4. Cultural-Economic Complementarity Framework

Ciccolini proposes moving beyond the dichotomy between cultural and economic explanations of PRR voting, arguing that voters interpret economic phenomena through cultural lenses:

> "A third strand of scholarship suggests that cultural and economic reasons should not be thought as mutually exclusive: This is because economic transformations may have consequences that are not exclusively financial in nature, which may then explain the success of the PRR." (p. 2)
> 

The author demonstrates empirically that economic status loss correlates with both PRR voting and cultural conservatism:

> "Additional analyses reveal that economic status loss boosts culturally conservative stances. Interestingly enough, it also boosts anti-redistribution preferences." (p. 12)
> 

> "I join other social scientists in considering that the dispute in scholarly and public debate around whether far-right voters are motivated more by cultural or economic reasons is a flawed one. This is because voters give a cultural reading to economic phenomena, namely those that they experience firsthand." (p. 12)
> 

This theoretical integration is vital for my research, suggesting that effective welfare policies must address both material needs and status concerns. Simply providing economic benefits might not reduce far-right support if those policies don't also address feelings of cultural displacement or status anxiety.

## Discussion and Relevance to My Research

### 1. Status-Sensitive Welfare Policies

Ciccolini's finding that economic status loss increases PRR support and anti-redistribution preferences has profound implications for welfare policy design. Traditional redistributive policies might inadvertently exacerbate status concerns if they're perceived as elevating other groups at the expense of the economically anxious middle and working classes.

For my research, this suggests exploring "status-sensitive" welfare policies that improve material conditions while preserving or enhancing recipients' social standing. Examples might include:

1. Universal rather than means-tested benefits that avoid stigmatizing recipients
2. Labor market policies that focus on quality job creation rather than just income support
3. Social investment approaches that emphasize upskilling and human capital development

### 2. Class-Based Policy Interventions

The paper's emphasis on class as a unit of analysis suggests that effective interventions should target class-based disadvantage rather than focusing exclusively on individual-level assistance:

> "Based on my data from the EU-SILC, I estimate the level of income stratification (Zhou 2012) by social class in European societies to be 0.29. This implies that there is a 65% chance that a randomly selected worker in a higher class will earn more than a randomly selected worker in a lower class." (p. 10)
> 

This finding supports investigating how labor market and industrial policies that address class-based disadvantage might more effectively reduce PRR support than individual-level transfers alone.

### 3. Education as a Moderating Factor

Ciccolini finds heterogeneous effects based on education level:

> "The overall coefficient is seemingly driven by voters with lower and medium levels of education, whereas voters with tertiary education may be immune to economic status loss." (p. 9)
> 

This suggests exploring education's role not just as a determinant of economic outcomes but as a psychological buffer against status anxiety. For my research, this points to examining how educational policies might moderate the relationship between economic change and far-right support.

### 4. Paradoxical Effects of Redistribution

Perhaps most importantly for my research, the paper reveals that economic status loss increases anti-redistribution preferences:

> "A one SD increase in positional income change is associated with a 0.02 standard deviation increase in anti-redistribution preferences, which is almost one-fourth of the equivalent for (class-level) absolute income." (p. 11)
> 

This creates a challenging policy puzzle: those most likely to support the PRR due to status loss are also likely to oppose the very redistributive policies that might improve their material conditions. This suggests exploring how welfare policies could be framed and designed to address status concerns while providing material benefits.

## Overview of Data Source and Empirical Methodology

### Detailed Description

The paper employs a multi-source approach combining individual-level voting data with class-level income data. For voting behavior and sociodemographic factors, the author uses the European Social Survey (ESS), a cross-national biannual survey covering European countries. The analysis focuses on rounds 4-9 (2008-2019), with a sample of 34,185 respondents across 23 elections in 11 Western European countries: Austria, Belgium, Switzerland, Spain, Finland, France, Italy, the Netherlands, Norway, Sweden, and the United Kingdom.

For income data, the author uses the EU Statistics on Income and Living Conditions (EU-SILC) survey from 2003-2019, provided by Eurostat. This dataset provides detailed information on household disposable income and occupations, with large sample sizes allowing for reliable class-level income estimates.

The case selection follows two criteria: data availability and the presence of electorally relevant PRR parties (defined as scoring above 5% in elections). The author classifies PRR parties using the PopuList classification (Rooduijn et al. 2019), which identifies parties based on a combination of nativism, authoritarianism, and populism.

Methodologically, the author employs logistic regression models predicting PRR voting based on class-level economic status change (positional income change), controlling for absolute income, absolute income change, social class, and individual-level characteristics (age, gender, education, migration background). The models include country and survey round fixed effects and use robust standard errors. The author also conducts linear regression models to examine the relationship between economic status loss and political attitudes (cultural conservatism, economic orientation, anti-redistribution preferences).

For the main independent variable, the author develops a novel "positional income" measure that quantifies a social class's relative economic position by measuring its distance from both the richest and poorest segments of society. Economic status loss is measured as the change in positional income over a five-year period.

### Reproducibility and Adaptability

The author provides a transparent description of the methodology, including the formula for calculating positional income and positional income change. The paper explains data sources, sample selection criteria, and model specifications in detail. The author also provides extensive robustness checks, testing alternative model specifications, data manipulation strategies, sample restrictions, and time spans.

For reproducibility, the ESS data is publicly available upon registration, though the EU-SILC microdata from Eurostat is not freely available for dissemination. The author notes that the replication package, including code and data, is available in Dataverse, which would facilitate reproduction of the analyses.

For adaptation to my thesis research, the methodology offers several valuable approaches:

1. The operationalization of economic status through positional income could be adapted to measure how different welfare regimes affect relative economic positioning across social classes.
2. The class-based approach could be extended to examine how welfare institutions mediate economic status for different social groups during economic transitions.
3. The methodology could be modified to compare how different welfare regime types (social democratic, conservative, liberal) perform in preventing economic status loss during economic shocks.

Potential challenges for adaptation include:

- Acquiring comparable income data across welfare regimes for sufficient time periods
- Developing appropriate measures of welfare institution characteristics to test their buffering effects
- Accounting for cross-national differences in class structure and political systems

The paper's robust statistical approach and extensive robustness checks provide a methodological template that could be adapted to examine how welfare institutions influence economic status and political behavior, though additional data on welfare policy characteristics would need to be incorporated.

## Findings and Evidence

### Summary of Key Findings

1. Social classes experiencing economic status loss (declining positional income) are significantly more likely to vote for populist radical right (PRR) parties.
2. This relationship holds even when controlling for absolute income levels and changes, indicating that relative economic positioning matters more than objective financial circumstances.
3. The magnitude of the effect is substantial: a one standard deviation increase in positional income decreases the probability of PRR voting by 1.7 percentage points, which is approximately half the effect of a one standard deviation increase in education.
4. Both left-wing and center-right parties perform worse among voters experiencing economic status loss, confirming the PRR benefits relative to all alternatives.
5. Economic status loss is associated with increased cultural conservatism (anti-immigration sentiment, Euroskepticism) but, notably, also with opposition to redistribution.
6. The relationship between economic status loss and PRR voting is stronger among voters with lower and medium levels of education, while highly educated voters appear less affected.
7. Neither class-level absolute income change nor absolute income level significantly predicts PRR voting, confirming that status concerns rather than material conditions drive support.
8. Economic status loss does not increase support for populist left or far-left parties, suggesting the effect is specific to right-wing populism.

### Supporting Evidence

The author provides several types of evidence to support these findings:

1. **Statistical evidence from regression analyses**: The coefficient for positional income change in the logistic regression predicting PRR voting is -0.14 (p<0.01), indicating that a one-unit increase in positional income decreases the probability of voting for a PRR party by 14 percentage points. The author calculates that a one standard deviation increase in positional income decreases the probability of PRR voting by 1.7 percentage points.
2. **Descriptive income trends**: The author presents detailed data visualization (Figures 2-4) showing how different social classes in Sweden experienced varying changes in economic status despite general income growth. For example, low-skilled service workers saw an 18% increase in absolute income but still experienced economic status loss (positional income drop of 0.12 units) because their income grew at the same pace as the poorest (15%) but much slower than the richest (35%).
3. **Predicted probabilities**: Figure 5 shows that PRR parties score 14% among voters experiencing the most typical decrease in positional income, compared to 11% among those experiencing the most typical increase, a substantial difference given the average PRR support level of 12%.
4. **Competitor effects**: Figure 6 demonstrates that both left-wing and center-right parties perform worse among voters experiencing economic status loss, confirming the relationship isn't driven by a specific competitor.
5. **Heterogeneity analyses**: Figure 7 shows the relationship between economic status loss and PRR voting is stronger among voters with lower and medium education levels but insignificant among those with tertiary education. The effect does not vary significantly by gender or migration background.
6. **Attitude analyses**: Economic status loss is associated with increased cultural conservatism (coefficient of -0.4 for a one-unit increase) and opposition to redistribution, but not with left-right self-placement or economic conservatism.
7. **Robustness checks**: The author conducts extensive robustness tests varying model specifications, data manipulation strategies, country samples, and time spans, all yielding comparable results.

### Implications for My Thesis

These findings have several important implications for my research on how welfare institutions mediate between economic vulnerability and political outcomes:

1. The evidence that relative economic positioning rather than absolute economic circumstances drives PRR support validates my theoretical emphasis on status stabilization as a key pathway through which welfare institutions prevent populist backlash.
2. The finding that economic status loss increases cultural conservatism supports my argument that welfare institutions shape political responses by influencing how economic grievances are translated into cultural frames.
3. The unexpected result that economic status loss increases opposition to redistribution helps explain why welfare retrenchment might drive PRR support - it's not just about material loss but about how reforms disrupt established status hierarchies.
4. The heterogeneous effects by education suggest welfare institutions may need to particularly focus on preserving status for less educated workers who are more vulnerable to status-based political appeals.
5. The evidence that class-level economic experiences affect political behavior even when controlling for individual characteristics validates my meso-level approach focusing on how welfare institutions shape group-level outcomes.

These findings suggest my research should focus on how different welfare regimes perform in maintaining relative economic positioning of social classes during economic transitions, particularly for more vulnerable groups, as a mechanism for preventing populist backlash.

## Discussion

### Interpretation of Findings

The author interprets the findings as confirming that economic status loss, not absolute economic decline, drives support for populist radical right parties. This interpretation challenges purely cultural or purely economic explanations for PRR voting by showing how voters give cultural readings to economic phenomena. The author argues that when economic resources are portrayed as a zero-sum game, those facing relative loss would be further harmed by redistributive policies that would benefit the poorest, explaining why economic status loss increases opposition to redistribution.

The author suggests these findings explain the apparent paradox of why PRR parties succeed among voters who are not absolute economic "losers" - these voters may be experiencing a decline in their relative economic position even while their absolute circumstances improve. This aligns with the paper's theoretical framework emphasizing that status operates as a zero-sum game, creating winners and losers even during periods of general economic growth.

The heterogeneous effects by education are interpreted as potentially reflecting differences in how voters with different educational levels subjectively interpret their economic circumstances, or the possibility that highly educated voters can draw on other types of status (like prestige) to compensate for economic status loss.

### Connections to Other Research

The author positions this research within three strands of scholarship on PRR voting:

1. **Cultural explanations**: The author acknowledges research arguing that cultural concerns (immigration, national identity) primarily drive PRR support (Norris and Inglehart 2019; Oesch and Rennwald 2018) but contends that cultural attitudes may themselves be shaped by economic experiences.
2. **Economic explanations**: The paper builds on research linking economic transformations (globalization, automation) to PRR support (Autor et al. 2020; Colantone and Stanig 2018) but offers a more precise mechanism - economic status loss rather than material deprivation.
3. **Status-based explanations**: The author extends work on subjective status perceptions and PRR voting (Gidron and Hall 2017; Hochschild 2016) by providing objective evidence of status loss rather than relying on self-reported perceptions.

The author also connects the findings to research on inequality and political behavior (Burgoon et al. 2019; Engler and Weisstanner 2020), arguing that the paper's class-based approach and comprehensive measure of economic status provides a stronger explanation for how inequality translates into political behavior.

The paper's discussion offers several crucial insights for my research on welfare institutions and political outcomes:

This connects to my interest in how welfare designs shape deservingness perceptions and solidarity boundaries.

This connects to my interest in how welfare reforms could be designed to maintain status while addressing economic vulnerabilities.
5. **Status versus resources**: The conceptual distinction between economic status and economic resources provides a framework for evaluating welfare institutions based on how they preserve relative social standing beyond merely providing material support.
