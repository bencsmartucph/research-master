# (Gingrich, 2019)Did State Responses to Automation Matter for Voters? challenges conventional wisdom about compensation policies. Rather than simply examining if such policies maintain electoral support in general, Gingrich specifically investigates whether they help shore up support for mainstream parties among those negatively affected by automation, and whether they prevent the rise of populist right parties among this same group.  Traditional compensatory policies primarily address economic insecurity through income protection but may fail to address status concerns. As Gingrich notes, right-populist parties often directly appeal to status concerns, while "mainstream left parties, dependent on a middle-class base (Gingrich and Hausermann, 2015), have reduced explicit class-based appeals (e.g., Evans and Tilley, 2017)."

    ## 1. Research Overview & Context
    
    ### Core Research Question & Significance
    
    - Primary question: "Does economic compensation actually shore up support for mainstream parties among the 'losers' of automation?" (p.1)
    - Broader significance: Tests whether the "embedded liberalism" thesis (that welfare states can moderate political extremism during economic disruption) applies to technological change
    - Timeliness: "In 2017, the New Yorker magazine featured a cover image portraying a group of marching robots, one turning to toss a coin to a bedraggled, left-behind human" (p.1) - symbolizing the political salience of automation
    
    ### Historical Context of Automation
    
    - "The employment share of 'routine jobs' – those using skills replaceable by technology – declined by 30–40% from the early 1990s to late 2000s in the European countries they examine" (p.1)
    - "Oesch and Rodrıguez Menes (2010) find that the employment share of 'routine jobs' – those using skills replaceable by technology – declined by 30–40% from the early 1990s to late 2000s in the European countries they examine" (p.1)
    - Automation created "substantial cross-country variation in experiences" based on differing welfare and labor market policies (p.1)
    
    ### Study Design & Methodology
    
    - Uses 20 waves of International Social Survey Programme (ISSP) data from 1995-2015
    - Categorizes respondents by occupation using Autor and Dorn's (2013) routine task intensity (RTI) index
    - "I convert varying occupational measures into a 2-digit ISCO-88 code, and link it to an aggregated 'routine task intensity' (RTI) index" (p.4)
    - Restricts analysis to "those in the labor market between the ages of 20 and 65, excluding students, non-working spouses, and the retired" (p.4)
    - Multinomial logistic regression analysis to examine the relationship between automation exposure and party preferences
    - Country fixed effects models to isolate within-country policy changes
    - Controls for "age, sex, having a college degree, having no upper secondary education, and being unemployed" (p.4)
    
    ### Key Operational Measures
    
    - Party preferences: Categorized into "non-social democratic left (Green, Left, and Communist parties), mainstream left (Social Democrats), mainstream right parties (Liberal, Christian Democrat, and Conservative), right-populist parties, and non-voters" (p.4)
    - Automation exposure: Five quintiles of routine task intensity (RTI) ranging from least (0) to most (1) exposed
    - "From least to most exposed: 0 (ISCO 12, 13, 21, 22, 24, 83); .25 (ISCO 31, 34, 51); 0.5 (ISCO 32, 52, 71, 91); .75 (ISCO 72, 81, 82 93); 1 (ISCO 41, 42, 73, 74)" (p.8, footnote 10)
    - Compensatory policies: Measured via inactivity rates (early retirement), in-kind social spending (public services), and employment protection legislation (EPL)
    
    ## 2. Detailed Compensatory Policy Framework
    
    ### Three Distinct Compensatory Approaches
    
    1. **Early Retirement Benefits** (passive compensation)
        - Measured by: "The percentage of men between 55 and 64 who are economically inactive" (p.2)
        - Primary beneficiaries: Older workers in declining occupations
        - Strongest in: Continental European welfare states
        - Example countries: Belgium, France, Germany
        - Purpose: "Cushioning against [income] effects" of job loss (p.3)
        - Policy logic: "For older workers threatened by automation, the Scandinavian and Continental paths offered substantially more protection, both reducing the likelihood of job loss and cushioning against its income effects" (p.3)
    2. **Public Service Expansion** (active compensation)
        - Measured by: "In-kind spending as a percentage of GDP – a measure of public service spending" (p.2)
        - Primary beneficiaries: All workers, especially service sector employees
        - Strongest in: Nordic welfare states
        - Example countries: Denmark, Sweden, Norway
        - Policy logic: "The Nordic countries expanded public sector jobs alongside a more regulated private labor market and less early retirement" (p.2)
        - This approach emphasized "expanded public sector jobs" (p.3) rather than passive transfers
    3. **Labor Market Regulation** (protective compensation)
        - Measured by: "Employment protection legislation (EPL), with higher numbers demonstrating stricter rules regarding tenure and dismissal of individuals in permanent jobs and more restrictions on hiring temporary workers" (p.2)
        - Primary beneficiaries: Workers in permanent employment
        - Strongest in: Continental and Nordic welfare states
        - Weakest in: Anglo-Saxon liberal market economies
        - Policy purpose: "Reducing the likelihood of job loss" (p.3)
        - Policy logic: Anglo-countries "tended to pursue a market approach to employment regulation (lower EPL) with less extensive support for either early retirement or public services" (p.2)
    
    ### Cross-National Variation in Compensatory Approaches
    
    - Three distinct policy clusters emerge following Iversen and Wren's (1998) analysis:
        - **Anglo-Saxon approach**: "Anglo-countries tended to pursue a market approach to employment regulation (lower EPL) with less extensive support for either early retirement or public services" (p.2)
        - **Continental European approach**: "Continental policymakers maintained regulated labor markets but limited public service expansion (relative to the Nordic countries), cementing a safety net built around transfers, especially early retirement" (p.2)
        - **Nordic approach**: "The Nordic countries expanded public sector jobs alongside a more regulated private labor market and less early retirement" (p.2)
    
    ### Temporal Evolution of Compensation
    
    - "Over time, these differences have blurred. The UK, for instance, substantially increased public service spending through the 2000s, whereas Germany limited both early retirement and cut parts of EPL" (p.3)
    - "Thus, there are substantial cross-place and cross-time differences in the type and degree of compensating approaches" (p.3)
    - This variation provides the analytical leverage to test compensation effects on political preferences
    
    ## 3. Dual Mechanisms: Material Insecurity vs. Status Anxiety
    
    ### Material Insecurity Mechanism
    
    - **Economic mechanism**: "Exposure to automation creates more economic insecurity among exposed workers, encouraging pro-redistributive attitudes" (p.3)
    - **Evidence basis**: "Recent work by Thewissen and Rueda (2019) shows that exposure to automation creates more economic insecurity among exposed workers, encouraging pro-redistributive attitudes" (p.3)
    - **Political expression**: "Those who are economically insecure, either because of threats to their own jobs, or changing demand for their skills, are more likely to support some form of redistributive policies. Such policies are traditionally associated with the mainstream left" (p.3)
    - **Expected pattern**: Economic insecurity should drive support for left parties offering material compensation
    
    ### Status Anxiety Mechanism
    
    - **Sociological mechanism**: "Automation has contributed to a decline in the overall demand for mid-skilled work" (p.3)
    - **Structural impacts**: "Existing mid-skilled workers thus face the risk of occupational downgrading (as upgrading through the career cycle is limited) while for new cohorts of workers mid-level skills no longer provide a secure route to a middle-class status" (p.3)
    - **Psychological impact**: Creates "'Status anxiety,' particularly among men" which "is a strong predictor of populist voting" (p.3)
    - **Political expression**: "Right-populist parties often directly appeal to status concerns, while mainstream left parties, dependent on a middle-class base, have reduced explicit class-based appeals" (p.3)
    - **Evidence basis**: "Im et al. (2018) show that those in occupations hard hit by technological change are more likely to support right populists" (p.3)
    - **Expected pattern**: Status anxiety should drive support for right-populist parties offering cultural/identity recognition
    
    ### The Fundamental Tension
    
    - **Dual threats**: "Automation then, simultaneously threatens the material well-being and social status of exposed workers, the former mechanism pushing voters to the mainstream left parties, while the latter pushes them to the populist right" (p.3)
    - **Political conflict**: Both mechanisms pull in different political directions, creating a cross-pressure on exposed voters
    - **Theoretical question**: "A large literature on compensation suggests that it should matter which of these mechanisms dominate" (p.3)
    
    ## 4. Compensation Theory & Hypotheses
    
    ### Embedded Liberalism Thesis
    
    - **Origins**: "Those studying economic integration have long argued that policies that directly reduce the negative effects of economic volatility on workers' material well-being can underpin workers' support for both open markets and mainstream left politics" (p.3)
    - **Prior Evidence (Globalization)**: "Swank and Betz (2003) investigate this logic of 'embedded liberalism,' finding that social welfare programs moderate the effect of global integration on support for the far-right" (p.3)
    - **Recent Application (Economic Shocks)**: "Halikiopoulou and Vlandas (2016) make a similar claim with regard to economic shocks, arguing that generous welfare states, in securing individual income, reduce the effect of unemployment on populist voting" (p.3)
    
    ### Application to Automation
    
    - **Theoretical Extension**: "When the above logic is extended to automation, it suggests that these compensatory approaches, in reducing economic insecurity, should reduce the appeal of populist parties among exposed workers and maintain their support for parties on the left" (p.3)
    - **Assumed Mechanism**: By addressing material insecurity, compensation should make mainstream parties more attractive than populist alternatives
    
    ### Formal Hypotheses
    
    1. H1: "All else equal, greater spending on public services, more access to early retirement benefits, and higher EPL – in cushioning the material effects of automation – should increase support for center-left parties among those negatively exposed to automation" (p.3)
    2. H2: "All else equal, these same compensatory mechanisms should reduce support for populist parties among those negatively exposed to automation" (p.3)
    
    ## 5. Detailed Empirical Findings
    
    ### Baseline Effects of Automation Exposure on Party Choice
    
    - **Dual Political Effect**: "For the sample as a whole, RTI is associated with increased probability of choosing the mainstream left (column 1) as well as right populists (column 5)" (p.4)
    - **Magnitude**: "The marginal effect of RTI on the probability of selecting a populist party is consistently around 1–2% – a non-trivial effect given that an average of 6% of respondents selected populists" (p.5)
    - **Confirmation of Dual Mechanisms**: This finding confirms that automation simultaneously activates both material insecurity (left voting) and status anxiety (populist voting)
    
    ### Effects of Compensation on Mainstream Left Support
    
    - **Cross-Level Interactions**: "The cross-level interactions between exposure to RTI and inactivity (early retirement), in-kind spending (public services) and EPL (labor market regulation) are all positive and significant (columns 2, 3, and 4)" (p.4)
    - **Interpretation**: "This outcome implies that there is a greater difference in support for the mainstream left among those with higher and lower levels of exposure in all three compensatory contexts" (p.4)
    - **Visualization**: "At low levels of inactivity, spending, and EPL, an increase in RTI is not a significant predictor of mainstream left vote choices, whereas at high levels, it is" (p.4-5)
    - **Partial Support for H1**: "More compensation is associated with greater relative support for the left among the exposed" (p.5)
    - **Unexpected Effect**: "This outcome, however, is driven in part by lower support among those with low exposure" (p.5)
    - **Key Insight**: "Compensation may shore up support for the left among one group, but reduce it among another" (p.5)
    
    ### Effects of Compensation on Right-Populist Support
    
    - **Early Retirement**: "Higher levels of inactivity (when RTI is at zero) is associated with increased populist choice, and it has no additional effect on the exposed (column 6)" (p.5)
    - **Public Services**: "In-kind spending has a negative coefficient, but it is not significant" (p.5)
    - **Restricted Sample Findings**: "When we restrict the analysis to places with a right populist present... there is a significant negative effect of spending on populist support among those with no exposure (RTI equal to zero), but it has no reducing effect among the highly exposed" (p.5)
    - **Labor Market Regulation**: "EPL is positively signed but not significant (column 8)" (p.5)
    - **Overall Pattern**: "These effects are similar, or even enhanced, in high-compensation contexts" (p.6)
    - **Counter-intuitive Finding**: "If anything, compensation is associated with more populist voting" (p.5)
    - **Rejection of H2**: "There is little evidence that compensation reduces populism, and may in fact enhance it" (p.6)
    
    ### Fixed Effects (Within-Country) Analysis
    
    - **Mainstream Left**: "We see a positive direct effect of inactivity on mainstream left choice, but no interactive effect with exposure (RTI) (column 2). As with the multinomial logit models, there is no direct effect of either in-kind spending or EPL on support for the mainstream left, but both, in relative terms, enhance support the mainstream l*eft among exposed workers (columns 3 and 4)" (p.6)*
    - ***Right-Populist**: "Here, inactivity has a relatively strong negative effect on populist support, and it reduces the effect of exposure. This finding suggests that downward changes within countries in levels of inactivity – cut backs – are associated with both increases in overall populist support and increases among those most negatively exposed to automation" (p.6)*
    - **Public Service Spending**: "The results for in-kind spending are similar to the multinomial logit models discussed above, exposed workers are more supportive in contexts with high in-kind spending" (p.6)
    - **Summary**: "They do not provide consistent evidence that compensation has the political effects hypothesized" (p.6)
    
    ### Key Overall Findings Summary
    
    - **Support for H1**: "In line with H1, there is a positive association between compensation and relative mainstream left support among the highly exposed group across place and time" (p.6)
    - **Rejection of H2**: "Across places, there is little evidence that compensation reduces populism, and may in fact enhance it" (p.6)
    - **Limited Evidence for Cutback Effects**: "The fixed effects models hint that cutbacks in early retirement are associated with more support for populism, but not cuts in either spending or EPL" (p.6)
    - **Contradiction of Embedded Liberalism**: "This evidence then, is unsupportive of the claim that more extensive compensation in the face of past automation substantially moderated populism among exposed workers" (p.6)
    
    ## 6. The Limitations of Material Compensation (The Populist Paradox)
    
    ### The Populist Spending Paradox
    
    - **Counterintuitive Finding**: Compensation did not reduce populist voting as expected, and in some models actually increased it
    - **Pattern Consistency**: "In the observational evidence presented here, we see that the compensatory approaches that deeply modified the structures of advanced labor markets appear to have weak or inconsistent effects on how voters responded to new economic pressures – in some cases shoring up support for both the mainstream left and right populists" (p.6)
    - **The Core Paradox**: Despite effective material compensation, status anxiety and populist appeal remained or even grew
    
    ### Compensation's Limited Efficacy Against Status Politics
    
    - **Beyond Material Insecurity**: "It is possible that compensatory policies are less important for how structural economic pressures play out politically in the face of automation than past forms of compensation were in the face of globalization, as affected workers face the risk of losing both income and status" (p.6)
    - **Dual Threat Hypothesis**: "If voters react to such structural pressures similarly, regardless of the range of compensatory approaches that address their dislocation, then it may point to the limits of such policies alone in reducing dislocation" (p.6-7)
    - **Status vs. Income**: Traditional compensation addresses income but fails to address status loss
    - **Psychological Dynamics**: Downward occupational mobility creates status anxiety that purely material compensation doesn't address
    
    ### Nuanced Assessment of Compensation
    
    - **Moderation vs. Elimination**: "Social programs very likely have prevented much radicalization among voters in the face of economic shocks" (p.6) even if they didn't eliminate populist appeal
    - **Against Austerity**: "Pronounced fiscal austerity and direct cuts to social programs may stimulate a backlash" (p.6)
    - **Limited but Important Role**: Compensation doesn't eliminate populism but may prevent its worst manifestations
    
    ## 7. Reimagining Policy: From Compensation to Inclusion
    
    ### Paradigm Shift: From "Losers" to "Winners"
    
    - **Central Conclusion**: "This latter interpretation suggests that compensating the 'losers' of change may not be enough to prevent the far-reaching political consequences of automation, instead we need to consider policies that make more people 'winners' in the new economic environment" (p.7)
    - **Reframing Need**: Move beyond compensating losses to creating new opportunities for positive identity
    - **Implied Policy Shift**: From passive transfers to active economic inclusion and status-preserving opportunities
    
    ### Age-Based Policy Considerations
    
    - **Differentiated Needs**: "For older workers, who are unlikely to retrain and join the public sector workforce, early retirement benefits and high EPL are particularly important" (p.8, footnote 14)
    - **Generational Differences**: "For younger workers, entering the labor force in an era of automation support for greater job opportunities (rather than compensation) is more crucial" (p.8, footnote 14)
    - **Job Creation Focus**: "Both expanded public employment and, more controversially, lower EPL, may create more job opportunities" for younger workers (p.8, footnote 14)
    
    ### Labor Market Structure vs. Individual Experience
    
    - **Structural Approach**: "Instead, we may need to examine the way in which economic pressures matter, and how they are mobilized, looking less at experienced insecurity, and more at how labor markets structure opportunities and choices for voters" (p.7)
    - **Systemic Solutions**: Address how labor markets create and distribute status and opportunities, not just compensate individual losses
    - **Implied Vision**: Create pathways to meaningful economic roles with social status, rather than just passive safety nets

## Introduction and Motivation

Gingrich frames this investigation within the context of the dramatic decline in "routine jobs" over recent decades: "Oesch and Rodríguez Menes (2010) find that the employment share of 'routine jobs' – those using skills replaceable by technology – declined by 30–40% from the early 1990s to late 2000s in the European countries they examine." While this decline was widespread, the author notes that social welfare programs and labor regulations created "substantial cross-country variation in experiences," providing the empirical variation needed to examine the political consequences of different policy responses.

## Theoretical Framework

### Policies Targeting Post-Industrial Transition

Gingrich begins by identifying three main policy approaches countries adopted to address the challenges of deindustrialization in the 1980s and 1990s:

1. **Early retirement benefits**: Measured as the percentage of men aged 55-64 who are economically inactive, this policy approach essentially removes older workers from the labor force.
2. **Labor market regulation**: Measured through employment protection legislation (EPL), this approach makes it more difficult to dismiss workers in permanent positions and restricts temporary employment.
3. **Expansion of public services**: Measured as in-kind spending as a percentage of GDP, this approach creates new jobs in the public sector.

These policies are contextualized within Iversen and Wren's (1998) framework of different national approaches to employment regulation:

"Iversen and Wren's (1998) early analysis of these differences maps onto Figure 1. They argue that Anglo-countries tended to pursue a market approach to employment regulation (lower EPL) with less extensive support for either early retirement or public services. Continental policymakers maintained regulated labor markets but limited public service expansion (relative to the Nordic countries), cementing a safety net built around transfers, especially early retirement (Ebbinghaus, 2006). Finally, the Nordic countries expanded public sector jobs alongside a more regulated private labor market and less early retirement."

### Compensation and Voter Preferences

The theoretical framework then explores how automation affects voter preferences through two distinct mechanisms:

1. **Economic insecurity mechanism**: "Recent work by Thewissen and Rueda (2019) shows that exposure to automation creates more economic insecurity among exposed workers, encouraging pro-redistributive attitudes. Those who are economically insecure, either because of threats to their own jobs, or changing demand for their skills, are more likely to support some form of redistributive policies. Such policies are traditionally associated with the mainstream left."
2. **Status anxiety mechanism**: "Automation has contributed to a decline in the overall demand for mid-skilled work (Goos and Manning, 2014). Existing mid-skilled workers thus face the risk of occupational downgrading (as upgrading through the career cycle is limited) while for new cohorts of workers mid-level skills no longer provide a secure route to a middle-class status. 'Status anxiety,' particularly among men, is a strong predictor of populist voting (Gidron and Hall, 2017; Kurer, 2018)."

These competing mechanisms create a theoretical tension - automation simultaneously threatens both material well-being (pushing voters toward mainstream left parties) and social status (pushing voters toward populist right parties). The central theoretical question is how compensatory policies influence the balance between these mechanisms.

Gingrich builds upon the literature on "embedded liberalism," which suggests that welfare policies can mitigate the negative effects of economic change on political attitudes:

"In an early study of populist voting, Swank and Betz (2003) investigate this logic of 'embedded liberalism,' finding that social welfare programs moderate the effect of global integration on support for the far-right. More recently, Halikiopoulou and Vlandas (2016) make a similar claim with regard to economic shocks, arguing that generous welfare states, in securing individual income, reduce the effect of unemployment on populist voting."

### Hypotheses

Based on this theoretical framework, Gingrich proposes two hypotheses:

"H1: All else equal, greater spending on public services, more access to early retirement benefits, and higher EPL – in cushioning the material effects of automation – should increase support for center-left parties among those negatively exposed to automation."

"H2 All else equal, these same compensatory mechanisms should reduce support for populist parties among those negatively exposed to automation."

## Hypothesis Tests and Results

### Hypothesis 1: Compensation and Support for Mainstream Left

Hypothesis 1 predicted that compensatory policies would increase support for center-left parties among those negatively exposed to automation. The findings offer partial support for this hypothesis but reveal a more complex relationship than initially theorized.

The multinomial logistic regression (Table 1) shows that none of the compensation variables have a direct effect on support for mainstream left parties when RTI equals zero. However, the cross-level interactions between exposure to RTI and all three compensatory measures (inactivity/early retirement, in-kind spending/public services, and EPL/labor market regulation) are positive and statistically significant.

This result indicates that there is a greater difference in support for mainstream left parties between highly exposed and less exposed workers in contexts with higher levels of compensation. As Gingrich explains: "At low levels of inactivity, spending, and EPL, an increase in RTI is not a significant predictor of mainstream left vote choices, whereas at high levels, it is."

Figure 2a visually demonstrates this interaction effect by showing the predicted marginal effect of moving from the least to the most exposed occupations on choosing the mainstream left, comparing contexts with low values (10th percentile) versus high values (90th percentile) of each compensatory measure.

However, Gingrich notes an important qualification to these findings: "This outcome, however, is driven in part by lower support among those with low exposure (supplementary Figure A1). Compensation may shore up support for the left among one group, but reduce it among another." This suggests a more complex dynamic where compensation policies create divergent effects among different groups defined by their exposure to automation.

The fixed effects models (Table 2) yield similar results, showing that within countries, increases in in-kind spending and EPL are associated with greater support for mainstream left parties among exposed workers. Meanwhile, early retirement benefits have a positive direct effect on mainstream left support but no significant interactive effect with RTI.

These findings suggest that while compensatory policies do help maintain or increase support for mainstream left parties among workers most exposed to automation (confirming part of H1), they may simultaneously reduce support among less exposed workers - a complexity not anticipated in the original hypothesis. This nuanced effect helps explain why overall support for mainstream left parties has not necessarily increased despite expansions in compensation in many countries.

### Hypothesis 2: Compensation and Support for Populist Right

Hypothesis 2 predicted that compensatory policies would reduce support for populist parties among those negatively exposed to automation. The findings do not support this hypothesis and, in some cases, suggest the opposite effect.

The multinomial logistic regression (Table 1) shows that higher levels of early retirement benefits (inactivity) are associated with increased populist choice overall, with no additional effect among the most exposed workers. This contradicts the hypothesis that compensation would reduce populist support.

For in-kind spending (public services), the coefficient is negative but not statistically significant in the full sample. When restricting the analysis to countries with right-populist parties present, there is a significant negative effect of spending on populist support among those with no exposure to automation (RTI equal to zero), but this effect does not extend to highly exposed workers. For EPL (labor market regulation), the effect is positive but not significant.

Gingrich summarizes these findings starkly: "These results suggest, if anything, compensation is associated with more populist voting." Figure 2b visualizes these effects, showing that the marginal effect of RTI on the probability of selecting a populist party remains consistently around 1-2% regardless of compensation levels - "a non-trivial effect given that an average of 6% of respondents selected populists."

The fixed effects models (Table 2) reveal additional nuances. Within countries, decreases in early retirement benefits are associated with both increases in overall populist support and increases in support among workers highly exposed to automation. This suggests that cutbacks to early retirement may drive populist voting. However, for in-kind spending, the results mirror the cross-sectional analysis, with spending associated with increased populist support among exposed workers - directly contradicting the hypothesis.

These findings represent a significant challenge to the "embedded liberalism" theory as applied to automation. While compensation may help maintain mainstream left support among exposed workers, it does not appear to reduce populist voting and may even enhance it. This suggests that the status anxiety mechanism driving populist support is not effectively addressed by material compensation alone.

## Results and Theoretical Implications

### Summary of Findings

The paper's key findings can be summarized as follows:

1. Workers in occupations most exposed to automation tend to support both mainstream left parties and right-populist parties more than the mainstream right.
2. Compensation policies are associated with increased support for mainstream left parties among workers exposed to automation, but this effect is partly driven by reduced support among less exposed workers.
3. Contrary to expectations, compensation does not reduce support for populist right parties among workers exposed to automation and may even enhance it in some cases.
4. Within countries, reductions in early retirement benefits are associated with increased populist voting, but there is no consistent effect for other forms of compensation.

### Theoretical Implications

These findings challenge the "embedded liberalism" thesis that compensation can fully mitigate the political consequences of economic disruption. As Gingrich argues:

"If voters react to such structural pressures similarly, regardless of the range of compensatory approaches that address their dislocation, then it may point to the limits of such policies alone in reducing dislocation."

The results suggest that economic insecurity and status anxiety operate simultaneously but are addressed differently by policy interventions. Compensation policies may address economic insecurity (benefiting mainstream left parties among exposed workers) without addressing status anxiety (failing to reduce populist right support).

This interpretation aligns with Gingrich's more recent work where she suggests "policy should focus on making more people 'winners' in the changed economic landscape, not merely offering after-the-fact compensatory solutions."

The paper also highlights the potential tension between different electoral constituencies. Compensation policies that benefit exposed workers may simultaneously alienate less exposed workers, creating a political dilemma for mainstream left parties.

## Social Psychological Mechanisms

The paper's findings point to complex social psychological mechanisms that help explain why welfare policies do not consistently moderate support for far-right parties among those exposed to automation. Several key mechanisms emerge:

### Status Anxiety vs. Economic Insecurity

A central insight from the paper is that automation creates two distinct forms of insecurity - material economic insecurity and social status anxiety - that operate simultaneously but respond differently to policy interventions:

"Automation then, simultaneously threatens the material well-being and social status of exposed workers, the former mechanism pushing voters to the mainstream left parties, while the latter pushes them to the populist right."

Traditional compensatory policies primarily address economic insecurity through income protection but may fail to address status concerns. As Gingrich notes, right-populist parties often directly appeal to status concerns, while "mainstream left parties, dependent on a middle-class base (Gingrich and Hausermann, 2015), have reduced explicit class-based appeals (e.g., Evans and Tilley, 2017)."

This creates a situation where compensation may successfully address one source of grievance (economic insecurity) while leaving another unaddressed (status anxiety), explaining why such policies do not reduce populist right support.

### Coalition Reconfiguration

Another important psychological mechanism relates to how welfare policies reshape voting coalitions. Gingrich's findings suggest that compensation policies may strengthen support for mainstream left parties among exposed workers precisely because they weaken support among less exposed workers.

This points to a deeper process of coalition reconfiguration, where welfare policies themselves become markers of group identity and interest. Workers with low exposure to automation may perceive compensatory policies as benefiting "others" at their expense, pushing them away from parties that champion such policies.

In her more recent work, Gingrich has elaborated on this dynamic, arguing that "there has been a class reconfiguration in voting in Europe over the last 30 years" where "the left in general has been increasingly relying on white collar voters in particular occupations such as professional services and teachers."

### Cohort Replacement Rather Than Voter Switching

A third psychological mechanism relates to how political preferences form and change over time. Gingrich has argued that shifts in voting patterns are frequently misunderstood: "Instead of working class voters flipping parties to vote for the radical right, 'what has actually happened is that there's been a cohort replacement,'" she explains. "The social democrats voting base among the manual working class has gotten much older, whereas new working class voters have gone into more different sets of parties."

This cohort replacement mechanism suggests that compensatory policies might have limited effectiveness because they're trying to maintain political coalitions that are naturally eroding through demographic change rather than persuading existing voters to maintain their party loyalty.

## Conclusion

Jane Gingrich's paper "Did State Responses to Automation Matter for Voters?" makes a significant contribution to our understanding of the political consequences of technological change and the limitations of policy interventions in addressing them.

The research reveals that workers exposed to automation are more likely to support both mainstream left parties and populist right parties. More importantly, it demonstrates that compensatory policies have not consistently prevented the rise of populist voting among these workers, challenging conventional wisdom about the political efficacy of welfare state interventions.

This finding has profound implications for policymakers seeking to address the challenges of technological disruption. As Gingrich concludes:

"If voters react to such structural pressures similarly, regardless of the range of compensatory approaches that address their dislocation, then it may point to the limits of such policies alone in reducing dislocation. Instead, we may need to examine the way in which economic pressures matter, and how they are mobilized, looking less at experienced insecurity, and more at how labor markets structure opportunities and choices for voters."

In her final assessment, Gingrich suggests a fundamental reorientation of policy approaches: "this latter interpretation suggests that compensating the 'losers' of change may not be enough to prevent the far-reaching political consequences of automation, instead we need to consider policies that make more people 'winners' in the new economic environment."

This conclusion represents both a challenge to existing policy paradigms and a call for more innovative approaches that address not just the material consequences of technological change but also its social and psychological dimensions. In a world where automation continues to reshape labor markets at an accelerating pace, understanding these dynamics becomes increasingly crucial for maintaining democratic stability.
