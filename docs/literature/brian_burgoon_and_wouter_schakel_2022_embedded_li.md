****investigates the complex relationship between welfare state provisions and anti-globalization nationalism in political party platforms across industrialized democracies. The study emerges in an era of increasing anti-globalization sentiment among political parties, particularly radical-right and radical-left parties, with nationalism reaching its "highest point in more than fifty years" (p. 53) in the study's time frame.

The authors identify a fundamental theoretical puzzle in the political economy literature: welfare states might either dampen or amplify anti-globalization nationalism. These opposing possibilities lead to the paper's central research question: "Do more generous welfare states tend to dampen, drive or have little impact upon anti-globalisation nationalism?" (p. 51).

The paper makes a valuable contribution by explicitly theorizing and empirically testing two competing mechanisms:

1. The "embedded liberalism" effect, where welfare policies mitigate economic insecurities associated with globalization, thereby reducing anti-globalization sentiment
2. The "embedded nationalism" effect, where welfare protections become cherished accomplishments to be defended against perceived threats from globalization, potentially intensifying anti-globalization sentiment

As the authors explain:

> "On the one hand, social protection has an 'embedded liberalism' effect, mitigating economic insecurities associated with globalisation and thereby dampening anti-globalisation nationalism. On the other hand, social protection has an 'embedded nationalism' effect, awakening worries that globalisation may undermine hard-won provisions, thereby deepening anti-globalisation" (p. 50).
> 

The research is particularly important for understanding the rise of nationalist populism and radical parties across the political spectrum. The authors test their theories using an extensive dataset of party manifestos from over 585 political parties in 21-32 industrialized democracies spanning from 1960 to 2017, applying rigorous methodological controls.

One of the most compelling aspects of this paper is how it nuances our understanding of the relationship between welfare states and nationalism by carefully distinguishing between different types of anti-globalization sentiment and different party families.

## Theoretical Framework and hypotheses

### The Embedded Liberalism Perspective

The paper first establishes the embedded liberalism perspective, rooted in Karl Polanyi's work and elaborated by John Ruggie, which suggests that welfare states can mitigate economic insecurities associated with globalization. The authors explain:

> "Rooted in the tradition of Karl Polanyi's Great Transformation (1944), many scholars argue that the welfare state's insurance, redistribution and/or compensation can ease the economic pain and insecurities accompanying political and economic globalisation. John Ruggie famously dubbed 'embedded liberalism' as the post-War system of mutually reinforcing multilateral economic openness with safeguards to protect national societies (Ruggie 1982)" (p. 54).
> 

This perspective holds that extensive welfare provisions help citizens and their political representatives embrace globalization rather than resisting it. Studies in this tradition suggest that generous welfare states foster support for economic openness by directly dampening nationalism or by moderating the tendency of economic hardship to drive anti-globalization sentiment. As the authors note:

> "The welfare state's fostering of support for globalisation, here, can involve social policies directly dampening nationalist backlash (Hays 2009), or negatively moderating, hence dampening, the tendency of economic suffering to drive anti-globalisation nationalism (Burgoon 2009, 2013)" (p. 54).
> 

### The Embedded Nationalism Perspective

Conversely, the embedded nationalism perspective suggests that welfare states may actually intensify anti-globalization sentiment by fostering concern about threats to cherished social protections. As the authors explain:

> "Welfare state protections are among the most popular, widely cherished policy accomplishments in the industrialised democracies to have developed such protections (Brooks and Manza 2007; Pierson 1994). This can colour the way political actors view their country's openness to and engagement with the world. Various aspects of economic and political globalisation threaten, or are portrayed to threaten, hard-won welfare protections at the national level" (p. 54).
> 

This dynamic can manifest through concerns about regulatory convergence, race-to-the-bottom pressures, or perceptions that immigration might overburden welfare systems. The authors highlight how:

> "Such real or perceived threats to generous social policies can, in turn, spur a dynamic that is the opposite of the more familiar embedded liberalism – they can spar, namely, 'embedded nationalism', involving not just welfare chauvinism but also anti-globalisation nationalism and radical populism to defend one's welfare state from hostile foreign threats" (p. 55).
> 

### Hypotheses Development

Building on these competing theoretical perspectives, the authors develop four sophisticated hypotheses:

1. Three competing general hypotheses about the relationship between welfare effort and anti-globalization nationalism (H1a, H1b, H1c)
2. A hypothesis about how welfare effort's effects vary across different faces of globalization (H2)
3. A hypothesis about how welfare effort's effects vary across political party families (H3)
4. A hypothesis specific to radical left parties and their responses to EU integration and immigration (H4)

For H2, they argue that welfare effort should have different effects depending on the aspect of globalization in question:

> "Welfare effort (extent of social policy intervention) is less likely to dampen the anti-globalization nationalism of political parties with respect to EU-integration or immigration than with respect to general political-economic globalization" (p. 57).
> 

They theorize that EU integration and immigration have more direct implications for national welfare states than other aspects of globalization, making them more likely to activate embedded nationalism dynamics.

For H3, they expect welfare effort to play out differently across party families:

> "More substantial or generous welfare effort will more strongly diminish anti-globalization nationalism among mainstream parties (Social Democratic, Liberal, Christian Democratic and Conservative party families) than among radical left and radical right parties" (p. 59).
> 

The authors argue that mainstream parties focus primarily on economic concerns, while radical parties emphasize issue ownership and multifaceted cultural and political concerns that make them more sensitive to how globalization might threaten national welfare autonomy.

Finally, H4 specifically focuses on radical left parties:

> "For radical left parties more than for other parties, more generous welfare effort should least dampen or more substantially deepen the anti-globalization nationalism with respect to immigration and EU integration than with respect to other faces of globalization" (p. 60).
> 

This hypothesis reflects the authors' expectation that radical left parties are particularly invested in defending social protection while also being more cosmopolitan on general issues than their radical-right counterparts.

## Methodology

### Data Sources and Sample

The study employs an impressive dataset covering political parties in industrialized democracies between 1960 and 2017. The sample includes:

- 585 political parties
- Between 21-32 countries (depending on availability of welfare state measures)
- The countries represented include Australia, Austria, Belgium, Canada, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Iceland, Ireland, Italy, Japan, Luxembourg, Netherlands, New Zealand, Norway, Portugal, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, United Kingdom, and United States

### Dependent Variables

The authors utilize four carefully constructed measures of anti-globalization nationalism:

1. **Anti-globalization (broad)**: A comprehensive measure including statements about internationalism, protectionism, European Union, and nationalism
2. **Anti-globalization (narrow)**: Focused specifically on internationalism and protectionism
3. **Anti-EU**: Focused on attitudes toward European Union integration
4. **Anti-immigration**: Based on Dancygier and Margalit's (2020) coding of party manifestos on immigration

The authors create these measures by calculating "net" measures that combine:

> "...positions embracing minus positions eschewing such anti-globalisation nationalism. To create more normal distributions, we take the natural logarithm of the 'positive' statements about these anti-global nationalism issues and subtract the natural logarithm of the 'negative' statements about them (adding 0.5 to both to avoid zeroes)" (p. 61).
> 

For all measures except anti-immigration, the authors rely on the Manifesto Data Project (MPD). They address the MPD's lack of immigration coding by incorporating Dancygier and Margalit's dataset, which:

> "...builds explicitly on the MPD-based methodology of coding manifestos and has the best over-time coverage of any attempted coding of party-positioning towards migration, making it comprehensive and compatible with our MPD-based measures of other aspects of anti-globalisation nationalism" (p. 61).
> 

The authors also carefully address validity questions, noting that their measures correlate highly with expert codings from sources like the Chapel Hill Expert Survey.

### Independent Variables

The study employs four different measures of welfare state effort:

1. **Social security transfers** (% of GDP): Captures social assistance provisions, primarily passive transfers for unemployment, old-age, sickness, etc.
2. **Total social expenditures** (% of GDP): A more encompassing measure of social policies including in-kind services
3. **Total welfare and education spending**: Combines total social expenditures with public education spending
4. **Welfare state generosity**: Based on the Comparative Welfare Entitlement Database's index of generosity

The authors explain their choice of spending-based measures:

> "The first three of our independent variables are focussed on expenditures as a percentage of GDP, capturing the take-up and economic footprint of social policies, and thereby their felt presence in the country's political-economic life. [...] we are interested here in the consequences of welfare state effort or extent, and spending captures such effort, also in terms of what enters political consciousness and observed importance to a country's economic health" (p. 64).
> 

### Control Variables and Estimation Approach

The methodology includes careful controls for potential confounders:

- Party-specific controls: party family, vote share in last election, and left-right platform positioning
- Country-year conditions: Polity IV democracy measure, KOF index of de facto globalization, and unemployment rate
- Fixed country and decade effects to address omitted variable bias, heteroskedasticity, and error correlation
- Lagged values based on five-year moving averages to address simultaneity issues

The authors employ OLS estimators with these controls and report extensive robustness checks.

## Key Findings

### General Effects of Welfare Effort on Anti-Globalization Nationalism

The study's first key finding is that welfare effort tends to dampen most measures of anti-globalization nationalism, suggesting that the "embedded liberalism" dynamic generally predominates:

> "It is clear from Figure 3 that welfare effort tends to diminish most measures of anti-globalisation nationalism. All of our measures of lagged welfare effort are statistically significantly associated with both of our anti-globalisation meaures (broad and narrow). With respect to anti-EU positioning, furthermore, we see that most measures of welfare effort are negatively signed (significantly so for social-security transfers and for total social expenditures), also suggesting welfare-effort dampening anti-EU position-taking" (p. 67).
> 

This pattern holds across different measures of welfare effort, indicating that more generous welfare states are generally associated with less anti-globalization sentiment in party platforms. The authors interpret this as evidence that the economic security provided by welfare states tends to outweigh concerns about globalization threatening welfare provisions.

### Variation Across Different Faces of Globalization

The second major finding supports H2, revealing that welfare effort has different effects depending on the type of anti-globalization sentiment being measured:

> "Every measure of welfare effort tends to correlate less negatively and less significantly with anti-EU and with anti-immigration measures than with the other and more composite measures of anti-globalisation nationalism" (p. 67).
> 

Specifically, anti-immigration is identified as "the obvious outlier, where our measures of welfare effort tend to have no significant impact, except for a significantly positive association for welfare state generosity" (p. 67). This suggests that the "embedded nationalism" dynamic is more prominent for faces of globalization that more directly threaten welfare states.

The authors present compelling evidence that while welfare effort generally dampens broad anti-globalization sentiment, it is less effective at reducing (or may even increase) opposition to EU integration and immigration specifically. They attribute this to the fact that these aspects of globalization more directly impact national welfare systems.

### Variation Across Party Families

The third major finding concerns differences in how welfare effort affects the anti-globalization positions of different party families. The results partially support H3 about mainstream versus radical parties:

> "The mainstream parties tend more often to have a negative association with measures of anti-globalisation backlash (15/16 for Social Democratic, 16/16 for Liberal, 9/16 for Christian Democratic and 14/16 for Conservative parties) than do either radical left or radical right parties (8/16 for Radical Left and 9/16 for Radical Right)" (p. 69).
> 

The authors also note that these negative effects are "more often statistically significant for mainstream than for radical parties," though there is "enough volatility across party families, and particularly for radical left parties, that one cannot speak of a consistent U-curve obtaining" (p. 69).

### Radical Left Parties and Different Faces of Globalization

The fourth major finding supports H4, showing that radical left parties respond distinctively to different aspects of globalization under more generous welfare conditions:

> "Radical left parties, often enough, respond to more generous welfare effort by dampening their anti-globalisation nationalism. But they are alone among all party families in responding to greater welfare effort with increased anti-EU and anti-immigrant position-taking. This response is statistically significant in 5 of the 8 models" (p. 70).
> 

The authors interpret this as evidence that radical left parties are torn between embedded liberalism and embedded nationalism reactions:

> "For aspects of globalisation involving less direct pressures on generous welfare states – as obtains for our anti-globalisation (narrow) measure – we see radical left parties respond more with 'embedded liberalism' than with 'embedded nationalism'. However, it is precisely the opposite with respect to the two faces of anti-globalisation nationalism, anti-EU-integration and anti-immigration, that involve reigning-in possible constraints on generous welfare states" (p. 70).
> 

## Robustness Tests

The authors conducted extensive robustness checks to ensure the validity of their findings:

1. Alternative specifications of the dataset (without linear interpolation between election years; focusing on common baselines of country-years)
2. Alternative specifications of anti-globalization nationalism (different composites of measures)
3. Alternative specifications of welfare state effort (spending per unemployed person or head of population; BTI social safety net codings)
4. Different control variables (either fewer controls, such as dropping country dummies, or additional controls, such as a party's welfare chauvinism or a country's macroeconomic position)
5. Alternative estimators (jack-knifing of standard errors, full jack-knife analysis, random intercept multi-level models with countries as level 2 variables)

The authors report that "all of the patterns reported in Figures 3–5 hold up to many sensitivity and robustness tests" (p. 70), strengthening confidence in their findings.

## Interpretation and Implications

### Theoretical Implications

The study makes several important theoretical contributions:

1. It clarifies when and how welfare states dampen or intensify anti-globalization nationalism, reconciling contradictory findings in previous literature
2. It demonstrates that the "embedded liberalism" and "embedded nationalism" dynamics coexist but predominate under different conditions
3. It reveals the complex, often contingent relationship between welfare states and political positioning on globalization
4. It highlights how different faces of globalization interact differently with welfare state politics

The authors offer a nuanced interpretation of their findings:

> "We see these as patterns that begin to sort out the offsetting and uneven 'embedded liberalism' and 'embedded nationalism' effects of social policy for anti-globalisation nationalism" (p. 71).
> 

### Political Implications

The findings have important implications for understanding contemporary politics:

1. They help explain why anti-immigration and anti-EU sentiment may persist or even increase in countries with generous welfare states
2. They illuminate why radical parties, particularly radical left parties, may have complex, seemingly contradictory positions on different aspects of globalization
3. They suggest that welfare states generally moderate nationalist backlash, but not uniformly across all domains of globalization or political actors

The results are particularly relevant for understanding the politics of radical parties. As the authors explain:

> "Among mainstream parties, the 'embedded nationalism' dynamic ought to be less salient relative to the 'embedded liberalism' dynamic, given the focus on economic concerns and opportunities quite central to the positioning of such parties. As a result, welfare effort may more readily dampen than deepen anti-globalisation among these parties. Among radical left and radical right parties, however, we expect the opposite, that the 'embedded nationalism' dynamic will receive more explicit framing and cuing attention in party discussions than the 'embedded liberalism' dynamic" (p. 59).
> 

### Methodological Contributions

The paper makes methodological contributions by:

1. Developing nuanced measures of different aspects of anti-globalization nationalism
2. Demonstrating how to analyze party positioning across multiple dimensions simultaneously
3. Employing multiple measures of welfare effort to ensure robust findings
4. Using a long-term historical perspective (1960-2017) to capture evolving patterns

## Limitations and Future Research

The authors acknowledge several limitations of their study:

1. Data limitations, particularly for the anti-immigration measure (available for only 12 countries)
2. The challenge of establishing causal identification with observational data
3. Limitations in fully capturing the complex mechanisms linking welfare states to party positioning

They suggest several avenues for future research:

> "This could involve more detailed analysis of party positioning and statements and legislative behaviour. And it can involve looking at which sub-features of welfare state effort play out for backlash or considering how welfare state effort interacts with other macro-political economic conditions" (p. 72).
> 

They also call for research that goes beyond broad quantitative associations to "develop research designs that, if narrower in empirical sweep, allow more refined causal identification" (p. 72).

The data reveals important patterns beyond the main findings. For instance, the authors note that:

> "For all four measures of anti-globalisation nationalism the standard deviations are greater than the means, and the range of variation within countries is greater than that between countries" (p. 62).
> 

They also observe that "periods of economic crisis, like the crisis of the 1970s and the post-2008 financial crisis, exhibit not only increasing but also more dispersed (or polarized) anti-globalisation positioning" (p. 63).

## Social Psychological Mechanisms

While not the primary focus, the paper implicitly addresses social psychological mechanisms that explain how welfare policies moderate support for nationalist positions:

1. **Economic security mechanism**: Welfare states reduce the economic anxiety that can drive anti-globalization sentiment. When citizens feel economically secure due to robust safety nets, they may be less threatened by global economic integration.
2. **Identity-protection mechanism**: When welfare states are seen as fundamental to national identity and social cohesion, perceived threats to these institutions from globalization may trigger defensive nationalism.
3. **Issue ownership dynamics**: Parties strategically emphasize certain positions based on perceived ownership of issues. As the authors explain:

> "Parties differ with respect to how much they cue voters and emphasise or claim ownership on globalisation, welfare states, or the links between them. Some parties may particularly emphasise the dangers or importance of globalisation generally, for instance, or of particular aspects of globalisation" (p. 58).
> 
1. **Framing effects**: How parties frame the relationship between welfare states and globalization shapes citizen responses:

> "Such differences involve more or less invocation of the 'embedded nationalism' or 'embedded liberalism' dynamics that can colour their politicking in light of the welfare state conditions within which they operate" (p. 58).
> 

The authors particularly highlight how radical parties frame globalization as threatening to welfare state autonomy:

> "Issue ownership should make radical parties, particularly radical left parties, more focussed on how globalisation might threaten national welfare-state autonomy or national sovereignty generally" (p. 59).
> 

## Conclusion

The authors conclude that welfare effort has complex and contingent effects on anti-globalization nationalism:

> "The effects of welfare states for anti-globalisation nationalism can involve either 'embedded liberalism' or 'embedded nationalism' dynamics depending on the party involved and the aspect of nationalism in question" (p. 52).
> 

They find that welfare state effort generally dampens anti-globalization nationalism, suggesting that the "embedded liberalism" dynamic tends to predominate. However, this pattern varies considerably across different faces of globalization and different party families.

Most strikingly, they find that extensive welfare state effort can actually increase radical left parties' opposition to EU integration and immigration while simultaneously decreasing their general anti-globalization sentiment. This reveals the tension within these parties between defending welfare states and embracing internationalism.

The authors caution that their empirical patterns "involve broad quantitative associations in history rather than sharper causal identification" (p. 52), but argue that their findings have important implications for understanding how social protection is intertwined with contemporary political dynamics.

This nuanced understanding of how welfare states shape nationalist politics contributes significantly to debates about the future of liberal democracy, welfare states, and international integration in an era of rising populism and nationalism.
