This paper by) presents a systematic review of over 100 articles and books examining the relationship between economic hardship and support for redistribution. The authors identify five key themes that moderate this relationship: a person's current income, their future expectations, their expectations about redistribution benefits, their income in early life, and their attitudes toward beneficiaries. The review reveals that responses to economic hardship are heterogeneous and vary systematically according to these themes. The authors highlight research gaps, especially around public perceptions of government spending and its beneficiaries, and the effects of time horizons on support for redistribution. This analysis is particularly relevant to understanding the psychological mechanisms that might influence support for far-right parties as an alternative to redistributive policies.

## Primary Theories Used

### 1. Material Self-Interest Theory (Meltzer-Richard Model)

The foundational theoretical framework for most material explanations of redistribution preferences is the Meltzer-Richard (1981) model, which extends work by Romer (1975). This is described as "a minimalist theory of taxation and public spending" where people are driven by their immediate economic self-interest or "pocketbook." In this model, individuals support public spending if they believe its benefits outweigh the cost of higher taxes. Under a flat tax rate, voters with mean (average) income see their "pocketbook" perfectly balanced between redistribution benefits and taxation costs. Since income is typically right-skewed in most countries, the median (middle) person falls below the mean, causing the majority to prefer increased tax rates to receive greater support. Consequently, aggregate support for redistribution should strengthen as median income falls further below the mean.

The authors note that this mechanism depends on an "economically self-interested 'rational' individual" and is "broadly, if indirectly, consistent with evidence showing that public support for increased spending rises in periods of welfare reduction, and falls in periods of increased spending – the 'public thermostat' model of public opinion for government spending (Wlezien, 1995)."

However, the authors point out limitations to this theory:

While there is evidence of a broad link between income and support for redistribution at the individual level, "this relationship is not perfectly correlated nor deterministic, and its mechanisms leave discrepancies to be explored." The authors note that "significant numbers of middle-class and wealthy people support higher levels of government spending, while significant proportions of society's poorest want less" - contrary to the expectations of the Meltzer-Richard model.

**Relevance to your research**: This theory provides a baseline economic explanation for why people might support redistributive policies, but its limitations suggest that psychological and social factors beyond pure economic self-interest influence political preferences. The disconnect between economic position and expected policy preferences could help explain why some economically vulnerable voters might support far-right parties despite their often less redistributive economic platforms.

### 2. Insurance and Risk Theory

To resolve tensions in the Meltzer-Richard model, scholars have "loosen[ed] the focus on present-day income and instead [think] about the longer term." This approach frames redistribution as "insurance against the risk of future financial losses." The authors explain: "while two workers might earn an identical wage, the worker whose job is less secure might support higher social spending to protect against the elevated risk of unemployment (Iversen and Soskice, 2001; Thewissen and Rueda, 2019)." This logic "considers an individual's ability to act with a longer time horizon in mind" and helps resolve empirical puzzles in redistribution preferences.

Rehm (2009) outlines that "overall support for redistribution and public spending is maximised when income and risk are negatively correlated; the poor support redistribution because of their present income, the rich through fear of one day losing their well-paid job."

Building on this insurance perspective, "Rueda and Stegmueller (2019) explicitly model individuals' expected income trajectory, using age and education as proxies for what an individual will earn over their life. They find that these future expectations explain a significant portion of variation in redistribution preferences."

The authors emphasize that "expectations condition the effect of income losses and may explain why people of different backgrounds, but with equivalent present income, respond differently to shocks. Some economic hardship produces great uncertainty over a significant period of time, leading to a substantial re-evaluation of expected lifetime earnings that drive a change in preferences. But a comparable shock that is more transient should have only trivial effects on long-run expectations."

**Relevance to your research**: This theory helps explain why economic insecurity (not just current economic hardship) might drive political preferences. The perception of risk and uncertainty about the future could be key psychological mechanisms in your research. Far-right parties often capitalize on economic insecurity and anxiety about the future, offering seemingly simple solutions to complex economic challenges.

### 3. Distributive Reality Theory

The self-interest and insurance frameworks rest on a "nonregressivity assumption" (Iversen and Soskice, 2006) - that "richer voters lose out from redistribution, while poorer voters benefit." However, the authors argue this assumption "is less well founded, and might be more variable than first thought."

The authors distinguish between "de jure and de facto beneficiaries" of redistributive policies - "those who the policy makes better off on paper versus those who actually stand to gain." They note that "existing work on welfare states has highlighted implicit middle-class biases in how public spending is targeted. Heavy government spending on or subsidisation of childcare, education, transport infrastructure and even tax-free allowances benefit educated 'sociocultural professionals' – such as teachers, doctors, civil servants and so on – more than they do members of the conventional working class."

This creates a situation where "many poorer voters might not perceive themselves as beneficiaries of government spending, current or proposed, and will hence have less material reason to support redistribution."

Additionally, "a push towards welfare conditionality, and the introduction of strict sanctions, has made it harder for the poor to access services to which they are entitled. O'Grady (2022) shows how waiting times for appointments, the necessity of evidencing job applications and a general presumption of wrongdoing have significantly increased the effort required for people to access services." These bureaucratic barriers make "people less supportive of welfare measures, less likely to think they benefit from them and less likely to feel well represented or trusting of government."

**Relevance to your research**: This perspective is crucial for understanding why economically vulnerable populations might not support welfare policies that theoretically should benefit them. The gap between theoretical and actual beneficiaries of redistributive policies could explain support for far-right parties that criticize the welfare state while promising alternative forms of economic security. The perceived inefficiency or inaccessibility of welfare systems could be a key mechanism driving political disaffection.

### 4. Economic Socialization Theory

Moving beyond cost-benefit frameworks, the authors explore how "income in one's early years" shapes political preferences. They note that "socialisation-based accounts of preference formation contend that people's early formative years shape how they think about politics for the rest of their lives. The role of parents, family and young adulthood is crucial, with lifestyle, attitudes and beliefs filtering through in a manner that profoundly, and somewhat permanently, shapes one's worldview (Sears and Funk, 1990)."

Several studies support this theory: "Neundorf and Soroka (2018) highlight how unemployment and precarity when one first enters the labour market (usually in a person's early twenties) have lasting pro-redistributive effects. Shorrocks and Grasso (2020) make a similar argument about gender differences in redistribution support, while O'Grady (2019) uses panel data from Switzerland to show the predictive power of parental income."

According to O'Grady (2019), "early experiences are not quite as permanent as certain proponents of socialisation might suggest." Rather, "socialisation creates a frame of reference against which future events are benchmarked, leading to rational decision-making that is 'bounded', where only highly noticeable income shocks have the power to substantively shift one's preferences." This explains "why income and preferences exhibit such striking cross-sectional differences, arising through socialisation and gradual long-run shifts in income," and "why evidence of individuals quickly changing their preferences, in response to particular shocks, is more elusive."

This reasoning helps explain "why only large income shocks may have effects on preferences for redistribution and why a person's baseline attitudes determine their subsequent responsiveness." For example, "Margalit (2013)...finds that job losses have larger, more positive effects on the redistribution preferences of Republicans than of Democrats in the US. Republicans have a lower baseline support, for partisan and ideological reasons, so – counterintuitively – have greater room for manoeuvre."

**Relevance to your research**: This theory provides a developmental perspective on political preferences, suggesting that early economic experiences create lasting effects on attitudes toward redistribution. For your research, this suggests that support for far-right parties might be influenced by generational experiences and economic conditions during formative years. The concept of "bounded rationality" in how people respond to economic shocks could be particularly valuable in understanding why some economic hardships translate into political shifts while others don't.

### 5. Deservingness and Social Identity Theory

The final theme focuses on "a key belief system that might pass from one generation to the next; a disposition to believe society is meritocratic, and the extent to which anyone deserves support from the state." Such debates are "motivated in the morality of taking from the wealthy to help the poor, an action that can be criticised if one believes that income is proportional to effort (Trump, 2020)."

The authors note cultural differences in these beliefs: "Norwegians – socialised into a highly egalitarian society – tend to see inequality as more 'unfair' in behavioural experiments than American counterparts (Almås et al., 2020). The wealthiest 5% of Americans are more likely to say their wealth stems from personal choices rather than luck (Suhay et al., 2021)."

The authors highlight how "different ideological dispositions might explain variation in the acceptance (Mijs, 2021; Trump, 2020) and awareness (Waldfogel et al., 2021) of social inequalities, and why exposure to them can lower support for redistribution (Sands, 2017)."

Regarding group perceptions, "Cavaillé and Trump (2015) show that attitudes towards which groups should receive support are not explained by income, and it is better accounted for by the literature on social affinity." Similarly, "Rueda (2018) proposes that the wealthy support redistribution when inequality is high and ethnic diversity is low, on the grounds that increased poverty and homogeneity increase the perceived deservingness of support."

"Moreover, people may even resist spending that they could also benefit from, if 'out-groups' also gain from those shared resources (Breznau and Eger, 2016; Mitchell, 2019; Shayo, 2009)."

There is "compelling evidence that support for redistribution rises when the poor are seen to exert effort, but is curtailed when they are seen to freeride off others (Aarøe and Petersen, 2014)."

Finally, the authors connect deservingness to social discrimination: "Empirically, aggregate support for redistribution declines when individuals believe that minority groups are its primary beneficiaries and are disproportionately taking scarce resources (Alesina and Glaeser, 2004; Baldwin and Huber, 2010; Gilens, 2000)."

**Relevance to your research**: This theme is perhaps most directly relevant to your research on far-right support. The mechanisms of social identity, group competition, and perceptions of deservingness are crucial for understanding how welfare policies might influence support for far-right parties. Far-right parties often frame welfare policies as benefiting "undeserving" out-groups at the expense of "deserving" in-group members. Understanding how welfare policies interact with these perceptions of deservingness could be central to your research question.

## Discussion and Relevance to Your Research

The theoretical frameworks in this paper offer several insights for your research on social psychological mechanisms connecting welfare policies and support for far-right parties:

1. **Beyond Economic Self-Interest**: The limitations of the Meltzer-Richard model suggest that purely economic explanations are insufficient. Your research should consider psychological and social factors that mediate between economic position and political preferences.
2. **Risk Perception as a Key Mechanism**: The insurance theory highlights how perceptions of economic risk and uncertainty influence political preferences. Far-right parties often exploit economic anxieties, and your research could examine how different welfare policies mitigate or exacerbate these anxieties.
3. **Policy Design and Accessibility**: The distributive reality theory suggests that how welfare policies are implemented matters as much as their existence. Your research could examine how the design, accessibility, and bureaucratic aspects of welfare policies influence perceptions and political preferences.
4. **Formative Experiences**: The economic socialization theory suggests that early economic experiences create lasting effects on political preferences. Your research might consider how different welfare regimes shape generational political attitudes.
5. **In-Group/Out-Group Dynamics**: The deservingness theory highlights how perceptions of who benefits from welfare policies influence support. This directly connects to far-right rhetoric that often portrays welfare as benefiting "undeserving" out-groups.

## Key Takeaways with Substantiating Quotes

1. **Heterogeneity in Responses to Economic Hardship**
    
    "Our review highlights several implications of this nuanced and wide-ranging literature. The first is that responses to economic hardship are heterogeneous, and vary systematically according to the themes we identify."
    
2. **Perceptions of Beneficiaries Matter**
    
    "Empirically, aggregate support for redistribution declines when individuals believe that minority groups are its primary beneficiaries and are disproportionately taking scarce resources (Alesina and Glaeser, 2004; Baldwin and Huber, 2010; Gilens, 2000). These studies point to the importance of group perceptions and deservingness as being intrinsically tied to support for redistribution."
    
3. **Bureaucratic Barriers Affect Support**
    
    "Increased hostility and bureaucracy make people less supportive of welfare measures, less likely to think they benefit from them and less likely to feel well represented or trusting of government (Campbell, 2012). Taken together, they might perceive minimal personal gain from welfare spending, and so have less incentive to support redistribution than is often assumed."
    
4. **Future Expectations Shape Current Attitudes**
    
    "Expectations condition the effect of income losses and may explain why people of different backgrounds, but with equivalent present income, respond differently to shocks."
    
5. **Policy Implications**
    
    "The third [implication] is for policymakers and political practice: public support for greater redistribution is not an inevitable consequence of losses to people's economic wellbeing, but depends on the factors we outline in this review."
