# 2025 Wagner -  Stuart, "Heroes and Villains: Motivated Projection of Political Identities" 2025)  develop and test the concept of "motivated political projection" - the tendency to project one's own political identity onto positively-valenced individuals (heroes) and to counter-project opposing political identities onto negatively-valenced individuals (villains).  counter-projection (assigning out-group identities to villains) is actually stronger than projection (assigning in-group identities to heroes), suggesting that political identity projection is primarily driven by negative out-group animosity rather than positive in-group affinity. The authors theorize that this pattern contributes to the solidification of antagonistic political identities and has significant implications for understanding affective polarization dynamics.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Political characteristics are inferred from valence signals**: "We argue that individuals engage in motivated political projection: the identities people project onto target individuals are strongly conditional on the valence of that target." This means people assign their political identities to those they view positively and opposing identities to those they view negatively.
2. **Counter-projection is stronger than projection**: "Respondents exposed to the positive valence condition believe that positively valenced targets may belong to the out-group 40% of the time... Conversely, however, respondents identify negatively valenced individuals as belonging to the out-group 74% of the time... individuals are more prone to disidentify from and counter-project the out-group identity onto negatively valenced targets, than they are to identify with and project their own identity onto positively valenced targets."
3. **Projection effects are stronger among strong party identifiers**: "In a pre-registered test of the moderating role of the strength of party attachments, we find that the more strongly one identifies with one's party, the more likely one is to perceive heroes as being one of 'us' and villains as belonging to the other side..."
4. **Political left engages more in projection than political right**: "The results of our exploratory partisan subgroup analyses demonstrate that in the case of both Study 1 and Study 2, and in both Britain and the USA, respondents who identify with parties associated with the liberal left are, on average, far more inclined to identify villains as out-group partisans and heroes as in-group partisans than respondents on the conservative right."
5. **Projection occurs organically, not just when prompted**: "In Study 2, we sought to replicate the findings from Study 1 in a setting closer to the real world and, more importantly, assess if the social projection of political identities also occurs organically when encouragement to do so is very weak. The results of Study 2, summarized in Figure 5, show that political projection follows similar patterns as in Study 1 and occurs even for very weak prompts."

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Social Projection Theory

Social projection theory examines how people expect others to be similar to themselves, using self-knowledge as an anchor for inferring traits and characteristics of others. The authors build upon but also significantly extend this established theoretical framework.

"The relevant research agenda in social psychology relates to social projection, which is 'a process, or a set of processes, by which people come to expect others to be similar to themselves.' As people know more about themselves than about others, they use the self as an anchor against which other target individuals are assessed and tend to assume that others are like them, even if erroneously."

Standard social projection theory suggests that people project their own traits onto others, particularly in-group members and positively valenced targets. However, it typically posits that projection is simply weaker for out-group members or negatively valenced targets. The authors argue that in political contexts, something more extreme occurs:

"However, we depart from these standard models by arguing that the projection of political attitudes and identities will likely strongly depend on people's valence evaluation of the target. Existing models of social projection suggest that this is merely weaker for low-valence targets, but we suggest that, for political evaluations, people will even engage in counter-projection, assigning disliked out-groups to targets with low or negative valence."

Key researchers associated with social projection theory include Robbins and Krueger (2005), Krueger (2007), and Davis (2017), who have established the foundational understanding of how people project traits onto others. The authors apply this theory by examining how political identities specifically are projected based on valence evaluations, showing that the political domain involves not just weaker projection for negatively valenced targets but active counter-projection.

For my research on support for far-right parties, this theory suggests that voters may not just be responding to explicit political cues, but may be inferring political alignments of candidates or parties based on perceived valence. If my research examines how welfare or social investment policies affect support for far-right parties, I should consider how voters might project political identities onto policy positions based on their valence rather than actual ideological content.

### Social Identity Theory

Social identity theory is another crucial theoretical framework underpinning this research. This theory, developed by Tajfel and Turner (1979), proposes that individuals derive part of their identity from their group memberships and are motivated to maintain a positive distinction between their in-group and relevant out-groups.

"Social identity theory suggests that in-group identifiers will place a premium on preserving the in-group's self-image to reduce cognitive dissonance. We argue that, as a result, people will assign their own partisanship and political views to those they view positively and assign distant partisanship and political stances to those they view negatively."

The authors apply social identity theory to explain why people engage in motivated political projection. They argue that assigning one's own political identity to positively valenced targets helps maintain a positive in-group image, while counter-projecting opposing identities onto negatively valenced targets helps maintain in-group distinctiveness.

"Moreover, people will overestimate how politically similar they are to people they like and how politically different they are to the people they dislike. Finally, our findings are consistent with parts of existing research on affective polarisation, which points toward out-group hate (over in-group love) as the driving force behind motivated projection."

Social identity theory connects with a large body of research on affective polarization (Iyengar & Westwood, 2015; Mason, 2018) and negative partisanship (Bankert, 2022; Lee et al., 2022), which highlight how political identities increasingly function as social identities with strong emotional components.

For my research on far-right support, this theoretical framework suggests the importance of understanding not just policy preferences but identity-based motivations. If welfare or labor policies are being evaluated through the lens of social identity, their perceived connection to group identities may be more important than their objective details. The finding that counter-projection is stronger than projection also suggests that negative out-group animosity may be a stronger driver of political behavior than positive in-group affinity.

### Moral Foundations and Dualistic Thinking in Politics

The authors also draw on theoretical perspectives related to moral foundations and dualistic thinking in politics to explain why political contexts are particularly conducive to projection and counter-projection:

"The political realm is likely particularly conducive to the social projection conditional on valence evaluations. Politics is linked to moral reasoning and, in one account, different moral foundations. Politics thus contains the potential for dualistic thinking where conflicts are devoid of nuance and (over-) simplified to a dichotomous divide between right and wrong."

This theoretical framework draws on work by researchers like Garrett and Bankert (2020) and Akkerman et al. (2014), who have examined how political conflicts often involve moral framing and simplification into good versus evil dichotomies. The authors apply this theoretical perspective to explain why counter-projection is particularly strong in political contexts compared to other domains.

For my research on redistribution preferences and far-right support, this perspective suggests that policy debates may not just be about material interests but about moral framing. The tendency toward dualistic thinking in politics means that welfare policies might be viewed not just on their merits but as signals that trigger identity-based reactions and projection processes.

### Negative Partisanship and Out-group Animosity

A key theoretical contribution of this paper is highlighting the asymmetric role of negative partisanship and out-group animosity in driving political projection:

"Given that such deepseated negative group-based political affect has consequences for political campaigning and may, as some argue, ultimately lead to willingness to engage in political violence, the potential implications of the self-reinforcing nature of political projection effects are likely far from trivial."

The authors connect their findings to research on negative partisanship by scholars like Bankert (2022) and Lee et al. (2022), showing that disliking the opposing party/ideology can be a stronger motivator than liking one's own party. Their finding that counter-projection is stronger than projection provides empirical support for this theoretical perspective.

For my research on far-right support, this theory suggests that opposition to perceived out-groups may be a stronger driver than affinity for in-groups. If my research examines how welfare policies affect far-right support, I should consider how these policies might be perceived not just in terms of benefits to one's own group but potential benefits to disliked out-groups.
