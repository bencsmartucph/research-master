# Burgoon, van Noort, Rooduijn and Underhill (2019) Populism and Prosperity: Positional Deprivation and Support for Radical Right and Radical Left Parties (2019) - Burgoon, van Noort, Rooduijn and Underhill  This research examines how a particular type of economic experience—"positional deprivation"—influences support for radical political parties in contemporary European democracies. The authors identify a critical gap in existing literature by noting that conventional economic indicators fail to capture the specific combination of dynamic (over-time) and positional (relative to others) economic factors that drive populist sentiment.

> "The rise of radical right and radical left parties surely rates among the most important developments in recent European political economy. Such radicalism has long simmered in Europe's post-War electoral politics, but the gains for radical parties and programmes have in recent years broadened and deepened." (p. 51)
> 

The paper provides a novel theoretical framework and empirical evidence for understanding how economic grievances translate into political radicalism, with important implications for addressing populist backlash in advanced democracies.

## Theoretical Framework: Beyond Static Economic Indicators

### Limitations of Existing Approaches

The authors critique existing literature for failing to capture the specific combination of temporal and social comparison that characterizes economic resentment:

> "An important problem, however, is that the economic conditions receiving the most systematic attention do not fully link-up to the distinct combination of dynamic (over-time) and positional (inter-group) economic anxieties that journalistic and qualitative studies suggest are at play for supporters of radical right and radical left parties and populism." (p. 54)
> 

They argue that conventional measures miss crucial dimensions:

> "The key is that the combination of dynamic and positional economic misfortune gets only partially captured by well-studied measures of income, unemployment, inequality, or skill level. These latter highlight the dynamic or the positional, not both simultaneously." (p. 55)
> 

The authors draw on qualitative research showing that radical party supporters express frustration about both temporal decline and unfair comparison:

> "Recent qualitative case and ethnographic work exploring anti-elite resentments and populism highlight this combination of dynamic and positional misfortune (Cramer, 2016a; Gest, 2016; Hochschild, 2016). Cramer's case study on the politics of resentment in Wisconsin finds people worried about increasingly scarce health care, jobs, and taxation in rural places, combined with 'a deeply felt sense of not getting their "fair share" ...'" (p. 55)
> 

### Key Concept: Positional Deprivation

The authors introduce "positional deprivation" as their central theoretical contribution:

> "We define positional deprivation as a situation where the increase (decrease) in disposable income of an individual is smaller (larger) relative to the growth in income of other groups in the same country's income distribution." (p. 52)
> 

This definition incorporates both:

1. **Temporal component**: How one's economic position changes over time
2. **Comparative component**: How one's economic trajectory compares to others' trajectories

The authors connect this concept to established theory on relative deprivation:

> "This notion evokes, of course, the widely debated concept of 'relative deprivation' (see Runciman, 1966; Crosby, 1976; Walker and Pettigrew, 1984). Runciman (1966, p. 10) defines 'relative deprivation' as a situation where a person: (i) desires to have X but does not have it; (ii) believes that someone else, or some other people, which may include him/herself at some previous point in time, do have X; and (iii) perceives it as both feasible and just that he/she has X." (p. 57)
> 

But they emphasize that their concept adds an explicitly dynamic dimension that traditional relative deprivation theory often lacks.

## Psychological Mechanisms Linking Positional Deprivation to Radical Party Support

The authors propose two primary causal mechanisms that explain how positional deprivation translates into support for radical parties:

### 1. Policy Attribution Mechanism

> "First, people who have seen their household incomes grow less strongly, or decrease more significantly, relative to others in society might be inclined to assign the cause of their relatively declining fortunes to existing/past government policy where radical right or radical left populist parties explicitly campaign against, such as: globalization, European Union (EU) integration, immigration, or elite political corruption or insider privileges." (p. 57-58)
> 

Through this mechanism, the positionally deprived voter engages in a somewhat rational assessment of which political party will best address their economic circumstances:

> "In this way voters may be attracted to radical right and radical left parties because they believe that the policies these parties propose will increase aggrieved voters' (relative and/or absolute) economic well-being more significantly when compared with the alternative policies available in the political space." (p. 58)
> 

The authors connect this to existing research on radical voting:

> "Van der Brug et al. (2005) find, for example, that voting for radical right parties, just like for other parties, is largely motivated by pragmatic considerations." (p. 58)
> 

### 2. Scapegoating Mechanism

> "Second, positional deprivation may increase support for radical right and radical left parties because the radical ideologies provide easy 'scapegoats' for the (allegedly) unjust deprivation that voters face. Much existing psychology literature, in line with this perspective, has linked relative deprivation to dynamics of intergroup hostility and prejudice (Runciman, 1966; Mummendey et al., 1999)." (p. 58)
> 

This mechanism involves emotional directing of resentment toward outgroups:

> "These studies suggest that the angry resentments generated by relative deprivation will most likely be emotionally directed towards 'out-group' members. Which group is perceived as the relevant 'other' surely varies across individuals and time, but the main foils of radical right and radical left populists constitute convenient scapegoats for positionally deprived voters. Obvious 'others' that could be blamed for relative and dynamic economic hardship are immigrants or other 'non-natives'; so too, however, are native ethnic minorities, or the native super-rich 1%, or the 'welfare-cheating' poor (Van Oorschot, 2008)." (p. 58)
> 

The authors note that scapegoating can extend beyond specific groups to include institutions:

> "Out-group scapegoating might also be directed towards the EU or other international or supranational institutions since these institutions constrain national-level autonomy while political identities are overwhelmingly national. Finally, out-group scapegoating can extend to broader blaming of economic globalization, trade openness, and internationalism more generally (van der Waal and de Koster, 2017)." (p. 58)
> 

## Hypotheses: Different Types of Positional Deprivation and Partisan Effects

### Hypothesis 1: General Effect on Radical Support

> "Hypothesis 1: Individuals experiencing lower gains than the gains of the average, the highest or the lowest segments of the country's income spectrum are more likely to support radical left or radical right parties than are individuals whose gains have outpaced the gains of others in the country's income spectrum." (p. 59)
> 

### Hypothesis 2: Differentiated Effects for Left vs. Right

The authors propose that different manifestations of positional deprivation will have distinct effects on support for radical left versus radical right parties:

> "Hypothesis 2: Individuals experiencing upper-register positional deprivation (lower income growth than experienced by the richest on the income distribution) should be more likely to support radical-left than -right parties, while individuals experiencing lower-register positional deprivation (lower income growth than experienced by the poorest on the distribution) should be more likely to support radical-right than -left parties." (p. 60)
> 

They justify this hypothesis by noting the differing ideological emphases of radical left and right parties:

> "Radical left parties and their voters are more focused on 'soak-the-rich' egalitarian appeals and have been found to be more focused on economic redistribution than have their radical right counterparts (March, 2011; Visser et al., 2014; Rooduijn et al., 2017). In contrast, radical right parties and supporters are often more focused on fairness for average citizens against opportunistic economic dependency and/or by an undeserving/lazy poor (Swank and Betz, 2003; Hoggett et al., 2013)." (p. 60)
> 

The authors provide a concrete example:

> "For instance, Geert Wilders's Dutch Party of Freedom (PVV) has long championed the interests of iconic 'Henk and Ingrid' (very popular old-fashioned Dutch names), who are 'hard-working Dutchman or Dutchwoman' aggrieved by both ends of the class spectrum, not just elites but also welfare cheats (welfare kings and queens often cast as having non-native backgrounds) (Rooduijn et al., 2014)." (p. 60)
> 

## Detailed Data Sources and Methodology

### Data Sources

The researchers faced a methodological challenge:

> "In practice, however, we face an important obstacle, because there is no data available that systematically contains (1), (2), and (3). True income panel datasets, such as the German Socio-Economic Panel (1984-), the British Household Panel Survey (1991-), the Swiss Household Panel (1999-), and the American Panel Study of Income Dynamics (1968-), provide very little data on essential political behavioural variables, and in any case do not allow for large cross-country comparisons." (p. 60-61)
> 

To solve this problem, they combined:

1. **European Social Survey (ESS) data (waves 1-7, 2002-14)**: Provides individual-level data on party preferences, socio-demographic variables, and income decile position.
    
    > "On the other hand, data that does contain cross-country comparable data on political attitudes and party identification, such as the ESS, measure a respondent's current (as opposed to past) income, and only measures income on the decile (rather than interval/ratio) level." (p. 61)
    > 
2. **Luxembourg Income Study (LIS) data**: Provides detailed household income data over time.
    
    > "LIS provides data from a large number of representative (cross-sectional) household income surveys held in more than 50 countries from the 1970s onwards. These data have gaps between particular years but can be used to calculate annual or longer-term change in income (in PPP terms) for a given country-decile-year (LIS, 2016)." (p. 61)
    > 

The researchers matched these data sources to create:

> "...substantial leverage to explore our hypotheses on how various measures of positional deprivation relate to support for radical left and radical right parties and programmes." (p. 61)
> 

### Measuring Positional Deprivation

The authors develop four distinct measures of positional deprivation:

1. **Mean positional deprivation**: "Mean decile growth minus respondent's decile growth, taking the average country-year growth for the ten deciles, minus the growth for a respondent's 'own' decile." (p. 63)
2. **Fifth-decile positional deprivation**: "...a country-year's 5th decile's real household income group minus a respondent's decile's growth, and captures middle-register positional deprivation." (p. 63)
3. **Tenth-decile positional deprivation**: "...the growth in real household income in the 10th-decile minus a respondent's own decile growth." (p. 63)
4. **First-decile positional deprivation**: "...the first-decile's growth minus a respondent's own decile growth." (p. 63)

The authors detail their approach to measurement:

> "For the purposes of the analysis below, our measures of mean positional deprivation, fifth-decile-based positional deprivation, tenth-decile positional deprivation, and first-decile positional deprivation are all calculated for 5-year periods or windows between 1997 and 2014 (to match the ESS data from 2002 to 2014). This is preferable to shorter periods that are less likely to be felt or registered in any meaningful way by people, and it is close to the average election cycle across our sample countries." (p. 64)
> 

### Measuring Radical Party Support

The authors use ESS data on which party respondents "feel closest to":

> "The most important involve coding of answers to the question of which political party a respondent 'feels closest to' at the time of the survey interview. This is preferable to using the related ESS question about which party a respondent 'last voted for', since it is often the case that elections took place years earlier than the date of the survey interview and since we are interested in the present effects of positional deprivation as an economic position that can change over time." (p. 65)
> 

Their primary dependent variable is coded as:

> "Party support (re: all parties), where answers are coded as: 1 = support any non-radical party; 2 = support a radical left party; 3 = support a radical right party; and 4 = support no party." (p. 65)
> 

The authors also analyze party platforms:

> "Beyond these two baseline specifications, robustness tests consider different conceptions of radicalism and their alternatives, including different categorizations of parties in the radical right and of radical left families." (p. 66)
>

The authors use multinomial logit models to examine how positional deprivation affects party support:

> "To address support for radical right and radical left relative to key alternatives, we estimate multinominal logit models of the following form: [equation provided]" (p. 69)
> 

They include numerous controls:

> "As controls, we consider a range of socio-demographic, economic, and attitudinal variables. We control for a respondent's highest level of completed education... We also control for whether respondents are unemployed (1= unemployed); their age; their sex (1 = female); their subjective religiosity (11-point scale; 0= not at all religious, 10 = very religious); whether they or their parents are born in a different country (1= foreign born); whether they live in a rural or urban surrounding (1= urban); and their general left–right self-identification (0 = left, 1= right)." (p. 69-70)
> 

The authors acknowledge potential methodological challenges:

> "We foresee three potential sources of bias in our estimates of the effect of positional deprivation. First, as is well understood in the discrete choice literature, the identification of multinominal logit models is contingent upon the independence of irrelevant alternatives (IIA) assumption." (p. 70)
> 

They discuss how they address these challenges, including:

> "To adjust for the autocorrelation generated by (i), we cluster the standard errors on the country-decile level in all our estimates." (p. 70)
> 

> "This, of course, is the most important motivation for using country- and time-fixed effects in all our baseline models. These fixed effects control by design for all omitted variables that vary across countries and for all omitted variables that affect all respondents at the same time (i.e., common shocks)." (p. 71)
> 

## Detailed Findings with Evidence for Psychological Mechanisms

### General Effect of Positional Deprivation on Radical Support (H1)

The authors find clear evidence for their first hypothesis:

> "Across both specifications, the controls perform in line with earlier estimates of radical right and radical left populism. For instance, supporters of radical right and radical left parties are more likely to be younger, subjectively less well-off, and secular than those supporting non-radical parties. And radical right respondents, more than their radical left counterparts, are more likely to be male, less educated, and self-report as ideologically right-oriented." (p. 72)
> 

Their key finding is that positional deprivation drives support for radical parties:

> "As for the main results, both models (M1 and M2) show that mean positional deprivation significantly correlates with support for radical left and radical right parties. Respondents in deciles whose 5-year gains in real household income are more outpaced by the average gains across all deciles in the income spectrum are statistically significantly more likely to support radical left and radical right parties." (p. 73)
> 

The effects are substantial:

> "Both results are substantively quite large, with the full range of mean positional deprivation predicting, counterfactually, a tripling of the chance of supporting radical right and more than quadrupling the chance of supporting radical left parties." (p. 74)
> 

> "Across the kind of variation in mean positional deprivation a given individual is likely to experience over the period, the substantive effects are more modest: about one-tenth of the full predicted range. Nonetheless, the substantive effect of mean positional deprivation is comparable to that of subjective income/wellbeing, though substantially smaller than the effect of education in our models." (p. 74)
> 

The authors also find that positional deprivation increases political withdrawal:

> "The right-hand panel also shows that mean positional deprivation predicts a substantively meaningful increase in the probability of respondents supporting 'no party'. In absolute terms this predicted increase – from about 42% to about 51% chance of supporting no party across the full range of mean positional deprivation – appears much larger than the effects for radical right and radical left." (p. 74)
> 

### Differentiated Effects by Type of Positional Deprivation (H2)

The authors find strong evidence for their second hypothesis about differing effects of upper-register versus lower-register positional deprivation:

### Upper-Register Positional Deprivation (Relative to the Rich)

> "M5 and M6 show the results for upper-register positional deprivation, focused on tenth-decile positional deprivation. This measure significantly increases the likelihood of supporting radical left parties, and also increases the likelihood that respondents support no party. However, tenth-decile positional deprivation does NOT significantly correlate with higher probability of supporting radical right parties (Column 2 for M5 and Column 5 for M6)." (p. 76)
> 

> "This pattern narrows the empirical support for Hypothesis 1, in that we see that at least upper-register positional deprivation may not spur support for radical right parties, only for radical left parties. On the other hand, the pattern is very much consistent with Hypothesis 2, where we expected upper-register positional deprivation to more strongly spur radical left than radical right support." (p. 76)
> 

### Lower-Register Positional Deprivation (Relative to the Poor)

> "Table 3's results for lower-register positional deprivation (M7 and M8) are similarly interesting. Here we see that first-decile positional deprivation significantly spurs support for radical-right parties – as or more strongly than did any other measures of positional deprivation. But such lower-register positional deprivation does NOT significantly spur the likelihood of supporting radical left parties, or for that matter of supporting no party." (p. 76)
> 

> "This pattern is again a narrowing or weakening of our support for Hypothesis 1, this time revealing that lower-register positional deprivation might spur radical right but not radical left partisanship. This pattern strongly and clearly supports our Hypothesis 2, this time showing that lower-register positional deprivation is certainly stronger in its spurring of support for radical right than for radical left." (p. 76)
> 

### Party Platform Analysis: Connecting to Nationalist Autarchy

The authors extend their analysis to examine party platforms, confirming an important connection:

> "Support for radical right parties is positively and highly significantly more likely to entail a party platform embracing net nationalist autarchy. Radical left parties, in contrast, are not significantly more likely to be parties embracing net nationalist autarchy." (p. 80)
> 

When examining which types of positional deprivation correlate with support for nationalist autarchy platforms, they find:

> "The results suggest that only first-decile positional deprivation – the upper-register positional deprivation in M21 – is significantly associated with a voter's revealed support for net nationalist autarchy based on the party to which a respondent feels closest." (p. 80)
> 

This further confirms that lower-register positional deprivation drives support for nationalist populism:

> "On the other hand, the significant results for lower-register positional deprivation comport with our main findings where lower-register positional deprivation is particularly strong in spurring radical right party support. Such aspects of Hypothesis 2, hence, find support in our (admittedly rough) measure of platform substance." (p. 82)
> 

## Implications for Status Politics and Populist Sentiment

The paper offers important insights about how economic experiences translate into status anxieties that radical parties exploit:

### The Psychology of Economic Resentment

The authors describe how positional deprivation creates a sense of unjust misfortune:

> "The core intuition behind this argument is that citizens whose own fortunes have regressed or been outpaced compared with others – that is, those experiencing more positional deprivation – will feel economically deprived and/or be attuned to feelings of economic unfairness." (p. 57)
> 

This connects to a sense of lost dignity and status:

> "It is also in line with the empirical finding that general happiness, life satisfaction, and perceptions of wellbeing more broadly are as or more strongly influenced by relative as by absolute material-economic position (Clark and Oswald, 1996; Smith et al., 2012)." (p. 57)
> 

### The Status-Based Appeal of Different Radical Parties

The paper reveals how different types of positional deprivation activate distinct status anxieties that are exploited by different radical parties:

For radical left parties:

> "Radical left parties and their voters are more focused on 'soak-the-rich' egalitarian appeals and have been found to be more focused on economic redistribution than have their radical right counterparts." (p. 60)
> 

For radical right parties:

> "In contrast, radical right parties and supporters are often more focused on fairness for average citizens against opportunistic economic dependency and/or by an undeserving/lazy poor." (p. 60)
> 

The distinct reactions to different forms of positional deprivation highlight how economic experiences activate specific status concerns:

- Upper-register positional deprivation activates resentment against elites who are seen as unfairly prospering
- Lower-register positional deprivation activates anxieties about losing status relative to those perceived as less deserving

## Conclusions and Policy Implications

The authors conclude with important reflections on how their findings might inform responses to populism:

> "Positional deprivation may well have implications that go above-and-beyond the role of well-known economic forces. Scholarly and popular commentary has often dismissed economic forces as less important than cultural experiences to shaping the palpable resentments at play in radical-populist politics." (p. 85)
> 

They argue that addressing positional deprivation requires understanding both temporal and comparative economic experiences:

> "Making such judgement, however, is premature, not only awaiting more nuanced research designs disentangling cultural from economic experiences, but also for the more basic reason that we need to measure the aspects of economic experience that capture the distinctive combination of dynamic and positional economic misfortune. Measures of positional deprivation may help capture such misfortune." (p. 85)
> 

The authors suggest their findings have important policy implications:

> "And if the patterns discussed above hold, then we know better the economic conditions that are afoot and can better learn which economic interventions can hope to solve one of the most important challenges to contemporary Western political stability and democracy." (p. 85)
> 

While not explicitly discussed, these findings suggest that traditional redistribution policies alone may be insufficient if they fail to address the status concerns underlying positional deprivation. More effective policies might need to:

1. Address both absolute and relative economic conditions
2. Consider how different types of economic experiences (relative to the rich vs. relative to the poor) trigger different status anxieties
3. Design interventions that maintain dignity and status, not just material compensation
4. Recognize that different segments of the population may respond to different types of socioeconomic policies based on their comparative reference points

The paper's powerful evidence about how different forms of positional deprivation drive support for different types of radical parties highlights the need for nuanced policy approaches that address both the material and status dimensions of economic discontent.
