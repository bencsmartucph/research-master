## Key Research Question

"What is the impact of unemployment on far-right party support?"

---

## Theoretical Framework

### Economic Insecurity and Far-Right Support

- Unemployment conceptualized as "one of the key drivers of economic insecurity"
- Economic insecurity theorized as a key driver of far-right support
- Support for the far-right understood as "a protest vote, a punishment of the establishment and the incumbent, or an endorsement of the 'national preference'"

### Differential Effects on Labor Market Groups

- **Outsiders**: Increasing unemployment directly affects the unemployed because they no longer receive income
- **Insiders**: Unemployment increases the actual or perceived insecurity of those in permanent employment by increasing fears of losing their job

### Mediating Role of Labor Market Policies

1. **Unemployment Benefits**: Mediates insecurity for outsiders by replacing some lost income
2. **Employment Protection Legislation (EPL)**: Mediates insecurity for insiders by making it more difficult for employers to dismiss workers

---

## Data Sources and Methodology

### Sample

- 14 Western European + 10 Eastern European countries
- Time period: 1991-2013
- Focus on national elections
- OLS regression with robust standard errors clustered by country

---

## Key Findings

### Direct Effects

- "Unemployment is on average not a significant determinant of far-right party support"
- Unemployment benefits have a "statistically significant negative effect" on far-right support
- EPL shows no significant independent effect

### Mediating Effects

1. **Unemployment Benefits**: "Unemployment is only associated with higher far-right party support when unemployment benefits are low"
2. **Employment Protection Legislation**: EPL "only mediates the impact of unemployment on far-right party support when the share of foreigners in the country is low"

---

## Theoretical Contributions

1. **Beyond demand and supply framework**: Captures the importance of policies as mediating factors
2. **Reconceptualizing the economy's role**: The limited correlation between unemployment and far-right support "does not mean that the economy is irrelevant" — the relationship is "complex and conditional on policies"
3. **Expanding beyond class-based analysis**: Economic insecurity is not only about the have-nots but affects "a broad range of social groups, including the middle classes"
4. **Bridging disciplinary divides**: Reconciles literatures on political economy and voting behaviour

---

## Policy Implications

- During economic crises, "a crisis-management approach that includes protective social policies is likely to limit far-right party support"
- Maintaining robust social safety nets may be important not only for economic reasons but also for political stability
- Austerity measures that reduce social protections during crises might inadvertently fuel far-right support
