# Left Behind and United by Populism? Populism's Multiple Roots in Feelings of Lacking Societal Recognition” Steiner, Schimpf, and Wuttke (2023) examines a critical question in contemporary politics: what unites seemingly diverse groups of people in their support for populism? The authors offer a novel theoretical framework that positions perceived lack of societal recognition as the common psychological mechanism that explains populist support across varied social groups.

### The authors articulate the key puzzle that motivates their research:

> "One lingering puzzle common to populist movements is their success in traversing the traditional cleavages of Western societies in which they managed to assemble a diverse coalition of societal groups that cuts across ideological and socioeconomic divides" (p. 108-109).
> 

The paper builds on the concept of "feeling left behind" that has appeared in both political discourse and academic literature, but provides a more theoretically grounded conceptualization through the lens of societal recognition. As the authors state:

> "Despite its popularity, previous scholarship has not fully appreciated that individuals can feel left behind in different ways and for different reasons. Moreover, scientific progress on the left-behind thesis has been hampered by conceptual vagueness" (p. 109).
> 

The central thesis is that different social groups may feel left behind in distinct ways, but this common perception of lacking the societal recognition one deserves unites them in their support for populism:

> "We argue that—because of this multifaceted character—the common perception of being neglected by society unites otherwise heterogeneous segments of the population in their support for populism" (p. 107).
> 

The authors conducted a preregistered study using data from the German Longitudinal Election Study (GLES) Pre-Election Cross-Section 2021, focusing on four prototypical social groups they expected to feel left behind in different ways: low-income individuals, low-skilled workers, rural residents, and sociocultural conservatives. They examined both how each group experienced different forms of lacking recognition and how these feelings related to populist attitudes.

This paper makes a significant contribution by providing a more nuanced understanding of the "left behind" thesis and offering a theoretical framework that can unite previously disparate explanations for populist support. It demonstrates how populism can cut across traditional social cleavages by appealing to a fundamental human psychological need for recognition, albeit one that manifests in different domains for different groups.

## 2. Theoretical Framework

### 2.1 The Concept of Societal Recognition

The authors ground their theoretical framework in the concept of societal recognition, which has deep roots in Western philosophical thought. They trace this concept through British thinkers like John Stuart Mill and Adam Smith as well as German idealists like Immanuel Kant and Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel. This historical foundation establishes recognition as a fundamental human need rather than just a contemporary political phenomenon.

The authors explain the evolutionary basis for the human need for recognition:

> "Human beings evolved as social animals. The unique skill that has given humans an edge over other species is the ability to cooperate with others of our kind. Hence, there is an evolutionary advantage in the evolved desire to seek group interaction, which might be the reason for the universal human tendency to become accepted members of human groups" (p. 111).
> 

This evolutionary perspective helps explain why recognition is so deeply ingrained in human psychology and why its absence can trigger profound psychological and behavioral reactions:

> "Being denied acceptance by a group to which we seek to belong goes to the core of who we are and feels like being denied the dignity we deserve. It has profound implications for one's self-concept, thereby affecting a person's sense of worth and psychosocial functioning" (p. 112).
> 

The authors conceptualize societal recognition as inherently multidimensional, encompassing economic, cultural, and regional aspects. This multidimensional nature is crucial to their argument about how different social groups might feel left behind in different ways:

> "Some citizens may react to economic exclusion; others may perceive neglect by elites of their sexual, regional, or religious identities, whereas for some citizens, rejection in terms of their values and attitudes might underlie their feelings of lacking recognition" (p. 113).
> 

### 2.2 Connection Between Lacking Recognition and Populist Attitudes

The authors theorize that feelings of lacking recognition can lead to populist attitudes as a form of psychological response to perceived exclusion. They explain:

> "One form in which the consequences of lacking recognition could materialize is populist attitudes. Populist attitudes describe a moralistic world view that pits the corrupt elite against the virtuous people. To those who feel they belong to the virtuous group of ordinary people, blaming the elites provides a useful narrative to explain one's exclusion from society without having to take any responsibility themselves" (p. 112).
> 

Alternatively, they suggest a more favorable interpretation where:

> "A more favorable depiction of the relationship between recognition and populism is to see all social struggle as a struggle for recognition and to see populism as an emancipatory process of democratization that strives for the inclusion of the unrepresented into the political process" (p. 112).
> 

### 2.3 Multiple Roots of Populism

The authors synthesize previously disparate strands of research on populism by showing how each can be understood through the lens of lacking societal recognition. They detail how different social segments might feel lacking recognition in domain-specific ways:

> "Among the socioeconomically disadvantaged, a lack of perceived societal recognition may emerge from a widespread perception that society does not care about their financial struggles. Apart from financial strain, there is another, perhaps more consequential, feeling of lacking recognition that may nurture populist sentiment among production and service workers... those working in jobs that require lower levels of formal education have gained the impression that their work is 'less worthy of social recognition and esteem', and this has made them susceptible to populist mobilization" (p. 113).
> 

For sociocultural conservatives, the authors note:

> "In the 'cultural backlash' argument of Norris and Inglehart (2019), the rise of (authoritarian) populism reflects a counterreaction of those with socioculturally conservative values to a societal shift in dominant value orientations. For these authors, sociocultural conservatives are 'likely to feel that they have become estranged from the silent revolution in social and moral values, left behind by cultural tides that they deeply reject'" (p. 113).
> 

And for rural residents:

> "Finally, the tendency of individuals in rural and declining 'left-behind' regions to vote for populists may reflect a sense that these places' needs are insufficiently recognized and respected, fueling a rural variety of populist resentment" (p. 113).
> 

Crucially, the authors propose that lacking recognition in just one domain may be sufficient to trigger populist sentiment, even if a person feels recognized in other domains:

> "Because the need for recognition is deeply ingrained in the human psyche, we argue that the perception of lacking recognition elicits consequences even when individuals feel excluded from just one central dimension of social life while enjoying high satisfaction in other domains" (p. 113).
> 

## 6. Discussion and Conclusion

### 6.1 Summary of Findings

The authors summarize their key findings:

> "Our preregistered study confirmed the overarching idea of populism having multiple roots in feelings of lacking societal recognition. Distinct feelings of lacking societal recognition each contribute to populist attitudes. This relationship seems to follow a logic of sufficient conditions, whereby a perceived lack of recognition in one domain suffices to trigger populist sentiment. Distinct feelings of lacking societal recognition are, in turn, systematically rooted in distinct social characteristics" (p. 126).
> 

However, they acknowledge surprising findings:

> "Some social characteristics (low income and, especially, conservatism) and some facets of recognition (opinion, economic) had a disproportionately large influence on populism relative to our expectations" (p. 126).
> 

### 6.2 Theoretical Implications

The authors highlight the explanatory power of societal recognition as a concept:

> "Our findings point to the explanatory power of societal recognition which, despite established bodies of literature across scientific disciplines, has not received systematic attention in political science. The concept has proven useful to reconceptualize the vague term of 'feeling left behind' and to unite previously disparate perspectives on the drivers of populism" (p. 126).
> 

They also emphasize how this framework connects macro-level changes to individual responses:

> "More generally, the feeling of lacking societal recognition as a concept that is seemingly remote from the political domain but deeply ingrained in the human psyche can serve as a theoretical bridge to connect macro-level socioeconomic transformations with individual-level reactions" (p. 126).
> 

### 6.3 Limitations and Future Research Directions

The authors identify several areas for future research:

1. **Politicization of feelings of lacking recognition**:
    
    > "For perceptions of recognition, which may feel very personal and remote from contemporary political debates, these feelings of lacking recognition must become politicized... Elite communication is likely key to this process" (p. 126-127).
    > 
2. **Cross-national comparison**:
    
    > "Future research should study populism's roots in feelings of lacking societal recognition in other countries to better understand the role of the context and elite communication. Such research may well reveal that the roots vary in importance across space and time" (p. 127).
    > 
3. **Operationalization refinement**:
    
    > "Some of our results might also be sensitive to how we operationalized key constructs. This could be an issue with regard to the two prototypical groups for which we found weaker connections to perceived lack of recognition, i.e., low-skilled workers and those living in rural places" (p. 127).
    > 
4. **Connection to voting behavior**:
    
    > "In our article we focused on populist attitudes rather than voting. Thus, the question remains how lacking societal recognition ultimately maps to vote choice through the support of populist attitudes" (p. 127).
    > 
5. **Causality**:
    
    > "Our study remains limited in terms of causality. We were careful not to interpret the empirical evidence in a causal way because the available cross-sectional data does not permit strong inferences on the causal relationship between the variables of interest" (p. 128).
    > 

### 6.4 Conclusion

The authors conclude by emphasizing the potential of their framework:

> "It has often been suggested that populism appeals to those who feel left behind by the development of society. Yet a theoretical framework centered on the insight that individuals can feel left behind in different ways and for different reasons has been lacking. Drawing on the established and inherently multifaceted construct of societal recognition, we aimed to contribute to the development of such a framework" (p. 128).
> 

They highlight the strength of their findings:

> "The results of our preregistered study indicate that this framework holds considerable potential to better understand how populism can unite heterogeneous segments of the population. In the context of the 2021 German federal election, we found distinct feelings of lacking recognition to dominate in different social segments and observed that each of these distinct feelings of lacking recognition in turn contributed to populist sentiment" (p. 128).
> 

## 8. Theoretical Significance and Contribution

### 8.1 Contribution to the Literature on Populism

This paper makes several important contributions to the literature on populism:

1. **Reconciling competing explanations**: By showing how different forms of lacking recognition can lead to populist attitudes, the paper helps reconcile economic, cultural, and regional explanations for populism that have often been treated as competing rather than complementary.
2. **Explaining cross-cutting support**: The framework offers a compelling explanation for why populism can unite diverse segments of society that traditional political cleavages would predict to be in opposition.
3. **Conceptual clarification**: By providing a more theoretically grounded conceptualization of "feeling left behind" through the lens of societal recognition, the paper addresses conceptual vagueness in existing literature.
4. **Multidimensional approach**: The recognition of multiple facets of lacking recognition and their distinct social roots offers a more nuanced understanding than unidimensional approaches to social status or deprivation.

### 8.2 Broader Theoretical Implications

The paper also has broader theoretical implications:

1. **Psychological foundations of political attitudes**: By grounding political attitudes in fundamental psychological needs, the paper contributes to the growing literature on the psychological bases of political behavior.
2. **Connecting micro and macro**: The framework connects macro-level societal changes to individual-level political attitudes through the mediating mechanism of perceived recognition.
3. **Integration of interdisciplinary perspectives**: By drawing on philosophy, psychology, and political science, the paper offers a more holistic understanding of populist support.
4. **Normative implications**: The paper suggests potential pathways for addressing populism that go beyond dismissing it as irrational or solely addressing economic grievances.
