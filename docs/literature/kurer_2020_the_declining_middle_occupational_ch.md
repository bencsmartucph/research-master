### 1.1 Key Classification Framework: Occupational Transitions

The study examines how workers in routine occupations (those most susceptible to automation) respond politically to different employment trajectories:

- **Survivors**: Maintain routine jobs despite declining demand
- **Upgraders**: Move from routine to nonroutine cognitive jobs
- **Downgraders**: Move from routine to nonroutine manual jobs
- **Dropouts**: Become unemployed after holding routine jobs

> "While routine workers all face similar initial threats from automation, the materialized occupational trajectories are diverse and strongly differ in their material implications." (p.1800)
> 

## 2. Psychological Mechanisms Limiting Redistribution

### 2.1 Status Anxiety vs. Material Hardship

A central finding is that material hardship and status anxiety produce fundamentally different political responses:

> "Routine workers' situation in an increasingly automated world of work thus provides an ideal case to disentangle the political consequences of fearing as opposed to experiencing economic adversity." (p.1800)
> 

> "I find that surviving in an environment of structural decline drives support for right-wing populist parties, whereas the actual experience of economic hardship immediately and substantively reduces this support." (p.1825)
> 

### 2.2 The Dual Challenge to Routine Workers

Routine workers face parallel challenges to both economic security and psychological well-being:

> "Consequently, routine workers who are threatened by automation have to cope with parallel challenges to both their material and psychological well-being." (p.1804)
> 

The paper distinguishes between two notions of "losing out":

1. **Economic disadvantage**: Direct material consequences of job transitions
2. **Social hierarchy shifts**: Changes in social status and relative position

> "The second notion is not necessarily tied to individual transitions but to broader changes in the social hierarchy. Jobs have meaning beyond the income they provide. As an 'unintended though inevitable' consequence (Jahoda, 1982, p. 39) of its main purpose, employment is also a source of psychological well-being by offering collective purpose and social status." (p.1804)
> 

### 2.3 Status Anxiety and Social Conservatism

Concerns about status produce responses that go beyond economic demands:

> "A rich body of work in social psychology has implicated threat and status anxiety in the development of socially conservative or even authoritarian character traits as a general response to fear of uncertainty." (p.1805)
> 

> "A related literature has argued that threats to the perceived value of an ingroup negatively affects members' self-image and might create hostility toward outgroups that are seen as competitors in the social hierarchy." (p.1805)
> 

> "Typically, comparably higher status groups seek to derogate comparably lower status groups as a strategy of comparative status enhancement." (p.1805)
> 

### 2.4 Competing Policy Demands

Kurer identifies a critical tension in the policy preferences that emerge from different forms of economic disadvantage:

> "Voters who experience material hardship become dependent on state support and should thus have a strong interest in a generous welfare state to cushion the economic downfall and support their re-entry in the labor market. Voters who primarily suffer from status anxiety are more likely to look for propositions that promise to maintain or reinstall their place in the social hierarchy." (p.1805)
> 

> "The point of the matter, however, is that these distinct policy demands are hardly on the same partisan menu." (p.1805)
> 

## 3. The Populist Spending Paradox

### 3.1 Welfare Expansion's Limited Effect on Populism

The paper presents what could be called a "populist spending paradox":

> "What makes this development so fascinating from a political science perspective is the difficulty of an adequate policy response. When it is not actual economic adversity that drives political resentment among relative losers of occupational change, 'more welfare' in the traditional sense might not help alleviate grievances." (p.1826)
> 

> "Such an interpretation of my findings is also reflected in the sobering conclusion of a recent study on electoral consequences of social spending by Gingrich (2019). Not unexpectedly, welfare retrenchment is electorally harmful and tends to benefit right-wing populist mobilization. However, importantly, the reverse mechanism (more spending, less populism) is not borne out by Gingrich's data." (p.1826)
> 

### 3.2 Status Politics Over Economic Policies

The author argues that routine workers in declining industries are seeking status restoration, not just economic compensation:

> "Rather than receiving financial support from governments, these voters want their perceived relative decline in the social hierarchy addressed." (p.1826)
> 

> "Right-wing populist parties have long recognized this. Their programmatic proposal, whether politically viable or not, should not be trivialized as mere populist seduction void of content but as a political offer to the sizable part of the population who faces rather bleak prospects and fears social regression." (p.1826)
> 

### 3.3 Strategic Disadvantage for Mainstream Parties

This creates a strategic disadvantage for mainstream parties:

> "The limited capacity of governments to effectively attenuate political grievances among those adversely affected by relentless technological progress highlights the strategic disadvantage of responsible center-right or center-left parties in contemporary democracy. It renders postindustrial societies vulnerable to political forces responding to voters on the grounds of less tangible identity politics, which are difficult to counter with mundane, precise, and politically feasible policy reactions." (p.1826)
> 

## 4. Research Evidence Supporting the Framework

### 4.1 Panel Data Analysis

The paper uses longitudinal panel data from three countries (Switzerland, Germany, and UK) covering the late 1980s to 2016 to track how occupational transitions affect voting behavior:

> "I leverage individual-level panel data from Germany, Switzerland, and the United Kingdom to analyze the effect of differential employment trajectories on routine workers' political behavior." (p.1800)
> 

### 4.2 Key Empirical Findings

1. **Survivors support right-wing populists**:
    
    > "The baseline probabilities in the first row confirm our expectation that right-wing populist parties (RPP) are successful in mobilizing survivors in routine work." (p.1815)
    > 
2. **Dropouts shift away from right-wing populism**:
    
    > "The most important finding is that a transition from routine work into unemployment results in a strong and consistent decline in support for the most conservative party in all three countries." (p.1815)
    > 
    
    > "The results for Switzerland and the United Kingdom provide consistent evidence that it is indeed primarily 'surviving' in routine work that fuels support for RPPs, while materialized economic hardship, that is, a transition into unemployment immediately and substantially reduces this support." (p.1815)
    > 
3. **Unemployed former routine workers either turn left or abstain**:
    
    > "Dropping out of the labor market either produces an economic response that leads to increasing support for left parties who stand for a strong social safety net... The second significant political reaction of dropouts is growing political disenchantment." (p.1819)
    > 
4. **Supplementary survey analysis confirms status anxiety mechanism**:
    
    > "...routine workers' social status has slightly decreased in absolute terms but especially in relative terms compared to lower status groups... these changes in perceptions of social hierarchies are related to subjective labor market prospects and susceptibility to automation, and... perceived status decline is a relevant determinant of right-wing populist voting." (p.1824)
    > 

## 5. Implications for Policy: The Status-Focused Approach

### 5.1 Limitations of Traditional Welfare Solutions

The central policy implication is that traditional welfare policies focused solely on material compensation are insufficient:

> "When relative societal decline rather than material hardship are at the heart of socially conservative resentment, traditional welfare policy may be an insufficient response to satisfy exposed workers and hence an ineffective remedy to counter the ascent of right-wing populist movements." (p.1801)
> 

> "The more nuanced interpretation of the structural drivers behind right-wing populist voting may also prove informative with respect to governments' leeway to respond to contemporary political disruptions." (p.1801)
> 

### 5.2 Need for Status-Enhancing Policies

While not extensively developed in the paper, there's a clear implication that policies need to address status concerns:

> "The presented theoretical argument and empirical findings provide a novel reading of the economic roots of populism. I add to a burgeoning literature... that relies on a relational understanding of economic disadvantage to reconcile some of the contradictions manifest in previous research." (p.1800)
> 

> "What they [right-wing populist parties] offer is not primarily relief from economic stress but remedies against status anxiety by promoting the values of an idealized past where routine workers were a valued and crucial pillar of society." (p.1806)
> 

The research suggests policies should acknowledge and address:

- The loss of social recognition for certain types of work
- The need for alternative pathways to maintain social status
- Recognition that relative position matters beyond absolute income levels

## 6. Status Politics and Policy Design

### 6.1 The Superiority of Right-Wing Populist Appeals for Status-Anxious Workers

The paper explains why right-wing populist parties are uniquely positioned to appeal to routine workers facing status anxiety:

> "What differentiates them from left-authoritarian parties that might purport a similarly nostalgic worldview is their derogative rhetoric toward migrants as well as 'undeserving' or 'lazy' parts of the native population, both of which offer an obvious lower status target at which to direct hostility in an attempt of comparative status enhancement." (p.1806)
> 

> "When it comes to an anxious lower middle class in search for status enhancement but not in need of economic support, right-wing populist promises are uniquely alluring." (p.1825)
> 

### 6.2 Status Consciousness Should Inform Policy Design

The findings suggest policies should be designed with awareness of how they affect relative status:

> "The 'last-place aversion' theory suggests that concerns about one's relative position in the social hierarchy might depress demand for redistribution even among citizens who, in theory, should be supportive (Kuziemko et al., 2014). This is due to concerns that welfare might disproportionately benefit lower status groups, which counteracts the goal of relative status enhancement." (p.1831, footnote 3)
> 

## 7. Structural Nature of the Challenge

### 7.1 Long-Term Persistence of the Problem

The paper suggests this is not a temporary political challenge but a structural one:

> "Given the structural roots of the presented findings, there is much reason to expect right-wing populist parties to become a constant feature of the political arena in postindustrial democracy." (p.1825-1826)
> 

> "Technological change will affect sections of the labor force that have so far been spared from the threat of automation and will thus constantly preserve or even enlarge the pool of voters who are receptive to political programs addressing status anxiety." (p.1825)
> 

### 7.2 Need for Further Research

The author identifies areas for future research:

> "Future research could examine differences between blue- and white-collar routine workers or differences between male and female routine workers. Furthermore, questions of intergenerational mobility might also contribute to political reactions as the occupational path of lifelong employment in routine work becomes increasingly unlikely." (p.1826)
> 

## 8. Combining Economic and Cultural Explanations

The paper offers a nuanced framework that integrates economic and cultural explanations for populist support:

> "The presented observational evidence supports the notion that survivors in routine work are conscious about their deteriorating position in the social hierarchy and that this experience can trigger political demands that are apparently satisfied by the promises of right-wing populist parties." (p.1824)
> 

> "Extending the focus beyond the usual indicators of material hardship shows that fundamentally economic processes like changing labor markets might very well result in noneconomic—or not purely economic—political responses." (p.1800)
> 

> "I add to this debate by studying how different employment trajectories within a vulnerable occupational environment affect vote choice. The strong risk concentration provides an opportunity to untangle whether economic threat or economic hardship better explains the appeal of right-wing populism among routine workers." (p.1804)
>
