## Comprehensive Summary

## Key Takeaways

1. **Multiple Pathways to Populism**: The study identifies four distinct high-populism classes, each with different combinations of religiosity, partisanship, victimhood, and identity attachments, supporting the authors' thesis that "there is no single reason individuals support populism" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120).
2. **Psychological Over Socioeconomic Predictors**: The analysis reveals that "populist support is more connected to psychological and political orientations than socioeconomic circumstances or even political predispositions, such as partisanship" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120), challenging economic explanations of populist support.
3. **Identity-Based Exclusionary Populism**: Some forms of populist support are driven by desires to "create exclusive sovereignty for their preferred identity group(s)" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120), particularly through racial and religious identity attachments.
4. **System-Based Victimhood Populism**: Other populist supporters are motivated by feelings of being "victims of the political system" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120), expressing general dissatisfaction with elite responsiveness and governmental corruption.
5. **Non-Universal Correlates**: The findings demonstrate that traditional predictors of populism operate differently across population subgroups, with "most relationships with populism 'seem non-universal, calling for future research into the contextuality of psychological predispositions for populist…attitudes'" (Hofstetter & Filsinger, 2024, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120).

## Introduction and Motivation Analysis

### Research Problem and Significance

The authors identify a critical empirical puzzle in contemporary political behavior: while populist sentiment has experienced a global uptick, the individual-level factors that attract Americans to populist ideas remain contested and poorly understood. This research addresses a fundamental gap in populism scholarship, which has largely focused on cross-national comparisons or elite-level phenomena while neglecting the heterogeneous nature of mass-level populist support within specific contexts.

The authors position their work as essential for understanding American democracy, arguing that "populism perverts '…the democratic principles of the majority and the people, in a way that is meant to celebrate one subset of the people...populism's ambition is to construct new forms of popular sovereignty that enhance partial inclusiveness…'" (Urbinati, 2019, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120). This framing establishes populism not merely as anti-elite sentiment but as a potentially exclusionary force that threatens pluralistic democracy.

The broader significance of this research extends to understanding how democratic societies can maintain stability and inclusiveness amid rising populist sentiment. The authors note that recent scholarship argues psychological predispositions for populist attitudes "seem non-universal, calling for future research into the contextuality of psychological predispositions for populist…attitudes" (Hofstetter & Filsinger, 2024, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120-121). This contextual understanding is crucial for both theoretical advancement and practical democratic governance.

The research problem gains additional urgency from contemporary political developments, as the authors observe that "recent years have witnessed a global uptick in populist candidates and sentiment" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120). Understanding the diverse motivations underlying this support becomes essential for predicting political behavior and crafting appropriate democratic responses.

### Theoretical Positioning and Innovation

Armaly and Enders make several innovative theoretical contributions that distinguish their work from existing populism scholarship. First, they challenge the linear model assumption that has dominated the field, arguing instead for a "multiple-paths approach" where "there is a shared, equifinal end (i.e., populism), but the paths they take can be distinct" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122).

The authors build upon Mudde's (2004) conception of populism as a "thin-centered" ideology, noting that this framework "enables populism to embrace anti-systemic ideas from different political sides" (Lüders et al., 2021, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120). However, they extend this insight to the individual level, arguing that if populism can accommodate diverse political agendas at the elite level, it should similarly attract diverse psychological and social profiles at the mass level.

Their theoretical innovation is further demonstrated through the maze versus labyrinth metaphor: "Though similar, a labyrinth is unicursal (meaning it has only one path to the center) and a maze is multicursal (meaning there are multiple paths)" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122). This conceptualization provides a powerful framework for understanding how different combinations of attitudes, identities, and circumstances can lead to similar political orientations.

The authors also contribute to populism theory by emphasizing contextual specificity, arguing that "allowing both underlying attitudes and context to vary is complicated. Because existing work indicates there are not universal pathways to populism, we argue it is critical to hold context constant" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120). This methodological insight has important implications for comparative populism research.

Finally, they position their work within broader debates about democratic governance and social identity, noting that their findings "aid in differentiating the types of sentiments among the masses on which we should keep a close eye" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 131), particularly given evidence that "populism is connected with support for political violence in the United States" (Piazza, forthcoming, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 131).

## Literature Review and Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Major Theoretical Traditions Engaged

### System-Based Grievance Theory

The first major theoretical tradition the authors engage focuses on elite- and government-driven sources of populist support. This theoretical foundation, exemplified by scholars like Berman (2021), emphasizes "the growing inability or unwillingness of elites and institutions to supply responses to citizens' demands" (Berman, 2021, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122).

The core assumption of this tradition is that populist support emerges from rational responses to elite failure and institutional unresponsiveness. Key mechanisms include perceived corruption, institutional distrust, and feelings of political inefficacy. The authors note that existing empirical evidence supports connections between populism and factors such as "trust in elites (Rooduijn, 2018), trust in institutions (Fieschi and Heywood, 2004), relative deprivation (Smith et al., 2012), external political efficacy (Rooduijn, Van Der Brug, and De Lange, 2016), and economic grievances (Eichengreen, 2018)" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122).

However, the authors identify limitations in this literature's focus on objective conditions rather than subjective perceptions. They argue that "we focus principally on subjective perceptions of and feelings about the state of affairs (rather than the objective truth of the matter)" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122), representing a significant theoretical refinement.

The authors build upon this tradition by operationalizing system-based grievances through perceived victimhood, which they conceptualize as both egocentric ("the belief that one gets less than is deserved") and systemic ("the view that 'the system' is rigged") (Armaly & Enders, 2022, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122). This theoretical innovation bridges individual psychology with institutional critique.

### Group-Based Grievance Theory

The second major theoretical tradition focuses on identity-based explanations of populist support, particularly reactions to perceived threats from minority racial and religious groups. This literature, including work by Bonikowski (2017), Marzouki, McDonnell, and Roy (2016), and Rydgren (2005), emphasizes "perceived threats from minority racial and religious groups" and "more general feelings of social marginalization" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123).

The theoretical foundation of this approach rests on social identity theory and intergroup conflict, predicting that populist support should correlate with strong in-group attachment and out-group hostility. Key scholars in this tradition include Jardina (2019) on white identity politics and Whitehead, Perry, and Baker (2018) on Christian nationalism.

Empirical evidence supporting this tradition includes findings that white identity "is related to a host of attitudes that are clearly linked to the social, racial, and economic grievances highlighted in explanations of populism" (Jardina, 2019, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123). The authors note that "Whites appear to view jobs, culture, and economic success as zero-sum, where 'more' for non-whites means 'less' for whites" (Jardina, 2019, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123).

However, the authors identify limitations in existing work's tendency to examine single identity dimensions in isolation. They argue for a more comprehensive approach that considers "three of the major identities that motivate political attitudes and behaviors: Racial, religious, and political (i.e., partisan and ideological)" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123).

### Psychological Predisposition Theory

A third theoretical tradition emphasizes underlying psychological orientations that predispose individuals toward populist attitudes. This literature includes work on authoritarianism (Feldman, 2021), conspiracy thinking (Stecula & Pickup, 2021), and various personality traits (Bakker, Rooduijn, & Schumacher, 2016).

The theoretical foundation suggests that certain psychological dispositions create cognitive and emotional openness to populist appeals. The authors note that "both authoritarianism and conspiratorial thinking usually entail a critique of the established power structure: Either it is too weak and insufficiently protective of particular norms, cultural practices, or groups, or it is, itself, part of a shadowy conspiracy to subvert those norms, cultural practices, and the will of 'the people'" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123).

Empirical support includes findings that "authoritarian politicians tend to employ both populist (Norris and Inglehart, 2019) and conspiratorial rhetoric (Oliver and Rahn, 2016)" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123). The authors build upon this tradition by examining how psychological predispositions interact with other factors rather than operating in isolation.

### Critiques and Theoretical Integration

The authors identify several unresolved debates within the populism literature. First, they note tensions between economic and cultural explanations, observing that some evidence suggests "the link between religiosity and voting for populists is weak (Arzheimer and Carter, 2009), and that more religious individuals are more tolerant of foreigners (Daenekindt, de Koster, and van der Waal, 2017)" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 124).

Second, they highlight the contextual specificity problem, noting that "most relationships with populism 'seem non-universal, calling for future research into the contextuality of psychological predispositions for populist…attitudes'" (Hofstetter & Filsinger, 2024, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120).

The authors address these limitations through their theoretical synthesis, which integrates insights from multiple traditions while acknowledging their interactive rather than additive effects.

### Theoretical Synthesis and Framework

Armaly and Enders develop an innovative theoretical framework that synthesizes insights from system-based, group-based, and psychological approaches to populist support. Their synthesis rests on several key theoretical innovations that transcend traditional approaches to understanding political behavior.

The authors' central theoretical insight involves rejecting additive models in favor of configurational thinking. Rather than assuming that populist support results from the cumulative effect of various predictors, they propose that "different people can possess populist ideas for entirely different reasons; for some it may be discontentment with the government (e.g., Fieschi and Heywood, 2004), for others it is social marginalization (e.g., Gidron and Hall, 2020), and for others still it may be both" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122).

This theoretical framework addresses tensions between competing explanations by suggesting they may all be correct within specific population subgroups. The authors argue that "for some individuals, socioeconomic deprivation may not promote populist ideas, but cultural orientations regarding white status threat or a perceived marginalization of Christianity do. For others, the process could be the exact opposite" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122).

The framework also incorporates insights about populism's "thin-centered" ideological nature, arguing that this characteristic "enables populism to embrace anti-systemic ideas from different political sides" (Lüders et al., 2021, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120). At the individual level, this suggests that populist attitudes can accommodate diverse psychological profiles and grievance structures.

The authors' theoretical model proposes that populist support emerges from the intersection of perceived victimhood and identity-based orientations, but these factors can combine in multiple ways. They theorize that "those who exhibit the strongest populist views see themselves as the victims of politics and feel that their salient in-groups—for instance, religious and racial—have experienced (relative) losses of power or have never been afforded the opportunity to wield power" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122).

Importantly, the framework specifies scope conditions by focusing on subjective rather than objective conditions. The authors argue that populist support can occur "even in the absence of 'genuine' losses of power" (Pettigrew, 2017, as cited in Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122), emphasizing the role of perception and interpretation in political attitude formation.

### Hypotheses and Theoretical Predictions

The authors' theoretical framework generates several specific hypotheses that flow from their multi-path model of populist support. Rather than proposing universal predictors, their hypotheses concern the configurational relationships between various factors within different population subgroups.

First, they hypothesize that perceived victimhood—both egocentric and systemic—should positively relate to populist sentiment across multiple pathways. They argue that "both of which may relate to support for populism" because "these (related, but distinct) constructs measure the level to which one feels they do not get a fair shake in the world, that there are systemic oppressors" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122).

Second, they predict that identity-based factors should relate to populist support but in different ways across population subgroups. For white identity, they hypothesize that "if one thinks policies that promote non-whites will harm whites and white culture, they will surely support points of view and leaders that promise to reverse those policies" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123).

Third, they hypothesize that psychological predispositions like authoritarianism and conspiracy thinking should correlate with populist attitudes because "individuals of both types are likely to be less satisfied with the government and political outcomes and more likely to support populist ideas, either because they critique those in power, champion the masses, or both" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123).

Importantly, the authors differentiate their predictions from alternative explanations by emphasizing configurational rather than linear relationships. They argue that their approach will reveal "categories of individuals who possess a constellation of the aforementioned orientations (e.g., white identity and powerlessness)" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122) rather than simple bivariate associations.

## Broader Theoretical Implications

### Connections to Other Research Programs

The theoretical contributions of this study extend well beyond populism research to inform several broader areas of political science and social psychology. The authors' configurational approach to political attitudes has important implications for understanding political behavior more generally, particularly regarding how individual-level factors combine to produce political orientations.

The study's emphasis on multiple pathways to similar political outcomes connects to broader debates in political psychology about equifinality—the principle that similar outcomes can result from different causal processes. This insight is particularly relevant for understanding political polarization, where "a multitude of identities are increasingly aligning to shape political behavior (Mason and Wronski, 2018), indicating that there are fewer cross-cutting cleavages across identity" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 124).

The research also bridges political behavior and social identity theory by demonstrating how group attachments operate differently across political contexts. The authors note that "when one wishes that government worked for her and her group, the group in question may be an amalgam of racial, religious, and political identities (among others)" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 124), suggesting complex interactions between social identity and political preference formation.

Additionally, the study's methodological innovation using latent profile analysis offers broader applications for political science research dealing with heterogeneous populations and complex attitude structures. The authors suggest that their approach could inform research on "both the formation and effect of populist attitudes, particularly those that are driven by social identity" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 130), indicating potential applications to other political phenomena.

The findings also connect to democratic theory and institutional design, particularly regarding how democratic systems can maintain legitimacy and inclusion amid diverse grievance structures and identity configurations. The authors note that understanding these patterns is crucial for determining "how to counter the non-democratic variants of populism, perhaps by employing or avoiding appeals to the various social identities that we find predict populism" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 131).

## Methodology Overview

### Data Sources and Coverage

The authors employed a unique national survey of 1,003 U.S. adults conducted by Lucid in February 2021, utilizing quota sampling to achieve representativeness based on age, race, sex, and education. The dataset was specifically designed to capture the comprehensive range of individual-level factors potentially related to populist support, representing one of the most extensive collections of populism correlates in existing scholarship.

The temporal coverage is particularly significant, as the survey was conducted during a period of heightened political tension following the 2020 presidential election and January 6th Capitol events, providing insights into populist attitudes during a critical moment in American democracy. The authors note that their data collection timing allows examination of populist attitudes when such sentiments were particularly salient in American political discourse.

The geographic scope encompasses the entire United States, allowing for generalizable conclusions about American populist attitudes while controlling for the contextual factors that the authors argue make cross-national comparisons problematic. They justify this approach by noting that "allowing both underlying attitudes and context to vary is complicated. Because existing work indicates there are not universal pathways to populism, we argue it is critical to hold context constant" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 120-121).

Data quality controls included multiple attention checks and restrictions based on response time, with the authors noting they "restricted our sample to only those respondents that spent an appropriate amount of time answering our questions—no less than one standard deviation below the median completion time" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 132). These procedures resulted in the final analytical sample of 1,003 respondents.

The comprehensiveness of the dataset represents a significant strength, as the authors collected information on 19 distinct individual-level factors spanning "the political, psychological, and social domains" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 124), allowing for the most comprehensive examination of populist correlates to date in the American context.

### Key Variables and Operationalization

### Dependent Variable: Populism

The authors operationalize populism using an eight-item additive index developed by Oliver and Rahn (2016), specifically validated for the American context. The scale captures core dimensions of populist sentiment including anti-elitism, Manichean worldview, and distrust of expertise. Key items include "Politics usually boils down to a struggle between the people and the powerful" and "The system is stacked against people like me" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 124).

The authors justify this operationalization by noting that "we opt for the Oliver and Rahn (2016) scale largely because it was developed and validated in the context of the United States" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 124). The scale demonstrates strong psychometric properties with a reliability coefficient of α = 0.83 and unidimensional structure confirmed through exploratory factor analysis.

### Independent Variables: System-Based Measures

For system-based grievances, the authors operationalize perceived victimhood through two distinct constructs: egocentric victimhood (personal sense of unfair treatment) and systemic victimhood (belief that the system is rigged). They also include measures of government trust, perceived corruption, authoritarianism, and conspiracy thinking to capture various aspects of system-level dissatisfaction.

The victimhood measures represent a theoretical innovation, as the authors note that "both egocentric and systemic victimhood are fairly general; they are not indicative of 'political victimhood,' per se" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123), yet they serve as "summary measures of subjective discontentment with the existing power system and one's place therein" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123).

### Independent Variables: Group-Based Measures

For group-based grievances, the authors include measures of white identity, racial resentment, Christian nationalism, and religiosity. White identity is operationalized following Jardina (2019) as "pride in one's whiteness and the belief that the political fortunes of white Americans are linked" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123).

Christian nationalism is measured using the Whitehead, Perry, and Baker (2018) scale, capturing beliefs that merge "American and Christian group memberships" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 123-124). The authors note that this measure moves "beyond denominational considerations" to capture identity-based rather than purely religious motivations.

### Control Variables and Demographics

The authors include standard demographic controls (age, sex, education, income) as well as political predispositions (partisanship, ideology) and psychological measures (generalized anxiety, powerlessness). This comprehensive approach allows them to isolate the effects of their theoretical variables while controlling for alternative explanations.

Their measurement approach reflects careful attention to construct validity, with the authors noting that they "include many sociodemographic characteristics (e.g., income, education, and health insurance) in order to operationalize socioeconomic grievance and deprivation" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 125) while also capturing psychological orientations that may operate independently.

### Analytical Model and Justification

The authors employ Latent Profile Analysis (LPA), a person-centered mixture modeling approach that represents a significant methodological innovation for populism research. LPA "hypothesizes that a latent (i.e., unobserved) variable divides a given sample into certain classes or groups" and "is often used to estimate the number of classes for some outcome of interest, as well as to determine what covariates relate to classification into a given class" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 125).

The analytical approach is specifically justified by the authors' theoretical framework emphasizing multiple pathways to populist support. They argue that "to appropriately test our theory, our empirical design must allow for this multiple-paths approach. Latent profile analysis (LPA) does just that" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 125).

The authors contrast their approach with traditional regression-based methods, noting that "we forgo standard regression-based approaches to determining the correlates of populism" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122) because such approaches assume universal rather than configurational relationships. They demonstrate the superiority of their approach by showing that traditional OLS regression identifies only 6 of 19 variables as statistically significant, "even though we know they relate to populist attitudes (per the analysis above)" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 130).

The LPA model specification involves several key decisions. First, they estimated models with 1 through 12 latent classes and selected the optimal number based on information criteria, ultimately choosing a 10-class solution based primarily on the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC). As they note, "Nylund, Asparouhov, and Muthen (2007) note that the AIC 'has been shown to overestimate the correct number of components' (537) and that the BIC is preferred when deciding on the number of classes" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 126).

The analytical strategy focuses on class composition rather than class prediction, with the authors emphasizing that "we are primarily interested in the composition of each class, rather than a focus on any given variable" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 127). This approach allows them to identify "what the profile of an individual high in populism looks like, and whether that differs from the profile of one low in populism" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 127).

The authors acknowledge important limitations of their analytical approach, particularly regarding causal inference. They emphasize that "we cannot make statements about causality with this analytical approach" and that "we do not intend to determine what causes populism" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 127). Instead, their goal is to "shed light on the complicated and heterogeneous psychological entanglements with populist attitudes" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 122).

The model estimation employs maximum likelihood estimation with careful attention to missing data patterns, particularly for the white identity variables that were not administered to non-white respondents. This analytical decision "effectively limits the inferences we can make to non-white respondents" (Armaly & Enders, 2025, p. 132), representing an important scope condition for the findings.

Overall, the methodological approach represents a sophisticated and theoretically grounded strategy for understanding heterogeneous political attitudes, with the LPA framework providing insights that would be impossible to obtain through traditional variable-centered approaches. The authors' careful justification of their analytical choices and acknowledgment of limitations demonstrate methodological rigor and theoretical sophistication that advances our understanding of complex political phenomena.
