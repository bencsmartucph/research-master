## Primary Theories Used

### 1. Task-Based Approach to Automation Risk

**Detailed Explanation:** The task-based approach distinguishes between routine and non-routine tasks, rather than entire occupations, as the key factor in determining automation risk. This theory, developed by economists like Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003), argues that automation doesn't typically eliminate entire jobs but rather replaces specific tasks within jobs that follow clear rules and procedures. The theory emphasizes "codifiability" as the critical feature that renders routine work susceptible to automation. It identifies both blue-collar manufacturing jobs and white-collar clerical jobs as containing high proportions of routine tasks vulnerable to technological displacement. The theory explains how automation reshapes the employment structure gradually rather than causing immediate mass unemployment, creating a process where declining job opportunities occur through attrition rather than lay-offs, with fewer new entrants replacing workers who retire or leave.

**Direct Quotes:**

- "For the likes of Autor et al (2003) and Cortes (2016), routineness defines a job's susceptibility to automation. Rather than a synonym for simplicity, routineness describes compliance with a set of tasks that, whilst initially often the result of considerable skill and training, can thereafter be performed by following a series of 'rules and procedures'. This 'codifiability', suggest Acemoglu and Autor (2011), is the aspect that renders routine work susceptible to automation, whether it occurs in factories, in offices or elsewhere." (p. 6)
- "...routine workers do not experience the process of technological unemployment through direct, immediate exits and lay-offs, so much as a gradual decline in new entrants to replace natural attrition in the workforce." (p. 3-4)

**Operationalization and Application:** The authors operationalize this theory by referencing the Routine Task Intensity Index as a proxy for automation risk. The theory is applied to explain why middle-income jobs have been most affected by recent technological change, impacting both "blue-collar" industrial jobs and "white-collar" desk-based jobs. The authors also use this approach to explain labor market polarization, where displaced routine workers either move into lower-income service jobs less amenable to automation or drop out of the labor market entirely, resulting in a "hollowing out" of the middle of the income distribution.

**Relevance to My Thesis:** This theory directly connects to my interest in economic insecurity and work-quality precarity. Rather than focusing solely on financial insecurity, the task-based approach suggests examining how changes in the nature and status of work create different forms of precarity. This supports my hypothesis that work-quality precarity might predict political attitudes differently than financial precarity, providing a theoretical foundation for analyzing the relationship between changing work structures and political behavior.

### 2. Status Anxiety Theory

**Detailed Explanation:** Status anxiety theory explains how perceived threats to social standing drive political behavior. Drawing from social psychology, this theory posits that individuals experiencing threats to their status may develop socially conservative or authoritarian tendencies as they attempt to maintain their relative position in a social hierarchy. The theory suggests that when individuals feel their social value declining, they often respond by creating greater social distance between themselves and groups perceived as lower-status. In the context of automation, routine workers who remain employed but witness the declining value of their work respond by reinforcing boundaries between themselves and groups like the unemployed or immigrants. The theory focuses on relative status rather than absolute economic position, explaining why voters who are objectively economically secure might still experience anxiety about their future position and status.

**Direct Quotes:**

- "Drawing from social psychology, Kurer associates threat and anxiety about status with 'the development of socially conservative or even authoritarian character traits', and threats to the 'perceived value' of some forms of work and those that perform them with increased hostility to groups 'seen as competitors in the social hierarchy', as a means of achieving a relative stabilisation of their own status and value." (p. 12)
- "Because social status is relative, these workers can shore up their status by 'maintaining their social distance' from other groups and individuals perceived as occupying a lower status in the social order." (p. 12)

**Operationalization and Application:** The authors apply this theory by distinguishing between objective and subjective risk of automation, emphasizing that the subjective perception of risk drives political behavior more than objective reality. They measure status anxiety through indicators like societal pessimism, nostalgia, and socially conservative preferences. The theory is used to explain why routine workers who are "surviving" in their current positions are more susceptible to right-wing populism, as they seek to preserve their threatened social status.

**Relevance to My Thesis:** This theory is highly relevant to my interest in how status concerns (rather than purely material concerns) might explain certain political behaviors and attitudes toward welfare programs. The concept of "Dignity in Decline" that I'm exploring aligns perfectly with status anxiety theory, offering a framework to examine how perceptions of declining social status affect political attitudes and policy preferences. This theory suggests examining not just economic indicators but also measures of perceived social standing and dignity in understanding populist support.

### 3. Anticipated vs. Experienced Hardship Framework

**Detailed Explanation:** This theoretical framework distinguishes between anticipated hardship (the fear of future economic decline) and experienced hardship (actual economic disadvantage). The theory proposes that these different temporal experiences of economic insecurity generate distinct political responses. Anticipated hardship activates status concerns and often leads to socially conservative, nostalgic politics associated with right-wing populism. In contrast, experienced hardship drives demand for economic compensation and security, often associated with left-wing policies. This framework challenges conventional economic analyses that focus solely on snapshot indicators of absolute economic hardship, instead emphasizing trajectories through the labor market and their psychological impacts. The theory recognizes that automation creates a unique temporal dynamic where effects unfold gradually over time, making the perception of risk as important as actual risk.

**Direct Quotes:**

- "The results carve a middle path through the competing economic and non-economic emphases of the literature on populism by moving away from direct material hardship to a broader array of economic processes – associated with 'relative social decline' – that in turn manifest in non-economic political responses epitomised in 'socially conservative resentment'." (p. 15)
- "This enables Kurer to differentiate between those who 'survive' in an 'increasingly vulnerable occupational environment' that 'activates concerns about status maintenance' and increases the appeal of nostalgic, socially conservative politics; and those who experience job loss and material hardship itself and thus reach for a politics geared towards economic compensation or security." (p. 15)

**Operationalization and Application:** The authors operationalize this framework by examining trajectories through the labor market rather than static economic positions. They distinguish between "survivors" (those who remain in routine jobs but fear future displacement) and those who experience downward mobility through job loss or demotion. The framework is applied to explain the seemingly contradictory political behaviors of routine workers, clarifying why some support right-wing populism while others seek redistributive policies.

**Relevance to My Thesis:** This framework provides a crucial temporal dimension to understanding how economic insecurity shapes political attitudes. For my research on welfare states and populism, it suggests that timing matters—policies addressing anticipated hardship might need to differ from those addressing experienced hardship. This connects to my interest in how different types of precarity affect political attitudes, suggesting that the anticipation of decline may be as politically significant as actual decline.

The paper does not detail specific sample sizes or data collection procedures for these studies but references multiple large-scale surveys and electoral datasets.

## Findings and Evidence

### Summary of Key Findings

1. **Routine workers and voting patterns**: Routine workers who perceive automation risk but remain employed ("survivors") are more likely to support right-wing populist candidates and parties.
2. **Status anxiety versus material hardship**: Status anxiety about potential future displacement drives support for right-wing populism, while actual experience of economic hardship tends to increase support for redistributive policies.
3. **Failure of compensatory policies**: Traditional redistributive policies have proven largely ineffective at reducing support for right-wing populism among routine workers concerned about automation.
4. **Role of political rhetoric**: How automation is framed through political rhetoric significantly influences voter preferences for policy responses.
5. **Demographic patterns**: The workers most affected by automation and most likely to support populist responses tend to be men without higher education who previously enjoyed employment security and stability.

### Supporting Evidence

For the finding that routine workers who perceive automation risk support right-wing populism:

- Frey et al. (2018) found that Trump's vote was higher in "local labour markets more exposed to the adoption of robots" to the extent that Trump would not have won without increased exposure to robots in key electoral battlegrounds (p. 11-12)
- Im et al. (2019) showed that "increased perceived risk of automation is associated with greater propensity to vote for radical right parties, largely at the expense of mainstream right-wing parties" (p. 15)

For the distinction between status anxiety and material hardship:

- Kurer (2020) provides evidence that "surviving" routine workers respond to status anxiety with right-wing populism, while those experiencing actual job loss or material hardship seek compensatory policies from the left (p. 15-16)
- The paper cites Antonucci et al. (2017) showing that the average Leave voter was not the "angry, unskilled and perhaps even unemployed outsider" but rather from "intermediate classes concerned about declining economic position" (p. 16)

For the failure of compensatory policies:

- Gingrich (2019) found that compensatory approaches do little to shift voter preferences and may in some cases increase support for populist candidates (p. 18-19)
- The authors note that traditional redistributive programs may have been effective against globalization but are "likely to prove much less effective against the effects of automation on 'both income and status'" (p. 18-19)

For the impact of political rhetoric:

- Jeffrey (2021) conducted a survey experiment showing that political rhetoric framing automation in terms of fairness or unfairness influenced support for redistributive policies (p. 20) The study found that while voters turned against welfare programs for the unemployed, they supported policies protecting workers from job losses (p. 21)

## Discussion

### Interpretation of Findings

The authors interpret their findings as demonstrating that the politics of work is not simply determined by economic factors but shaped by complex interactions between technology, labor markets, and politics. They suggest that automation creates a unique challenge because its effects unfold gradually, making it difficult for voters to directly connect their anxieties to technological change. This leads voters to seek more tangible explanations for their insecurity, which populist parties have successfully provided.

The authors interpret the failure of compensatory policies to counter populism as evidence that status concerns cannot be addressed through material benefits alone. They argue that policies must address both material interests and status anxiety, with particular attention to protecting workers' dignity and social standing.

The authors also interpret their findings to suggest that politics is not simply "downstream" from technological change but can actively shape how technology affects work and workers. This perspective challenges technological determinism and emphasizes the importance of policy interventions in job design and quality.

### Connections to Other Research

The authors connect their findings to several research streams:

1. **Labor market dualization**: They link to insider/outsider theories but suggest these frameworks must be expanded to account for anxiety about potential movement between insider and outsider status.
2. **Cultural versus economic explanations of populism**: They position their analysis as bridging competing perspectives, showing how economic processes generate cultural and emotional responses.
3. **Task-based approach to automation**: They connect to the established literature on task automation (Autor et al., 2003; Acemoglu & Autor, 2011) but extend its implications to political behavior.
4. **Status politics**: They link to social psychological research on status anxiety and authoritarian responses (Gidron & Hall, 2017, 2019).

The authors identify future research needs in understanding the specific mechanisms through which political rhetoric shapes voter perceptions of automation and in developing policy responses that address both material and status concerns.
