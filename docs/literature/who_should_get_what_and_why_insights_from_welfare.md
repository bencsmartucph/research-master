## Paper Summary

This chapter by Laenen and Roosma examines welfare deservingness theory, which explains how citizens determine which groups are worthy of social benefits and services. The framework centers on the CARIN criteria (Control, Attitude, Reciprocity, Identity, and Need) that people use to evaluate welfare recipients. The authors present a comprehensive model showing how individual characteristics and contextual factors shape both deservingness perceptions (how target groups are scored on criteria) and deservingness valuations (which criteria are deemed important). They demonstrate how these deservingness judgments ultimately influence public support for social rights and obligations. The chapter explores individual variations based on structural factors (age, gender, income) and cultural factors (political ideology, values), as well as contextual differences stemming from policy design. The authors argue that welfare deservingness theory extends beyond public opinion research to various fields where judgments about who deserves assistance are made, including migration, humanitarian aid, and criminal justice.

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Primary Theory: Welfare Deservingness Model

The welfare deservingness model is the central theoretical framework presented in this paper. It conceptualizes how citizens form opinions about which groups deserve welfare support and why.

**Explanation**: The model posits that people evaluate welfare recipients on five criteria known as CARIN criteria: Control (responsibility for situation), Attitude (gratitude, compliance), Reciprocity (past/present/future contributions), Identity (similarity to self), and Need (financial/health necessity). These evaluations form "deservingness perceptions." The model also incorporates "deservingness valuations" (the importance people attach to each criterion). Crucially, both perceptions and valuations are influenced by individual characteristics and contextual factors, which together determine overall deservingness judgments that influence support for welfare policies.

**Key quotes**:
"The core components: the CARIN criteria. The first criterion, control, is about the perceived degree of personal responsibility welfare claimants have for being in the situation they are in... The second criterion, attitude, refers to a broader collection of norm-conforming attitudes and behaviours, such as people's gratitude, docility, compliance, honesty and pleasantness. The third criterion, reciprocity, relates to perceived contributions to society claimants make (either in the past, present or future), such as paid work, tax payments, caring responsibilities and job-seeking efforts. The fourth criterion, identity, has to do with feelings of similarity, proximity and cultural closeness between those judging the deservingness and those being judged... The fifth criterion, need, refers to the perceived financial destitution and/or poor health status of those seeking help."

"In general, target groups are considered more deserving if they (a) are not considered personally responsible for their situation ('Control'), (b) adhere to prevailing societal norms and values, such as showing gratitude when help is offered ('Attitude'), (c) make a contribution to our society, in the past, present or future ('Reciprocity'), (d) belong to the dominant social or cultural group with which most people can easily identify ('Identity'), and (e) have severe financial and/or health needs ('Need')."

"From this perspective, deservingness theory helps to explain why the public at large ranks some target groups –such as the elderly and the sick– consistently higher than others –such as unemployed people and immigrants– when it comes to their deservingness of social welfare."

**Historical context**: The theoretical framework builds on prior work, particularly by van Oorschot (2000, 2006) who identified the five CARIN criteria. Earlier American scholars like Cook (1979), Gilens (1999), and Will (1993) had already recognized deservingness as a key driver of welfare attitudes, but van Oorschot systematized these insights into a coherent framework.

**Application**: The authors apply this theory to explain differences in public support for various welfare recipients. They illustrate the framework by contrasting how elderly people and unemployed people are evaluated differently on each criterion, explaining the consistently higher public support for the former group.

### Theoretical Extension: The Four Mechanisms Model

The authors extend the basic deservingness theory by proposing four specific mechanisms through which individual and contextual characteristics influence deservingness opinions.

**Explanation**: The authors identify four distinct theoretical mechanisms: 1) General perceptions mechanism - where characteristics influence overall evaluations across all criteria; 2) Specific perceptions mechanism - where characteristics influence evaluations on specific criteria; 3) General valuations mechanism - where characteristics influence the overall importance attached to deservingness; and 4) Specific valuations mechanism - where characteristics influence the importance attached to specific criteria.

**Key quotes**:
"Individual/contextual characteristics influence how people evaluate a target group in general, across all deservingness criteria ('general perceptions mechanism'); Individual/contextual characteristics influence how people evaluate a target group on specific deservingness criteria ('specific perceptions mechanism'); Individual/contextual characteristics influence the importance people attach to deservingness in general, across all deservingness criteria ('general valuations mechanism'); Individual/contextual characteristics influence the importance people attach to specific deservingness criteria ('specific valuations mechanism')."

**Theoretical innovation**: This four-mechanism framework constitutes an important theoretical advancement as it systematically maps the complex relationship between individual/contextual factors and deservingness judgments. It moves beyond treating deservingness as a simple determinant of welfare support and instead examines the nuanced pathways through which various factors shape deservingness opinions.

### Theoretical Innovation: Deservingness Perceptions vs. Valuations

The authors make an important theoretical distinction between "deservingness perceptions" and "deservingness valuations," which adds sophistication to the model.

**Explanation**: Deservingness perceptions refer to how target groups are scored on the CARIN criteria, while deservingness valuations concern the importance people attach to each criterion. This distinction helps explain why people with similar perceptions might reach different deservingness judgments if they prioritize different criteria.

**Key quotes**:
"Up until now, our discussion of the deservingness model has been focused on the way in which target groups are perceived, or 'scored', on the CARIN criteria. This is, however, only part of the welfare deservingness model. For such deservingness perceptions to have real consequences, people should also attach importance to the underlying (CARIN) criteria."

"What this illustration demonstrates is that the overall perceived welfare deservingness is determined not only by people's perceptions of a target group, but also by the value they attach to the different deservingness criteria. The stronger the valuation of a deservingness criterion, the heavier the weight of the score on that criterion."

**Theoretical debate**: The authors position themselves in an ongoing theoretical debate about the relative importance of the CARIN criteria:

"This brings us to the ongoing academic debate about the relative importance of the CARIN criteria, which is stretched between two extreme positions. At one end, there is the idea of a law-like hierarchy of deservingness criteria that is the same for everyone, always and everywhere... At the other end of the debate, there is the view –endorsed in this chapter– that a universal, fixed rank order of deservingness criteria does not exist, because their relative importance is (1) target-specific, (2) person-specific and (3) context-specific."

## Findings & Discussion

### Key Finding 1: Policy Design Shapes Deservingness Opinions Through Feedback Mechanisms

The authors present evidence that welfare policy design can shape both deservingness perceptions and valuations through multiple feedback mechanisms.

**Evidence**:
"Many scholars have argued that highly selective welfare schemes which are for instance only available to poor people through means testing, foster the stereotypical image that welfare claimants are a 'different kind of people', characterized by a deeply rooted unwillingness to contribute meaningfully to society. Universal schemes, which are accessible to all on the basis of citizenship or residency regardless of actual financial needs, are claimed to be much less divisive, and therefore generate less stigmatizing attitudes."

"In a similar vein, it has been argued that different types of welfare provision trigger different deservingness logics emphasizing different criteria. When people decide who should get what from a selective, means-tested scheme, for example, the criterion of need is claimed to play a pivotal role. In the case of contributory social insurance, by contrast, the criterion of reciprocity is likely to play a more important part in determining perceived deservingness."

**Relevance to your research**: This finding is directly relevant to your research on how welfare/labor policies might moderate support for far-right parties. It suggests that policy design not only affects who gets what benefits but shapes public perceptions of deservingness, which could influence political preferences. Universal welfare programs may reduce stigmatization of recipients and foster social cohesion, potentially reducing support for far-right parties that often capitalize on social divisions. Conversely, highly selective welfare may reinforce perceptions of certain groups as undeserving, potentially increasing support for far-right parties.

### Key Finding 2: Individual Factors Shape Deservingness Judgments

The paper identifies how structural factors (e.g., age) and cultural factors (e.g., political ideology) influence deservingness opinions through different mechanisms.

**Evidence**:
"At the individual level, to begin with, two main types of factors have been widely recognized in the literature: a 'structural' factor, referring to a person's sociodemographic position within society (e.g., age, gender, income), and a 'cultural' factor, relating to his or her ideological orientations towards that society (e.g., egalitarianism, individualism, political ideology)."

"For political (left-right) ideology... To left-wingers, the whole concept of deservingness seems less important when they form welfare opinions, because they have a strong ideological allegiance to the welfare state... To right-wingers, by contrast, questions of deservingness prove to be much more relevant to distinguish deserving from undeserving beneficiaries."

**Relevance to your research**: This finding suggests that structural and cultural factors may moderate the relationship between welfare policies and support for far-right parties. For instance, the effectiveness of universal welfare policies in reducing far-right support might vary depending on the demographic composition and ideological distribution in a society. Your research might examine how welfare policies interact with individual-level characteristics to influence political preferences.

### Key Finding 3: Different Deservingness Logics for Rights vs. Obligations

The authors identify a nuanced aspect of deservingness theory - that different deservingness criteria may matter for judgments about social rights versus social obligations.

**Evidence**:
"Crucial to note, however, is that there seem to be slightly different deservingness logics underlying support for social obligations and support for social rights. A recent study, for example, demonstrates that German citizens prioritize other deservingness considerations in relation to different aspects of the unemployment benefit system. When assessing the generosity thereof, claimants' child caring responsibilities prove to be most important for determining deservingness: the more children a claimant has, the higher the level of benefit should be (i.e., social rights). When considering the conditionality of those benefits, by contrast, the age of the claimants was of primary significance: the older unemployed were exempted more frequently from work-related requirements than their younger counterparts (i.e., social obligations)."

**Relevance to your research**: This finding suggests the need to distinguish between policies that provide benefits (rights) and those that impose requirements (obligations) when examining how welfare policies moderate support for far-right parties. The political impact of these different types of policies may operate through distinct psychological mechanisms and affect different segments of the population.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Welfare deservingness is multidimensional**: The CARIN framework (Control, Attitude, Reciprocity, Identity, Need) provides a nuanced understanding of how citizens evaluate welfare recipients, with implications for understanding the underlying psychological mechanisms that might link welfare policies to political preferences.
2. **Contextual factors matter**: The institutional design of welfare policies shapes public perceptions of deservingness through feedback mechanisms. Universal programs tend to generate less stigmatizing attitudes toward recipients compared to selective programs.
3. **Individual characteristics influence deservingness judgments**: Structural factors (sociodemographic position) and cultural factors (ideological orientations) affect both perceptions and valuations of deservingness criteria, suggesting that welfare policies may have different effects across population segments.
4. **Theory has broader applications**: Deservingness theory extends beyond welfare attitudes to other domains where judgments about deservingness are made, including migration policy, which directly connects to research on far-right support.
