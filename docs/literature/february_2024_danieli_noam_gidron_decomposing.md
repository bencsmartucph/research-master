## Key Takeaways

1. **Changing priorities rather than changing opinions drive PRRP support**: "Of the three channels, rising support for PRRP is mostly driven by changing priorities. Specifically, we find that over the past two decades, voters have increasingly prioritized cultural issues over economic issues."
2. **Voter opinions have not shifted dramatically toward PRRP positions**: "In aggregate, we find almost no right-wing shift in public opinion on issues associated with PRRP. For example, in contrast to multiple media accounts, attitudes have not moved en masse toward greater opposition to immigration. This supports the hypothesis that a reservoir of PRRP voters existed before the dramatic rise in their support."
3. **Certain demographic groups are more likely to prioritize cultural issues**: "We find a shift to the right for men, voters without a college degree, without a union membership, and living in rural areas. These voters are increasingly more likely to reward parties for holding conservative cultural positions (or less likely to penalize them)."
4. **Party positions don't explain PRRP success**: "Our decomposition estimates reveal that changes in party positions contribute rather little to the rise in PRRP support since 2005, in contrast to several theories... We find no evidence for convergence in the economic positions of center-left and center-right parties."
5. **The prioritization of cultural issues is consistent across countries**: "While there is variation in the magnitude of the change, the shift toward cultural issues occurs in almost all countries. This homogeneous trend across countries is especially interesting as we do find substantial variation in voter attributes across countries."

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### 1. The Voter Priorities Framework

**Detailed explanation**:
The authors develop a theoretical framework centered on voter priorities to explain political behavior. This framework distinguishes between (1) voter attributes (opinions and demographics) and (2) voter priorities (the parameters that determine how much weight voters place on different issues when deciding which party to support). The key insight is that voting decisions are not merely a function of a voter's opinions, but also of how much importance they attach to each issue when choosing a party.

The authors formalize this distinction in their probabilistic voting model, where a voter's utility from supporting a specific party is a function of that party's positions (supply) weighted by the voter's individual "voting weights" (demand). These weights can be positive or negative depending on whether the voter supports or opposes that position, and their magnitude reflects how much the voter cares about that position relative to others.

The authors' innovation is in disaggregating demand-side explanations for PRRP support into these two components. They argue that previous literature had not systematically distinguished between changes in voter opinions and changes in voter priorities, with some scholars focusing on opinion shifts (e.g., increased nativism) and others on priority shifts (e.g., cultural issues becoming more salient). Their decomposition method allows for directly comparing these mechanisms.

**Direct quotes**:
"The two demand channels—voter attributes and voter priorities—affect voting decisions through the voting weights. We allow the weights to vary across voters by assuming that they are a function of the voter attributes. The mapping between voter attributes and the weights is determined by the voter priorities, which correspond to the parameters of the utility function. We refer to these parameters as priorities since they determine which party positions voters prioritize given their attributes."

"This result provides empirical evidence for the claim that cultural issues became more important than economic issues, which has important ramifications for trade policy, redistribution, support for democratic norms, and polarization."

**Important associated literature**:
The voter priorities framework builds on research examining issue salience and voting behavior. The authors cite work by Johns (2010), De Vries et al. (2013), Kendall et al. (2015), and Sides et al. (2019) who have estimated voter priorities in various contexts. The paper also connects to political economy theories (Bonomi et al., 2021; Enke et al., 2022) that incorporate voter priorities, and research on the growing importance of cultural issues (Kriesi et al., 2008; Enke, 2020; Gethin et al., 2021; Margalit et al., 2022).

**How the authors apply the theory**:
The authors operationalize their voter priorities framework through a two-stage estimation process. First, they estimate voter priorities (model parameters) from their data using machine learning techniques. Then, they decompose the rise in PRRP support between 2005-2009 and 2017-2020 to determine what portion is attributable to changes in voter priorities versus other factors.

**Connection to your research**:
This framework offers valuable insights for your research on the relationship between social/welfare policies and far-right support. Rather than focusing solely on how policies might change voter opinions, you should consider how they might affect the weight voters place on different issues. For example, expansive social policies might not change voters' cultural attitudes, but they could reduce the priority voters place on cultural issues by making economic security less of a concern, thus potentially reducing PRRP support.

### 2. The Reservoir Activation Theory

**Detailed explanation**:
The paper provides strong empirical support for what could be called the "reservoir activation theory" of PRRP support. This theory suggests that the rise of PRRP doesn't reflect a dramatic shift in public opinion toward more nativist or authoritarian views, but rather the activation of a preexisting reservoir of culturally conservative voters who previously based their voting decisions on other factors, particularly economic issues.

The reservoir activation theory challenges dominant narratives about a rightward shift in public opinion driving PRRP success. Instead, it suggests that PRRP succeeded by mobilizing voters who already held culturally conservative views but previously didn't prioritize these views in their voting decisions. The authors find that voter opinions have remained relatively stable in aggregate, but the priorities attached to these opinions have changed substantially, with cultural issues gaining salience relative to economic issues.

The theory also has a spatial dimension: the authors find that the size of the potential PRRP voter reservoir varies substantially across countries, which helps explain cross-national variation in PRRP success.

**Direct quotes**:
"Our findings significantly bolster the hypothesis that the rise of PRRP support is primarily driven by the 'activation' of a preexisting reservoir of potential PRRP voters rather than a dramatic change in attitudes. Since the rise of PRRP is not driven by observed voter attributes or party positions, it could theoretically be driven by either the voter priorities or the residual."

This supports the hypothesis that a reservoir of PRRP voters existed before the dramatic rise in their support."

"The size of this preexisting reservoir of potential PRRP voters varies substantially across countries and can explain some of the geographical variation in PRRP support."

**Important associated literature**:
The authors connect their findings to literature showing that PRRP can gain support by activating reservoirs of culturally conservative voters. They cite Cantoni et al. (2019) showing that the AfD gained votes by activating right-wing cultural voters, Voigtländer and Voth (2012) on how Nazis gained support by activating latent historical reservoirs of anti-Semitic attitudes, and research from the US (Bursztyn et al., 2020), Italy (Magistro and Wittstock, 2021), and the UK (Sobolewska and Ford, 2020) on how salience, legitimacy, or priorities can activate conservative cultural attitudes.

**How the authors apply the theory**:
The authors test this theory through their decomposition analysis, which allows them to separate the effects of changing voter attributes from changing voter priorities. They also develop a spatial method to measure the size of potential PRRP voter reservoirs across countries, simulating counterfactual PRRP support if voters in each country faced the same party choice set.

**Connection to your research**:
The reservoir activation theory has important implications for your research on welfare policies and far-right support. It suggests that welfare policies might affect PRRP support not by changing voters' cultural attitudes, but by influencing whether voters prioritize these attitudes when voting. Generous welfare policies might reduce economic insecurity and thus decrease the likelihood that culturally conservative voters will prioritize cultural grievances at the ballot box. Conversely, welfare retrenchment or perceived competition for welfare resources might activate latent cultural concerns among voters who already hold conservative cultural views.

### 3. The Economic-Cultural Cleavage Theory

**Detailed explanation**:
The paper contributes to theoretical debates about the shifting nature of political cleavages in Western democracies. The authors find strong evidence for what might be called the "economic-cultural cleavage theory" - the idea that political competition is increasingly structured around cultural issues rather than economic ones.

This theory suggests that Western democracies are experiencing a fundamental transformation in political competition, with cultural issues like immigration, national identity, and traditional values increasingly displacing economic issues like taxation, redistribution, and market regulation as the primary axis of political conflict. The authors find that voters' weights on economic positions have become more concentrated around zero (meaning they matter less for vote choice), while cultural positions remain highly salient.

The paper also provides evidence for a shift in political identities, with left-right self-identification becoming more associated with cultural views rather than economic views. This suggests that the very meaning of "left" and "right" is changing, with cultural issues increasingly defining these political camps.

**Direct quotes**:
"Figure 5 shows that the distribution of the weights placed on the CMP economy index became substantially more concentrated around zero in 2017–2020, compared to weights placed on CMP cultural index. Ceteris paribus, the economic positions of parties became relatively less decisive in shaping vote choice. This result provides empirical evidence for the claim that cultural issues became more important than economic issues."

"The results in Figure 7 show that between the 2005–2009 and the 2017–2020 waves, the cultural dimension became more dominant in shaping left-right self-identification."

This result provides suggestive evidence supporting the argument that voters' identities shifted from class to culture."

**Important associated literature**:
The economic-cultural cleavage theory builds on an extensive literature examining how the structure of political conflict has changed in Western democracies. The authors cite Kriesi et al. (2008), who argue that globalization has created a new cleavage between "winners" and "losers" of globalization that crosscuts traditional left-right divisions. They also reference work by Enke (2020), Gethin et al. (2021), and Margalit et al. (2022) on the growing importance of cultural issues, as well as Gennaioli and Tabellini (2023) who argue that voter identities have shifted from class to culture.

**How the authors apply the theory**:
The authors test this theory through their analysis of changing voter priorities, showing that the weights voters place on economic positions have decreased while cultural positions remain salient. They also provide additional evidence through their analysis of left-right self-identification, showing that this has become more associated with cultural views than economic views.

**Connection to your research**:
The economic-cultural cleavage theory offers important context for your research on welfare policies and far-right support. It suggests that welfare policies might affect political behavior differently now than in the past, as voters increasingly prioritize cultural concerns over economic ones. Your research could explore whether certain types of welfare policies might re-prioritize economic issues for voters, potentially reducing support for PRRP. Alternatively, you might investigate how the framing of welfare policies in cultural versus economic terms affects their political impact.

### 4. The Demographic Differentiation Theory

**Detailed explanation**:
The paper develops what could be called a "demographic differentiation theory" of changing political priorities. This theory suggests that the shift toward prioritizing cultural issues over economic issues has not been uniform across demographic groups, but has varied significantly based on factors such as gender, education, age, union membership, and urban-rural location.

The authors find that men, voters without a college degree, older individuals, non-union members, and rural residents have increasingly prioritized conservative cultural positions when making voting decisions. In contrast, college graduates, women, younger voters, union members, and urban residents have not experienced a similar shift to the right and in some cases have shifted left. This demographic differentiation helps explain why PRRP have been particularly successful among certain demographic groups.

The theory connects to broader debates about the changing social bases of political parties, with working-class voters (particularly men) increasingly supporting right-wing parties based on cultural issues, while educated professionals increasingly support left-wing parties. This represents a significant realignment from traditional class-based voting patterns.

**Direct quotes**:
"Figure 6 shows that cultural weights shift in different directions across groups. We find a shift to the right for men, voters without a college degree, without a union membership, and living in rural areas. These voters are increasingly more likely to reward parties for holding conservative cultural positions (or less likely to penalize them)."

By contrast, college graduates, women, younger voters, union members, and urban residents did not experience a similar shift to the right and, in some cases, their voting weights shifted to the left."

We also find a rightward shift in priorities for older voters, consistent with the cultural backlash theory arguing that many older voters feel disconnected from current cultural values (Norris and Inglehart, 2019)."

**Important associated literature**:
The demographic differentiation theory connects to literature on changing electoral coalitions, including work by Norris and Inglehart (2019) on cultural backlash among older voters, Gidron and Hall (2020) on the social bases of populist support, and Piketty (2020) on the changing class basis of political parties. It also relates to research on the "left behind" thesis of populist support, which suggests that PRRP draw support from those who feel economically, culturally, or socially marginalized by contemporary social changes.

**How the authors apply the theory**:
The authors test this theory by estimating voter priorities separately for different demographic groups and examining how these have changed over time. They also analyze how the prioritization of conservative cultural issues is driven by potential PRRP supporters (defined by their PRRP score), finding that "the shift to the right of the cultural weights is only apparent for these voters."

**Connection to your research**:
The demographic differentiation theory offers important insights for your research on welfare policies and far-right support. It suggests that welfare policies might affect different demographic groups differently, potentially having stronger effects on reducing PRRP support among groups that have most strongly shifted toward prioritizing cultural issues (men, non-college educated, older individuals, non-union members, rural residents). Your research could explore whether targeted welfare policies that address the economic concerns of these specific groups might be particularly effective at reducing PRRP support.
