## Executive Summary

This paper by Lisanne de Blok and Staffan Kumlin examines how both welfare state outputs (service satisfaction) and procedural fairness (experienced voice opportunities) affect political trust in democratic institutions. The authors investigate this relationship using a three-wave panel study in Norway, employing a random-effects within-between (REWB) framework that allows them to separate between-person and within-person effects. Their findings reveal that both procedural fairness and outcome satisfaction affect political trust. Crucially, they discover that procedural fairness moderates the negative effects of poor service outcomes on political trust - particularly for "losers" (those dissatisfied with outcomes). This suggests that ensuring fair processes in welfare service delivery could mitigate declining political trust even in times of welfare state resource constraints and reforms.

## Introduction and Problem Context

The authors begin by highlighting the significant challenges facing mature welfare states, which include:

> "Mature Western welfare states have long faced slowly growing challenges and resource scarcity. Demographic change, persistent unemployment, international economic competition – to mention a few oft-debated 'reform pressures' – strain public finances while fuelling competition between legitimate needs. Recently, periodic shocks like the 2008 financial crisis and the 2015 refugee influx have contributed further to this situation." (p. 867)
> 

They note that welfare states are implementing various reforms in response to these pressures, including retrenchment of traditional social rights, recalibration to address "new social risks," redesign to stimulate employment incentives, and increasing "social investment" in human capital. The authors suggest these reforms highlight how:

> "Welfare states must handle an expanding set of needs and goals with smaller resources." (p. 868)
> 

A key observation driving their research is that citizens often experience a divergence between their normative support for the welfare state and their more critical evaluations of its actual performance. The authors cite previous research showing that:

> "Perceived output malperformance of welfare state services and benefits constitute 'the weakest link in welfare state support'" (p. 868, citing Roosma et al., 2013)
> 

This leads to their central research question:

> "How can political trust be maintained in an era of resource scarcity and consequential output-related dissatisfaction?" (p. 868)
> 

The authors propose examining the procedural fairness argument - that citizens care not only about welfare state outputs but also about the fairness of service delivery processes. They specifically focus on whether experienced "voice opportunities" in welfare state encounters can mitigate negative effects of output dissatisfaction on political trust.

## Theoretical Framework

### Trust-as-Evaluation Approach

The authors define political trust as a relational concept involving uncertainty:

> "A defines political trust as a relational concept. It is a subjective evaluation of the relationship between a subject (A) and an object (B), where 'A trusts B to do x'... A defining feature of this relationship is the subject's degree of uncertainty about the future behaviour of the object." (p. 869)
> 

They position their work within the "trust-as-evaluation" approach, which:

> "...implies that good performance (on various aspects) induces high levels of political trust." (p. 869)
> 

The authors review prior research showing how perceptions of government performance, particularly macroeconomic performance and welfare state outputs, affect political trust. They note that such associations appear to have grown stronger in Europe after the 2008 financial crisis and the 2015 refugee spike.

### Procedural Fairness Theory

The authors present procedural fairness theory as a counterargument to purely output-oriented theories:

> "Procedural fairness theory was introduced as a counterargument to output-oriented theories. It predicts that people care about the decision-making and implementation process preceding the output. This approach can help explain why citizens accept decisions, even though they do not benefit directly from them or otherwise assess outcomes as dissatisfactory." (p. 870)
> 

They focus specifically on "experienced voice opportunities" as a key aspect of procedural fairness:

> "Voice involves having the opportunity to 'kick up a fuss' in any form of activity, with the intention of producing some form of change in service, management etcetera... a process will be perceived as more fair if citizens feel they 'can exercise influence and communicate their views to public employees during the interaction process leading up to welfare state outcomes'" (p. 871)
> 

The authors distinguish between formal voice opportunities (institutionalized situations where input directly affects processes or decisions) and informal voice opportunities (expressing opinions without obvious direct influence on decisions).

### Mechanisms Linking Procedural Fairness to Political Trust

The authors identify two potential mechanisms:

> "First, how an authority treats its citizens can offer citizens a signal of its trustworthiness. If citizens perceive that they are being treated fairly by public officials, they will believe that the political system in general is trustworthy and will, consequently, hold higher levels of political trust... Alternatively, from the trust-as-evaluation perspective, procedural fairness can also directly affect trust when citizens evaluate government performance based on the quality of its policy implementation procedures." (p. 871)
> 

### Procedural Fairness as a Moderator

A key theoretical contribution is exploring how procedural fairness might moderate the relationship between outcome dissatisfaction and political trust. The authors present three possibilities:

1. Procedural fairness matters more for "losers" (their primary hypothesis):
    
    > "One common assumption is that citizens are first and foremost result-oriented but that fair procedures, as it were, soften the blow of not getting one's desired outcome. Conversely, procedures perceived as unfair exacerbate the harmful influence of output dissatisfaction. Under this view, then, procedural fairness makes a larger difference for output 'losers' than for 'winners', that is, those who are satisfied with the outcome." (p. 872)
    > 
2. Procedural fairness is a universal value that affects trust independently of outcomes (no interaction).
3. Procedural fairness is a "luxury item" that only matters once outcome needs are satisfied:
    
    > "...it is only once outcome preferences are satisfied do people pay attention to non-material aspects like voice. Procedural fairness would be, as it were, a 'luxury item' that becomes interesting once a more primary need for a satisfactory outcome is fulfilled." (p. 872)
    > 

## Hypotheses

The authors propose four hypotheses:

1. H1 (Cross-sectional hypothesis): Individuals who perceive welfare state procedures as more fair have more political trust compared to others.
2. H2 (Dynamic hypothesis): An increase in perceived procedural fairness of welfare state services is related to an increase in political trust.
3. H3 (Cross-sectional moderation): Individual differences in welfare state service satisfaction have a smaller effect on political trust among people with more positive perceptions of procedural fairness.
4. H4 (Dynamic moderation): A decline in welfare state service satisfaction has a smaller effect on change in political trust if at the same time perceived procedural fairness was increasing.

## Methodology

### Norwegian Case Context

The authors explain that while Norway might seem like a special case due to its high-trust, high-generosity welfare state with remarkable economic development, it also faces similar reform pressures to other European welfare states. During the data collection period (2014-2017), Norway experienced a sudden economic shift due to falling oil prices, which created a new discourse on economic adjustment and its implications for welfare state policies.

### Data Collection

The study uses three waves of the Norwegian panel study "Support for the Affluent Welfare State (SuppA)." The data was collected through:

- A two-staged sampling procedure drawing from TNS Gallup's access panel
- Response rates from the pre-recruited access panel were 50%-60%
- Final sample consisted of 5,420 respondents between ages 18-75 years
- 52% participated in the first two waves, 29% in all three waves
- Data collected through an online survey

### Variables

**Dependent Variable:**

- Political trust: Measured by an index of three items asking about trust in political parties, parliament, and the government (4-point scales, Cronbach's alpha: 0.82)

**Independent Variables:**

- Outcome satisfaction: Mean level of satisfaction with 18 types of welfare services, including healthcare, childcare, eldercare, education, and labor/welfare administration services
    - Responses ranged from very dissatisfied (1) to very satisfied (5)
    - Only includes evaluations from individuals with direct or indirect experience of services
- Procedural fairness (voice): Measured by response to "Employees were helpful in the sense that they listened to what I had to say"
    - Responses on a 4-point scale from very poorly (1) to very well (4)
    - Those answering "not relevant" set to median score (2.5)

**Control Variables:**

- Gender
- Age
- Education level
- Immigrant status
- Political interest
- Income
- Whether they voted for a government party ("home team")

### Analytical Approach

The authors employ a random-effects within-between (REWB) framework for analyzing panel data, which allows them to:

1. Separate between-person effects (comparing different individuals) and within-person effects (changes within individuals over time)
2. Control for time-invariant unobserved heterogeneity in the within-person models
3. Make stronger causal inferences about the relationships between variables

While noting the possibility of reverse causality, the authors argue that their approach makes progress on understanding the contrast and interaction between outputs and procedures.

## Results

### Direct Effects

In the between-person (cross-sectional) analysis, the authors find:

- Those satisfied with welfare state services hold more trust compared to others
- Individuals who perceive procedures as more fair compared to others also hold higher levels of political trust (supporting H1)

In the within-person (dynamic) analysis:

- A positive but non-significant relationship between changes in welfare service satisfaction and political trust
- A significant positive relationship between increases in perceived procedural fairness and higher levels of political trust (supporting H2)
- The within-effect of procedural fairness appears to be a stronger predictor of changes in political trust than changes in welfare state service satisfaction

### Conditional Effects (Interactions)

For between-person interactions:

- The interaction between procedural fairness and outcome satisfaction is significant and negative
- Among people with more positive perceptions of procedural fairness, individual differences in welfare state service satisfaction have a smaller effect on political trust (supporting H3)
- Among those perceiving procedures as unfair, welfare state service dissatisfaction has a more detrimental effect on political trust
- Procedural fairness makes little difference in trust levels among those highly satisfied with welfare services

For within-person interactions:

- The interaction effect is also negative and significant
- When a decline in welfare satisfaction coincides with a decline in procedural fairness, political trust declines most
- When a decline in welfare satisfaction coincides with an increase in procedural fairness, political trust hardly changes (supporting H4)
- Among those experiencing improved welfare services, changes in procedural fairness make little difference

Key finding:

> "Crucially, we also found that perceived procedural fairness moderates detrimental effects of unfavourable outcomes. Thus, it is particularly losers (those dissatisfied with outcomes) who attach greater weight to procedural voice in the formation of political trust." (p. 880)
> 

## Discussion and Implications

The authors highlight several important implications of their findings:

1. Procedural quality can buffer negative impacts of welfare state reform and resource scarcity:
    
    > "Losers' consent can be gained by improving the quality and perceived fairness of these procedures. Resource scarcity and growing challenges to the welfare state may not need to result in a decline in political trust, if governments invest in the processes of service delivery." (p. 881)
    > 
2. Their findings contribute to the trust-as-evaluation approach by providing stronger causal evidence through panel data analysis:
    
    > "Our panel data allowed us to study dynamic variation to provide credence to a causal relationship between political performance and political trust." (p. 881)
    > 
3. They demonstrate the importance of procedural performance in addition to output performance:
    
    > "Our results suggest that citizens also care about the quality of the procedures and that this too affects their levels of political trust." (p. 881)
    > 

The authors also acknowledge limitations of their study:

- Focusing on one affluent welfare state (Norway)
- Examining local-level services' impact on national-level trust
- Using subjective evaluations rather than objective measures

Finally, they conclude with a positive message about maintaining political trust in changing welfare states:

> "Not only do experienced voice opportunities seem causally related to political trust, controlling for evaluated outcomes. Such opportunities also seem to reduce detrimental effects of poor outcomes. Thus, paying attention to procedural fairness in service delivery may constitute one avenue through which political trust can survive resource scarcity and widespread output dissatisfaction in changing welfare states." (p. 882)
>
