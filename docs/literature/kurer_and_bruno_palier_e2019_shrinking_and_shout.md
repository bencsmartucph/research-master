- 😮Mechanisms linking job polarization and political behavior
    
    The relative economic decline of historically dominant core groups is a likely source of discontent and insecurity and a nascent literature has linked these perceptions to an increased demand for social conservatism in the political arena. Two recent studies focusing on the economic roots of authoritarian and socially conservative preferences, respectively, provide an explicit theoretical discussion of the underlying mechanisms based either on a negative change in social identity or a sense of a loss of control ([Ballard-Rosa et al., 2018](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr6-2053168019831164); [De Vries et al., 2018](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr12-2053168019831164)). In a similar vein, a relatively novel literature on demand-side factors of right-wing populist parties emphasizes the role of societal pessimism and nostalgia among losers of economic modernization ([Gest et al., 2017](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr17-2053168019831164); [Steenvoorden and Harteveld, 2018](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr35-2053168019831164)). What both types of argument have in common is the emphasis on a gradual shift in relative societal position that creates a specific perception of insecurity and loss of control, which can even emerge in the absence of absolute material hardship. As a consequence, we might not observe the strongest political reaction among the hardest-hit but rather among those who are most concerned about their economic well-being and future prospects in the labor market ([Im et al., 2019](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr21-2053168019831164); [Kurer, 2017](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr24-2053168019831164)). The recent rise of the “*gilet jaune*” movement in France is an apt illustration of our argument since most protesters do have jobs but are increasingly concerned about making ends meet.
    
    - The most recent literature has become increasingly skeptical toward an overly simple narrative emphasizing direct effects of material disadvantage and economic hardship (e.g., [Antonucci et al., 2017](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr1-2053168019831164); [Gidron and Hall, 2017](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr18-2053168019831164); [Mutz, 2018](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr30-2053168019831164)). For example, Antonucci et al. show on the basis of the British Election Study that the typical Leave voter does not fit the image of an angry, unskilled and perhaps even unemployed outsider. Rather, voting Leave is associated with intermediate classes who suffer from a perceived decline in their economic position.
    
    This description very much resembles our understanding of routine occupations: moderately skilled but increasingly less valued work with rather bleak prospects in labor markets due to susceptibility to automation. Based on comparative survey data, [Im et al. (2019)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr21-2053168019831164) confirm the conjecture that the endangered prospect of social upward mobility among routine workers is a powerful driver of political behavior, and they provide empirical evidence for one of the guiding hypotheses of this special issue: the risk of automation is positively related to support for social conservatism.
    
    **The right wins**
    
     Given that structural transformations in the economy create uncertainty, which in turn increases the demand for socially conservative policies, the mainstream right as well as the (populist) radical right might appeal to losers of automation. However, we expect that right-wing populist parties are more successful in this endeavor. According to expert surveys, in most cases their actual position on social conservatism is more pronounced than the one of mainstream right parties. In addition, they very often have the benefit of the newcomer who has not been part of the machine, which after all is (made) responsible for the state of the matters. By implication, we would expect mainstream right parties’ attempts to mobilize said constituency to remain relatively unsuccessful in the presence of a more radical competitor on the right. This reasoning is indeed confirmed by existing empirical work, including one of the contributions to the special issue ([Burgoon et al., 2018](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr7-2053168019831164); [Im et al., 2019](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr21-2053168019831164); [Kurer, 2017](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr24-2053168019831164)).
    
    Political actors promoting socially conservative platforms have identified the still significantly large group of routine workers as electorally relevant and actively seek to gain their support at the ballot box. Anecdotal evidence is abound. Donald Trump carried Rust Belt states on the promise of reviving industries and ending job loss and population stagnation. Theresa May rallied so-called Jams (“just about managing”), i.e., hardworking but financially struggling families just not poor enough to profit from welfare state benefits. In addition, Nicolas Sarkozy in 2007 called upon the French population “who gets up early” in order to work more and earn more.[1](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#fn1-2053168019831164) What these calls have in common is the explicit reference to “honest work.” They are not about increasing welfare benefits to cushion economic vulnerability but about appreciating the value of ordinary work ([Lamont, 2000](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr26-2053168019831164)). We believe that this appeal to personal dignity is key to winning routine workers’ support. Perhaps even more than social protection, they demand economic and cultural protection. They feel attracted by promises to re-establish the values of a bygone era of a more homogenous demography, more rigid hierarchies and an economic system that protects domestic workers ([Gest et al., 2017](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr17-2053168019831164)). An exclusive understanding of the nation state and citizenship, which often figures prominently on the right-wing populist agenda, certainly adds decisively to their success.
    
    An important implication of the central role of dignity and social status in routine workers’ election calculus is that political contestation tends to be skewed in favor of political challengers or newcomers. It is far from obvious which concrete policy response could mitigate the perceived decay of traditional values and the declining esteem of ordinary work. If routine workers’ grievances are not primarily about material concerns, expanding social security will be an ineffective remedy and mainstream parties will have a hard time satisfying routine workers’ demands. Indeed, [Gingrich (2019)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/2053168019831164#bibr19-2053168019831164) provides sobering evidence on mainstream parties’ limited leeway to compensate the losers of economic modernization. As expected, welfare retrenchment is electorally harmful and benefits right-wing populist mobilization. However, the reverse mechanism (more spending, less populism) is not borne out by the data – despite demonstrably positive effects on individual welfare. This finding highlights the strategic disadvantage of responsible mainstream parties in competition with challenger parties that thrive on a less policy-based, less programmatic appearance, which makes it much easier to appeal to the subtle, perhaps slightly diffuse fears and demands of those fearing the negative consequences of technological change.
    

# Comprehensive Analysis of "

## Paper Summary

This 2019 paper byxamines how technological change creates distinct distributive consequences that help explain contemporary political disruptions in post-industrial democracies. Unlike previous economic transformations that primarily affected low-skilled workers, technological automation disproportionately impacts routine workers in the middle of the skill and income distribution. The authors argue that this "hollowing of the middle" creates anxiety among the lower middle class, who had historically benefited from economic stability and upward mobility. As these routine workers face increasingly bleak labor market prospects, they develop a demand for social, cultural, and economic protectionism. The paper contends that right-wing populist parties have recognized and capitalized on this electoral potential by addressing routine workers' anxieties and status concerns. The authors conclude that a "shrinking and shouting" middle class that no longer feels protected from economic modernization has become a critical electoral force behind political turmoil in advanced democracies.

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Primary Theory: Employment Polarization and the "Hollowing of the Middle"

The paper's central theoretical framework draws on labor economics literature about routine-biased technological change (RBTC) and how it creates a distinct pattern of employment polarization that differs from other economic transformations.

"In contrast to other structural economic developments like deindustrialization or globalization that have been shown to primarily hit the lowest skilled workers, the adverse effects of new technologies first and foremost affect routine workers in the middle of the earnings- and skill distribution."

The authors build on work by David Autor and colleagues, who established that:

"studying the distributive implications of technological innovation, Autor et al. (2003) established that computers are particularly powerful in replacing routine jobs characterized by tasks that follow explicit, clearly defined rules. At the same time, non-routine jobs, even those with limited skill requirements, are complemented rather than substituted by new technology."

This theory posits that technological change produces employment polarization - growth in both high-skilled non-routine cognitive jobs and low-skilled non-routine manual jobs, while eliminating middle-skilled routine jobs. This creates a distinct "hollowing of the middle" that the authors argue has been overlooked in political science literature:

"The 'hollowing of the middle' is strikingly consistent all across Europe. Therefore, even if we do not observe perfectly balanced growth in both kinds of non-routine work, we think that polarization is an appropriate term to describe recent trends in post-industrial labor markets."

### Theory: Lower Middle Class Vulnerability and Political Consequences

The paper develops a theoretical linkage between the specific distribution of economic disadvantage from technological change and its political consequences. The key innovation is focusing on the lower middle class position of routine workers:

"From a political science perspective, this disproportionate strain on routine workers is of crucial importance. Technology-induced employment polarization does not only affect mid-skilled blue-collar routine jobs in production, e.g., machine operators, but also seriously threatens many routine jobs in back offices and administration, e.g., secretaries or bank tellers."

The authors emphasize that these routine occupations have historically provided economic stability and social respectability:

"A large part of routine jobs are occupations 'at the fringes of the lower-middle class' that require certain skills and training, used to secure middle-range wages and thus provided for a relatively comfortable standard of living."

Their theoretical contribution is connecting this specific economic position to its political implications:

"Routine workers are a large and electorally relevant group with the capacity to actively voice dissatisfaction in the political arena. A lower middle class no longer protected from the vagaries of economic modernization and in fear of losing its acquired position in society is a potential electoral game changer."

### Theory: Status Anxiety and Social Conservatism

The paper develops a nuanced theoretical mechanism linking economic change to political behavior that goes beyond material hardship to emphasize status concerns and social identity:

"The relative economic decline of historically dominant core groups is a likely source of discontent and insecurity and a nascent literature has linked these perceptions to an increased demand for social conservatism in the political arena. Two recent studies focusing on the economic roots of authoritarian and socially conservative preferences, respectively, provide an explicit theoretical discussion of the underlying mechanisms based either on a negative change in social identity or a sense of a loss of control."

This mechanism emphasizes subjective perceptions of decline rather than objective economic hardship:

"As a consequence, we might not observe the strongest political reaction among the hardest-hit but rather among those who are most concerned about their economic well-being and future prospects in the labor market."

The authors highlight that this theoretical approach helps resolve contradictions in existing research:

"The most recent literature has become increasingly skeptical toward an overly simple narrative emphasizing direct effects of material disadvantage and economic hardship."

## Findings & Discussion

### Key Finding: Routine Workers as "Survivors" Rather Than Economic Victims

The paper makes a crucial finding that routine workers often manage to "survive" economically despite their occupations being in structural decline:

"That said, while fears of falling down the social scale are certainly well-founded, many routine workers actually manage to avoid the experience of economic hardship. Routine work often disappears through 'natural turnover,' that is lower entry and higher exit rates, and only a minority effectively ends up unemployed."

This explains why traditional measures of economic hardship fail to capture their political grievances:

"Although 'survivors' in routine work face economic stagnation compared with highly skilled and highly specialized non-routine workers who benefit from technological complementaries, they have and keep the traits of (former) labor market insiders with salaries above the lowest ones and with permanent job contracts."

### Finding: Dignity and Status Concerns Over Material Needs

The paper finds that routine workers' political demands center more on recognition of their worth and status than on economic redistribution:

"We believe that this appeal to personal dignity is key to winning routine workers' support. Perhaps even more than social protection, they demand economic and cultural protection. They feel attracted by promises to re-establish the values of a bygone era of a more homogenous demography, more rigid hierarchies and an economic system that protects domestic workers."

This helps explain why welfare policies may be insufficient to address their concerns:

"If routine workers' grievances are not primarily about material concerns, expanding social security will be an ineffective remedy and mainstream parties will have a hard time satisfying routine workers' demands."

### Finding: Right-Wing Populist Advantage in Addressing Status Anxieties

The paper finds that right-wing populist parties have been particularly successful at mobilizing routine workers:

"According to expert surveys, in most cases their actual position on social conservatism is more pronounced than the one of mainstream right parties. In addition, they very often have the benefit of the newcomer who has not been part of the machine, which after all is (made) responsible for the state of the matters."

It further suggests that mainstream parties, particularly on the left, face strategic disadvantages in addressing these concerns:

"This finding highlights the strategic disadvantage of responsible mainstream parties in competition with challenger parties that thrive on a less policy-based, less programmatic appearance, which makes it much easier to appeal to the subtle, perhaps slightly diffuse fears and demands of those fearing the negative consequences of technological change."

### Finding: Limited Effectiveness of Welfare Expansion

A key finding with direct relevance to your research is that welfare expansion appears ineffective at reducing support for populist parties:

"Indeed, Gingrich (2019) provides sobering evidence on mainstream parties' limited leeway to compensate the losers of economic modernization. As expected, welfare retrenchment is electorally harmful and benefits right-wing populist mobilization. However, the reverse mechanism (more spending, less populism) is not borne out by the data – despite demonstrably positive effects on individual welfare."

## Relevance to Your Research

This paper offers crucial insights for your research on how welfare/labor/social investment policies might moderate support for far-right parties:

1. **Beyond Material Benefits**: The paper suggests that traditional welfare approaches focused on material compensation may be insufficient because routine workers' grievances are rooted in status anxiety and dignity concerns. Your research might explore whether social investment policies that emphasize skill enhancement and employment dignity could be more effective than simple redistributive measures.
2. **Measuring Status Concerns**: The paper indicates that standard economic indicators (income, unemployment) fail to capture routine workers' grievances. Your research could benefit from developing measures of subjective status, fears of downward mobility, and perceptions of economic insecurity that go beyond objective indicators.
3. **Welfare Policy Design**: The finding that welfare expansion doesn't automatically reduce populist support suggests you should focus on the qualitative design of welfare policies rather than simply their scale. Policies that acknowledge and address dignity and status concerns might prove more effective.
4. **Timing and Prevention**: The paper's emphasis on routine workers who "survive" despite their occupation's decline suggests that proactive labor market policies targeting workers in vulnerable occupations before they experience hardship might be more effective than reactive welfare measures.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Specific Pattern of Disadvantage**: The paper demonstrates that technological change produces a distinct pattern of employment polarization that primarily affects middle-skilled routine workers rather than the lowest-skilled, creating specific political dynamics different from other economic transformations.
2. **Status Over Material Concerns**: The political reactions of routine workers are driven more by concerns about status, dignity, and cultural recognition than by material hardship, suggesting that solutions must address these status concerns rather than focusing solely on economic compensation.
3. **Strategic Advantage of Populist Actors**: Right-wing populist parties have a strategic advantage in addressing routine workers' concerns because they can acknowledge and validate status anxieties without being constrained by policy feasibility concerns.
4. **Limits of Welfare Policy**: Traditional welfare expansion appears insufficient to reduce populist appeal, suggesting the need for more innovative approaches that address status concerns and offer a credible vision of renewed dignity for routine workers.
