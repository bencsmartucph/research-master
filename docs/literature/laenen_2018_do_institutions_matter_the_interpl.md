## Primary Theories Used

### Institutional Theory of Welfare Attitudes

**Detailed Explanation**: This theory proposes that welfare institutions shape public attitudes by structuring incentives, performance experiences, and normative frameworks. It draws from both rational and normative institutionalism. Rational institutionalism suggests that welfare institutions create structured patterns of winners and losers, thereby shaping citizens' calculations of self-interest. Normative institutionalism posits that institutions embody values and principles that socialize citizens into particular ways of thinking about welfare. The theory distinguishes between three ideal-typical welfare benefit designs: citizenship-based universal benefits (targeting all citizens regardless of contributions or need), contribution-based social insurance (targeting those who have paid into the system), and poverty-based social assistance (targeting only those in financial need). These different institutional arrangements are theorized to generate different patterns of public support because they structure different perceptions of self-interest, program performance, and deservingness criteria.

**Direct Quotes**:

> "The idea of a close connection between welfare institutions and welfare attitudes is well-entrenched and well-researched in welfare state literature." (p.4)
> 

> "Existing literature distinguishes three theoretical rationales that connect welfare institutions with welfare legitimacy... from which we have deduced the three types of mediating popular perceptions used in our theoretical model." (p.6-7)
> 

**Operationalization and Application**: In this study, the author operationalizes this theory by examining three Dutch income benefit schemes representing the three ideal types: people's pension (citizenship-based), unemployment insurance (contribution-based), and social assistance (poverty-based). The author measures public perceptions about each scheme using survey data on perceived self-interest, program performance (complexity, efficiency, effectiveness, fairness, cost), and deservingness criteria emphasis (reciprocity and need). He then tests whether perceptions align with theoretical expectations based on each scheme's institutional design.

**Relevance to My Research**: This theory directly informs my research question about how welfare institutions mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics. It suggests that universal welfare regimes might create different patterns of perceived self-interest, performance evaluations, and deservingness judgments than selective regimes, potentially explaining why economic vulnerability translates into populist politics in some contexts but not others. The mediating mechanisms identified could help explain how welfare institutions shape both status perceptions and moral frameworks.

### Welfare Deservingness Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Welfare deservingness theory proposes that public support for welfare benefits depends significantly on how citizens evaluate target groups on five key deservingness criteria: Control (responsibility for need), Attitude (gratitude/compliance), Reciprocity (contributions to society), Identity (belonging to in-group), and Need (level of necessity). Originally developed by van Oorschot, this theory suggests that different welfare target groups are evaluated differently on these criteria, explaining variations in support for policies targeting different groups. In Laenen's application, the theory is modified to examine how institutional designs emphasize different deservingness criteria, particularly reciprocity (emphasized in contribution-based schemes) and need (emphasized in poverty-based schemes). The theory proposes that when deserving criteria become more salient in public discourse about a welfare program, support for that program decreases because the conversation shifts toward judging recipients rather than assuming universal entitlement.

**Direct Quotes**:

> "The 'deservingness-related' argument builds on the theory of welfare deservingness, which suggests that people's welfare preferences are informed by five crucial deservingness criteria: control, attitude, reciprocity, identity and need." (p.8)
> 

> "The criterion of reciprocity refers to the idea that target groups with higher perceived contributions to society in the past, present or future are more deserving of welfare support in the public's eye. Need denotes the assumption that target groups with higher perceived (financial) need are judged to be more deserving of welfare support." (p.8)
> 

**Operationalization and Application**: Due to data limitations, Laenen focuses on two deservingness criteria: reciprocity and need. Reciprocity is measured by asking respondents whether they believe benefits should be higher for those who made higher contributions or have longer work histories. Need is measured by asking whether benefits should be lower for those with other income sources (partner's income or savings). The author examines whether citizens emphasize different deservingness criteria depending on the institutional design of the benefit scheme, and whether this emphasis affects support for welfare provision to the target group.

**Relevance to My Research**: This theory is highly relevant to my research on how welfare institutions shape moral frameworks that mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics. If universal welfare regimes de-emphasize deservingness considerations compared to selective regimes, this could help explain their greater resilience against populist appeals during economic downturns. The emphasis on different deservingness criteria (reciprocity vs. need) might relate to how economic vulnerability is interpreted through different moral frameworks, potentially making some welfare systems more vulnerable to populist narratives about "undeserving" beneficiaries.

### Institutional Logic of Welfare Attitudes (Larsen)

**Detailed Explanation**: This theory, developed by Christian Albrekt Larsen, argues that the institutional design of welfare programs shapes public attitudes by influencing how people perceive welfare recipients and the criteria used to evaluate them. Larsen proposes that universal programs "blur the line" between "us" (contributors) and "them" (recipients), making deservingness discussions less relevant for public opinion formation. Selective programs, in contrast, heighten distinctions between contributors and recipients, intensifying deservingness debates and potentially undermining support. The theory suggests that universal welfare states generate higher public support because they minimize public debates about deservingness by treating all citizens as potential beneficiaries, while selective programs trigger deservingness discussions by distinguishing between those who receive benefits and those who don't. Larsen particularly emphasizes how institutional design influences the perceived "line of demarcation" between the deserving and undeserving in different welfare regimes.

**Direct Quotes**:

> "According to Larsen (2006), universal policies have higher levels of public support because they supposedly blur the line between 'us' (the better-off benefactors) and 'them' (the poor beneficiaries) (Rothstein, 1998), thereby making discussions about welfare deservingness more or less irrelevant to the general public." (p.8)
> 

> "Implicit in Larsen's (2006) institutional line of reasoning is the notion that deservingness perceptions are negatively related to public support for social welfare, as more intensive public deservingness discussions are claimed to foster lower levels of welfare state legitimacy." (p.8-9)
> 

**Operationalization and Application**: Laenen tests Larsen's theory by examining whether deservingness criteria are emphasized differently across benefit schemes with different institutional designs, and whether this emphasis negatively affects public support for welfare provision. The hypothesis derived from Larsen's theory is that stronger emphasis on deservingness criteria (reciprocity or need) will reduce support for welfare provision to the target group.

**Relevance to My Research**: Larsen's theory provides a crucial theoretical link between welfare institutions and populist politics by explaining how institutional design shapes moral frameworks through which economic conditions are interpreted. If universal welfare systems suppress deservingness discussions that distinguish between "deserving" and "undeserving" welfare recipients, they might reduce vulnerability to populist appeals that often exploit such distinctions. This could help explain why some countries are more resilient to populist politics despite experiencing similar economic pressures.

## Data Source and Empirical Methodology

Laenen analyzes data from the 2006 Dutch Welfare Opinions Survey, a large-scale online survey conducted by CentERdata with 1,972 respondents (73% participation rate). The survey is particularly well-suited for this research as it includes measures of public support for three different target groups (pensioners, unemployed people, social assistance recipients) as well as perceptions of self-interest, program performance, and deservingness criteria for each corresponding benefit scheme (people's pension, unemployment insurance, social assistance).

The methodological approach unfolds in two stages. First, descriptive statistics are used to examine whether popular perceptions align with the institutional design of benefit schemes. The author compares respondents' perceived self-interest, program performance evaluations, and emphasis on deservingness criteria across the three benefits to test whether perceptions match theoretical expectations. Second, three ordinary least squares (OLS) regression models are estimated to test how these perceptions influence public support for welfare provision to the target groups. Missing values were imputed using the expectation maximization algorithm to maximize the reliability of estimates.

Dependent variables measure social legitimacy by asking respondents (on a scale from 1-10) to what extent they feel society should grant each target group the right to financial support. Independent variables include: (1) perceived self-interest, measured as the self-predicted likelihood of ever receiving the benefit; (2) perceived program performance, measured through five indicators (benefit fraud, complexity, efficiency, fairness, and societal cost); and (3) deservingness criteria, measured as emphasis on reciprocity (whether benefits should be higher for those with greater contributions) and need (whether benefits should be lower for those with other resources).

This methodology has several strengths, particularly its ability to compare perceptions across different benefit schemes within the same welfare state context, isolating the influence of institutional design from national cultural factors. However, the study's cross-sectional design limits causal inferences, and the author acknowledges difficulty in separating the effects of institutional features from target group characteristics.

## Findings and Evidence

1. **Different institutional designs generate different patterns of perceived self-interest**: Citizens' perceived self-interest (likelihood of receiving the benefit) is highest for the citizenship-based people's pension (mean=5.13 on 1-7 scale), moderate for contribution-based unemployment insurance (mean=2.70), and lowest for poverty-based social assistance (mean=2.54). This finding confirms the hypothesis that universal programs generate higher perceived self-interest due to their broader target population. For my research, this suggests that universal welfare systems might better prevent populist responses to economic vulnerability because they generate broader perceived self-interest, even among middle-class voters who might otherwise be receptive to populist appeals.
2. **Program performance perceptions vary systematically by institutional design**: Citizens perceive the universal pension system to perform better than the selective schemes across most dimensions. Benefit fraud is perceived as rare in the pension system (57.9% say it rarely occurs) but common in unemployment insurance and social assistance (45.7% and 44.7% say it often occurs). The pension system is also perceived as simpler, more efficient, and fairer than the other schemes. These differences in perceived performance help explain why universal programs maintain stronger public support. For my research, this suggests welfare regimes that emphasize universalism might maintain stronger institutional trust during economic downturns, potentially reducing vulnerability to populist appeals that typically exploit declining trust in institutions.
3. **Institutional design shapes which deservingness criteria citizens emphasize**: The reciprocity criterion is emphasized most strongly for unemployment insurance (mean=0.60 on 0-1 scale), followed by pensions (mean=0.41), and least for social assistance (mean=0.29). Conversely, the need criterion is emphasized most strongly for social assistance (mean=0.46), followed by unemployment insurance (mean=0.32), and least for pensions (mean=0.21). This pattern suggests citizens internalize the normative principles embedded in different institutional designs. For my research, this indicates welfare institutions shape the moral frameworks through which citizens interpret economic conditions, potentially influencing vulnerability to populist narratives that often emphasize deservingness considerations.
4. **Perceived self-interest positively affects support for welfare provision**: Across all three regression models, higher perceived likelihood of receiving a benefit significantly increases support for welfare provision to that benefit's target group (β=0.061 for pensioners, β=0.099 for unemployed, β=0.059 for social assistance recipients). This confirms rational institutionalist expectations. However, self-interest is not the strongest predictor in any model, suggesting non-material factors also strongly influence welfare attitudes. For my research, this suggests that economic vulnerability alone may be insufficient to explain populist support, as perceptions of institutional performance and deservingness also matter substantially.
5. **Deservingness emphasis negatively affects welfare support**: With the exception of need in the social assistance model, emphasis on deservingness criteria consistently reduces support for welfare provision. This finding supports Larsen's theory that deservingness discussions undermine welfare legitimacy. Particularly interesting is that reciprocity emphasis has a strong negative effect on support for social assistance (β=-0.156) but not pensions (β=-0.014), while need emphasis strongly reduces support for pensions (β=-0.086) but not social assistance (β=0.001). This suggests deservingness criteria have different effects depending on how target groups are evaluated on those criteria. For my research, this implies that welfare systems emphasizing universal citizenship might reduce the salience of deservingness considerations that populist parties often exploit.

## Discussion

The author interprets the findings as revealing the meaningful relationship between income benefit design, popular perceptions, and social legitimacy. He argues that the different institutional designs of the Dutch benefits are related to systematic differences in how citizens perceive them, which in turn shapes public support for welfare provision to their target groups. Citizens perceive higher self-interest in universal schemes than selective ones, view universal schemes as better performing, and emphasize different deservingness criteria depending on a scheme's institutional principles. These perceptions then influence support for welfare provision, with higher perceived self-interest and better perceived performance increasing support, while stronger emphasis on deservingness criteria generally decreases support.

The author connects these findings to existing literature on welfare institutions and attitudes, noting that they support theories from rational institutionalism (regarding self-interest), institutional performance theories (regarding program functioning), and normative institutionalism (regarding deservingness criteria). The findings particularly support Larsen's argument that universal programs suppress deservingness discussions that can undermine welfare legitimacy. However, the author acknowledges two limitations: difficulty separating the effects of institutional design from target group characteristics, and uncertainty about causal direction. While institutions likely shape attitudes for well-established policies like pensions and unemployment insurance, the cross-sectional design can only establish correlation, not causation.

These interpretations have important implications for my research on welfare institutions and populism. The finding that institutional design shapes how citizens perceive welfare programs and their target groups helps explain why similar economic vulnerabilities might produce different political responses across welfare regimes. If universal welfare systems generate higher perceived self-interest, better perceived performance, and less emphasis on deservingness distinctions, they might be more resilient against populist appeals that often exploit perceptions of undeserving recipients or ineffective institutions. The study suggests welfare institutions shape both the material incentives citizens perceive and the moral frameworks they use to interpret economic conditions, potentially mediating between economic vulnerability and populist politics.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Welfare institution design shapes perceived self-interest beyond material calculations**: The finding that citizens perceive higher self-interest in universal schemes than in targeted ones, even when controlling for objective likelihood of receipt, suggests institutions shape how citizens conceptualize their stake in welfare programs. This is crucial for understanding how welfare regimes mediate economic vulnerability and populist politics. Universal welfare systems that generate broader perceived self-interest across social classes might create cross-class coalitions that resist populist attempts to pit "deserving" against "undeserving" recipients. When middle-class citizens see themselves as potential beneficiaries rather than just contributors, they may be less susceptible to populist narratives framing welfare as benefiting only undeserving others.
2. **Perceived performance of welfare programs influences their legitimacy more than self-interest**: The regression results show that perceptions of benefit fraud, efficiency, and fairness have stronger effects on welfare support than perceived self-interest. For example, the perception of frequent benefit fraud strongly reduces support for welfare provision across all three programs (β=-0.097 to -0.243). This highlights how welfare institutions influence political responses to economic vulnerability not just through material compensation but through experiences of institutional quality. Welfare regimes that maintain strong perceived performance during economic downturns might better preserve institutional trust and legitimacy, reducing vulnerability to populist appeals that typically exploit declining trust in government institutions.
3. **Universal welfare designs suppress deservingness discussions that can undermine welfare legitimacy**: The findings show that deservingness criteria are emphasized less for universal pensions than for more selective benefits, and that emphasis on deservingness criteria generally reduces welfare support. This supports my hypothesis that universal welfare regimes create different moral frameworks than selective ones, potentially explaining their greater resilience against populist politics. By reducing debates about who deserves welfare, universal programs might prevent the moral distinctions between "deserving" and "undeserving" recipients that populist parties often exploit to mobilize support during economic downturns.
4. **The relationship between deservingness criteria and welfare support depends on how target groups are evaluated**: The finding that reciprocity emphasis strongly reduces support for social assistance but not pensions, while need emphasis reduces support for pensions but not social assistance, suggests a more complex relationship than previously theorized. This pattern suggests that deservingness criteria reduce welfare support primarily when target groups are perceived to perform poorly on those criteria (e.g., social assistance recipients on reciprocity, pensioners on need). This nuance helps explain why economic vulnerability might translate into populist politics more readily in some welfare contexts than others, depending on which deservingness criteria dominate public discourse and how target groups are evaluated on those criteria.
5. **Meso-level benefit schemes may better predict welfare attitudes than macro-level regime types**: The study demonstrates that examining specific benefit schemes within a welfare state provides more precise insights into the institutions-attitudes relationship than broad regime typologies. This methodological insight is valuable for my research on welfare institutions and populism, suggesting that focusing on specific welfare programs rather than regime types might better capture how institutions mediate economic vulnerability and political responses. Different components of welfare states might create different patterns of self-interest, performance perceptions, and moral frameworks, explaining why economic vulnerability sometimes leads to populist politics and sometimes doesn't, even within supposedly similar welfare regimes.
