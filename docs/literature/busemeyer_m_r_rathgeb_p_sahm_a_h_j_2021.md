The authors argue that voters of populist radical right parties (PRRPs) support a distinct "particularistic-authoritarian" welfare state model, which differs from the welfare preferences of both mainstream left and right party voters. This model is characterized by: (1) moderate support for social transfers but only for "deserving" recipients (like the elderly), (2) strong support for workfare policies that enforce labor market participation, and (3) remarkably low support for social investment policies (education, childcare, etc.). This particularistic-authoritarian model reflects the authoritarian values of PRRP voters, who distinguish sharply between deserving and undeserving welfare recipients based on conformity to social norms of "hard work."

### 1. Deservingness Perceptions

The most prominent social-psychological mechanism discussed is the concept of deservingness perceptions, which mediates between authoritarian values and welfare policy preferences:

> "We argue that authoritarian values translate into particularistic deservingness conceptions, which implies a preference for the punishment of out-groups that are perceived to break with the social norm to be 'hard-working' and seek paid employment. In this way, the unemployed get often associated with 'lazy free-riders' that are unwilling to work as opposed to otherwise 'hard-working citizens' that are unable to work due to old age, disability or sickness." (p. 82-83)
> 

The authors draw on Van Oorschot's (2000, 2006) work on deservingness criteria to explain why certain benefit recipients (elderly, sick) are perceived as more deserving than others (unemployed, poor).

### 2. Social Status Concerns

The authors connect support for workfare policies to status concerns among PRRP voters, who tend to be from the "losing" social classes in the knowledge economy:

> "On the contrary, the punishment of the 'lazy free-riders' may help to enhance the individual self-esteem and social status of voters who consider themselves to be in social decline as well as to free up resources to provide support for truly 'hard-working' citizens (Bornschier et al. 2012: 27; Rydgren 2012: 7). As PRRP voters are primarily composed of social classes – blue-collar workers and petite bourgeoisie – that are indeed part of the 'losers' in the new knowledge economy, their preference to exclude out-groups is likely to be more related to the desire to restore their relative social status in society (Engler and Weisstanner 2020; Gidron and Hall 2017)." (p. 83)
> 

This is a sophisticated analysis that connects material economic interests to status psychology - PRRP voters may support punitive policies toward the "undeserving" as a way to maintain their relative status position in society.

### 3. Value Conflict with Social Investment

The authors explain opposition to social investment policies by highlighting how these policies conflict with the authoritarian values of PRRP voters:

> "In line with the authoritarian values described above, PRRP voters can be expected to oppose spending on social investment. Social investment policies such as training-based ALMP or the public provision of childcare 'aim at creating, mobilising, and preserving skills' (Garritzmann et al. 2017: 36 ff.) – policies that are broadly popular with the general public, but not necessarily among supporters of the radical right, as social investment policies imply progressive gender values and a commitment to 'lifelong learning'." (p. 84)
> 

## Theoretical Framework

The theoretical contribution of this paper is substantial. The authors develop a novel concept called the "particularistic-authoritarian welfare state" that characterizes the distinct welfare preferences of PRRP voters. This model combines elements from both party politics literature on the ideological core of radical right parties (nativism, authoritarianism, and populism) with welfare state literature on deservingness perceptions.

### The Three Dimensions of Welfare State Policies

The authors conceptualize social policy preferences along three dimensions:

> "To sum up and in line with existing work (Beramendi et al. 2015; Garritzmann et al. 2017), our analysis conceptualizes social preferences along three dimensions: the extent to which individuals demand (1) more or less generous social transfers and other forms of compensatory social spending; (2) 'social investments' in the form of policies that promote human capital formation, lifelong learning and labour market participation; and (3) workfare policies that set strong negative incentives for unemployed persons to re-enter the labour market." (p. 81)
> 

This three-dimensional framework provides analytical clarity and represents an advance over more simplistic conceptualizations of welfare policy as simply "more" or "less" government. This framework allows the authors to identify the distinctive combination of preferences that characterize PRRP voters.

### The Role of Authoritarianism in Shaping Social Policy Preferences

Authoritarian values theory posits that individuals with authoritarian predispositions emphasize order, conformity, and traditional hierarchies while endorsing the use of state power to enforce social norms. In the welfare domain, this translates to particularistic notions of deservingness based on perceived adherence to dominant norms like being "hard-working." The authors build on Altemeyer's (1981) conception of right-wing authoritarianism and apply it specifically to welfare attitudes. They argue that authoritarian values lead individuals to support punitive measures for groups perceived as violating work norms (the unemployed) while supporting benefits for groups seen as deserving (the elderly, who are assumed to have contributed through work). This theory helps explain the seemingly paradoxical welfare preferences of PRRP voters, who support social transfers for some groups while endorsing strict workfare policies for others. The authors extend this theory by linking authoritarian values to opposition toward social investment, arguing that authoritarians resist policies promoting equal opportunity and progressive gender relations, which challenge traditional status hierarchies.

While acknowledging the importance of welfare chauvinism (the nativist component) and anti-elite sentiments (the populist component), the authors emphasize that authoritarianism is the defining ideological feature that shapes PRRP voters' social policy preferences:

> "We argue that authoritarian values translate into particularistic deservingness conceptions, which implies a preference for the punishment of out-groups that are perceived to break with the social norm to be 'hard-working' and seek paid employment. In this way, the unemployed get often associated with 'lazy free-riders' that are unwilling to work as opposed to otherwise 'hard-working citizens' that are unable to work due to old age, disability or sickness." (p. 82-83)
> 

This theoretical insight connects authoritarian values to substantive policy preferences through the mediating concept of "deservingness perceptions" - a key social-psychological mechanism that explains how abstract values translate into concrete policy positions.

### The Particularistic-Authoritarian Welfare State Model

The authors define their novel conceptual contribution as follows:

> "We define the policy mix described above – strong support for workfare, moderate support only for 'deserving' recipients of social transfers, and low support for social investment – as a particularistic-authoritarian type of welfare state. First, building on the welfare state literature (e.g. Beramendi et al. 2015), the particularistic dimension of this preference structure stems from narrow conceptions of welfare deservingness, which exclude the unemployed and poor from entitlements to social transfers, as they are considered to break with the social norm to be 'hard-working'." (p. 85)
> 

This integrated theoretical framework allows the authors to generate three main hypotheses about PRRP voters' preferences:

1. PRRP voters will strongly support workfare policies (Hypothesis 1)
2. PRRP voters will show moderate support for social transfers overall (Hypothesis 2a), but with significant variation across different transfer programs based on deservingness perceptions (Hypothesis 2b)
3. PRRP voters will display little support for social investment policies (Hypothesis 3)

### Theory 2: Deservingness Theory

### Detailed Explanation

Deservingness theory focuses on how welfare attitudes are shaped by perceptions of which groups deserve public support based on specific criteria. Drawing primarily on Van Oorschot's (2000, 2006) work, the authors explain that people make distinct judgments about the deservingness of different social groups, creating hierarchies of deservingness. The elderly typically rank highest in deservingness perceptions across European countries, followed by the sick and disabled, while the unemployed rank lowest. For PRRP voters, these distinctions are particularly sharp. The theory holds that deservingness judgments are based on several criteria: control (whether recipients are responsible for their need), attitude (gratefulness), reciprocity (prior contributions), identity (similarity to the perceiver), and need. The authors emphasize reciprocity in particular, arguing that PRRP voters strongly differentiate between recipients who are perceived to have contributed through prior work (pensioners) versus those who haven't (unemployed). This theory helps explain variation in social transfer support among PRRP voters across different program types.

### Direct Quotes

"As Van Oorschot (2000; 2006) shows, the elderly are typically considered most deserving of support from the welfare state across European countries, followed by disabled and sick people, whereas the unemployed are perceived as least deserving. This rank order should also hold for PRRP voters." (p. 83)

"In this way, the unemployed get often associated with 'lazy free-riders' that are unwilling to work as opposed to otherwise 'hard-working citizens' that are unable to work due to old age, disability or sickness (Van Oorschot 2006)." (p. 83)

### Theory 3: Multidimensional Welfare State Theory

### Detailed Explanation

The authors employ a multidimensional understanding of welfare states, moving beyond the traditional focus on welfare state size to distinguish between different types of welfare policies. Drawing on comparative political economy literature (Beramendi et al. 2015, Hemerijck 2018), they identify three distinct dimensions: (1) compensatory social transfers that insure against social risks after they materialize (pensions, unemployment benefits), (2) social investment policies that aim to prevent risks through human capital formation (education, childcare), and (3) workfare policies that use regulatory mechanisms to enforce labor market participation (stricter conditions for benefit receipt). This multidimensional approach allows the authors to identify the specific welfare preference profile of PRRP voters, showing how these voters might support some welfare dimensions while opposing others. This theory challenges the simplistic characterization of welfare attitudes as pro- or anti-welfare, demonstrating that individuals can hold complex, seemingly contradictory positions across different policy types.

### Direct Quotes

"A central basic function of modern welfare states is to insure against social risks such as sickness, old age, being unemployed or in need of care. Providing social insurance via compensatory social spending on pensions, unemployment schemes, social assistance and similar transfer programs constitutes the historical core of European welfare states." (p. 80-81)

"To sum up and in line with existing work (Beramendi et al. 2015; Garritzmann et al. 2017), our analysis conceptualises social preferences along three dimensions: the extent to which individuals demand (1) more or less generous social transfers and other forms of compensatory social spending; (2) 'social investments' in the form of policies that promote human capital formation, lifelong learning and labour market participation; and (3) workfare policies that set strong negative incentives for unemployed persons to re-enter the labour market." (p. 81)

## Overview of Data Source and Empirical Methodology:

### Detailed Description

The research is based on the INVEDUC (Investing in Education in Europe) survey conducted in 2014 across eight Western European countries: Sweden, Denmark, UK, Ireland, Germany, France, Spain, and Italy. This representative survey focused on public opinion regarding education and related social policies with a total sample of 8,905 respondents, of whom 439 were identified as PRRP voters. The authors specifically analyze the preferences of voters from six PRRPs: Alternative für Deutschland (Germany), Dansk Folkeparti (Denmark), UKIP (UK), Lega Nord (Italy), Sverigedemokraterna (Sweden), and Front National (France).

The survey employed a multidimensional approach to measure welfare state preferences through questions about specific policy reforms. Using principal component factor analysis, the authors identified three distinct factors corresponding to: (1) support for social transfers (primarily pensions), (2) support for social investment policies (education, childcare), and (3) support for workfare policies. They supplemented this with questions about spending preferences across seven policy areas: education, healthcare, unemployment benefits, poverty assistance, labor market programs, pensions, and family support.

The empirical methodology primarily employed multivariate regression analysis with the three policy dimensions as dependent variables. The analysis controlled for socio-demographic factors (education, income, gender, household composition, age) and included country fixed effects to account for cross-national differences. The authors also ran models with and without ideological control variables measuring economic left-right and authoritarian-libertarian values to isolate the effect of PRRP support. Multiple robustness checks were conducted, including country jackknife tests, bootstrapped standard errors, design-based standard errors, and alternative specifications controlling for social class.

### Reproducibility and Adaptability

The methodological approach is well-documented and transparent, making it relatively straightforward to reproduce. The authors provide detailed information about their measurement strategy, statistical techniques, and all control variables. They also conduct and report numerous robustness checks, strengthening confidence in their findings.

This methodology could be readily adapted for your thesis research on welfare states, status politics, and populism. The three-dimensional conceptualization of welfare preferences (transfers, workfare, social investment) offers a useful framework for understanding the multifaceted nature of welfare attitudes. The survey questions used to measure each dimension could be adapted to your specific research context.

However, there are limitations to consider. The sample size for PRRP voters (439 total, with as few as 33 in some countries) restricts country-level analysis and within-group comparisons. This might be particularly problematic for your interest in work-quality precarity versus financial precarity, as the sample isn't large enough to support fine-grained distinctions among PRRP voters. Additionally, the survey data from 2014 may not capture more recent developments in populist politics. If your research focuses on status concerns rather than purely economic factors, you might need to supplement this approach with measures more directly capturing perceptions of social status and dignity.

## Findings and Evidence:

### Summary of Key Findings

1. PRRP voters exhibit a distinct preference profile across welfare dimensions: moderate support for social transfers, strong support for workfare, and remarkably low support for social investment.
2. Unlike voters from other parties, PRRP supporters vary significantly in their support for different types of social transfers. They favor spending on pensions and healthcare (for "deserving" recipients) but oppose spending on unemployment benefits and poverty assistance (for "undeserving" recipients).
3. PRRP voters show the strongest support for workfare policies among all party voters, endorsing stricter conditions for unemployment benefit receipt.
4. PRRP voters demonstrate the lowest support for social investment policies (education, childcare, labor market programs) among all party families, differing significantly from both left and mainstream right voters.
5. Even when controlling for socioeconomic factors and ideological dispositions, PRRP support remains a significant predictor of these welfare attitudes, suggesting that partisanship has an independent effect beyond socioeconomic position and general ideology.

### Supporting Evidence

The authors provide robust statistical evidence for these findings through descriptive statistics and regression analyses. For workfare support, they show that PRRP voters have a mean factor score of 0.29, significantly higher than supporters of left parties (-0.51 for communists/socialists, -0.23 for greens) and similar to mainstream right voters. For social investment, PRRP voters score -0.42, far below all other party families, including conservative parties (-0.14).

For specific spending items, the data shows only 30% of PRRP voters support increased unemployment benefits (compared to 54% of non-PRRP voters), and 39% support increased assistance to the poor (versus 60% of non-PRRP voters). In contrast, 58% support increased pension spending and 68% support increased healthcare spending, which are much closer to the general population averages.

The authors' regression models demonstrate that even when controlling for demographic factors and ideological orientation (economic left-right and GAL-TAN values), PRRP support remains a significant predictor of opposition to social investment (coefficient: -0.22) and support for workfare (coefficient: 0.16). This suggests that PRRP support captures welfare attitudes that aren't fully explained by either socioeconomic position or general ideological orientation.

## Discussion:

### Interpretation of Findings

The authors interpret their findings as evidence that PRRP voters favor a distinct "particularistic-authoritarian" welfare state model that differs from both the traditional left-wing universalistic welfare state and the right-wing market-liberal model. This interpretation emphasizes how authoritarian values shape welfare preferences by linking benefit entitlement to conformity with social norms, particularly around work.

The authors argue that these preferences reflect deeper socio-cultural divisions beyond simple economic interests. They suggest that PRRP voters' support for punitive measures against the unemployed may enhance their own self-esteem and social status by differentiating themselves from "lazy free-riders." This interpretation frames welfare attitudes as a form of status politics rather than pure material self-interest.

The paper also interprets the strong opposition to social investment among PRRP voters as resistance to policies that challenge traditional status hierarchies and gender relations. By rejecting education and childcare expansion, PRRP voters express opposition to the progressive values these policies embody, not just the spending they require.

### Connections to Other Research

The authors connect their findings to several research traditions. They bridge literature on radical right party politics (Mudde 2007, Kitschelt 2007) with welfare state research (Esping-Andersen, Hemerijck), showing how these fields can inform each other. They also link their findings to research on status politics and relative social decline (Gidron and Hall 2017), suggesting that perceptions of declining status may activate authoritarian predispositions.

The paper challenges earlier work suggesting PRRPs have evolved into generally pro-welfare parties (Afonso 2015, Roth et al. 2018). Instead, they show that PRRP welfare support is highly selective and conditional. The findings also contribute to debates on the multidimensionality of welfare politics (Beramendi et al. 2015, Häusermann 2012), providing evidence that voters distinguish between different types of welfare policies.

For future research, the authors suggest examining cross-national variation in PRRP welfare preferences and investigating differences within PRRP electorates (e.g., between working-class and petite bourgeois supporters).

### Relevance to My Research

This paper offers several valuable insights for your research on welfare states, status politics, and populism:

First, the multidimensional welfare framework provides a useful analytical tool for understanding how status concerns shape different types of welfare preferences. By distinguishing between transfers, workfare, and social investment, you could explore how perceptions of dignity and status decline affect each dimension differently.

The finding that PRRP voters strongly support workfare while opposing benefits for "undeserving" groups suggests that status politics operates strongly through deservingness perceptions. This could help you investigate how status concerns (rather than purely material concerns) might explain certain political behaviors and attitudes toward welfare programs.

The paper also offers methodological insights by demonstrating how to measure and analyze complex, multidimensional welfare preferences. Their approach to disentangling different dimensions of welfare support could be adapted to your research, particularly if you're interested in comparing how different types of precarity (work-quality versus financial) predict welfare attitudes.

Finally, the authors' theoretical framework linking authoritarian values to particularistic welfare preferences provides a compelling explanation for the seeming contradiction in populist welfare politics: supporting some welfare programs while opposing others. This could help you develop a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between economic insecurity, status concerns, and welfare attitudes in your own research.
