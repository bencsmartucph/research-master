## Literature Review and Theoretical Framework

### Two Faces of Activation: Enabling vs. Demanding Policies

The authors delineate two distinct approaches to activation:

1. **Enabling activation**: This approach follows a social investment logic, focusing on enhancing human capital through training, skill development, and job search programs. It provides recipients with resources to develop their skills.

> "First, the enabling approach focuses on enhancing human capital and providing welfare recipients with resources to develop their skills, such as training schemes or mobility grants. This approach finds its roots in Sweden, where activation policies were introduced in the 1950s to empower workers by upskilling and re-inserting them into the labour market and society, thereby promoting full employment (Bonoli, 2010; Eichhorst et al., 2008; Fossati, 2018; Weishaupt, 2011)."
> 
1. **Demanding activation**: This approach focuses on work conditionality, requirements, and sanctions for non-compliance. It urges jobless individuals to quickly re-enter the labor market through strict monitoring and penalties.

> "Second, the demanding approach focuses on work conditionality, strict requirements on welfare recipients as well as sanctions for non-compliance (Dingeldey, 2007; Eichhorst et al., 2008). This workfare approach is based on the "work-first" idea that originated in the United States (Handler, 2004). While the enabling approach uses behavioural incentives rather than punitive measures (Dingeldey, 2007), demanding activation uses sanctions as repressive instruments (Eichhorst et al., 2008; Raffass, 2017), urging jobless people to re-enter the labour market quickly, often at the expense of job quality (Fervers, 2021)."
> 

The authors argue that the Belgian case is particularly relevant, as the country has experienced an intensification of activation measures over the past two decades:

> "In Belgium – the research site of this study – internal political and linguistic fragmentations have complicated the path towards activation (Hemerijck & van Kersbergen, 2019). Despite these difficulties, the past two decades have seen an intensification of activation measures."
> 

### Explanatory Frameworks for Activation Support

The authors draw on two main explanatory frameworks to understand the drivers of public support for demanding and enabling activation:

1. **Self-interest theory**: This framework suggests that individuals make calculations about the personal costs and benefits associated with policy implementation:

> "Self-interest theory postulates that individuals make calculations about the personal costs and benefits associated with the implementation of social policies (Kangas, 1997). Individuals are assumed to maximise their interests and benefits relative to costs, leading them to support policies that yield individual economic advantages and oppose policies that harm their interests."
> 
1. **Ideological dispositions**: This framework emphasizes that policy support is embedded in broader normative beliefs and ideological worldviews:

> "This is based on a second line of argument that stresses that policy support is embedded in a broader ideological worldview or system of moral orientations (Jaeger, 2008; Staerklé et al., 2012). According to this framework, individuals support government initiatives that align with their normative beliefs."
> 

Within the ideological framework, the authors highlight several specific dimensions:

a) **Left-right political orientation**: The authors hypothesize that right-leaning individuals will favor demanding activation, while left-leaning individuals will support enabling activation.

b) **Social justice principles**: Drawing on distributive justice theory, the authors identify three principles:

- Equality: Providing everyone with the same resources
- Equity: Linking benefits to previous contributions
- Need: Granting resources to those genuinely in need

> "Commonly, three principles of distributive justice are distinguished, organising the balance between rights and obligations in different ways: equality, equity and need (Deutsch, 1975). While equality aims to provide everyone with the same resources irrespective of additional requirements, equity links benefits to previous contributions such as taxes and work history. Need grants welfare state resources only to those genuinely in need of support, reflecting a residual conception of the welfare state."
> 

c) **Attitudes toward the unemployed**: The authors suggest that stereotypical views of the unemployed as lazy or undeserving would influence activation support.

> "Demanding activation is connected to a relatively bleak and suspicious view towards the unemployed, assuming that they must be forced into paid work. In this sense, this policy agenda subscribes to the stereotypical view that unemployed are essentially unwilling to take control of their own situation and are work-shy (Buffel & Van de Velde, 2019; Furåker & Blomsterberg, 2003; Rossetti et al., 2022; van Oorschot et al., 2017)."
> 

### Hypotheses

Based on this theoretical framework, the authors develop specific hypotheses for each type of activation:

For demanding activation:

- H1a: Being unemployed and having a lower socio-economic position will lead to less support
- H2a: Right-wing individuals will be more supportive
- H3a: Equity as a justice principle will increase support
- H4a: Negative attitudes toward the unemployed will increase support

For enabling activation:

- H1b: Being unemployed and having a lower socio-economic position will lead to more support
- H2b: Right-wing individuals will be less supportive
- H3b: Equality as a justice principle will increase support
- H4b: Negative attitudes toward the unemployed will decrease support

## Methodological Approach

### Data Source and Sample

The study uses data from the 2020 Belgian National Election Study, a two-stage random probability sample of Belgian adults eligible to vote. The survey was conducted between December 2019 and October 2020, with a temporary interruption due to the COVID-19 pandemic. The final sample included 1,659 respondents, with a response rate of 32.8%. A drop-off questionnaire was completed by 1,129 respondents (68.1%), which is the analytical sample for this study.

### Measures

The authors operationalize their key concepts as follows:

1. **Dependent Variables**:
    - **Support for demanding activation**: Measured using three Likert-type items referring to obligations for the unemployed to accept jobs, monitoring job search activities, and imposing sanctions for non-compliance.
    - **Support for enabling activation**: Measured using three items focusing on investing in upskilling the unemployed, providing job counseling, and offering financial incentives to re-enter the labor market.
2. **Social-Structural Predictors**:
    - **Education**: Categorized as lower secondary or less, higher secondary, and tertiary education.
    - **Income**: Net equivalized household income divided into four quartiles.
    - **Occupational class**: Five categories based on the Erikson-Goldthrope-Portocarero class scheme.
    - **Unemployment status**: Current employment status.
    - **Controls**: Gender and age.
3. **Ideological Predictors**:
    - **Political left-right self-placement**: Measured on a scale from 0 (very left) to 10 (very right).
    - **Social justice principles**: Measured using a modified version of the Basic Social Justice Orientations scale, with three items per principle (equality, equity, need).
    - **Attitudes toward the unemployed**: Measured with three items capturing negative stereotypes.

### Analytical Strategy

The authors employ Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) for several reasons:

1. It allows for assessment of measurement quality and accounts for measurement error.
2. It facilitates mediation analysis to examine direct and indirect effects of social-structural variables.

The models are estimated using Mplus 8.4 with Full Information Maximum Likelihood (FIML) to handle missing data. The authors use standardized parameters for continuous variables and semi-standardized parameters for categorical variables. For indirect effects, bootstrapped standard errors with 5,000 replications are reported.

## Empirical Findings

### Measuring Demanding vs. Enabling Activation

The authors first validate their two-dimensional conceptualization of activation support:

> "The CFA confirms that a two-dimensional structure, differentiating between demanding from enabling activation support, adequately accounts for the pattern of covariances between the items. The fit indices (such as RMSEA, CFI, TLI and SRMR) indicate that this two-dimensional model has a good fit to the data and is hence appropriate to describe the data."
> 

A key finding is that these two dimensions are distinct but both receive high levels of support:

> "The CFA model detects a correlation between demanding and enabling activation support that is negative but small and statistically insignificant (−0.063; p = 0.071). Respondents' stance on demanding activation is unrelated to their view on enabling activation, and vice versa. Although both types of activation policies receive rather similar levels of support, they represent two clearly distinct attitude objects."
> 

The descriptive results show strong support for both types of activation among Belgian citizens:

> "Almost 6 out of 10 Belgians agree with the statement that long-term unemployed persons should be required to accept job offers, even if it entails a decrease in salary compared to their previous employment (D36_1). Similarly, 72% of the population supports the idea of strict government controls of search activities (D36_2), while 66% endorse sanctions for unemployed individuals who fail to comply with the obligations (D36_3)."
> 

> "Interestingly, support for enabling activation measures is at least as strong as support for demanding activation. More than 70% of respondents supports higher investments in re-education opportunities (D36_4) or personal guidance (D36_5) for unemployed individuals."
> 

### Explaining Support for Demanding and Enabling Activation

The authors find markedly different ideological underpinnings for the two dimensions of activation support:

1. **Justice Principles**:
    - Support for demanding activation is strongly related to the equity principle (β = 0.467; p < 0.001), confirming H3a.
    - Support for enabling activation is linked to the equality principle (β = 0.150; p < 0.05), confirming H3b.
    - The need principle is negatively associated with demanding activation (β = -0.145; p < 0.05).
2. **Attitudes toward the Unemployed**:
    - Negative stereotypes about the unemployed are strongly and positively related to demanding activation (β = 0.454; p < 0.001), confirming H4a.
    - Negative stereotypes are negatively related to enabling activation (β = -0.179; p < 0.001), confirming H4b.
3. **Political Left-Right Placement**:
    - Contrary to expectations, political orientation is not significantly related to either dimension when other ideological factors are controlled.
    
    > "Interestingly, in contrast to prior studies (Buss, 2018; Fossati, 2018; Laenen & Meuleman, 2019), political left–right placement is not related to either dimension of activation support, thereby rejecting H2a and H2b."
    > 
4. **Self-Interest Variables**:
    - Effects of social-structural variables are weaker and mostly indirect:
    
    > "Analysing the total effects (that is, the effect of the social-structural variables without controlling for ideological dispositions), only a limited number of the variables significantly relate to activation support. Support for demanding activation is weaker among the unemployed and those with a low subjective income."
    > 
    - These effects operate primarily through ideological dispositions rather than direct self-interest:
    
    > "Notably, none of the direct effects on support for demanding activation are statistically significant. Instead, the observed effects are primarily indirect, mediated by ideological factors."
    > 
5. **Explanatory Power**:
    - The model explains a much larger portion of variance in demanding activation support (R² = 63.2%) than enabling activation support (R² = 10.5%).
    
    > "This demonstrates that demanding activation is much more transparent as a crystallised ideological project for the public at large, clearly targeting an undeserving group with stringent control and sanctions. On the other hand, enabling activation draws upon ideas from both the political left and right, rendering its ideological impetus less evident."
    > 

## Discussion and Theoretical Implications

### Divergent Roots of Activation Support

The authors interpret their findings as demonstrating clear distinctions between attitudes toward the two faces of activation:

> "First, our findings demonstrate that individuals differentiate clearly between the two policy paradigms (even if both forms receive widespread support in Belgian population). That both policies represent distinct attitude objects underscores the necessity to disentangle the roots of both types of support."
> 

The central finding is that these attitudes have markedly different ideological foundations:

> "Second, our study highlights that support for both types of activation are strongly rooted in ideological dispositions, albeit with markedly distinct underpinnings in terms of justice preferences and stereotypes about the unemployed."
> 

### Justice Principles and Activation Support

The authors draw important connections between justice principles and activation preferences:

> "Normative beliefs of distributive justice play a crucial role in shaping activation preferences. Support for enabling activation is associated with the social justice principle of equality, signifying that individuals who value equality – probably, equality of opportunity- are more inclined towards enabling policies. From this perspective, activation considers public investments in human capital as universal and unconditional rights aimed at empowering citizens (Watts & Fitzpatrick, 2018)."
> 

> "Conversely, support for demanding activation policies is strongly linked to a preference for equity, as individuals endorsing this principle are more likely to support rigorous monitoring and sanctioning of the unemployed for non-compliance with obligations, thereby rebalancing the social contract between citizens and state."
> 

The authors note an unexpected finding regarding the need principle:

> "The principle of need was, conversely, associated with less support for demanding ALMPs. Theoretically, it is equally plausible that need would promote support for work obligations as it aligns with a residual form of welfare distribution that promotes self-reliance. Our finding thus points to an alternative interpretation of need as a preoccupation with the well-being of those in need (Kittel, 2020)."
> 

### Attitudes Toward the Unemployed

The authors highlight how perceptions of the unemployed shape activation preferences:

> "Negative stereotypes about the unemployed were found to be positively correlated with support for demanding activation policies, but negatively associated with enabling activation support. Specifically, individuals who perceive the unemployed as lazy and unwilling to find a job are more likely to support strict sanctions and benefit cuts as effective means to force those with a weak work ethic to re-enter employment (Dwyer, 2000)."
> 

> "Conversely, those who attribute unemployment to a lack of individual responsibility are less inclined to endorse supportive public interventions that aim to improve employability through 'social investment'."
> 

### Self-Interest vs. Ideology

The authors emphasize that their findings challenge the assumption that self-interest directly drives policy attitudes:

> "That certain social strata show greater resistance against demanding activation is not driven by material self-interest or cost–benefit calculations but rooted in normative beliefs. For the unemployed for instance, their aversion of negative stereotyping about the unemployed plays a crucial role in shaping their opposition against demanding activation."
> 

> "Our findings indicate that these socio-economic characteristics should not be treated as univocal indicators of self-interest, but rather be seen as a part of a habitus that connects social structure with cultural dispositions."
> 

### Ideological Profiles of Activation Support

The authors argue that demanding activation has a clearer ideological profile than enabling activation:

> "Overall, our study reveals that support for demanding activation policies is firmly rooted in a specific justice conception that prioritises contributions to the common good and a quid-pro-quo welfare model, alongside discourses that stereotype the unemployed as non-reliant. The approach to demanding activation policies aligns with producerism (Abts et al., 2021), a doctrine that individualises the causes and solutions of unemployment and divides society into 'productive' and 'unproductive' groups."
> 

> "In contrast, public support for enabling activation displays a blurrier ideological profile and normative foundation, as it incorporates ideas originating from both the political left and right. Due to its ideological ambiguity, support for enabling activation can be interpreted through various heuristics, making its ideological foundations less evident to the public, but also able to attract a broader part of the public."
> 

## Policy Implications and Generalizability

The authors note that their findings have important implications for understanding the political dynamics of labor market reforms:

> "This type of information is not only interesting from a scientific perspective but is also relevant for policy makers. Public opinion cannot answer the question what approach towards activation is preferable. Yet, it does show where potential conflict lines in society are located and what type of policies are perceived as legitimate by which social groups."
> 

Regarding generalizability beyond Belgium, the authors note:

> "While it is difficult to predict whether our findings could be applied to other policy contexts, we expect similar patterns at least in other European countries where unemployment benefit system are in the centre of societal debates and politicians use the rhetoric of the 'lazy unemployed' as a strategy to bring forward strict measure against this group. It remains to be seen whether in countries with a longer tradition of active labour market policies, such as Scandinavian countries, the electorate could be divided more strongly on the two sides of activation."
> 

## Connections to Broader Literature on Welfare Attitudes and Far-Right Politics

Although the paper does not directly focus on far-right politics, its findings have implications for understanding the appeal of far-right parties, which often invoke narratives about the "undeserving" unemployed. The connections between demanding activation attitudes and negative stereotypes about the unemployed align with the producerism doctrine described by the authors:

> "The approach to demanding activation policies aligns with producerism (Abts et al., 2021), a doctrine that individualises the causes and solutions of unemployment and divides society into 'productive' and 'unproductive' groups. This focus on moral discipline implies a punitive role for the government in establishing and preserving this moral order, including the implementation of sanctions for those perceived as lacking self-responsibility and non-compliance with societal norms."
> 

This producerism aligns with narratives often employed by far-right parties to distinguish between "deserving" and "undeserving" recipients of welfare benefits. The authors cite Abts et al. (2021), which explicitly connects welfare attitudes with populist radical right politics:

> "Abts, K., Dalle Mulle, E., Van Kessel, S., & Michel, E. (2021). The welfare agenda of the populist radical right in Western Europe: Combining welfare chauvinism, producerism and populism. Swiss Political Science Review, 27(1), 21–40."
> 

The authors' findings also connect to the broader literature on welfare chauvinism, which suggests that welfare support may be conditioned on ethnic or national boundaries, a key component of far-right messaging. Their emphasis on the role of stereotyping in shaping activation preferences could help explain how negative perceptions of specific groups (including immigrants) might influence redistribution preferences.

## Methodological Strengths and Limitations

### Strengths:

1. **Measurement validity**: The paper employs confirmatory factor analysis to validate the two-dimensional structure of activation attitudes.
2. **Sophisticated modeling**: The use of SEM allows for disentangling direct and indirect effects of social-structural variables.
3. **Comprehensive theoretical framework**: The paper integrates self-interest and ideological explanations, providing a nuanced understanding of the drivers of activation attitudes.

### Limitations:

1. **Cross-sectional data**: The study cannot establish causal relationships between variables.
2. **Single-country focus**: Although the authors discuss potential generalizability, the study is limited to Belgium.
3. **Measurement of enabling activation**: One item in the enabling activation scale (financial incentives) shows a relatively weak factor loading (0.40), suggesting potential conceptual ambiguity.

## Conclusion

This paper makes several important contributions to our understanding of public attitudes toward labor market activation policies:

1. It empirically demonstrates that attitudes toward enabling and demanding activation are distinct dimensions.
2. It shows that these attitudes have different ideological underpinnings, with demanding activation related to equity and negative stereotypes about the unemployed, and enabling activation related to equality and more positive views of the unemployed.
3. It challenges the self-interest explanation of activation attitudes, showing that social-structural effects are primarily mediated by ideological dispositions.
4. It connects activation attitudes to broader discussions about justice principles and welfare state legitimacy.

The findings have implications for both scholarly understanding of welfare attitudes and practical considerations for policymakers seeking to implement and communicate about activation reforms. The paper illuminates how different segments of society might respond to different types of activation policies, providing insights into potential political coalitions and conflicts around labor market reforms.
