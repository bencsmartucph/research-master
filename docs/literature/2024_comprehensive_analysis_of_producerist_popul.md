## Theoretical Framework: Understanding Producerism and Populism

### Core Definition of Producerism

The authors define producerism as being centered on the antagonism between:

> "On the one hand, the 'productive members of society', portrayed as 'virtuous' producers and potential victims and, on the other hand, 'parasitical strata both above and below them in the social hierarchy', which include both 'economic and political elites' and 'the poor, unemployed, and those who receive social welfare'" (p. 3)
> 

This Manichean opposition between "producers" and "parasites" aligns with populism as a logic of resentment. The authors argue that producerism can be understood through Laclau's conceptualization of populism, where producerism represents a specific "content" for the general "form" of populism's empty signifiers:

> "We propose that, within the economic realm, producerism represents one specific mode of interpretation (i.e. a possible 'content') of typical populist signifiers and antagonism (i.e. the general 'form' of populism)." (p. 4)
> 

### Ideological Flexibility of Producerism

A key theoretical insight is the "malleability" of producerism, which can accommodate different ideological orientations. The authors note:

> "The principle of producerism is very malleable and may accommodate different ideologies (Abromeit, 2016). Historically, producerist ideas and principles have been found in various movements, including Republican thought, the French Revolution, the Chartists and among trade unions in the 19th century. These ideas have also been identified in socialism, communism, left-liberalism and fascist movements, as well as the progressive wing in the USA." (p. 4)
> 

This malleability makes producerism a valuable concept for understanding different manifestations of populism, from left to right on the political spectrum.

### American Exceptionalism vs. Western Convergence

The authors challenge the notion of American exceptionalism regarding producerism. While acknowledging that "producerism has played a crucial role in mainstream national narratives in the history of the USA" (p. 5), they propose that producerism has become increasingly relevant in European contexts as well.

This motivates their comparative approach:

> "In this article, we take a transatlantic perspective by proposing a cross-national examination of producerism in six countries: the USA, France, Germany, Italy, Switzerland and the UK. These countries have experienced significant levels of popularity and/or electoral support for both left- and right-wing populist parties and leaders, making them relevant cases for our comparative examination of producerism in contexts where populism is found." (p. 5)
> 

This selection also captures important variation in welfare state regimes and economic traditions, which may shape different manifestations of producerism across contexts.

## Theoretical Implications and Conclusions

### Bridging Economic and Cultural Dimensions of Populism

A key theoretical contribution is showing how producerism bridges the economic and cultural dimensions of populism. The authors argue that the concept helps understand how populist movements combine economic grievances with cultural resentments:

> "Populist grievances can be viewed as arising from an opposition between the 'true people' and 'others', rooted in producerist antagonism, as well as the expression of cultural and materialist aspects." (p. 20)
> 

This helps explain why right-wing producerists combine cultural conservatism (high welfare chauvinism and authoritarianism) with economic preferences that are more redistributive than the traditional conservative right.

### Moral Economy and Fairness

The authors connect their findings to broader debates about moral economy and fairness perceptions:

> "In producerism, wealth and social justice are not solely material entities but also depend on how individuals perceive and experience practices, objects, political causes and their interactions with others. This links producerism with the concept of 'moral economy', which has been extensively discussed in social sciences and, more recently, in political science." (p. 21)
> 

This suggests that populist movements tap into deeper normative judgments about who deserves society's resources and rewards, beyond mere material self-interest:

> "Fairness, the authors explain, stipulates that the rewards from cooperation should be principally divided according to individual contribution ( ... ) When someone violates the fairness rule for their own benefit, they are considered a 'cheater', often with commensurately severe consequences." (p. 21)
> 

### Transatlantic Convergence

The study challenges notions of American exceptionalism by showing similar patterns of producerist attitudes across Western democracies:

> "Our research shows that producerist attitudes are not exclusive to American politics, as they are shared by significant groups of citizens on both sides of the Atlantic. Such similarities are particularly remarkable given the significant political, institutional and cultural differences that exist between European countries and the USA." (p. 19)
> 

This finding suggests that common mechanisms may be driving populist attitudes across different contexts, despite institutional and cultural differences.

### Evolution of Right-Wing Populism

A particularly important finding is how right-wing producerists diverge from traditional conservatism in their economic views:

> "This is an important finding concerning the broader discussion about the economic dimension of right-wing populism and its alignment with producerist ideas. It also aligns with the literature on the relationships between social structures, social class, identities and party choices." (p. 16)
> 

The finding that right-wing producerists are critical of economic elites, contradicting the traditional notion of right-wing populism as pro-business, suggests an evolution in right-wing populist movements:

> "This is an important finding that suggests right-wing populism has grown more suspicious of corporate power and large businesses in recent years, converging with the populist left in this respect." (p. 11)
> 

## Conclusion

The authors conclude that producerism offers a valuable theoretical framework for understanding contemporary populism, especially its socio-economic dimensions:

> "Our study has shed light on how the principle of 'fairness' based on individual contribution can be empirically examined through our measure of producerism." (p. 21)
> 

They emphasize that their findings reveal producerist attitudes as a significant factor in right-wing populist voting in both European and American contexts, challenging conventional wisdom about American exceptionalism:

Finally, they call for further research to systematically examine the relationship between populism, producerist attitudes, and voting behavior across different contexts, as well as to explore cross-national similarities and differences in contemporary manifestations of producerism.

This research makes significant empirical and theoretical contributions to our understanding of populism by identifying distinctive patterns of producerist attitudes among citizens and demonstrating their relationship to populist voting behavior. By focusing on how citizens make normative judgments about who contributes to and who takes advantage of society, the study illuminates the moral underpinnings of populist movements beyond mere material interests or cultural grievances.
