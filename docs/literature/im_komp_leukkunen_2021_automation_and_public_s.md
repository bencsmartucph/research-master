## Core Argument

Routine workers facing automation risk support **workfare** (punitive conditions on benefits) not out of economic self-interest, but as a **status politics** response. They shore up their own social standing by 'maintaining their social distance' from groups perceived as lower-status — particularly the unemployed.

> "Because social status is relative, these workers can shore up their status by 'maintaining their social distance' from other groups and individuals perceived as occupying a lower status in the social order."
> 

---

## Status Threat vs. Economic Threat

### The Puzzle

Studies show vulnerable workers prefer policies which reduce their risk and oppose those which worsen it. So why would routine workers — who themselves risk unemployment — support harsh conditions on benefits?

### The Answer: Status Threat Dominates

> "Most survive in their routine jobs; only a minority become unemployed. Although their 'survival' does not yield economic duress in the form of actual unemployment, it still creates status anxiety arising from perceptions that their relative position in the social hierarchy is declining."
> 

> "Automation thus 'reshapes the employment structure and hence the relative importance and value attached to different kinds of work' (Kurer, 2020: 1804). As routine occupations slowly die out, the value of routine work also diminishes."
> 

---

## The "Kicking Down" Mechanism

When individuals worry about status decline:

1. They seek to draw sharp boundaries between themselves and lower-status groups
2. Driven by **'last place aversion'** — fear of falling into the lowest-status social group
3. They judge unemployed workers as lazy and lacking responsibility
4. This diminishes perceived welfare deservingness of the unemployed
5. Results in support for stringent conditions on unemployment benefits

> "Routine workers may seek to differentiate themselves from unemployed workers by casting the latter in an unfavourable light... Such views may diminish the extent to which routine workers consider unemployed workers as deserving of welfare."
> 

**Hypothesis**: As automation risk increases, workers support workfare more.

---

## Exclusion Experiences and Welfare Feedback

The paper also identifies how exclusion experiences trigger defensive responses:

> "Urgent problem pressure (high stakes) meets perceived misrecognition of one's needs and capacities by the state as well as a perception of double standards."
> 

Examples:

- Being forced to write numerous job applications without realistic prospects under threat of sanctions
- Being compelled to participate in training perceived as unhelpful
- Suffering pension deductions for missed contribution years despite factors beyond their control

> "Welfare recipients who experience exclusion or misrecognition often justify their own deservingness by blaming groups perceived as less deserving ('exclude them – rather than me')."
> 

---

## Key Implication

Workfare support among automation-vulnerable workers is a form of **status politics**, not material self-interest. This has profound implications for welfare state design: policies that stigmatize recipients may actually amplify support for further punitive measures rather than building solidarity.
