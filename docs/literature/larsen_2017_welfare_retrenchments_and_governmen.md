# ⭐Larsen 2017 - Welfare Retrenchments and Government Support: Evidence from a Natural Experiment: The paper investigates how welfare retrenchment policies affect government support, with a special focus on the concept of "proximity" - whether people who are directly affected by a policy change respond differently than the general public. Using a natural experiment involving a 2013 education grant reform in Denmark that coincided with European Social Survey data collection, the authors find that people proximate to the retrenched policy (students receiving education grants) punished the government significantly more than the general public. This provides causal evidence that proximity to welfare policies matters for political responses to retrenchment.

# Primary Theories Used:

## Policy Proximity Theory (Soss and Schram, 2007)

**Detailed Explanation:** The paper builds on Soss and Schram's (2007) theoretical framework that distinguishes between proximate and distant policies. According to this theory, a policy's impact on public opinion depends on how directly it affects citizens' everyday lives. Proximate policies create tangible experiences that shape political attitudes, whereas distant policies operate with less visibility and personal relevance. Larsen extends this theory by providing causal evidence through a natural experiment design, showing that proximity to a policy is not just correlated with but actually causes differential responses to policy changes. The theory posits that people with personal stakes in policies will be more informed about changes, more likely to attribute responsibility to government, and more motivated to adjust their political support accordingly when benefits are cut. This creates a feedback mechanism between personal policy experiences and political attitudes that varies significantly between affected and unaffected populations.

**Direct Quotes:** "The contribution of this article is to resolve and test an important part of this puzzle, namely, the relevance of how proximate welfare reforms are to people's everyday lives (as proposed by Soss and Schram, 2007)." (p. 41)

"In short, most studies on welfare retrenchments examine how policies have implications for the general electorate with limited information on how proximate people are to the policy being retrenched." (p. 41)

**Operationalization and Application:** Larsen operationalizes proximity through a binary measure of educational status - whether respondents were currently undergoing education at the time of the survey. This operationalization creates a clear distinction between those directly affected by the education grant reform (proximate group) and those not affected (general public). The methodological approach exploits the timing of the European Social Survey fieldwork, which coincidentally occurred during the announcement of the education reform in Denmark. This natural experiment design allows for causal inference by comparing government satisfaction between those interviewed before and after the reform announcement, and between those proximate to the policy and the general public. The author uses matching procedures to ensure comparability between treatment and control groups.

**Relevance to My Thesis:** This theory directly supports my dual pathway model by demonstrating how welfare institutions create different relationships with citizens that mediate political responses. The proximity concept helps explain why similar economic pressures produce different political outcomes across countries with different welfare regimes. My thesis argues that universal welfare systems create broader proximity effects (more citizens have personal stakes), while targeted systems create narrower proximity effects (fewer citizens have personal stakes). The Danish case represents the Nordic model with typically universal benefits, demonstrating that even in systems designed to create broad proximity, policy retrenchments affecting specific groups can still trigger targeted political responses.

## Policy Feedback Theory (Mettler and Soss, 2004; Campbell, A.L., 2012)

**Detailed Explanation:** Policy feedback theory examines how public policies, once enacted, reshape politics by altering citizens' capacities, beliefs, and orientations toward government. Larsen applies this theory to understand how welfare retrenchments affect government support through citizens' direct experiences with policies. The theory posits that policies create "interpretive effects" that frame how citizens understand their relationship with the state. These frames then influence how citizens respond to policy changes. In the context of welfare retrenchments, policy feedback theory suggests that citizens' prior relationships with welfare institutions condition their responses to cuts. The theory distinguishes between resource effects (material benefits) and interpretive effects (how policies shape citizens' understandings of their relationship with government), both of which are relevant to understanding responses to retrenchment.

**Direct Quotes:** "Policies are for some people proximate and provide tangible personal relations to political institutions (Kumlin, 2004; Bruch et al., 2010). More generally, welfare policies have feedback effects on mass publics (Mettler and Soss, 2004; Campbell, 2012; Lerman and McCabe, 2017)." (p. 42)

**Operationalization and Application:** While policy feedback theory provides a conceptual foundation, Larsen operationalizes this primarily through the natural experiment design. The author tests not only for direct proximity effects but also examines alternative types of proximity (policy socialization through education level, family solidarity through partner's educational status, and labor market proximity through employment status). These alternative operationalizations allow for testing different mechanisms through which policy feedback might occur. The author finds that only direct personal proximity significantly conditions responses to the reform, suggesting that immediate material concerns may outweigh broader interpretive effects in this context.

**Relevance to My Thesis:** This theory directly supports my status-moral pathways framework by highlighting how welfare institutions create both material and interpretive relationships with citizens. In my thesis, I argue that welfare institutions shape political responses through status stabilization (maintaining subjective status) and moral framing (promoting universalistic versus particularistic moral frameworks). Larsen's findings suggest that direct material threats (affecting status) may trigger more immediate political responses than interpretive effects (affecting moral frameworks), which may develop over longer time periods. This helps refine my understanding of how these dual pathways operate under different temporal conditions.

# Overview of Data Source and Empirical Methodology:

**Detailed Description:** The study utilizes data from the European Social Survey (ESS) Round 6, collected in Denmark between January and May 2013. The key methodological innovation is the exploitation of a natural experiment created by the coincidental timing of the ESS fieldwork with the announcement of a major education grant reform in Denmark on February 19, 2013. This allows the author to compare respondents interviewed before the reform announcement (control group) with those interviewed after (treatment group). The sample includes 1,186 respondents after preprocessing through a matching procedure.

The empirical approach is a difference-in-differences design that examines the interaction between reform exposure (pre/post announcement) and proximity to the policy (education status). The author employs OLS regression with government satisfaction (measured on a 0-10 scale) as the dependent variable. To ensure robust causal inference, the author uses a 1:1 nearest neighbor matching with replacement and caliper constraints to create comparable treatment and control groups balanced on covariates including gender, age, education level, subjective class, political interest, news consumption, and religiosity. Placebo tests using alternative satisfaction outcomes (life satisfaction, economy satisfaction, democracy satisfaction) verify that the findings are not driven by general dissatisfaction unrelated to government performance.

**Reproducibility and Adaptability:** The author provides transparent documentation of the methodological approach, including detailed supplementary materials covering the matching procedure, robustness checks, and alternative specifications. The research design could be reproduced with similar survey data that coincides with policy announcements. The matching procedure is well-documented, making the preprocessing steps reproducible.

The main adaptation needed would be to incorporate measures of both status perceptions and moral frameworks as potential mediators between welfare retrenchments and political responses.

# Findings and Evidence:

**Summary of Key Findings:** The study finds that people proximate to the education policy (students) reacted significantly more negatively to the retrenchment reform than the general public. While the reform led to an overall decrease in government satisfaction (-0.30 points on an 11-point scale), this effect was primarily driven by those directly affected by the policy. Students showed a substantially stronger negative reaction (-0.73 points) compared to non-students after the reform announcement. This effect remained robust when controlling for demographic and political characteristics. Importantly, the study found no evidence that the reform affected satisfaction with life, the economy, or democracy, confirming that the effect was specific to government evaluation.

**Supporting Evidence:** The author provides statistical evidence through regression models showing the interaction between reform exposure and education status. In the fully specified model (Table 1, Model 4), the interaction term (Reform × Education) is -0.73 with a standard error of 0.33, statistically significant at p<0.05. This effect size is substantial, comparable to the difference in government satisfaction between people with primary and tertiary education. Placebo tests (Table 2) show no significant effects on other satisfaction measures, validating that the observed effect is specifically related to government evaluation. Additional robustness checks using alternative matching procedures, time specifications, and proximity measures confirm the main findings.

**Implications for My Thesis:** These findings strongly support my argument that welfare institutions mediate political responses to economic vulnerability through status perceptions. The strong reaction from students directly affected by benefit cuts suggests that welfare retrenchments trigger status concerns among those dependent on the policy. The findings also imply that universal welfare systems (like Denmark's) can maintain political stability by minimizing retrenchments, as cuts that do occur generate significant backlash from affected groups. This supports my hypothesis that universal welfare regimes show greater political resilience by maintaining status security for broader populations. However, the study also suggests that even in universal systems, targeted retrenchments can create localized political responses, indicating that the insulating effect of universal welfare systems has limits.

# Discussion:

**Interpretation of Findings:** Larsen interprets the findings as evidence that proximity is a crucial condition for understanding public responses to welfare retrenchments. The author argues that previous mixed findings on public reactions to welfare cuts may be explained by the fact that studies often examine aggregate responses without considering how proximate different segments of the population are to the policies being cut. The author suggests that governments may strategically target retrenchments to specific groups with limited political influence, explaining why some unpopular reforms do not result in significant electoral penalties.

**Connections to Other Research:** The author connects the findings to broader literature on welfare retrenchments and electoral politics (Armingeon and Giger, 2008; Giger, 2010, 2012), policy feedback effects (Mettler and Soss, 2004; Campbell, 2012), and proximity theories (Soss and Schram, 2007). The study extends previous research by providing causal evidence rather than correlational findings and by focusing specifically on the conditioning effect of proximity. The author acknowledges limitations in generalizability, noting that the findings come from a context of austerity in a multiparty system where opposition parties largely supported the reform.

**Relevance to My Research:** The discussion provides crucial insights for my thesis by demonstrating that welfare institutions create differential relationships with citizens that shape political responses. This supports my dual pathway model, particularly the status stabilization pathway. The findings suggest that welfare retrenchments primarily affect political attitudes through direct material concerns rather than broader moral frameworks, at least in the short term. This might indicate that status threats operate on a shorter timescale than moral framing effects. The study's focus on education policy is particularly relevant to my case studies, as education represents a policy domain with broad public support but targeted beneficiaries, creating interesting dynamics for studying status-moral pathways. The Danish context provides a useful contrast to the Brexit and Trump cases I plan to examine, as it represents a universal welfare system compared to the liberal welfare regimes of the UK and US.
