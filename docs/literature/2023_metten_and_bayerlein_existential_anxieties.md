## Key Takeaways

1. **Existential anxieties predict right-wing populist voting regardless of objective impacts from globalization**:
    
    > "In our answer to this question, we move beyond previous approaches and connect globalisation and populism research to the concept of ontological insecurity. This concept defines the condition of being deeply threatened by societal transition processes that pressure an individual's identity and sense of security as well as societal stability in connection with the perceived inability of the state to shelter an individual from these transformation processes" (p. 3).
    > 
2. **The effect of existential anxieties on RWPP voting is strongest among those with moderate globalization attitudes**:
    
    > "The question, then, is how people with moderate attitudes are influenced by existential anxieties... These voters are not per se drawn towards RWPPs based on their anti-globalisation attitude but become susceptible to RWPP crises narratives if they experience existential anxieties irrespective of their globalisation attitudes" (p. 10-11).
    > 
3. **Right-wing populist parties exploit existential anxieties through crisis narratives and securitization**:
    
    > "By occupying globalisation issues and defining globalisation in and by itself as existential threats to society, culture, religion, and tradition, populists securitise globalisation processes and stress the need for quick action to halt allegedly dangerous societal changes. A need for action that is—for selfish reasons—not addressed by the 'ruling elite' of the political system, but only by the RWPP" (p. 8).
    > 
4. **Existential anxieties have both exogenous and endogenous dimensions**:
    
    > "First, an intersubjective or exogenous dimension, where individuals 'are concerned with maintaining a consistent notion of self to enhance their ontological security with other[s]'... Second, an intra-subjective or endogenous dimension, in which the state serves 'as a provider of ontological security for its citizens'" (p. 7).
    > 
5. **The study provides empirical evidence distinguishing existential anxieties from other explanatory frameworks**:
    
    > "Other than status anxiety, our mechanism works on the level of societal changes and the perception of these changes as dangerous irrespective of individual affectedness, status concerns or alienation" (p. 10).

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### 1. Ontological Security Theory / Existential Anxiety

**Detailed explanation:**
Ontological security theory, originally developed by psychiatrist R.D. Laing (1960) and later adapted by sociologist Anthony Giddens (1984, 1991), forms the conceptual foundation for the authors' notion of "existential anxiety." The theory explains how individuals require a basic sense of security about their identity and place in the world to function effectively. Giddens describes ontological security as "confidence or trust that the natural and social worlds are as they appear to be, including the basic existential parameters of self and social identity" (p. 4).

The authors adapt this concept specifically for political analysis, focusing on how globalization disrupts people's sense of security and stable identity. They articulate two key dimensions: an exogenous dimension concerning identity maintenance and an endogenous dimension concerning the state's role in providing security. The exogenous dimension relates to individuals' need to maintain consistent self-concepts, especially through social interactions, routines, and stability. The endogenous dimension pertains to the state's perceived ability or inability to provide security against threats, particularly during periods of rapid change or crisis.

What distinguishes this approach from other explanations of right-wing populist voting is its focus on subjective experiences of anxiety rather than objective impacts. As the authors note, "What is at issue here is the subjective experience of anxiety of change—be it objectively rational or irrational" (p. 6). This helps explain why people who aren't objectively harmed by globalization might still vote for anti-globalization parties.

**Direct quotes:**

> "Giddens (1991) describes this trust as an 'emotional inoculation against existential anxieties' that, when destabilised, leads to ontological insecurity. He attributes this destabilising process in particular to modernity, which tends to eliminate the sense of security usually provided by the community and traditional settings" (p. 6).
> 

> "The perceived disruption of the traditional order, the impression of chaos, and the threat of the 'other' links the concept of ontological insecurity to globalisation processes, as globalisation 'challenges simple definitions of who we are and where we come from'" (p. 6).
> 

**Important associated literature:**
The authors build upon foundational works by Laing (1960) and Giddens (1984, 1991), who established the concept in psychiatry and sociology. They also incorporate international relations applications by Huysmans (1998) and Kinnvall (2004), who introduced the concept to security studies and showed how states and national identities relate to ontological security. Recent work by Steele and Homolar (2019) and Kinnvall (2018) has begun applying these concepts to populism specifically, though the authors note these contributions were "only conceptual in nature or undertake single case studies" (p. 5) rather than providing systematic empirical testing as their paper does.

**How the authors apply the theory:**
The authors operationalize existential anxiety through a novel index (EAS) that captures both dimensions. For the exogenous dimension, they measure individuals' need for safety, attachment to traditions, and desire for state protection. For the endogenous dimension, they measure trust in political parties, politicians, and parliaments. Their empirical analysis then tests whether these anxieties predict RWPP voting, particularly controlling for various socioeconomic factors that might indicate objective vulnerability to globalization.

**Relevance to your research:**
This framework offers a valuable psychological dimension to understanding support for far-right parties beyond economic self-interest or cultural threat theories. For your research on welfare policies and redistribution preferences, it suggests that policies providing stability and security (not just economic but also identity security) might moderate support for far-right parties. You might investigate whether certain types of welfare policies (universal vs. targeted) or social investment approaches better address existential anxieties and thus potentially reduce far-right support.

### 2. Right-Wing Populist Party Appeal and Crisis Narratives

**Detailed explanation:**
The authors provide a detailed theoretical framework for understanding how right-wing populist parties (RWPPs) specifically appeal to voters with existential anxieties. Building on Mudde's (2004) definition of populism as a thin ideology that sees society divided between "the pure people" and "the corrupt elite," they explain how RWPPs combine this with a right-wing "host ideology" focused on national identity, traditional culture, and anti-cosmopolitanism.

What makes their analysis particularly powerful is their focus on the strategic communication techniques that RWPPs use to mobilize voters: crisis narratives and securitization. RWPPs construct narratives that present globalization as an existential crisis threatening national identity, culture, and security. Through securitization—the framing of issues as existential threats requiring urgent action—these parties position themselves as the only ones willing to address these threats. This appealing narrative works particularly well on those experiencing existential anxieties, even if they haven't personally suffered negative consequences from globalization.

The authors argue that this mechanism explains the "puzzle" of why people without strong anti-globalization attitudes still vote for anti-globalization parties: "people with high levels of anxiety are not per se ideologically close to the anti-globalisation policy position of RWPPs but can be mobilised to vote for these parties if RWPPs are able to fuel their existential anxieties without necessarily changing their globalisation attitudes" (p. 11).

**Direct quotes:**

> "What is important here is that these need not be objective crises but perceived crises, as 'populist actors actively perform and perpetuate a sense of crisis'. The crisis is being conjured up as a permanent condition giving rise to a direct strategy of populists to securitise" (p. 8).
> 

> "Anxiety, therefore, is what directly links ontological insecurity to anti-globalisation RWPPs, as these parties fuel both dimensions of existential anxiety. First, in the impression or articulation of disorder or chaos both induced by modernity, as well as the attempt to restore order by turning back time. Second, the perception that the state does not prevent this nor established parties are capable or willing to manage the crisis situation" (p. 8-9).
> 

**Important associated literature:**
The authors draw on Buzan et al.'s (1998) concept of securitization from international relations theory, as well as Moffitt's (2015) work on populist crisis performance. They also engage with Mudde's (2013) analysis of right-wing populist host ideology and Inglehart and Norris's (2016) work on cultural backlash and value change. Additionally, they incorporate insights from Taggart (2004) on populism's relationship with crisis and Homolar and Scholz (2019) on crisis narratives and ontological insecurity.

**How the authors apply the theory:**
The authors use this theoretical framework to explain why RWPPs are particularly effective at mobilizing voters with existential anxieties, even those without strong anti-globalization attitudes. They test this empirically through an interaction effect between their EAS measure and globalization attitudes, finding that "the effect of existential anxieties on the RWPP voting probability is larger when the anti-globalisation attitudes are moderate" (p. 18).

**Relevance to your research:**
This framework offers insights into how welfare policy messaging might counteract or inadvertently reinforce RWPP narratives. For your research, you might explore whether welfare policies that explicitly address security concerns (economic, cultural, identity) could reduce support for far-right parties by offering alternative solutions to existential anxieties. Conversely, you might consider how redistributive policies could be framed to avoid triggering securitization narratives from the right.

### 3. Globalization Attitudes and Voting Behavior

**Detailed explanation:**
The paper engages deeply with existing theories linking globalization (both economic and cultural) to right-wing populist voting. The authors acknowledge two main strands in this literature: (1) the economic losers thesis, which posits that those negatively affected economically by globalization (e.g., through job loss or wage depression) are more likely to vote for anti-globalization parties; and (2) the cultural backlash thesis, which suggests that opposition to cultural aspects of globalization (immigration, multiculturalism) drives RWPP support.

While recognizing the validity of these approaches, the authors identify a key puzzle: many RWPP voters don't show strong anti-globalization attitudes or aren't objectively harmed by globalization. This is evident in their empirical data showing significant overlap in globalization attitudes between RWPP voters and non-RWPP voters (Figure 1, p. 11).

The authors introduce existential anxieties as a mediating variable that can explain this puzzle. Building on work by Hetherington and Suhay (2011) and Vasilopoulos et al. (2018), they argue that "individuals who do not have strong authoritarian dispositions make more restrictive and aggressive policy choices due to perceived threats to their security than those individuals that already have strong authoritarian predispositions" (p. 11). Applied to globalization attitudes, this suggests that voters without strongly negative views on globalization might still vote for anti-globalization parties if they experience high levels of existential anxiety.

**Direct quotes:**

> "While the voters of RWPPs have more negative attitudes toward globalisation on average, the overlapping interquartile range in the figure also strikingly underscores that a large share of RWPP voters have moderate or positive attitudes toward globalisation" (p. 10).
> 

> "People with high levels of anxiety are not per se ideologically close to the anti-globalisation policy position of RWPPs but can be mobilised to vote for these parties if RWPPs are able to fuel their existential anxieties without necessarily changing their globalisation attitudes. In that regard the vote choice becomes not a question of attitude but of anxiety" (p. 11-12).
> 

**Important associated literature:**
The authors engage with economic globalization literature from Autor et al. (2020), Rodrik (2018), and Dippel et al. (2015), who link economic dislocation to populist voting. They also address cultural backlash theories from Inglehart and Norris (2016) and research on immigration attitudes by Becker and Fetzer (2016) and Dustmann et al. (2019). Additionally, they incorporate Hetherington and Suhay's (2011) work on threat perceptions and policy choices, as well as Vasilopoulos et al.'s (2018) research on emotional responses to threat.

**How the authors apply the theory:**
The authors test their theoretical framework by examining the interaction between existential anxieties and globalization attitudes in predicting RWPP voting. Their empirical analysis confirms that existential anxieties have the strongest effect on RWPP voting among those with moderate globalization attitudes, precisely the group that wouldn't be predicted to support these parties under conventional economic or cultural explanations.

**Relevance to your research:**
This theory has significant implications for understanding how welfare policies might affect political preferences differently across groups with varying levels of existential anxiety. For your research, you might explore whether certain welfare policies are more effective at reducing support for far-right parties among those with moderate globalization attitudes but high existential anxieties. You could also investigate whether the effectiveness of welfare policies in reducing far-right support varies depending on how they address (or fail to address) existential concerns rather than just economic needs.

### 4. Status Anxiety and Social Psychology Approaches

**Detailed explanation:**
The authors carefully distinguish their existential anxiety concept from related psychological explanations of right-wing populist voting, particularly status anxiety and authoritarianism. Status anxiety refers to concerns about declining social status and position in social hierarchies. As articulated by Gidron and Hall (2017), status anxieties involve "the fear of losing the hierarchical position in society once held due to one's statues" (p. 9). The authors contrast this with existential anxieties, which concern "maintaining self-concepts and identity security" (p. 9) rather than hierarchical position.

Similarly, they differentiate existential anxiety from authoritarianism, which involves "authoritarian aggression, submission, and conventionalism" (p. 9). While acknowledging possible overlaps, they emphasize that existential anxieties are distinct because they specifically involve the interaction between individual psychology and perceptions of societal change—what they call the "interplay between individual anxieties activated by societal circumstances" (p. 7).

This theoretical distinction is important because it allows the authors to isolate a specific psychological mechanism through which right-wing populist parties can appeal to voters who might not otherwise support their policies. Unlike status anxiety, which primarily affects those who have something to lose in terms of social position, existential anxieties can affect anyone who perceives threats to identity and security from rapid social change, regardless of their objective socioeconomic position.

**Direct quotes:**

> "Status anxieties are individual psychological preconditions that are related to a declining position in the societal hierarchy felt by the individual. In that sense, status anxieties are rooted in the fear of losing the hierarchical position in society once held due to one's statues, while existential anxieties are centred around maintaining self-concepts and identity security" (p. 9).
> 

> "The finding of Gidron and Hall (2017) who show that individuals with a low social status show a reduced likelihood of right-wing populist voting, as one has to have a moderate social status to be afraid of losing it. However, the probability of voting for left-wing populist parties could be even enhanced by having a low social status. In comparison to status anxiety, our mechanism works on the level of societal changes and the perception of these changes as dangerous irrespective of individual affectedness, status concerns or alienation" (p. 10).
> 

**Important associated literature:**
The authors engage with Gidron and Hall's (2017) influential work on status anxiety and right-wing populism, as well as Bolet's (2021) research on social status and political preferences. They also reference Altemeyer's (1981) foundational work on authoritarianism and more recent applications by Ballard-Rosa et al. (2021) and Hetherington and Suhay (2011). Additionally, they cite psychological research on threat perception by Stephan and Stephan (2000) and work on prejudice and ideology by Duckitt (Sibley and Duckitt 2013).

**How the authors apply the theory:**
The authors control for status anxiety in their empirical models to demonstrate that existential anxieties have an independent effect on RWPP voting. They also check the robustness of their findings by testing different constructions of their EAS measure and conducting various sensitivity analyses to ensure they are capturing a distinct construct.

**Relevance to your research:**
The distinction between existential anxieties and status anxieties offers a more nuanced understanding of the psychological drivers of far-right support. For your research on welfare policies, this suggests the importance of considering how policies might address different types of anxieties. Traditional redistributive policies might address economic insecurities but fail to address existential anxieties related to identity and societal change. You might explore whether certain welfare approaches (universal vs. targeted, work-oriented vs. security-oriented) differently affect existential versus status anxieties, and thus have different impacts on far-right support.
