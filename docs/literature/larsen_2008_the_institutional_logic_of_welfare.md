Larsen challenges previous explanations that struggled to establish the micro-foundations of regime differences in welfare attitudes. He argues that welfare institutions create distinctive "deservingness discussions" in different societies that mediate between welfare regime types and public support. The article proposes three key regime characteristics that influence these deservingness perceptions:

1. The degree of universalism vs. selectivism in welfare policy
2. The differences in economic resources between "the bottom" and "the majority"
3. The degree of job opportunities in the labor market

These institutional differences help explain why liberal welfare regimes (like the US) show low support for welfare policy, conservative regimes (like Germany) show moderate support, and social democratic regimes (like Sweden) show high support.

### Assumptions

- Public perceptions of the poor and unemployed are not merely reflections of self-interest, class interests, or cultural values but are influenced by institutional structures
- Citizens evaluate welfare recipients based on common "deservingness criteria" that are applied across different welfare states
- Different welfare regimes create institutional logics that frame public perceptions of welfare recipients in systematically different ways
- These perceptions, rather than direct economic self-interest, primarily drive welfare attitudes

## Primary Theories Used

### 1. Deservingness Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Deservingness theory examines how people make judgments about whether individuals or groups deserve help from society. Larsen synthesizes previous work by Feagin, Cook, De Swaan, Will, and Van Oorschot to identify five key criteria people use when making these judgments:

1. **Control**: The less control people have over their neediness, the more deserving they are perceived to be. If unemployment or poverty is seen as resulting from personal choices rather than structural factors, support decreases.
2. **Need**: The greater the level of need, the more deserving people are perceived to be. This criterion involves assessments of the severity of hardship faced by potential recipients.
3. **Identity**: The more potential recipients are seen as "one of us" rather than "them," the more deserving they are perceived to be. This criterion involves judgments about whether welfare recipients belong to the same moral community as the majority.
4. **Attitude**: The more grateful, docile, and compliant recipients are, the more deserving they are perceived to be. Recipients who appear entitled or demanding are viewed less sympathetically.
5. **Reciprocity**: The higher the level of past or future contribution to society, the more deserving people are perceived to be. Those who have "paid in" or are likely to "give back" receive more support.

Larsen emphasizes that the control criterion is particularly important, as reflected in consistent findings that the public ranks groups by deservingness, with the elderly and disabled generally seen as most deserving, followed by families with children, with the unemployed and those on social assistance perceived as least deserving.

**Direct Quotes**:

- "The literature on deservingness seems capable of explaining the pattern of public support for social policy that Coughlin (1980) found in his pioneering cross-national study. He found what he calls 'a universal dimension of support' because the ranking of the deserving groups followed the same line in all the countries included in his study." (p. 149)
- "Both Van Oorschot's empirical findings on the Dutch case and the previous studies show that the issue of control is especially important. Thus, the key to explain modest support for the unemployed is the perception that they are much more in control of their situation than the disabled, the sick, and pensioners." (p. 149)

**Operationalization and Application**: Larsen operationalizes deservingness judgments through survey questions about why poverty exists. His primary measure comes from the World Values Survey question asking "why there are people in this country who live in need," with the response option "laziness and lack of willpower" indicating a perception that the poor are in control of their situation (undeserving) versus options indicating structural causes (deserving).

He analyzes this measure across 16 countries grouped by welfare regime type, showing that perceptions of the poor as "lazy" are highest in liberal regimes (39% in the US), moderate in conservative regimes (23% in Germany), and lowest in social democratic regimes (16% in Sweden). His regression models demonstrate that these regime differences remain significant even when controlling for unemployment levels and ethnic fractionalization.

### 2. Welfare Regime Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Larsen builds on Esping-Andersen's influential typology of welfare regimes—liberal, conservative, and social democratic—but goes beyond categorization to theorize how these different institutional arrangements shape public perceptions. Rather than treating regime types as mere descriptive categories, Larsen draws out specific institutional characteristics that influence deservingness discussions.

For universal welfare states (social democratic regimes), benefits and services are provided as rights of citizenship, eliminating the need to distinguish between "deserving" and "undeserving" poor. This universalism reduces stigma, promotes social solidarity, and frames welfare as addressing "our common problems" rather than "their problems."

In selective welfare states (liberal regimes), means-testing requires identifying who truly needs help and why, leading to intense scrutiny of recipients' behavior and character. This selectivism creates sharp boundaries between taxpayers and beneficiaries, highlighting the perceived costs to the former and fostering resentment toward the latter.

Conservative regimes occupy an intermediate position, with welfare rights tied to labor market participation and social insurance principles. This creates moderate levels of deservingness discussion focused primarily on contributory rather than need-based criteria.

**Direct Quotes**:

- "Following Rothstein (1998), the first step of the argument is simply to point to the fact that a selective policy that aims to provide the needy with economic resources must determine (a) who is needy and (b) how much they need. Therefore, 'The public discussion of social policy in a selective system often becomes a question of what the well-adjusted majority should do about the less well-adjusted, in varying degrees, socially marginalized minority'" (p. 152)
- "The logic of a system dominated by universal welfare policy is in all aspects contrary to the logic within a system dominated by selective policies. In the Scandinavian systems of 'Rolls-Royce universalism,' no line needs to be drawn between the needy and the non-needy. Thus, the discussion of need and to what extent the poor and unemployed are in control of their neediness becomes more or less irrelevant." (p. 153)

**Operationalization and Application**: Larsen operationalizes welfare regime type as a categorical variable, grouping 16 countries into three clusters based on Esping-Andersen's typology:

- Liberal: United States, Canada, United Kingdom, Ireland
- Conservative: Germany, France, Belgium, Austria, Spain, Portugal, Italy
- Social Democratic: Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland, Netherlands

He uses these regime categories as independent variables in regression models predicting perceptions of the poor as "lazy." His results show that belonging to a social democratic regime reduces the share answering "laziness" by 13.4 percentage points compared to liberal regimes, while belonging to a conservative regime reduces the share by 5.0 percentage points.

### 3. Institutional Feedback Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Larsen's approach draws on institutionalist theories of policy feedback, particularly the idea that policies create their own political support through various mechanisms. This perspective sees public attitudes not as exogenous preferences that determine policies, but as endogenous responses shaped by existing institutional arrangements. As Larsen notes, welfare institutions create "self-reinforcing feedback mechanisms" that either sustain or undermine public support.

This theory challenges both rational choice approaches (which assume fixed preferences based on self-interest) and cultural explanations (which treat values as deeply rooted and unchanging). Instead, it portrays citizens as reflective actors whose perceptions and judgments are framed by institutional contexts.

The feedback processes operate through multiple channels. Universal programs create inclusive identities and obscure calculations of who benefits and who pays, generating broad constituencies of support. Generous benefits reduce social distance between recipients and the majority, fostering solidarity. And labor market institutions affect perceptions of individual agency and responsibility in ways that influence judgments about the deservingness of the unemployed.

**Direct Quotes**:

- "In Esping-Andersen's words, 'Each case will produce its own unique fabric of social solidarity' (p. 58). The idea is even more prominent in Pierson's (2001) pioneering work on 'the new politics of the welfare state.' Within his work, and in the following bulk of studies on 'the new politics,' it is claimed that the conflict between political leaders wanting to change the welfare state and a reluctant electorate is one of the most salient in contemporary Western politics." (p. 146)
- "The overall idea is that the institutional structure of the different welfare regimes influences or—using another terminology—frames the way the public perceives the poor and unemployed. Thus, the political preferences of individuals are not exogenous, as in rational choice theory, but are highly influenced by the institutional structures (e.g., March & Olsen, 1984, 1989)." (p. 148)

**Operationalization and Application**: While Larsen doesn't directly test institutional feedback effects (which would require longitudinal data), his cross-sectional analysis supports the existence of these mechanisms. He shows how institutional differences correlate with different patterns of deservingness perceptions, which in turn influence policy support.

His analysis of how different levels of inequality across regimes affect perceptions of the poor provides one clear example of institutional feedback. He notes that in social democratic regimes, where benefits allow recipients to maintain an "ordinary" lifestyle, there is greater identification between the majority and welfare recipients, reinforcing support for generous policies.

**Relevance to My Thesis**: Institutional feedback theory is central to my thesis about how welfare institutions mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics. ## Overview of Data Source and Empirical Methodology

### Detailed Description

Larsen's empirical analysis draws primarily on the 1990 World Values Survey, supplemented by references to other survey data including the International Social Survey Programme (ISSP). His main dataset covers 16 countries across the three welfare regime types, with a total of 25,679 respondents.

The core dependent variable is a measure of deservingness perceptions drawn from the WVS question about why poverty exists. Respondents were asked "why there are people in this country who live in need" and given four response options:

- "Because they have been unlucky"
- "Because of laziness and lack of willpower"
- "Because there is much injustice in our society"
- "It's an inevitable part of modern progress"

Larsen focuses on the "laziness and lack of willpower" response as an indicator that respondents view the poor as being in control of their situation and therefore less deserving of support.

His independent variables include:

1. Welfare regime type (categorical variable based on Esping-Andersen's typology)
2. Level of unemployment (standardized OECD measure)
3. Ethnic fractionalization (measure from Alesina et al. calculating the probability of two randomly selected individuals belonging to different ethnic groups)

His analytical approach consists of several components:

1. Descriptive analysis comparing perceptions across the three countries closest to the ideal welfare regime types (US, Germany, Sweden)
2. OLS regression models predicting the percentage answering "laziness and lack of willpower" based on regime type and control variables
3. Analysis of the correlation between deservingness perceptions and welfare policy support at the aggregate level
4. Supplementary analysis using individual-level data from a Danish survey to examine connections between deservingness perceptions and welfare attitudes

## Findings and Evidence

### Summary of Key Findings

1. **Deservingness perceptions follow welfare regime patterns**: Perceptions of the poor as "lazy" (undeserving) are highest in liberal regimes, moderate in conservative regimes, and lowest in social democratic regimes. In the United States, 39% explain poverty with "laziness and lack of willpower," compared to 23% in Germany and only 16% in Sweden.
2. **Regime effects remain significant with controls**: The relationship between welfare regime type and deservingness perceptions holds even after controlling for unemployment levels and ethnic heterogeneity. Belonging to a social democratic regime reduces the share answering "laziness" by 14-16.6 percentage points compared to liberal regimes.
3. **Deservingness perceptions predict welfare support**: At the aggregate level, there is a strong negative correlation (-.69) between the share viewing the poor as lazy and support for welfare policy. Countries where fewer people blame poverty on laziness show higher support for welfare policies.
4. **Individual-level evidence confirms the mechanism**: Analysis of Danish survey data shows strong connections between perceptions of control and identity among welfare recipients and support for social assistance, independent of political orientation.
5. **Unemployment affects deservingness judgments**: A 1 percentage point increase in unemployment reduces the share answering "laziness" by 0.8 percentage points, suggesting labor market conditions influence perceptions of individual control.

### Supporting Evidence

Larsen presents several types of evidence to support his arguments:

- **Descriptive statistics**: Comparison of the percentage answering "laziness" across countries shows clear regime patterns (39% in US, 23% in Germany, 16% in Sweden).
- **Regression analysis**: OLS models (Table 2, p. 161) show statistically significant effects of regime type on perceptions of the poor as lazy. Model II, which includes unemployment as a control, explains 54% of the variation in deservingness perceptions across countries.
- **Correlational evidence**: Aggregate-level correlation (-.69) between deservingness perceptions and welfare support across eight countries.
- **Supplementary individual-level analysis**: Evidence from a Danish survey showing strong connections between deservingness perceptions and welfare support at the individual level, controlling for political orientation.
- **Robustness checks**: Models controlling for ethnic fractionalization show that regime effects remain significant, challenging competing explanations based solely on ethnic diversity.

## Discussion

### Interpretation of Findings

Larsen interprets his findings as strong evidence for an institutional explanation of cross-national differences in welfare attitudes. He argues that the institutional structure of welfare regimes shapes how citizens perceive the poor and unemployed, which in turn influences support for welfare policies.

He emphasizes that these institutional effects operate through several mechanisms:

1. **Universalism vs. selectivism**: Universal welfare programs eliminate the need to distinguish between "deserving" and "undeserving" poor, reducing stigma and promoting solidarity.
2. **Economic inequality**: Generous welfare states reduce differences between the bottom and the majority, fostering social identification between them.
3. **Labor market structures**: Employment opportunities and wage-setting mechanisms influence perceptions of whether the poor and unemployed are in control of their situation.

Larsen rejects alternative explanations based solely on ethnic heterogeneity or cultural differences. He argues that his institutional account provides a more convincing explanation for cross-national variation in welfare attitudes, with stronger empirical support.

He also notes the policy implications of his findings, warning that trends toward more selective welfare policies, increased inequality, and labor market deregulation could undermine public support for welfare policy in the long run through negative institutional feedback effects.

### Connections to Other Research

Larsen positions his research within several scholarly debates:

1. **Welfare regime literature**: He extends Esping-Andersen's typology by specifying how different regime characteristics influence public perceptions and attitudes, providing micro-foundations for macro-institutional theories.
2. **New politics of the welfare state**: He engages with Pierson's work on policy feedback, offering a more specified account of how welfare institutions generate their own political support.
3. **Ethnic heterogeneity thesis**: He challenges Alesina and Glaeser's claim that ethnic diversity primarily explains low welfare support in the US, showing that institutional differences have independent effects.
4. **American exceptionalism**: He contests Lipset's cultural explanation of American attitudes, arguing that institutional structures rather than deeply rooted values explain cross-national differences.

Larsen's work also connects to the deservingness literature, bringing these insights into comparative welfare state research for the first time and showing how institutional contexts influence deservingness judgments.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Institutional structures shape deservingness perceptions**: Welfare regimes influence public attitudes not primarily through self-interest or cultural values, but by framing perceptions of whether vulnerable groups "deserve" support. This institutional effect on moral judgments provides a crucial mechanism connecting welfare regimes to political outcomes.
2. **Universal welfare programs promote social solidarity**: By eliminating the need to distinguish between "deserving" and "undeserving" poor, universal welfare programs reduce stigmatization, foster inclusive identities, and generate broader political support than selective programs. This validates my thesis that universalistic welfare regimes promote moral frameworks that reduce vulnerability to divisive populist appeals.
3. **Economic inequality affects group identity**: Generous welfare states that reduce inequality between the bottom and the majority foster greater social identification across income groups. This supports my status pathway hypothesis that welfare institutions influence political responses by shaping status relations between groups.
4. **Labor market structures affect attributions of control**: Employment opportunities and wage-setting mechanisms influence perceptions of whether economic vulnerability results from individual choices or structural constraints. This highlights how welfare institutions shape attributional processes that affect moral judgments about economic outcomes.
5. **Institutional feedback processes sustain or undermine support**: Welfare institutions create feedback effects through their impact on perceptions and identities, generating self-reinforcing cycles that either sustain or undermine public support. This dynamic perspective helps explain path dependency in welfare attitudes and potentially in vulnerability to populist politics.
