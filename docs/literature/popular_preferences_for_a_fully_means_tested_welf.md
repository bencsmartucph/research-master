# ⭐Popular preferences for a fully means-tested welfare provision model: social and cross-national divides in Europe by Gugushvili and van Oorschot investigates public opinion regarding a hypothetical policy shift toward complete means-testing of welfare benefits in Europe. Their central thesis is that support for means-testing is influenced by both self-interest and ideological factors at the individual level, as well as by welfare state characteristics at the national level. Specifically, they argue that upper and middle classes oppose means-testing because they would lose benefits, while egalitarians and those dependent on social transfers support it despite historical leftist promotion of universalism. They also contend that people in less generous and less effective welfare states (with higher poverty rates) are more likely to favor means-testing.

### Underlying Assumptions

The paper operates on several key assumptions:

1. People make rational assessments about welfare policy preferences based on self-interest and ideology
2. Institutional contexts (welfare regimes) shape public attitudes toward policy through socialization and vested interests
3. The three welfare state characteristics (degree of means-testing, generosity, and effectiveness) represent separate dimensions
4. Survey respondents can meaningfully evaluate complex hypothetical welfare system transformations
5. Cross-sectional survey data can adequately capture the relationships between individual/contextual factors and policy preferences

### Evaluation

The paper presents a clear and well-structured argument. Its strength lies in addressing a neglected area in welfare attitude research by examining preferences for means-testing rather than just general support for welfare spending. The paper is persuasive in its finding that both individual characteristics and welfare state contexts influence preferences. However, it sometimes oversimplifies the complexity of welfare attitudes, potentially overlooking how people may support different principles for different welfare domains (healthcare versus unemployment benefits, for example).

## Primary Theories

### Status-Interest Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: This theory posits that individuals' positions in social hierarchies significantly shape their policy preferences through rational self-interest calculations. People evaluate policy proposals based on anticipated gains or losses relative to their social position. In welfare contexts, this means those with higher income, education, and class status typically oppose redistributive policies benefiting others at their expense, while those with lower socioeconomic status favor such policies. The authors extend this to means-testing specifically, arguing that upper and middle classes would rationally oppose a shift to means-testing since it would remove their benefit eligibility, while those already dependent on welfare would support a system that prioritizes their needs.

**Direct Quotes**: "Provided that, as rational individuals, respondents understand these likely consequences of the hypothetical proposition, their preferences about such a profound change will most likely be influenced by their perceived interests vis-a-vis welfare provision. In other words, there will be social divides in preferences" (p. 1458).

"One common finding in welfare opinions research is that people's preferences in this regard are guided by perceived self-interest: people are more likely to express (stronger) support for policies and re-distributive principles in which they have a direct stake" (p. 1459).

**Operationalization**: The authors operationalize social position through two primary variables: social class (using Oesch's five-class schema that categorizes respondents into higher-grade service class, lower-grade service class, small-business owners, skilled workers, and unskilled workers) and primary source of income (differentiating between market sources, pensions, unemployment benefits, and other social transfers). This approach allows them to capture both occupational status and welfare dependency status.

**Relevance to My Research**: This theory aligns directly with my thesis's exploration of how different status positions influence responses to economic vulnerability. Their findings support my hypothesis that welfare systems shape political responses by stabilizing or destabilizing status perceptions. The five-class schema they use offers a more nuanced approach to operationalizing class status than my current approach, which could be adapted to better capture subjective status variations.

### Policy Feedback Effects Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: This theory, originally developed by Paul Pierson, argues that existing policy arrangements not only reflect past policy preferences but also actively shape current attitudes by socializing citizens into specific normative frameworks and creating groups with vested interests in particular provisions. In the welfare context, it suggests that different welfare state designs create their own constituencies and legitimacy. The authors apply this to explain why specific welfare state characteristics (generosity, effectiveness, and existing means-testing) might influence public support for a means-tested model. The theory suggests that citizens in generous welfare states develop expectations and norms that make radical changes less appealing, while citizens in less effective welfare states with higher poverty might be more open to system transformations.

**Direct Quotes**: "The influential 'policy feedback effects' theory posits that existing welfare arrangements not only reflect the public's previous policy preferences, but once in place they also shape current attitudes by socializing people into specific normative frames and generating groups that have vested interests in particular types of provision" (p. 1460).

"In line with this theory, our expectation is that the key features of the existing welfare state will have an effect on people's attitudes toward fully means-tested welfare provision" (p. 1460).

**Operationalization**: The authors operationalize welfare state characteristics through three country-level variables: (1) proportion of spending on means-tested benefits out of total social benefit spending (to measure existing degree of means-testing), (2) net social spending as a proportion of GDP (to measure welfare generosity), and (3) risk of poverty (percentage of households with income less than 60% of median income, to measure welfare state effectiveness).

**Relevance to My Research**: This theory directly supports my project's dual pathway model of how welfare institutions mediate economic vulnerability and political responses. Their finding that welfare generosity and effectiveness (but not degree of means-testing) influence attitudes helps refine my hypothesis about institutional buffering. Their approach of separating institutional characteristics rather than using regime typologies offers a more nuanced method for testing how institutional features shape moral frameworks.

### Egalitarian Values Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: This theory addresses how ideological preferences for equality and redistribution influence welfare policy attitudes independently of self-interest factors. It suggests that individuals with more egalitarian worldviews tend to support welfare policies that redistribute resources more aggressively toward disadvantaged groups, even when this conflicts with simple self-interest or traditional approaches. The authors posit that despite historical leftist support for universalism, egalitarians might actually prefer means-testing because it more directly targets resources to those with greatest need and creates more vertical redistribution. This represents a tension between procedural equality (treating everyone the same) and substantive equality (ensuring equal outcomes, which may require unequal treatment).

**Direct Quotes**: "At the same time, we also know from literature that individuals are rarely guided only by the pure self-interest that a 'homo economicus' paradigm would imply... Instead, their preferences are also influenced by their general worldviews, such as notions about social justice and political ideology" (p. 1459).

"We find that... people who are more egalitarian are more likely to support the means-tested provision, even though to some extent it runs counter to the idea of equal treatment of everyone. However, this is not particularly surprising, as positive discrimination is often perceived as essential for tackling deeply-entrenched disadvantages, whether racial, gender or social" (p. 1466).

**Operationalization**: The authors operationalize egalitarianism through a single survey item asking whether the government should take measures to reduce differences in income levels, with responses ranging from "agree strongly" to "disagree strongly."

**Relevance to My Research**: This finding challenges my presumption that universalistic moral frames typically promote political stability. Instead, it suggests that egalitarian moral frameworks might sometimes favor targeted approaches that more aggressively address inequality. This helps refine my moral framing pathway hypothesis by introducing important nuance around how universalism operates in practice versus theory.

## Methodology Analysis

### Data Sources & Methods

The study utilizes data from the eighth round of the European Social Survey (ESS8) conducted in 2016, covering 21 European countries with nationally representative samples (total n=39,400 respondents). 

<aside>
💡

- The dependent variable measures support for a fully means-tested welfare model through the question: "Would you be against or in favor of the government providing social benefits and services only for people with the lowest incomes, while people with middle and higher incomes are responsible for themselves?"
</aside>

This was dichotomized for analysis (0=against, 1=in favor). Independent variables include individual-level factors (social class, primary income source, gender, egalitarianism) and country-level characteristics (proportion of means-tested benefits, total social spending as % of GDP, and poverty rates). The authors employ multilevel random intercept logistic regression models, first with individual-level predictors only, then exploring bivariate correlations between country-level support and contextual variables, and finally combining individual and country-level predictors.

### Reproducibility Assessment

The methodology is described with sufficient transparency to allow reproduction. The authors clearly identify data sources, variable operationalizations, and analytical approaches. The ESS8 is publicly accessible, as are the Eurostat indicators used for country-level variables. Their multilevel modeling approach appropriately addresses the nested structure of the data. The dichotomization of the dependent variable represents a potential limitation, though they conducted robustness checks with the original ordinal scale. Overall, their approach could be adapted to my research context, particularly if examining cross-national differences in populist attitudes.

### Methodological Insights

Several methodological elements could inform my research:

1. Their multilevel approach effectively separates individual-level factors from institutional influences, which would be valuable for testing my dual pathway model
2. Their use of specific welfare state characteristics rather than regime typologies offers a more nuanced way to test institutional effects than the typical Nordic/Anglo-Saxon/Continental categorizations
3. Their bivariate correlations before multilevel modeling help visualize country-level patterns before controlling for composition effects
4. The dichotomization approach simplifies interpretation but requires robustness checks with original scales

For my research, I would need to modify their approach by integrating status perceptions as mediating variables between economic vulnerability and political responses, something not directly measured in their study.

## Findings & Evidence

### Key Results

The study presents several significant findings with supporting evidence:

1. Overall, 45% of Europeans supported the idea of a fully means-tested welfare state, with highest support in Italy (65%), Hungary (62%), and Lithuania (61%), and lowest in the Netherlands (22%), Norway (30%), and Belgium (30%)
2. Social class significantly predicted preferences: compared to the upper service class, unskilled workers had 54% higher odds of supporting means-testing (OR=1.539, p<0.01)
3. Primary income source showed strong effects: compared to those with market incomes, pension recipients (OR=1.531, p<0.01), unemployment assistance recipients (OR=1.406, p<0.01), and other social transfer recipients (OR=1.588, p<0.01) had higher odds of supporting means-testing
4. Egalitarian attitudes positively predicted support for means-testing (OR=0.868, p<0.01 for the reversed scale where lower values indicate stronger egalitarianism)
5. At the country level, total social spending as percentage of GDP was negatively associated with support for means-testing (OR=0.965, p<0.05), while poverty rate was positively associated (OR=1.053, p<0.05)
6. Contrary to expectations, the existing proportion of means-tested benefits had no significant effect on support for a fully means-tested model (OR=0.997, ns)

### Quality of Evidence

The evidence is relatively strong given the large, representative samples and appropriate statistical methods. The multilevel models appropriately account for the nested data structure. The authors test for robustness using both dichotomized and original ordinal dependent variables. However, several limitations exist: the cross-sectional design prevents causal claims, the operationalization of egalitarianism relies on a single survey item, and respondents' understanding of the hypothetical scenario likely varies across countries, potentially introducing measurement error.

### Implications for My Work

These findings offer several implications for my research:

1. The strong social class gradient in support for means-testing supports my hypothesis that welfare institutions shape political responses through status perceptions
2. The finding that egalitarians favor means-testing despite theoretical arguments for universalism suggests my moral framing pathway may need refinement
3. The significant effect of welfare state generosity supports my institutional buffering theory about how resource availability shapes responses to economic vulnerability
4. The positive association between poverty rates and support for means-testing suggests economic context affects how moral frameworks develop

## Discussion & Integration

### Authors' Interpretation

The authors interpret their findings as supporting their hypotheses about self-interest and ideological motivations driving preferences for means-testing. They emphasize the "mature/immature" welfare state distinction as potentially more important than traditional regime typologies for understanding cross-national variation. They suggest their findings have practical implications for policymakers, noting the paradox that right-wing politicians who typically advocate means-testing face opposition from their core upper-class constituency, while left-wing politicians might find support for "targeting within universalism" among their egalitarian base.

### Literature Connections

The paper connects to several key debates in welfare state literature. It challenges Korpi and Palme's (1998) "paradox of redistribution" thesis by showing that egalitarians may actually prefer means-testing despite its theoretical limitations for building political coalitions. It builds on Arts and Gelissen's (2001) work on redistributive justice principles and welfare preferences. The authors identify a need for more research on the mature/immature welfare state dichotomy as an alternative to regime typologies for comparative welfare attitude research.
