## Key Takeaways

1. **Threat-induced anger is not a universal driver of populist attitudes and declining political support**: "We argue that threat-induced anger is not a universal driver of populist attitudes and waning political support. Instead, we hypothesize that anger erodes democracy-relevant political attitudes only when a threat affects citizens' norms and only when blame for said threat is attributed to the domestic political system."
2. **Two key threat characteristics determine anger's political effects**: "We argue that the extent to which threat-induced anger promotes populist attitudes and erodes political support depends on two key characteristics of threats: first, the threat has to be perceived as noxious, that is, must violate norms and upset social order. Second, and more importantly, (parts of) the political system must be seen as responsible for the threat and its attendant circumstances."
3. **Responsibility attribution is crucial for political support erosion**: "Evidently, among angry respondents, only threats for which the public can hold the domestic political system responsible undermine political support, no matter how diffuse or specific."
4. **Quality of political response matters more than the threat itself**: "Even angry citizens overall retain the ability to differentiate whom they hold accountable, such that it is not necessarily the quality of the threat but the quality of the political response that determines the future of democracy."
5. **Effects can be substantial**: "The effects are sizable. For instance, increasing anger from lowest to highest when induced by the economic crisis treatment is associated with a roughly 25 percent increase in the pool of antidemocrats (sample mean of respondents for which democracy is not always the preferred rule of government is around 20 percent)."

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### 1. Emotional Responses to Threats and Political Behavior

### Explanation:

This theoretical framework examines how emotions, particularly anger and fear, shape human responses to existential threats. From an evolutionary perspective, anger and fear have been crucial for survival - fear triggers flight and safety-seeking behaviors, while anger prompts punitive actions against perceived perpetrators. Although threats have evolved over time, these primal emotional responses remain consistent and significantly influence political attitudes and behaviors.

The authors emphasize that anger is particularly relevant when threats challenge fundamental social norms. When threats violate deeply held convictions about freedom, security, justice, or welfare, people's "disposition system"—described as a "neural watchdog"—becomes alert, triggering anger. The strength of anger corresponds to the perceived degree of norm violation.

Importantly, "subjective evaluations and interpretations of threats, rather than the objective situation, shape whether anger is felt and to whom this emotional response is directed." This subjectivity means that emotional responses can vary based on individual and contextual differences.

### Key literature:

The authors build on established research in this area, citing Marcus et al. (2019), Lerner et al. (2015), and Albertson and Gadarian (2015), which examine the relationship between threat-induced emotions and political behavior. This literature has demonstrated that angry responses to threats like terrorism, economic downturns, and pandemics significantly impact political attitudes.

### Application in the paper:

The authors apply this theoretical framework by experimentally testing how different threats (Ukraine war, COVID-19 pandemic, Monkeypox, economic crisis) trigger anger and subsequently affect political attitudes. They empirically demonstrate that not all threats induce the same level of anger, and not all anger has the same political consequences.

### Relevance to your research:

This framework offers valuable insights for understanding how emotions might mediate the relationship between economic/welfare threats and support for far-right parties. The subjectivity of threat perception could explain why the same economic conditions might drive some individuals toward far-right parties while others remain unaffected. You might incorporate this by examining how different welfare or economic threats are perceived by different demographic groups and how these perceptions trigger emotional responses that influence political preferences.

### 2. Noxiousness of Threats

### Explanation:

The authors introduce "noxiousness" as a key characteristic of threats that determines their impact on political attitudes. Noxiousness refers to the extent to which a threat endangers or violates norms considered fundamental to the social order. Not every negative event perceived as a threat will provoke the same level of anger; rather, "the extent to which norms of the social order are endangered determines the level of anger affected citizens will feel." The authors argue that "the extent to which a threat and its surrounding circumstances are perceived as noxious, that is, norm violating, determines the anger-induced action tendency deemed appropriate by the threatened individual."

This concept helps explain why some threats generate stronger emotional and political responses than others. Threats that deeply challenge social norms about freedom, security, welfare, and justice are more likely to provoke intense anger and subsequent political reactions.

### Key literature:

The concept draws on work by Lerner et al. (2015), Marcus et al. (2019), and Brader and Cikanek (2019), who examine how threats to core societal values trigger emotional responses that influence political behavior. The authors note that while previous research has linked threat-based anger to specific threats, there's been limited investigation of how threats with varying degrees of noxiousness might have different political consequences.

### Application in the paper:

The authors operationalize noxiousness in their experimental design by using threats that vary in their perceived violation of social norms. The war in Ukraine and economic crisis represent highly noxious threats, while the COVID-19 pandemic and Monkeypox outbreak are considered less noxious in the German context at the time of the study. Their findings confirm that more noxious threats generally trigger higher levels of anger, though this alone doesn't necessarily translate to changes in political attitudes.

### Relevance to your research:

This concept of noxiousness can be particularly useful for your research on welfare policies and far-right support. Different types of welfare reforms or economic policies might be perceived as more or less "noxious" depending on how they challenge core social norms and values. For instance, policies that appear to threaten economic security or traditional social arrangements might be perceived as highly noxious by certain groups, potentially triggering stronger emotional responses that drive support for far-right alternatives. Incorporating a measure of perceived noxiousness might help explain variation in political responses to similar economic conditions.

### 3. Responsibility Attribution Framework

### Explanation:

The responsibility attribution framework is perhaps the most crucial theoretical contribution of this paper. The authors emphasize that "the typical episode of anger involves an attribution of responsibility." As a "specifically moral emotion," anger is connected to "the condemnation of the alleged perpetrator(s) of a threat-induced norm violation and to punitive attitudes and behaviors toward them."

This framework posits that citizens' anger will only be directed toward the political system when they believe that system is responsible for either causing the threat or failing to adequately address it. The authors argue that "the anger-based action tendencies of ostracism and forceful removal of obstacles are often targeted at the political system" when "the nation-state, its agencies, and its actors are punished if they can be held plausibly responsible for the development of a threat and/or for not defusing the threatening situation, meaning the established domestic political system is perceived to be ignoring the people's justice, welfare, or freedom."

The framework suggests that even intense anger about a serious threat may not erode political support if citizens don't attribute responsibility to their political system.

### Key literature:

This framework builds on research by Steenbergen and Ellis (2006), Sell, Tooby, and Cosmides (2009), Petersen (2010), and Webster (2020), who explore how responsibility attribution shapes emotional responses and political behavior. It also connects to work on the principal-agent relationship in politics (Wagner, 2014) and how perceived mismanagement of threats can damage this relationship.

### Application in the paper:

The authors experimentally test this framework by using threats that vary in the degree to which the German political system could plausibly be held responsible. Their findings strongly support the importance of responsibility attribution: "Evidently, among angry respondents, only threats for which the public can hold the domestic political system responsible undermine political support, no matter how diffuse or specific. From an angry respondent's perspective, the Russian aggression on Ukraine and the Monkeypox episode can hardly be blamed on the German political system, while the raging COVID-19 pandemic and the looming economic crisis can."

### Relevance to your research:

The responsibility attribution framework offers a powerful lens for understanding why economic hardship or welfare state changes might drive support for far-right parties in some contexts but not others. Your research could examine how citizens attribute responsibility for economic challenges - whether to domestic politics, globalization, immigration, or other factors - and how these attributions determine whether economic discontent translates into far-right support. This might help explain cross-national variations in the relationship between economic conditions and far-right voting.

### 4. Political Support Framework (Norris, 2017)

### Explanation:

The authors employ Norris's (2017) conceptualization of political support, which distinguishes between different layers of support "ranging from specific to diffuse components (evaluation of incumbent political officeholders [most specific], confidence in regime institutions, regime performance evaluations, approval of core regime values, and national identities [most diffuse])." This multi-dimensional framework allows the authors to examine whether threat-induced anger affects all aspects of political support equally or whether it has differentiated effects across these dimensions.

This framework is particularly valuable for understanding democratic stability because it recognizes that citizens can withdraw support from specific politicians or institutions while maintaining support for core democratic values, or vice versa. The multi-layered nature of political support means that threats to democracy can manifest in different ways and at different levels.

### Key literature:

The primary source for this framework is Norris's (2017) "The Conceptual Framework of Political Support" in the Handbook on Political Trust. The framework has been widely applied in studies of democratic legitimacy and stability, including work by Armingeon and Guthmann (2014) and Van Erkel and Van Der Meer (2016), which examine how economic crises affect political support.

### Application in the paper:

The authors operationalize this framework in their survey experiment by measuring different dimensions of political support, from specific evaluations of politicians to diffuse support for democracy as a system. This allows them to assess whether threat-induced anger has different effects on different aspects of political support. Interestingly, they find that when responsibility is attributed to the political system, anger undermines "political support, no matter how diffuse or specific." This suggests a potentially concerning spillover effect where dissatisfaction with specific political responses can erode broader support for democratic institutions and values.

### Relevance to your research:

Norris's multi-dimensional framework of political support could be valuable for your research on far-right parties and redistribution preferences. It would allow you to distinguish between opposition to specific welfare policies or politicians and broader challenges to the democratic system. You might examine whether economic threats and social investment policies affect different dimensions of political support, and whether far-right parties strategically target specific dimensions of support in their messaging. This could help explain why some economic or welfare challenges translate into anti-system politics while others remain within conventional political debates.
