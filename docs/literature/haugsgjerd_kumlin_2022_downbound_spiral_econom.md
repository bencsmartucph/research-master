## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Primary Theories Used

### 1. Performance Theory of Political Trust

**Explanation:** The authors build on a foundational theory that citizens' trust in political institutions depends significantly on their evaluations of government performance. This theory, dating back to David Easton's work in the 1970s, proposes that trust is generated and sustained through citizens' satisfaction with government outputs and outcomes. While much previous research focused on macroeconomic performance, the authors extend this to include welfare state performance.

**Key Quote:**

> "Already foundational work on political trust considered the possible importance of political and economic performance. David Easton (1975:449), for example, argued that the 'evaluation of outputs and performance may help to generate, and probably at all times will help to sustain, confidence in authorities.'"
> 

**Operationalization:** The authors operationalize government performance through multiple measures:

1. Satisfaction with schools and health care services
2. Overall welfare satisfaction across 13 different public services
3. Perceived living standards of vulnerable groups
4. Procedural fairness in service delivery
5. Multiple measures of economic grievances (unemployment, income, economic risk perceptions)

### 2. The Political Relevance of Economic Grievances Theory

**Explanation:** This theoretical framework posits that individual economic grievances, risks, and worries impact political attitudes. The authors incorporate Hacker's insight that economic insecurity includes multiple, intersecting risks beyond just employment concerns, extending to family, wealth, and health changes.

**Key Quotes:**

> "While much older research has been skeptical about the explanatory power of economic self-interest (e.g. Sears and Funk 1991), such explanations have come to the forefront of political behavior research in the wake of the Great Recession."
> 

> "As Hacker et al (2013:44) argues, 'though job loss looms large in people's economic thinking … insecurity is a reflection of multiple, intersecting risks. These extend beyond employment risks to include major economic losses driven by family, wealth and health changes.'"
> 

### 3. Political Trust as a Heuristic Theory

**Explanation:** This innovative theoretical component explores how political trust functions as a cognitive heuristic that shapes citizens' processing of political information. Unlike party identification or ideology, which have substantive policy content, this theory examines if generalized trust in political institutions serves as a cognitive lens through which citizens interpret specific performance aspects.

### 4. Welfare State Development and Performance Theory

**Explanation:** The authors incorporate theories about the changing nature of welfare states, including concepts like "dualization," "recalibration," and "social investment." This framework recognizes that mature welfare states struggle to finance previous commitments, resulting in benefit deterioration, increased inequalities, and bifurcation between insiders and outsiders.

## Findings & Discussion

### Key Findings

### 1. Reciprocal Relationship Between Welfare State Evaluations and Political Trust

Cross-lagged panel models showed that initial levels of welfare state evaluations affect changes in political trust, and initial levels of political trust affect subsequent welfare state evaluations.

### 2. Stronger Effects for Welfare State Satisfaction than Economic Grievances

Fixed effects models showed that three out of four welfare state satisfaction measures had significant positive effects on political trust, while economic indicators had weaker, inconsistent effects.

### 3. Economic Risks Beyond the Labor Market Matter for Political Trust

Within the economic risk measures, perceived risk of divorce showed a significant negative effect on political trust, while unemployment effects were non-significant.

### 4. Political Trust Functions as a Heuristic for Economic Risk Perceptions

Cross-lagged models showed that initial political trust levels affected subsequent economic risk perceptions, but not objective economic circumstances like income or employment status.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Reciprocal Causal Process:** The relationship between welfare state performance evaluations and political trust is bidirectional, creating potential for positive or negative spirals.
2. **Broader Performance Measures:** Welfare state evaluations should be conceptualized and measured broadly, encompassing various services, procedural fairness, and perceived living standards of vulnerable groups.
3. **Economic Risks Beyond Employment:** Economic insecurity affecting political attitudes should be conceptualized beyond labor market risks to include family, health, and financial risks.
4. **Negative Bias in Performance Information:** Citizens appear more likely to receive and react to negative welfare state performance information, contributing to downward spirals.
5. **Trust as a Heuristic:** Political trust serves as a cognitive lens through which citizens interpret both welfare state performance and their personal economic situation.
