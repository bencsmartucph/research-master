## Introduction and Overview

This  published in the European Journal of Political Research examines the electoral consequences of coal industry phase-outs in Germany, with particular attention to how such structural economic changes influence voting behavior even when compensation mechanisms and interest group representation are present. The research makes an important contribution to our understanding of how green transition policies can trigger political backlash despite efforts to mitigate economic impacts.

The author frames the research within a "least-likely case" design, arguing that Germany represents a context where electoral backlash to coal closures should be minimal due to two key factors: (1) generous compensation schemes for affected workers and regions, and (2) a corporatist system of interest group intermediation with strong involvement of coal stakeholders in policy-making processes.

As Stutzmann states in the introduction:

> "Past research has often attributed electoral backlash to structural economic change to a lack of compensation and interest group representation for affected groups. Is that backlash then mitigated in contexts where both of these conditions are fulfilled? I argue that perceived economic deprivation fuelling political disengagement as well as disappointment with the issue-owning party are important factors contributing to such a backlash." (p. 1)
> 

The paper finds that despite these mitigating circumstances, coal closures between 2007 and 2022 resulted in what the author terms an "asymmetric backlash" - specifically:

> "I find that these closures resulted in an asymmetric backlash in the form of lower vote shares for the issue owner, the Social Democratic Party and higher abstention rates in affected municipalities." (p. 1)
> 

This research is particularly significant because it challenges prevailing assumptions in the literature that compensation and stakeholder involvement are sufficient to prevent political backlash to structural economic change. Instead, the author highlights the importance of perceived relative deprivation and disappointment with traditional political allies as crucial mechanisms driving electoral consequences.

## Theoretical Framework

### Literature Review and Theoretical Positioning

Stutzmann situates her research within multiple literatures: (1) research on structural economic change and political backlash, (2) studies on economic grievances and political behavior, and (3) work on just transitions in the context of climate policy. The paper notes that while substantial research has examined how losers of globalization, deindustrialization, and technological change often turn to populist parties, less attention has been paid to the efficacy of compensation measures, particularly in the context of green transitions.

The author reviews previous research on this topic:

> "Despite its certain relevance over the upcoming decades, the green transition as a source of structural economic transformation in advanced capitalist economies has only received limited scholarly attention so far. A first cluster of contributions has examined the link between climate policy outcomes, such as wind turbine construction, and electoral behaviour in different countries, with mixed findings to date (Otteni & Weisskircher, 2022; Stokes, 2015; Urpelainen & Zhang, 2022)." (p. 3)
> 

Additionally, the author addresses a gap in the literature regarding compensation effectiveness:

> "However, whether the presence and the design of compensation measures for groups negatively affected by structural economic change have the potential to attenuate possible electoral backlash remains a somewhat open question." (p. 3)
> 

### Theoretical Expectations and Mechanisms

The theoretical framework developed in the paper revolves around three main hypotheses, each connected to distinct causal mechanisms:

1. **Hypothesis 1**: Coal closures increase vote shares for the far-right Alternative für Deutschland (AfD) party due to economic grievances and status anxiety among affected communities.
2. **Hypothesis 2**: Coal closures increase abstention rates in affected municipalities due to political disengagement stemming from economic grievances.
3. **Hypothesis 3**: Coal closures decrease vote shares for the Social Democratic Party (SPD) due to disappointment with the issue-owning party's inability to prevent the policy.

The paper elaborates particularly on the social psychological mechanisms through which economic change manifests in political behavior:

> "Past research has linked relative economic grievances to support for populist and extremist parties (Burgoon et al., 2019; Engler & Weisstanner, 2021). This is corroborated by Gidron & Hall (2020), who show that feelings of social marginalisation are associated with greater support for radical parties. In addition, there is a growing literature focussing on place-based explanations of support for the far right, such as declining regions, urban development and local rent levels, which also centre around the theme of relative local deprivation (Bolet, 2021; Held & Patana, 2023; Patana, 2022)." (p. 4)
> 

The paper also highlights the role of social status and identity in shaping responses to coal closures:

> "Over the course of many years, coal workers became endowed with high social status and a distinct community identity would form in related areas (Carley et al., 2018; Diluiso et al., 2021; Mayer, 2022). This was especially the case in Germany, where the historical strength of the coal industry can be seen as the starting point for European integration after the Second World War and the foundation of the country's current prosperity (Herpich et al., 2018). The loss of this social status induced by coal closures is expected, then, to contribute to feelings of status decline." (p. 4)
> 

Regarding abstention, the author draws on literature connecting economic disadvantage to political disengagement:

> "Another channel through which one's perceived structural economic disadvantage relative to other people's has been shown to affect voting behaviour is by undermining political participation, in particular among those from disadvantaged social groups (Kurer et al., 2019; Schwander et al., 2020; Solt, 2008). This has indeed been found to be the case in Germany (Schäfer & Schwander, 2019; Schäfer et al., 2016)." (p. 5)
> 

For the third hypothesis regarding decreased SPD support, the author highlights the concept of "issue ownership" and historical party-constituency relationships:

> "In line with the conceptualisation of an 'issue owner' as the party associated with a specific topic (Budge, 2015; Budge & Farlie, 1983), the SPD can be regarded as the party which had always represented the interests of coal workers. Not only were many of the latter party members, but also the influential trade union IG BCE, which represented coal workers, had close ties to the SPD, allowing these individuals to wield considerable political influence (Renn & Marshall, 2016)." (p. 5)
> 

## Historical and Policy Context

### The German Coal Phase-Out Process

The paper provides a comprehensive overview of the German coal industry's decline and the 2007 phase-out law, which forms the policy context for the study. This section is crucial for understanding why Germany represents a "least-likely case" for electoral backlash.

Stutzmann explains:

> "The German coal industry experienced a long and gradual decline in employment numbers, which started in the 1950s. Originally, this was mainly for economic reasons as domestic hard coal became less competitive compared to imported hard coal and other forms of energy generation (Herpich et al., 2018). However, extensive subsidies from the national government kept the uneconomical German coal sector alive (Brauers et al., 2020)." (p. 6)
> 

The 2007 phase-out law represented a turning point:

> "The year 2007, however, would arguably mark the point at which environmental concerns first came to the fore as a contributing factor to the decline of the German coal industry." (p. 6)
> 

### Compensation and Labor Market Policy

The author details the generous compensation offered to affected workers:

> "In terms of compensation and labour market policy, the agreement included the guarantee that every coal-mine employee aged 37 (for those working below ground) or 42 (for those working above) or older was protected against unemployment via an early-retirement programme. The cost of the latter to the government would be approximately 2 billion euros (IG Metall, 2021; Oei et al., 2020). Representing 80 per cent of the worker's last net salary, these early-retirement benefits were thus higher than standard German unemployment benefits and more generous than the compensation paid to coal-mine workers in other European countries." (p. 7)
> 

This section also highlights the corporatist elements of German interest representation that should theoretically mitigate backlash:

> "Due to a specific law promulgated in 1952, German companies in the coal, iron and steel industries with more than 1000 workers must ensure the equal representation of shareholders and employees within each's supervisory board. Thus companies need approval from their employees to enact any changes to working conditions, making coal workers an essential part of the decision-making process here." (p. 7-8)
> 

## Methodology and Data

### Research Design

The paper employs a staggered difference-in-differences (DiD) design to estimate the effects of coal closures on electoral outcomes at the municipal level. This approach allows for identifying causal effects while accounting for time-varying treatment effects across municipalities.

> "I use a series of staggered difference-in-differences (DiD) models to estimate the effect of the first coal closure in a municipality on electoral results. More specifically, I zoom in on the case of NRW to analyse how vote shares for all parties represented in the regional parliament changed after the first coal closure in a municipality occurred." (p. 8)
> 

The author justifies focusing on the municipality level based on both theoretical and methodological considerations:

> "Following Toshkov (2016), the level of analysis should be chosen according to the level at which the treatment in question can be isolated. In the case of coal closures, the most fine-grained administrative level at which this effect can be isolated is the municipal one, as representative data on voting behaviour at the individual level for the period of observation is not available for NRW." (p. 11)
> 

### Data Sources and Operationalization

The study uses multiple data sources:

1. Location and dates of coal closures (plants and mines) from 2007-2022
2. Municipal-level election results for North Rhine-Westphalia (1990-2022)
3. Socioeconomic indicators from the Wegweiser Kommune database
4. Survey data from the Inequality Barometer (2020, 2022) and Politbarometer

The primary independent variable is operationalized as:

> "For my empirical analysis, the independent variable of interest, namely the closure of coal plants and mines, is operationalised as the year a municipality was treated, meaning the one in which it experienced its first coal closure." (p. 9)
> 

The dependent variables include:

> "The main dependent variables for the main analyses are the election results of all parties currently represented in NRW's regional parliament as well as the turnout rate between 1990 and 2022, for which I collected data from the regional statistical office." (p. 9-10)
> 

### Estimation Strategy

The paper employs the Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021) estimator for staggered DiD designs to address challenges with traditional two-way fixed effects models:

> "In contrast to two-way fixed-effects models, staggered DiD ones are able to take into account potentially heterogeneous treatment effects and use a clearly defined control group (Roth et al., 2023). This makes them better suited than two-way fixed-effects models in the event of staggered treatment rollout." (p. 10)
> 

To address concerns about pre-treatment differences between treated and control municipalities, the author employs coarsened exact matching (CEM):

> "To mitigate for these variables' influence on results, I conduct coarsened exact matching (CEM) to identify a set of control municipalities which are as similar as possible in nature to those municipalities to be treated (Ho et al., 2007; Iacus et al., 2012)." (p. 11)
> 

## Results

### Main Findings

The paper's main results align with two of the three hypotheses. Coal closures are associated with:

1. No significant effect on AfD vote shares (failing to support H1)
2. Higher abstention rates (supporting H2)
3. Lower vote shares for the SPD (supporting H3)

The author presents these findings graphically and numerically:

> "Both the full sample and the matched sample analysis show that the first coal closure in a municipality is significantly associated with a decrease in the vote share for the SPD in the subsequent regional and federal elections. For the local elections, the negative effect is not significant. Moreover, the first coal closure increases the abstention rate in the affected municipality for all three levels of elections." (p. 11)
> 

The magnitude of these effects is substantial:

> "It shows that the first coal closure has the largest effects on SPD vote shares (–1.46 percentage points with the full sample and –0.99 percentage points with the matched sample) and abstention rates (+1.52 percentage points with the full sample and +1.33 percentage points with the matched sample). In substantive terms, the effect sizes for both abstention and SPD vote-share losses correspond to around one quarter of the standard deviation of the two variables between 2010 and 2022." (p. 12)
> 

### Robustness Checks and Alternative Explanations

The paper addresses several potential concerns through robustness checks:

1. Analysis using the complete sample of municipalities
2. Investigation of incumbency effects
3. Examination of announcement effects (versus closure effects)

On the incumbency question, the author finds:

> "The results of these regressions are presented in Figures G.17 to G.20 in the Appendix of the Supporting Information, with black bars indicating that a particular party was part of the governing coalition during a certain election period and grey bars denoting it was part of the opposition. These figures reveal no consistent pattern as to whether parties experience a decline or an increase in their vote shares due to coal closures when they are incumbents." (p. 18)
> 

## Mechanisms and Explanatory Evidence

A significant contribution of the paper is its examination of potential mechanisms linking coal closures to electoral outcomes. The author conducts additional analyses using municipal economic indicators and survey data to uncover the causal pathways.

### Perceived Economic Deprivation

Interestingly, the analysis reveals no objective economic decline in affected municipalities after matching:

> "Tables F.11 and F.12 in the Appendix of the Supporting Information present the coefficients of the overall ATT of first coal closures on the employment rate and on purchasing power, showing that while coal closures have a significant negative effect on both, these repercussions disappear for the matched sample. Thus, when comparing treated municipalities to their most-similar control ones, there does not seem to be a negative effect on the community's economic well-being." (p. 14)
> 

However, survey data reveals different perceptions among those in affected municipalities:

> "Figure 5 shows that, in 2022, individuals in treated municipalities rated economic inequality in their home region compared to the rest of Germany significantly higher than those living in control municipalities did." (p. 15)
> 

Similarly:

> "Figure 6 shows that individuals in treated municipalities perceive their position in the income distribution to be significantly lower than individuals in control municipalities." (p. 15)
> 

### Status Concerns and Social Identity

The paper also discusses the role of social status loss:

> "In the absence of survey data on social-status perceptions, it is not possible to directly assess whether individuals who experienced a coal closure in their municipality deem there to have been a decline or threat to their social standing beyond materially speaking. However, evidence from other studies supports the notion that this mechanism might also be at play here, including studies commissioned by the blue-collar union IG Metall... many of the jobs which former coal workers were transferred to were in lower-paid service-sector employment, resulting in experiences of status decline, frustration and dissatisfaction." (p. 15-16)
> 

### Disappointment with the Issue-Owning Party

The paper provides evidence that SPD voters in coal regions were most opposed to the phase-out:

> "Figure 8 demonstrates that, in 2007, individuals living in NRW who stated that they would vote for the SPD in the next election had a clear preference for ending the government subsidies for hard coal later than 2018, while all other groups would choose to do so earlier. It thus seems plausible that those directly affected by the policy are those least satisfied with the policy of phasing out coal until 2018 and that they subsequently turn their back to the issue-owning party." (p. 17)
> 

## Discussion and Implications

### Theoretical Contributions

The paper makes several important theoretical contributions:

1. It demonstrates that compensation and interest group representation are insufficient to prevent electoral backlash to structural economic change.
2. It highlights the importance of perceptions versus objective economic outcomes:

> "All in all, this section has shown that, on the one hand, coal closures have not led to an objective decline in community-wide economic well-being in affected municipalities. However, on the other, the descriptive patterns presented here also paint a picture of perceived economic deprivation and widespread disappointment with the introduced phase-out policy among those living in affected communities, as leading to higher abstention rates and lower vote shares for the Social Democrats." (p. 18)
> 
1. It suggests that issue ownership and historical party-constituency relationships matter for understanding asymmetric electoral responses.

### Policy Implications

The paper has significant implications for climate policy and just transitions:

> "With the significant politicisation around coal phase-outs and the high salience of fossil fuel-based power generation, the asymmetric backlash to coal closures in NRW increases the importance of understanding the conditions under which such a response can be mitigated if other countries are to adopt sound policies going forwards." (p. 20)
> 

The author notes that many more coal closures are planned in Germany:

> "In NRW alone, as of 2024, 38 CFPPs as well as three lignite open-cast mines still remain in operation, all of which will be shut down by 2038 at the latest." (p. 20)
> 

### Limitations and Future Research

The paper acknowledges several limitations:

1. Difficulty in testing the AfD hypothesis due to the party's recent formation:

> "Regarding the potential effect of coal closures on AfD vote shares, empirical analysis is constrained by the fact that the latter was founded in 2013, thus participating only in the last two elections in NRW. Therefore, this article can only offer first insights on the specific relationship between coal closures and far-right voting patterns." (p. 19-20)
> 
1. Limited individual-level data to test specific mechanisms:

> "Given the lack of representative time-series data available at the individual level in NRW, focus-group interviews in coal-producing regions might be a promising pathway for future scholarship to disentangle the specific roles which social status and compensation play in influencing voter choices." (p. 20)
> 

## Critical Assessment

### Methodological Strengths

The paper employs rigorous methods to identify causal effects:

1. Use of staggered DiD with the Callaway & Sant'Anna estimator to address heterogeneous treatment effects
2. Coarsened exact matching to improve balance between treated and control units
3. Multiple robustness checks and alternative specifications
4. Triangulation across different data sources (electoral, economic, and survey data)

### Theoretical Nuance

The paper contributes nuanced theoretical insights about the complex relationships between economic change, psychological reactions, and political behavior. It challenges simplistic accounts that suggest compensation can fully mitigate political backlash.

The finding that objective economic outcomes diverge from perceptions is particularly important, as it highlights the role of relative deprivation and social status concerns beyond material impacts.

### Limitations

Several limitations might be noted:

1. The analysis focuses primarily on one German state, which may limit generalizability.
2. The mechanism tests rely on cross-sectional survey data rather than panel data that would allow tracking individuals over time.
3. The paper primarily examines short- to medium-term effects, whereas longer-term adaptation might yield different patterns.
4. While the paper examines perceptions of economic inequality, it could benefit from more direct measures of status anxiety and identity threat.

## Connections to Broader Literature

### The Politics of Just Transitions

This paper contributes to growing literature on the political challenges of implementing climate policies, particularly when they threaten existing industries. It connects to work by scholars like Mildenberger (2020) on the political economy of climate policy and the barriers to decarbonization.

The findings align with research suggesting that the politics of climate policies are shaped not just by material interests but by questions of identity, status, and recognition. The importance of coal to regional identity in NRW parallels findings from studies in the United States (Bell & Braun, 2010) and Australia.

### Economic Grievances and Political Behavior

The paper extends research on economic grievances and political behavior by highlighting how even generous compensation may not prevent feelings of relative deprivation. This connects to work by Kurer (2020) on "the declining middle" and Gidron & Hall (2017, 2020) on status anxieties and populism.

The findings suggest that material compensation alone may be insufficient if it does not address status concerns and identity threats that accompany economic restructuring. This aligns with research highlighting the importance of recognition alongside redistribution (Fraser, 1995).

### Party-Constituency Relations and Electoral Realignment

The findings about SPD vote loss connect to broader literature on the transformation of social democratic parties and their changing relationship with traditional working-class voters (Abou-Chadi & Wagner, 2020; Gingrich & Häusermann, 2015).

The concept of "asymmetric backlash" targeting the issue-owning party contributes to understanding how structural economic changes reshape party systems. It suggests that parties historically aligned with particular industries may face disproportionate electoral costs when those industries decline, even when the party secures relatively generous compensation.

## Conclusion

Stutzmann's paper makes important contributions to our understanding of how structural economic changes associated with green transitions affect electoral politics. The finding that perceived economic deprivation and disappointment with issue-owning parties drive backlash, even in the presence of generous compensation, has significant implications for climate policy implementation.

The research highlights the limits of material compensation in addressing the social-psychological dimensions of economic change, particularly when industries are deeply connected to regional identities and social status. As countries worldwide consider coal phase-outs and other climate policies that threaten established industries, addressing these deeper concerns beyond material compensation may be crucial for maintaining political support for green transitions.

The paper's methodological rigor, theoretical nuance, and policy relevance make it an important contribution to understanding the complex politics of structural economic change in advanced democracies.
