    ## **Introduction**
    
    Class features prominently in the literature on political attitudes. Since Lipset and Rokkan’s ([1967](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR57)) classic analysis of the social bases of voting behavior, a large body of literature has examined the extent to which patterns of voter-party alignments may be attributed to socio-demographic group memberships (Lipset [1992](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR56); Lucassen and Lubbers, [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR58); Oesch and Rennwald, [2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR68)). While the premise that class shapes voting behavior has been widely questioned, and in some instances empirically refuted (e.g. Brooks, Nieuwbeerta, and Manza [2006](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR6)), it remains an extensively researched topic, with certain social classes often linked to support for particular political parties.
    
    Studies on far-right voting behavior often focus on the common features of the typical far-right voter, who is likely to be a lowly educated, male individual with poor prospects in the labor market (Vlandas and Halikiopoulou [2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR88)). Manual workers in Western democracies, seen as the “losers of modernization” (Swank and Betz [2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR85)), are expected to be more likely far-right party supporters because they experience stronger labor market insecurity and higher levels of competition with immigrants (Lucassen and Lubbers [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR58)). While scholars agree that there are strong theoretical reasons to expect a range of objective and subjective factors to drive individuals from certain social classes to support the far-right, empirical studies, however, report significant variations across countries and across time (see Bolet [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR4); Vlandas and Halikiopoulou [2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR88)). The far-right’s electoral support base appears to be particularly diverse, with parts of the working class highly supportive of far-right parties in some cases (Lucassen and Lubbers [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR58)), and lower middle class individuals supporting the far-right in others (e.g. Kurer [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR53)).
    
    Literature that attempts to explain why middle class voters sometimes support far-right parties focuses mainly on relative subjective decline (Bolet [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR4); Kurer [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR53)). These works suggest that far-right party support is less the outcome of actual economic hardship and more the result of perceived decline and relative deprivation. While such explanations shed light on the ways in which perceptions of decline might augment the far-right, we still lack a comprehensive explanation that takes into account the objective mechanisms of class decline, or in other words, the ways in which actual intergenerational class mobility, specifically in the downward direction, may explain why certain individuals opt for far-right parties.
    
    This article theorizes and empirically tests the effects of actual intergenerational class mobility on far-right party support. Our analytical starting point is that mixed results about the role of socio-economic status and social class in explaining far-right voting behavior may be partly driven by insufficient attention to the important role of changing class positions over time. It is indeed possible that changing class patterns (Oesch and Vigna [2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR69)) matter more than one’s current class position, as the effects of the latter on far right party support may depend on whether the individual had parents in higher, the same, or lower classes. To address this gap, we examine the ways in which the relationship between downward class mobility and far-right party support can be affected both through mediation and moderation.
    
    First, we derive several hypotheses about how downward class mobility might be linked to far-right voting by drawing on a framework that focuses on discontent, loss, and blame: individuals in classes lower than those of their parents are likely to experience discontent with their standard of living, face material losses and blame domestic elites and outsider groups for this decline. Specifically, to test these mechanisms, we explore four types of attitudes that may *mediate* the relationship between downward class mobility and far-right party support: life satisfaction (discontent), income insecurity (loss), distrust of elites (internal blame attribution), and anti-immigration attitudes (external blame attribution). Second, we derive a set of hypotheses about whether these individual-level variables, as well as overall national-level mobility, moderate the effect of downward mobility. We expect the effects on far-right voting to be particularly strong among individuals who already hold some of these attitudes, especially in contexts where other individuals in society have not experienced similar declining trajectories. Third, we assess the extent to which downward class mobility is important for far-right party success by examining the share of downwardly mobile individuals within the far-right electorate.
    
    We test our hypotheses about the potential link between actual intergenerational class mobility and far-right support using data from five waves of the European Social Survey (ESS). Specifically, using Diagonal Reference Models (DRMs), a specialized statistical approach prevalent in quantitative sociology literature, we test the proposition that class decline shapes the different forms of discontent feeding far-right party support empirically. This method has been used to explore the extent and consequences of class mobility, not least for attitudes, but to the best of our knowledge, without explicitly considering implications for far-right voting behavior.
    
    Our findings show that downward class mobility matters for far-right party support: individuals who experience social decline, particularly those falling from the salariat to the working class, are more likely to vote for the far-right. Indeed, working class voters whose parents come from the most advantaged social class experience both objective (i.e., material) decline and disillusionment (i.e., a discrepancy between their reality and expectations) and are, as such, the most likely supporters of the far-right. In terms of the hypotheses concerning mediation, our results show that downward mobility only affects life satisfaction (discontent) and income insecurity (loss), whereas distrust in politicians (internal blame attribution) and anti-immigration attitudes (external blame attribution) are driven by position and upward mobility. Nonetheless, the effect of downward mobility on far-right voting remains when controlling for all considered mechanisms. In terms of the hypotheses concerning moderation, we find that overall national-level mobility moderates the effect of downward mobility, but mobility effects do not vary depending on individual economic insecurity, life dissatisfaction, distrust in politicians, and anti-immigration attitudes. Finally, our analysis of the extent to which downward class mobility is important for far-right party success suggests that the association between class decline and far-right party support does not necessarily drive the electoral success of these parties: while individuals from salariat origins and working-class destination are the most likely far-right supporters, these individuals constitute only a small percentage of the far-right electorate.
    
    In sum, our findings suggest that downward class mobility significantly affects far-right voting but only under specific conditions. Our study contributes to the literature in three ways. First, we are among the first to illustrate how the distinction between current class position and class mobility matters for far-right party support by using the DRM approach, a method particularly appropriate for estimating class mobility effects. Second, we provide a systematic comparative and cross-country account of how class mobility affects the far-right vote and some evidence about the mediating and moderating roles of different attitudes that have been shown to affect far-right support. Third, our findings add nuance to theories of class and voting behavior by illustrating that scholars should pay attention not only to current class position but also to class movement, and by taking into account the share of individuals that experience class decline within the far-right electorate.
    
    The article proceeds as follows. First, we theorize our expectations with regards to mediation and moderation. Second, we present our data and method. Third, we describe our results. We conclude with a discussion of the broader implications of our argument and some avenues for future research.
    
    ## **Class and Far-Right Party Support**
    
    ### **Class Position and the Perception of Socio-Economic Status**
    
    The claim that there is an association between class and the far-right vote is hardly new. A wealth of voting behavior literature documents a strong association between working-class individuals and far-right party support (Lucassen and Lubbers [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR58); Vlandas and Halikiopoulou [2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR88)), showing that lowly educated, male individuals with poor prospects in the labor market are more likely to be far-right supporters (Swank and Betz [2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR86)). Indeed, routine manual workers have increasingly endorsed far-right parties (Oesch and Rennwald [2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR68)) suggesting that the core far-right electorate has undergone a process of “proletarianization” in many Western European countries including Austria, France, Denmark, and Norway, among others (Oesch [2008](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR67)).
    
    Theoretically, this can be explained by austerity and labor market insecurity (Baccini and Sattler [2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR3)): individuals experiencing economic marginalization are more likely to vote for far-right parties because they worry about wage pressures and competition with immigrants for jobs and benefits. The lower social strata are often referred to as the “losers of modernization” (Swank and Betz [2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR85)) who experience such pressures because of economic globalization and trade openness. Greater exposure to labor-market competition likely reinforces prejudices against immigrants, which therefore may have material economic foundations (Dancygier and Donnelly [2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR15); Stockemer et al. [2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR83)). In sum, working class individuals are more likely to support parties with an interest in limiting immigration because of (perceived) labor market competition (Lucassen and Lubbers [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR58)). In turn, far-right parties have increasingly adopted welfare chauvinist policies to appeal to these voters (Enggist and Pinggera [2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR19)).
    
    However, the predictive power of socio-economic factors varies, and findings are often mixed. On the one hand, income, unemployment, and economic situation are often found to be weaker predictors of far-right party support than cultural factors (Bolet [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR4); Lucassen and Lubbers [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR58)). On the other hand, far-right parties have also attracted support from other social groups, for example, the self-employed or parts of the middle classes depending on the country and time period under consideration (Bolet [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR4); Halikiopoulou and Vlandas [2016](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR39); Kurer [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR53)). In addition, labor market competition does not solely affect the lower social strata- indeed its impact often varies across countries, occupational sources, and skill level (Dancygier and Donnelly [2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR15)).
    
    Scholars have sought to explain this puzzling diversity of the far-right voter base by focusing on *perceptions* of status and socio-economic position. Recent studies argue that relative shifts in the social hierarchy (Kurer [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR53)) and perceived decline drive individuals from different class origins to support the far-right (Engler and Weisstanner [2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR20); Gest, Reny, and Mayer [2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR27); Gidron and Hall [2017](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR28)). According to such approaches the threat of decline could matter more than actual decline (see Engler and Weisstanner, [2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR20); Im et al. [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR45); Kurer [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR53)). While such studies shed light on the perceptions or threat of class decline, the role of actual intergenerational class mobility remains underexplored in the far-right voting behavior literature. Few notable exceptions focus on individual case studies (see Kurer and Van Staalduinen, [2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR54) for Germany; Mcneil and Haberstroh, [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR60) for Brexit). We still lack, however, a systematic comparative account of the effect of actual intergenerational mobility on far-right voting across Western Europe. This is important because having a different social position than one’s parents had can potentially have major effects on an individual’s outlook on life and society and have significant consequences for voting preferences (Daenekindt, van der Waal, and de Koster [2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR13); Day and Fiske [2019](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR16); Gugushvili [2016a](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR31), [b](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR32)).
    
    ### **Theorizing the Role of Actual Class Decline and Mediation**
    
    Western European countries have experienced widespread changes in intergenerational class mobility (Bukodi et al. [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR7)). Following the so-called post-World War II ‘golden era of social mobility’ (Goldthorpe [2016](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR29)) upward class mobility in absolute terms has been in decline, while downward class mobility has increased across Europe (Bukodi, Paskov, and Nolan [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR7)). These developments have had significant political consequences. Various forms of intergenerational social mobility have been linked to a wide range of outcomes, such as support for democracy (Gugushvili [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR34)), political participation (Fan and Yan [2019](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR25)), voting behavior (Nieuwbeerta et al. [2000](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR66)), anti-immigration attitudes (Paskov, Präg, and Richards [2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR71)) and psychological well-being (Zelinska, Gugushvili, and Bulczak [2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR90)).
    
    Class decline, therefore, is important for our understanding of how people behave politically (Gest et al., [2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR27); Jackson and Grusky [2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR48); Lipset [1992](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR56)). Unlike their stable class counterparts, mobile individuals are not only attached to two different social environments, their origins and destination classes, they also experience a change in position from one class to another. In this study, we theorize how the *experience* of downward mobility may have effects on far-right voting behavior, independently of an individual’s class origin, which may shape which attitudes they have assimilated, or their class destination which determines the social environments in which they are socialized (cf. McNeil and Haberstroh [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR60)). Regardless of attachment to a particular class, the *experience* of class decline itself can be expected to affect far-right voting behavior via four main mechanisms, represented in Fig. [1](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#Fig1): life satisfaction (discontent), income insecurity (loss), distrust of elites (internal blame attribution) and anti-immigration attitudes (external blame attribution).

    First, downward class mobility generates discontent by reducing well-being and life satisfaction (Chan [2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR11); Kaiser and Trinh [2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR51)). The experience of class decline can lead to a loss of social status, which is closely tied to an individual’s identity and self-worth and can result in feelings of failure, inadequacy, and a diminished sense of self-esteem, all of which negatively impact well-being and life satisfaction. Downward mobility also often results in unfavorable social comparisons, where individuals compare themselves to their own past social status or to peers who have maintained or improved their status (Gugushvili [2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR35)). This can lead to feelings of envy, resentment, and social stigma, further reducing life satisfaction. In addition, changes in social class can disrupt existing social networks and support systems. The loss of social connections and support from peers in the higher social class can leave individuals feeling isolated and unsupported, which adversely affects their emotional and psychological well-being. In sum, downward mobility often leads to a perceived loss of control over one’s life circumstances. This lack of control and predictability can contribute to feelings of helplessness and hopelessness, which are detrimental to overall well-being and life satisfaction (Newman [1999](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR65)). In turn, dissatisfaction has been linked to political behavior in general (Esaiasson, Dahlberg, and Kokkonen [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR21)) and also far-right party support in particular (Lindholm, Lutz, and Green [2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR55)).
    
    Second, the experience of downward class mobility is directly linked to material losses. Downwardly mobile individuals who experience a loss, both of status and standard of living, are more likely to experience actual deprivation, hardship, and economic insecurity (Kurer [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR53)). They face poor labor market prospects and are often unable to cope with economic, social, and cultural acceleration (Rydgren [2007](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR77); Swank and Betz [2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR85)). These individuals may develop grievances if they experience discrepancies between their value expectations, i.e. those goods and services they believe they are entitled to, and their value capabilities, i.e. the goods and services they actually attain (e.g. Klandermans et al., [2001](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR52)) (e.g. Gidron and Hall [2017](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR28)). As a result, they are more likely to have different political preferences from the intergenerationally stable members of their classes of destination (Clifford and Heath [1993](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR12)). In addition, comparisons that individuals make across time with their past selves and social origins can often result in the development of grievances associated with the intergenerational decline (e.g. Gurr [2015](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR38); Runciman [1966](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR76); Kurer and Van Staalduinen [2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR54)). A wealth of literature has shown that material losses are often associated with far-right party support (Swank and Betz [2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR85); Halikiopoulou and Vlandas [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR40)).
    
    Third, downward class mobility may trigger blame-shifting attitudes. Blame attribution theory (Cochrane and Nevitte, 2014) sheds light on how individual grievances may become expressed through blaming or scapegoating. In short, aggrieved individuals are likely to express their grievances by turning against those they can hold responsible for their real or perceived plight (Stockemer and Halikiopoulou [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR82)). Individuals experiencing class decline are more likely to exhibit high levels of dissatisfaction with the system, domestic elites, and institutions whom they blame for the lack of opportunities and their movement down the social ladder (Daenekindt et al. [2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR13)). As such the experience of downward class mobility is likely to decrease trust in/satisfaction with political institutions (Day and Fiske [2019](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR16)). In turn, institutional distrust and overall dissatisfaction with the democratic process tend to be associated with the rise of the far-right (Vasilopoulou and Halikiopoulou [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR87); Vrakopoulos [2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR89)). Far-right parties often present themselves as challenger or anti-establishment alternatives to the political mainstream (Oude Groeniger et al. [2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR70)), drawing on issues such as “trust, performance and policy-related grievances over elites, institutions, the government and, overall, the existing mechanisms of democratic representation” (Vasilopoulou and Halikiopoulou [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR87): 5).
    
    Fourth, blame-shifting may also be directed against external groups or elites (Goodfellow [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR30)). Immigrants are often the recipients of blame because they are perceived as direct competitors in the labor market, or for access to public goods and state services (Dancygier and Donnelly [2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR15); Mayda [2006](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR59)). Prejudice also plays a role, as individuals often tend to blame groups they perceive in negative ways and/or “prejudge individuals on the basis of their group memberships” (Healey [2006](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR41), p. 26). Indeed, studies report a link between downward mobility and increases in anti-immigration attitudes (Paskov et al. [2021](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR71)). In turn, far-right parties often engage in blame-shifting narratives. They center their agendas on purported conflicts between in-groups and out-groups, and often frame the immigration issue, which they own, around other issues such as the economy, access to welfare, policy performance and public goods provision, scapegoating immigrants for a range of domestic policy failures (Vasilopoulou and Halikiopoulou [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR87)).
    
    In sum, we have strong theoretical reasons to expect far-right parties to benefit from the experience of downward class mobility (Burgoon et al. [2019](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR9); Derndorfer [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR17); Hochschild [2016](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR42); Kurer [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR53); Lucassen and Lubbers [2012](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR58); Mutz [2018](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR64)).     ### **Individual and Contextual Level Moderation**
    
    Our theoretical framework so far has focused on the relationship between downward class mobility and far-right party support, as well as the mechanisms that underpin the relationship. This approach allows us to gauge the potential effect of downward class mobility on four types of attitudes, i.e.,life satisfaction (discontent), income insecurity (loss), distrust of elites (internal blame attribution), and anti-immigration attitudes (external blame attribution), which in turn are likely to predict far-right party support. It is also, important, however, to examine the factors that may have an effect on the *strength* of the relationship between downward class mobility and far-right party support. In order to do this, we account for moderation by distinguishing between and separately examining the individual and national level factors that could accentuate or lessen the effect of downward class mobility among different individuals. At the individual level, a substantial body of literature on the far-right has shown that certain individual attitudes make it more or less likely that individuals choose far-right parties. These include the four types of attitudes we discussed in the context of mediation, but which could also potentially moderate the effects. Thus, for instance, one could imagine that the effect of class decline may be stronger among individuals who report low levels of life satisfaction (Esaiasson et al. [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR21)), are already highly economically insecure (Swank and Betz [2003](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR85)), and/or distrustful of politics and institutions (Vasilopoulou and Halikiopoulou [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR87)). Conversely, individuals with favorable views of immigration would be less likely to support anti-immigrant far-right parties (Halikiopoulou and Vlandas [2020](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR40)) even if they experienced downward mobility. As such we hypothesize:
    
    > H2.1– The moderating role of discontent: the effect of downward class mobility on far-right party support is likely to be stronger among individuals with lower levels of life satisfaction.
    > 
    > 
    > *H2.2– The moderating role of loss: the effect of downward class mobility on far-right party support is likely to be stronger among economically insecure individuals.*
    > 
    > *H2.3– The moderating role of internally directed blame: the effect of downward class mobility on far-right party support is likely to be stronger among individuals with lower levels of political trust.*
    > 
    > *H2.4– The moderating role of externally directed blame: the effect of downward class mobility on far-right party support is likely to be stronger among individuals with higher anti-immigration attitudes.*
    > 
    
    We also focus on the contextual effect of class mobility at the national level. Our logic is as follows. A rich body of literature suggests that political attitudes vary substantially between inhabitants of different areas and/or countries (e.g., Huijsmans [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR44); Nieuwbeerta et al. [2000](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR66)). Citizens are likely to be affected by the context in which they live (Arzheimer et al. [2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR2)) because they tend to compare their own experiences with others’ and thus perceive these experiences not in isolation but vis-à-vis a more generalized sense of how their own situation fares within the broader context (Gugushvili et al. [2019](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR36)). In other words, context affects people’s egotropic concerns, i.e., their perceptions of their own relative position, and sociotropic concerns, i.e. their perceptions of the general state of society (Huijsmans [2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11109-025-10033-7#ref-CR44)). Contextual developments regarding class mobility can contribute to a generalized sense of societal decline or affluence against which individuals are likely to compare to their own class circumstances. If downward class mobility takes place in the context of widely prevalent downward mobility, then psychologically, the adverse effect on downward mobility might not be as strong. Its effect is likely to be stronger in a context where downward mobility is generally low, or when upward mobility is common, as individuals may feel singled out in their trajectory of decline.
    
    From this discussion, we derive the following hypothesis:
    
    > H3– The moderating role of context: Overall social mobility is likely to moderate the effect of downward class mobility on far-right voting
    > 

## Introduction and Research Context

The authors situate their research within the broader literature on social class and political behavior, noting that while the relationship between working-class status and far-right voting has been extensively studied, findings have been mixed and sometimes contradictory:

The far-right voter base appears particularly diverse across countries and time periods, which has led to scholarly debate:

> "The far-right's electoral support base appears to be particularly diverse, with parts of the working class highly supportive of far-right parties in some cases (Lucassen and Lubbers 2012), and lower middle class individuals supporting the far-right in others (e.g. Kurer 2020)" (p. 2).
> 

The authors argue that this diversity may be explained by examining not just current class position but the experience of class mobility across generations:

> "Our analytical starting point is that mixed results about the role of socio-economic status and social class in explaining far-right voting behavior may be partly driven by insufficient attention to the important role of changing class positions over time" (p. 2).
> 

## Theoretical Framework

The authors develop a sophisticated theoretical framework centered around how downward class mobility might generate support for far-right parties. They identify four key mechanisms that potentially mediate this relationship: discontent (life satisfaction), loss (economic insecurity), internal blame attribution (distrust of elites), and external blame attribution (anti-immigration attitudes).

This framework is visually represented in Figure 1 (p. 6) showing how downward class mobility may operate through these four pathways to influence far-right voting. The authors explain:

> "In this study, we theorize how the experience of downward mobility may have effects on far-right voting behavior, independently of an individual's class origin, which may shape which attitudes they have assimilated, or their class destination which determines the social environments in which they are socialized" (p. 5).
> 

The first mechanism focuses on discontent reflected in reduced life satisfaction:

> "Downward class mobility generates discontent by reducing well-being and life satisfaction (Chan 2018; Kaiser and Trinh 2021). The experience of class decline can lead to a loss of social status, which is closely tied to an individual's identity and self-worth and can result in feelings of failure, inadequacy, and a diminished sense of self-esteem, all of which negatively impact well-being and life satisfaction" (p. 6).
> 

The second mechanism involves material loss and economic insecurity:

> "Downwardly mobile individuals who experience a loss, both of status and standard of living, are more likely to experience actual deprivation, hardship, and economic insecurity (Kurer 2020). They face poor labor market prospects and are often unable to cope with economic, social, and cultural acceleration" (p. 7).
> 

The third mechanism relates to blame attribution directed internally at elites:

> "Individuals experiencing class decline are more likely to exhibit high levels of dissatisfaction with the system, domestic elites, and institutions whom they blame for the lack of opportunities and their movement down the social ladder (Daenekindt et al. 2018)" (p. 7).
> 

The fourth mechanism involves external blame attribution toward immigrants:

> "Immigrants are often the recipients of blame because they are perceived as direct competitors in the labor market, or for access to public goods and state services (Dancygier and Donnelly 2013; Mayda 2006)" (p. 7).
> 

The authors also theorize how the national context might moderate these relationships:

> "If downward class mobility takes place in the context of widely prevalent downward mobility, then psychologically, the adverse effect on downward mobility might not be as strong. Its effect is likely to be stronger in a context where downward mobility is generally low, or when upward mobility is common, as individuals may feel singled out in their trajectory of decline" (p. 9).
> 

## Research Design, Data, and Methodology

### Data Source and Sample

The study uses data from the first five waves (2002-2010) of the European Social Survey (ESS), a comprehensive dataset that includes information on individuals' voting behavior and class mobility experiences. The authors focus on 12 Western European societies: Austria, Belgium, Switzerland, Germany, Denmark, Finland, France, Greece, Italy, the Netherlands, Norway, and Sweden.

The authors explain why they limited their sample:

> "Due to the specific historical trajectories of class mobility in post-communist societies as well as the different nature of far-right politics in these countries (Brils et al. 2022; Gugushvili 2017; Mudde 2019), we run our analysis only for the following 12 Western European societies that had far-right parties that received votes in the national election that took place during the period under consideration" (p. 10).
> 

The authors note they limited their analysis to the first five waves of the ESS because:

> "We limit our sample to the first five biannual waves of the ESS (2002–2010) because of the unavailability of detailed information on parental class in more recent waves (2012–2022), which makes a thorough analysis of intergenerational class mobility using the entire ESS sample challenging" (p. 10).
> 

### Variables and Operationalization

**Dependent Variable:**
The dependent variable is a binary outcome measuring whether respondents voted for a far-right party in the last general election (coded 1) or not (coded 0). The authors classify far-right parties according to the PopuList database:

> "We classify far-right parties in accordance with the PopuList (Rooduijn et al. 2024), a widely referenced database that has been used extensively in the literature to classify far-right parties" (p. 10).
> 

**Main Independent Variable:**
The main independent variable is intergenerational class mobility, operationalized by comparing respondents' class origins (parents' class when respondents were 14 years old) with their destination class (current class). The authors use the European Socio-economic Classification (ESeC) to create a three-class scheme:

> "Following the established approach in the relevant literature, social origin was defined as the highest class between the maternal and paternal classes. For ease of interpretation and comparability of results across a large number of European societies, we use ESeC's three class scheme with salariat, intermediate, and working classes" (p. 11).
> 

The authors specifically focus on four mobility trajectories:

1. Working to salariat class (long-range upward)
2. Other upward mobility (short-range)
3. Other downward mobility (short-range)
4. Salariat to working class (long-range downward)

**Mediating Variables:**
Four mediating variables are examined:

1. Life satisfaction (discontent): Range from "extremely satisfied" (0) to "extremely dissatisfied" (10)
2. Economic insecurity: Based on feelings about household income, from "living comfortably on present income" (1) to "very difficult on present income" (4)
3. Distrust in politicians: Range from "complete trust" (0) to "complete distrust" (10)
4. Anti-immigration attitudes: Factor average of answers on three survey items, all ranging from 0 (positive attitudes) to 10 (negative attitudes)

**Control Variables:**
The authors control for several demographic and socioeconomic characteristics:

- Gender
- Age (in 10-year categories from 26 to 75)
- Ethnic minority status
- Marital status
- Urban locality
- Unemployment
- Education

### Statistical Method: Diagonal Reference Models (DRMs)

A significant methodological contribution of this study is its application of Diagonal Reference Models (DRMs), which allow the authors to disentangle the effects of origin class, destination class, and mobility between classes. The authors explain the challenge:

> "One challenge when trying to estimate the effect of class mobility by comparing parents' and respondents' classes is perfect collinearity. Indeed, in a conventional regression analysis, we cannot simultaneously consider (a) a mobility effect (i.e. the difference between parents' and respondents' social class), (b) an origin effect (i.e. parental class), and (c) a destination effect (i.e. respondents' social class)" (p. 12).
> 

They justify their choice of DRMs as follows:

> "DRMs have been widely recognized and employed in empirical studies, demonstrating their superiority over conventional regressions (Bulczak and Gugushvili 2022; Jaime-Castillo and Marqués-Perales 2019; Paskov et al. 2021)" (p. 12).
> 

The authors specifically explain how DRMs work:

> "The focal point in the DRM approach is the assumption that intergenerationally immobile individuals represent typical voting behaviors for corresponding classes, and therefore, they are used in statistical estimations as reference groups" (p. 12).
> 

The model is formally outlined in Equation 1 (p. 13), which shows how the DRM approach combines the effects of origin class, destination class, and different types of class mobility, while also including control variables.

## Key Findings

### Main Hypothesis Testing

**H1: Individuals experiencing downward class mobility are more likely to vote for far-right parties.**

The results partially confirm this hypothesis. The authors find that only downward mobility from the salariat to the working class (long-range downward mobility) is consistently associated with far-right voting:

> "Our findings show that downward class mobility matters for far-right party support: individuals who experience social decline, particularly those falling from the salariat to the working class, are more likely to vote for the far-right" (p. 3).
> 

The coefficient for downward mobility from the salariat to the working class is statistically significant across all model specifications, with odds ratios ranging from 1.75 in the baseline model to 1.55 in the full model with all controls. Other types of mobility, including short-range downward mobility and upward mobility, do not show statistically significant associations with far-right voting.

> "These results partially confirm our H.1 suggesting that downward class mobility indeed matters for far-right party support, but only when the fall is severe from the highest class origin to the lowest class destination" (p. 15-17).
> 

**H1.1-H1.4: Mediating mechanisms (discontent, loss, internal blame, external blame)**

The authors examine whether the relationship between downward mobility and far-right voting is mediated by the four hypothesized mechanisms. The results show:

> "Our results show that downward mobility only affects life satisfaction (discontent) and income insecurity (loss), whereas distrust in politicians (internal blame attribution) and anti-immigration attitudes (external blame attribution) are driven by position and upward mobility" (p. 3).
> 

This partially confirms hypotheses H1.1 and H1.2 but does not support H1.3 and H1.4. Interestingly, the authors note:

> "Nonetheless, the effect of downward mobility on far-right voting remains when controlling for all considered mechanisms" (p. 3).
> 

**H2.1-H2.4: Moderating roles of individual attitudes**

The authors test whether individual attitudes (life satisfaction, economic insecurity, political trust, and anti-immigration attitudes) moderate the effect of downward mobility on far-right voting. Contrary to expectations, they find:

> "The results suggest that life dissatisfaction, economic insecurity, political distrust, and anti-immigration attitudes do not moderate the association between class mobility and voting for far-right parties" (p. 19).
> 

This does not support hypotheses H2.1 through H2.4.

**H3: The moderating role of context**

The authors examine whether national-level class mobility moderates the effect of individual-level downward mobility on far-right voting. They find support for this hypothesis:

> "We find that while mobility effects do not vary depending on these individual traits, overall national-level mobility does moderate the effect of downward mobility, suggesting that context matters for individual-level associations between class mobility and far-right voting" (p. 3).
> 

Specifically, they find that "downward mobility from the salariat to the intermediate and from the intermediate to the working class has a positive association with voting for the far-right in countries where the levels of upward class mobility are high" (p. 22).

### Additional Findings

The authors also find a clear class gradient in far-right voting, with working-class individuals more likely to vote for far-right parties and salariat individuals less likely to do so. This effect persists even after controlling for various socio-demographic and socio-economic characteristics, though it is somewhat reduced when education is accounted for.

The weight parameter in their models indicates that destination class plays a slightly more important role than origin class in predicting far-right voting, especially after controlling for socio-demographic and socio-economic factors.

An important finding relates to the composition of the far-right electorate:

> "Our analysis of the extent to which downward class mobility is important for far-right party success suggests that the association between class decline and far-right party support does not necessarily drive the electoral success of these parties: while individuals from salariat origins and working-class destination are the most likely far-right supporters, these individuals constitute only a small percentage of the far-right electorate" (p. 3).
> 

## Methodological Strengths and Limitations

### Strengths

The paper's methodology has several notable strengths:

1. **Advanced Statistical Technique**: The use of Diagonal Reference Models (DRMs) allows the authors to effectively disentangle the effects of origin class, destination class, and mobility between classes, which overcomes the collinearity problem inherent in conventional regression analyses.
2. **Comprehensive Data**: The European Social Survey provides high-quality, comparable data across multiple countries, allowing for a cross-national analysis of class mobility and far-right voting.
3. **Multiple Robustness Checks**: The authors conduct several robustness checks, including using alternative class schemes (Oesch class scheme), alternative specifications of the dependent variable (excluding "borderline" far-right parties), and alternative model specifications (linear probability models).
4. **Analytical Distinction Between Support and Success**: The authors make an important analytical distinction between the predictive power of downward mobility for far-right voting (support) and its substantive importance for far-right party success.

### Limitations

The authors acknowledge several limitations of their study:

1. **Sample Limitations**: The study is confined to Western European countries and data from 2002-2010, limiting the generalizability of findings to other regions and more recent periods.

> "Our sample is confined to Western European far-right parties therefore limiting the generalizability of our findings" (p. 25).
> 
1. **Causality**: While DRMs allow for sophisticated statistical modeling, they do not establish causal relationships:

> "While our DRMs allow us to distinguish between class origin and destination, thus yielding some original results, this method does not allow us to draw causal inferences" (p. 26).
> 
1. **Data Availability**: The unavailability of detailed information on parental class in more recent ESS waves (2012-2022) restricts the analysis to earlier time periods.

> "Because of the unavailability of detailed information on parental class in more recent ESS waves, we have only examined the initial five ESS biannual waves (2002–2010)" (p. 25).
> 

## Theoretical and Practical Implications

### Theoretical Implications

The study makes several important theoretical contributions to our understanding of far-right voting behavior:

1. **Class and Mobility**: The findings highlight the importance of considering not just current class position but also class mobility in understanding political behavior:

> "Our study contributes to the literature in three ways. First, we are among the first to illustrate how the distinction between current class position and class mobility matters for far-right party support by using the DRM approach, a method particularly appropriate for estimating class mobility effects" (p. 3-4).
> 
1. **Mediating Mechanisms**: The study sheds light on the specific mechanisms through which downward class mobility affects far-right voting:

> "It also points to the possibility that the effects of position and mobility are associated with far-right party voting via different attitudinal mechanisms and provides a novel explanation for how social class may itself partly shape the attitudes that had been shown to influence far-right party voting" (p. 25).
> 
1. **Context Matters**: The findings emphasize the importance of national context in moderating the relationship between class mobility and far-right voting:

> "Country-level class mobility, therefore, matters for individual-level associations between class mobility and far-right voting. We can interpret this finding by the adverse psychological effect of downward mobility on an individual being less strong in a country with widespread downward mobility than in a country where most people have not experienced downward class mobility" (p. 25).
> 

### Practical Implications

The study also has practical implications for understanding the rise of far-right parties in Western Europe:

1. **Electoral Base Heterogeneity**: The findings highlight the heterogeneity of the far-right electorate:

> "One of the main implications of our findings is that downward class mobility is an important driver of far-right party support, yet it affects only a small proportion of the far-right electorate. This suggests that to understand the rise of challenger parties, scholars should pay more attention to the heterogeneity of their electorates" (p. 26).
> 
1. **Electoral Coalitions**: The authors suggest that understanding coalitions between different voter groups is key to understanding far-right party success:

> "If, similarly to other party families, the far-right is becoming increasingly successful by broadening its electoral base, then understanding coalitions between different voter groups is the key to understanding far-right party success" (p. 26).
> 

## Conclusion

The paper makes a significant contribution to our understanding of the relationship between class mobility and far-right voting in Western Europe. By employing sophisticated methodological techniques (DRMs), the authors are able to disentangle the effects of origin class, destination class, and mobility between classes.

The key finding is that downward class mobility matters for far-right party support, but only when the fall is severe—specifically, from the highest (salariat) class to the lowest (working) class. This relationship is mediated partly by life satisfaction (discontent) and economic insecurity (loss), but not by distrust in politicians or anti-immigration attitudes. National-level class mobility also moderates the effect of individual-level downward mobility on far-right voting.

Importantly, while downwardly mobile individuals are more likely to vote for far-right parties, they constitute only a small percentage of the far-right electorate. This suggests that understanding far-right party success requires attention to the heterogeneity of their electorates and the coalitions between different voter groups.

The authors conclude:

> "Overall, our article has attempted to shed new light on the relationship between class mobility and far-right party support within the Western European context. One of the main implications of our findings is that downward class mobility is an important driver of far-right party support, yet it affects only a small proportion of the far-right electorate. This suggests that to understand the rise of challenger parties, scholars should pay more attention to the heterogeneity of their electorates" (p. 26).
> 

They suggest several avenues for future research, including extending the analysis to other regions and time periods, applying causal methods to better understand the mechanisms at play, examining the effect of downward class mobility on other types of "challenger" parties, and investigating supply-side dynamics in terms of how far-right parties try to capitalize on downward class mobility in their programmatic agendas.
