## Comprehensive Summary

This groundbreaking study addresses a fundamental puzzle in contemporary electoral politics: what specific aspects of status-related grievances drive far-right voting behavior? While previous research has established connections between subjective social status and far-right support, Zollinger and Häusermann argue that this conceptualization is insufficient to capture the distinctive nature of far-right voters' concerns. The authors introduce two novel theoretical concepts—recognition entitlement and relative deprivation—to better understand the psychological and social mechanisms underlying far-right electoral appeal.

The research represents a significant theoretical advancement by moving beyond simple measures of subjective social status to examine the comparative and evaluative dimensions of status perception. Using original survey data from France, Germany, the UK, and Switzerland, the authors demonstrate that far-right voters are characterized not primarily by low subjective status per se, but by a sense of relative deprivation—the feeling of being unfairly disadvantaged compared to other social groups. This finding has profound implications for understanding electoral realignment in advanced democracies and the challenges facing progressive political movements seeking to address status-related grievances.

The study's core contribution lies in revealing the "zero-sum" nature of far-right status concerns, which distinguishes them from other forms of political mobilization and creates particular challenges for universalistic, inclusive political appeals.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Relative deprivation, not low status per se, distinguishes far-right voters**: "A sense of relative deprivation (which combines an explicit social comparison with an expression of dissatisfaction) is the only one that relates positively to the far right vote, but negatively to all other electoral outcomes."
2. **Zero-sum thinking structures the new political divide**: "It is this vision of zero-sum rivalry in social hierarchies that seems to emerge as the main principle setting far right voters apart from the (new) left."
3. **Far-right voters derive recognition from narrow sources**: The research reveals that "far right voters feel particularly under-recognized when it comes to their values, their worldliness, and their contribution to society–but not when it comes to more socio-economic aspects of their lives."
4. **Particularistic identities fuel relative deprivation**: "Respondents who characterize themselves as 'of humble means', 'hard-working', or 'lower and medium education' report higher levels of relative deprivation, and the same holds for voters who feel close to 'co-nationals', 'rural people' and 'people who are down-to-earth and rooted to home.'"
5. **Progressive appeals face fundamental constraints**: "Any attempts to convince or 'win back' far right voters by improving, or promising to improve, their absolute social position may not be effective, as long as far right parties cater to these groups' sense of relative status deprivation."
6. **Center-right voters, not greens, are the status elite**: "Center right voters stand out with a very strong sense of status, recognition and a sense of being relatively privileged in society. Moreover, center right voters derive social recognition from a broad range of sources."

## Introduction and Motivation Analysis

### Research Problem and Significance

The authors address a critical empirical puzzle that has emerged from the growing literature on status and far-right voting. While research has established that status-related grievances play a role in far-right support, "the nature of the status concerns characterizing voters of the far right, or this party family's typical working/lower-middle-class electoral constituencies, remain hard to pin down both conceptually and empirically." This puzzle is particularly acute because "far right voters on average are by no means lowest on the economic social ladder" and there exists "inconclusive evidence about the working classes even having lost subjective social status over time."

The research problem extends beyond simple measurement issues to fundamental questions about the nature of political mobilization in advanced democracies. The authors position their work within broader debates about electoral realignment, noting that "transforming social hierarchies – for instance, of occupation or gender – are increasingly acknowledged as being deeply implicated in the reconfiguration of party systems across advanced knowledge societies." This positioning reflects an understanding that contemporary political competition cannot be adequately understood through traditional left-right economic frameworks alone.

The significance of this research lies in its potential to illuminate the mechanisms underlying what many scholars have identified as a fundamental realignment of political competition. The authors argue that "non-material aspects of social hierarchies, unmet expectations in terms of recognition, and perceived prospective threats to status appear to play a role" in far-right mobilization, suggesting that understanding these dynamics is crucial for comprehending contemporary democratic politics.

The theoretical innovation proposed by the authors addresses limitations in existing approaches. They contend that "even studying voters' level of subjective social status may be insufficient to grasp the specific nature of status grievances mobilized by the far right." This insight reflects a sophisticated understanding of how social psychology intersects with political behavior, recognizing that absolute positions in social hierarchies may be less politically consequential than comparative evaluations and unmet expectations.

### Theoretical Positioning and Innovation

The authors situate their theoretical contribution within the broader literature on status and politics while proposing conceptual innovations that advance understanding of far-right mobilization. Their approach builds on "a growing body of work on far right identities" while integrating insights from research on social comparison and recognition theory.

The theoretical innovation centers on distinguishing between different dimensions of status-related grievances. The authors argue that "recognition entitlement" refers to "a subjective sense of deserving more social recognition than one gets and the related dissatisfaction and perception of unfairness." This concept captures an evaluative dimension that goes beyond simple self-placement on social hierarchies to encompass normative expectations about deserved treatment.

Even more significantly, the authors propose that "relative deprivation denotes the sense that others are unfairly advantaged relative to oneself" and argue that this concept "captures both the sense of unfairness and the social comparison that the other two concepts only partially reflect." This theoretical move is crucial because it identifies the comparative and zero-sum elements that may be particularly important for understanding far-right political mobilization.

The authors position their theoretical framework as addressing gaps in existing scholarship while building on established foundations. They note how their approach "dovetails with the idea that status pursuit/defense centered around positive but threatened identities–such as national or rural identity–underpins successful far right mobilization." This integration demonstrates theoretical sophistication in connecting individual-level psychological processes with group-level political dynamics.

## Literature Review and Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Major Theoretical Traditions Engaged

The paper engages with several major theoretical traditions, each contributing essential insights to the authors' synthetic framework. The social identity tradition provides foundational understanding of how group membership shapes political attitudes and behavior. Drawing on classic work by "Tajfel and Turner", the authors understand how "positive identity formation" can "serve the same psychological goal of maintaining self-respect and reducing uncertainty in a context of economic and social change."

The relative deprivation literature provides another crucial theoretical foundation. Building on "Runciman" and contemporary work by scholars like Elchardus and Spruyt, the authors locate their analysis within established understanding of how comparative disadvantage generates political grievances. However, they extend this tradition by arguing that "this form of zero-sum-thinking may also stretch to immaterial aspects of voters' self-perceptions."

The recognition theory tradition, drawing from philosophers like Honneth and political theorists like Fraser, provides normative grounding for understanding status grievances. The authors build on "the fundamental normative claim that individuals are entitled to respect from others for their contributions to cooperative social interactions, be it in the spheres of the economy, politics, or civil society." This theoretical foundation allows them to conceptualize recognition entitlement as a legitimate claim that can become politically mobilized when unmet.

The literature on knowledge economy transformation provides crucial context for understanding which groups might experience status threats. Drawing on work by scholars like Iversen and Soskice, Kurer, and others, the authors understand how "the dynamics of upskilling, task-biased technological change and co-location of productive investments in centers" creates winners and losers that map onto political coalitions.

### Theoretical Synthesis and Framework

The authors' theoretical synthesis represents a sophisticated integration of these diverse traditions into a coherent framework for understanding far-right mobilization. Their key insight is that different dimensions of status-related grievances may have distinct political consequences. "The concept of recognition entitlement builds on the fundamental normative claim that individuals are entitled to respect from others for their contributions to cooperative social interactions" while "relative deprivation, by extension, expresses resentment about unfair disadvantage relative to other groups in society."

This distinction is theoretically crucial because it identifies different causal mechanisms linking status concerns to political behavior. Recognition entitlement focuses on the gap between deserved and received recognition, while relative deprivation explicitly incorporates social comparison. The authors argue that "relative deprivation is more explicitly an expression of what has been aptly termed 'zero-sum thinking'...i.e. a view of the world in which the struggle for recognition and status will necessarily favor certain groups at the expense of others."

The theoretical framework also incorporates insights about the changing nature of status hierarchies in knowledge societies. Drawing on Hall and Lamont's work, the authors theorize that "social recognition increasingly came to hinge on a person's individuality, and above all, their human capital in terms of productivity, income, and success in competitive markets." This transformation creates particular vulnerabilities for groups whose status was historically based on different criteria.

The authors propose that "a precarious basis of perceived social recognition exacerbates feelings of not getting the respect from society that one is due among this electorate." This insight connects individual-level psychological processes with broader structural changes in how status is allocated and recognized in contemporary societies.

### Hypotheses and Theoretical Predictions

The theoretical framework generates four key hypotheses that structure the empirical analysis. The first hypothesis predicts that "perceptions of recognition entitlement and relative deprivation are more distinctive of far right supporters than low subjective social status." This prediction flows directly from the theoretical argument that simple status measures miss crucial evaluative and comparative dimensions of status grievances.

The second hypothesis addresses the comparative dimension of electoral competition, predicting that "while higher subjective social status is distinctive for center right supporters (vs. far right and left), higher recognition entitlement and relative deprivation are distinctive for far right support (vs. left and center right)." This hypothesis reflects theoretical understanding of how different forms of status concerns may structure distinct political coalitions.

The third hypothesis links status grievances to group identities, predicting that "cultural and economic identities threatened by structural change are positively associated with recognition entitlement and relative deprivation, while cultural and economic identities favored by structural change relate to lower levels of recognition entitlement and relative deprivation." This prediction connects the individual-level psychological processes to broader patterns of social change.

The fourth hypothesis addresses the interaction between different dimensions of status concerns, predicting that "the positive association between recognition entitlement/relative deprivation and far right party support is particularly pronounced among voters with a narrow basis social recognition and status." This hypothesis captures the theoretical insight that vulnerability may be exacerbated when individuals have few alternative sources of status.

## Broader Theoretical Implications

### Cross-Cutting Themes and Mechanisms

The research reveals several theoretical insights that extend beyond the specific context of far-right voting to illuminate broader questions about political behavior and democratic governance. The finding that relative deprivation operates as a distinctive mechanism points to the importance of social comparison processes in political attitude formation. "The more traditional measure of relative deprivation captures an element of zero-sum thinking (the comparison to 'others') and runs counter to the universalist vision of society that green/left-libertarian parties advocate."

This insight has broader implications for understanding how different political movements mobilize support and the constraints they face in building coalitions. The zero-sum nature of relative deprivation suggests that appeals based on expanding recognition and rights may inadvertently activate countermobilization among groups that perceive such expansion as threatening their relative position.

The research also illuminates general principles about the relationship between structural change and political mobilization. The finding that "particularistic" identities threatened by structural change are positively associated with relative deprivation while "universalistic" identities are negatively associated suggests broader patterns about how social transformation generates political conflict.

The interaction between narrow recognition bases and relative deprivation points to psychological mechanisms that may operate across different contexts. "The positive association between relative deprivation and far right support is particularly pronounced for voters who rely on a narrow range of sources of recognition." This finding suggests that psychological vulnerability created by limited sources of self-worth may make individuals more susceptible to political appeals that promise to restore their status.

### Connections to Other Research Programs

The theoretical contributions connect to several other research programs in political science and related disciplines. The emphasis on zero-sum thinking links to research on intergroup conflict and realistic group conflict theory. The finding that "far right voters' recognition deprivation has a clear zero-sum flavor: their distinctive status grievance lies in feeling unfairly disadvantaged relative to other social groups" suggests connections to broader literatures on prejudice and intergroup relations.

The focus on recognition and respect connects to research on political trust and system legitimacy. The authors note that "predictable, reliable and good-quality public good provision, e.g. via the welfare state, enhances trust and system support." This insight suggests that status-related grievances may be partially addressed through institutional design and policy implementation that conveys recognition and respect.

The findings also connect to research on political polarization and democratic backsliding. The zero-sum nature of status competition identified in the research suggests mechanisms through which democratic competition might become increasingly conflictual and potentially destabilizing. "Appeals by particularistic and nationalistic parties in favor of excluding social outgroups–such as immigrants or ethnic minorities–from equal rights or advantages, or for re-establishing an allegedly 'lost past' with its traditional social hierarchies" naturally flow from relative deprivation dynamics.

## Methodology Overview

### Data Sources and Coverage

The empirical analysis draws on "original survey data from four West European countries – Germany, the UK, France, and Switzerland" collected through "online surveys fielded...between November 2020 and January 2021." The data collection employed "2,000 participants in France and Germany and 3,000 participants in Switzerland and the UK" with samples that are "representative for education, age, and gender."

The country selection reflects theoretical considerations about variation in far-right success and electoral systems. The authors note that "France and Switzerland saw particularly early and sustained increases in far right support, but far right vote shares have also risen by now in Germany. In the UK, the underlying tensions additionally became manifest in Brexit." This variation allows examination of the theoretical mechanisms across different institutional and temporal contexts.

The timing of data collection during the COVID-19 pandemic presents both opportunities and challenges for the research. While this timing may have heightened certain social tensions and status concerns, it also provides a unique window into how fundamental social and political attitudes operate during periods of crisis and uncertainty.

### Key Variables and Operationalization

The study's methodological innovation lies in its operationalization of the three distinct concepts of status-related grievances. Subjective social status is measured using "a 'social ladder' question similar to that used e.g. in Gidron and Hall (2017)" asking respondents "Where would you place yourself on this scale running from 'top' (10) to 'bottom' (1)?"

The measurement of recognition entitlement represents a significant methodological contribution. The authors develop "a two-step process, asking first about the sources of recognition, then about evaluation." Respondents are asked "To what extent do you feel recognized and respected by society for..." various aspects of their lives, followed by the question "Are you satisfied with this recognition? Or do you feel that you deserve rather more or rather less recognition and respect from society?"

Relative deprivation is operationalized using an established measure that captures the zero-sum comparison essential to the concept: "It is always other people who can benefit from all kinds of advantages offered in this society" measured on a five-point agreement scale.

The measurement of group identities draws on "a battery developed by Bornschier et al. 2021 and designed to capture both identities linked to classic cleavages (such as class) and newer, emerging cleavages related, for instance, to education or cosmopolitanism-particularism."

### Analytical Model and Justification

The analytical approach employs multiple complementary strategies to test the theoretical hypotheses. The authors use "linear probability models in which support for left, center right, and radical right parties are the dichotomous dependent variables" as well as "multinomial specification" to capture the full range of electoral choices.

The choice of linear probability models reflects practical considerations about interpretation and comparability with existing research. The authors note that their approach is "analogous to the analyses shown in the influential Gidron and Hall (2017) article on far right support." This methodological consistency facilitates comparison with established findings while extending the analysis to include their novel theoretical concepts.

The analytical strategy addresses potential confounding through the inclusion of relevant control variables. The models "include country fixed effects and control for education, age, and gender" to account for observable differences across contexts and individuals that might influence both status perceptions and political preferences.

The interaction analysis represents a sophisticated approach to testing the theoretical prediction about the conditional effects of narrow recognition bases. The authors examine "the interaction between having a narrow basis of recognition, on the one hand, and relative deprivation, on the other, in predicting far right party support."

## Synthesis and Broader Insights

### Theoretical Contributions

The research makes several important theoretical contributions that advance understanding of contemporary electoral politics. Most fundamentally, it demonstrates that "it is not relatively lower subjective social status per se that distinguishes far right voters from all other electorates, but a sense of relative deprivation tied to very specific particularistic identities." This finding challenges simplified accounts of far-right mobilization while pointing toward more nuanced understanding of status-based political appeals.

The identification of zero-sum thinking as a distinctive mechanism has broader implications for political mobilization and democratic governance. The research reveals that "green/left-libertarian voters are the polar opposite of far right voters in that they do not share the latter's feelings of relative deprivation." This asymmetry suggests fundamental differences in how different political movements conceptualize social progress and institutional change.

The finding that "it is the more known-to-be elite center right electorates (high-income, business/finance/tech workers etc.) who stand apart from all other electorates in their sense of high social status and recognition" reveals the complexity of contemporary political alignments and challenges simple narratives about status winners and losers.

### Future Research Directions

The research opens several promising avenues for future investigation. The identification of relative deprivation as a key mechanism suggests the need for research examining how this psychological process operates across different institutional and cultural contexts. Cross-national variation in the strength and political consequences of relative deprivation could illuminate the boundary conditions of the theoretical framework.

The interaction between narrow recognition bases and relative deprivation points to questions about the sources and stability of different forms of status. Research examining how recognition sources develop and change over time could provide insight into the dynamic processes underlying status-based political mobilization.

The zero-sum nature of far-right status concerns raises questions about the possibilities for political accommodation and democratic responsiveness. "The challenge for a progressive agenda in the knowledge economy would be to develop and negotiate visions of inclusive and equitable knowledge societies in which the relative differences between groups are limited." Research examining successful cases of such accommodation could provide practical insights for democratic governance in the face of status-based political conflict.

The research also suggests the need for investigation into the supply-side dynamics of political mobilization. Understanding how political entrepreneurs activate and channel relative deprivation into electoral support could illuminate the strategic dimensions of contemporary political competition and the conditions under which status grievances become politically consequential.
