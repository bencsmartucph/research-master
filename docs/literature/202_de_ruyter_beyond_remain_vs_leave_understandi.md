# 202 De Ruyter - Beyond remain vs. leave: understanding changing voter perceptions and attitudes towards Populism – evidence from Scotland and the West Midlands" This paper explores the link between populism and governance arrangements by comparing voter perceptions in the West Midlands (which strongly voted to leave the EU) and Scotland (which strongly voted to remain). The central thesis is that despite divergent voting patterns, both areas share perceptions of being "left behind," but the existence of stronger devolved governance in Scotland helped mitigate populist tendencies by providing greater political agency to citizens.

The authors argue that economic disparities and subnational governance arrangements are important factors explaining discontent in "left behind" places in the UK. They contend that meaningful devolution would be key in addressing these grievances, as citizens in areas with more accountable regional governance (Scotland) express greater satisfaction with political representation despite similar economic challenges.

The argument is well-developed and supported by both quantitative data and qualitative insights from focus groups. The authors successfully demonstrate that the lack of coherent devolved governance structures in England contributes to feelings of political alienation that fuel populist voting, while Scotland's more accountable devolved government provides an alternative channel for addressing similar grievances.

## Primary Theories Used

### Spatial Theory of Populism

**Detailed Explanation:** This theory posits that populist voting patterns are spatially concentrated and linked to geographic inequalities. The authors draw on Rodríguez-Pose's (2018) concept of "the revenge of places that don't matter" to explain how economic decline in certain regions creates political resentment that fuels populist voting. The theory suggests that while populism is often framed as a response to economic grievances or cultural anxieties, its geographic manifestation reveals the importance of place-based factors. Areas that experience long-term economic decline, disinvestment, and outmigration develop a sense of being "left behind" not just economically but politically as citizens perceive that governance structures ignore their concerns. This theory emphasizes how the geographical concentration of economic decline creates shared perceptions among residents that transcend individual socioeconomic characteristics, explaining why spatial factors remain significant predictors of voting patterns even after controlling for demographic variables.

**Direct Quotes:** "These processes share a strong spatial element, although much voter polarisation was as evident intra-regionally as between regions" (p.7).

"There is substantial evidence of spatial clustering in the referendum results even after controlling for an area's socio-demographic characteristics... further supporting the notion that it is those living in left-behind areas who had the highest propensity to vote for Brexit" (p.9).

### Political Agency and Governance Theory

**Detailed Explanation:** This theory examines how different governance arrangements influence citizens' sense of political agency and representation. The authors explore how the institutional design of subnational governance affects perceptions of political efficacy and legitimacy, which in turn shapes political behavior. The theory suggests that governance structures that are perceived as more democratic, accountable, and responsive provide citizens with a greater sense of political agency, which can mitigate populist tendencies even in economically disadvantaged areas. Conversely, fragmented, opaque, or unaccountable governance arrangements amplify perceptions of being "politically left behind," fostering conditions for populist voting. The theory emphasizes that political disaffection isn't just about economic outcomes but about processes of decision-making and the perceived ability of citizens to influence those processes through democratic channels.

**Direct Quotes:** "This raises questions around whether voter perceptions of 'being left behind' (particularly in the peri-urban fringes of the Midlands and North of England) are in part driven by a sense of being politically as well as economically left behind" (p.4).

"Scotland has a devolved administration in the form of the Scottish Government, which has a clearly defined role and is widely considered to be democratically accountable to the public... In contrast, sub-national governance arrangements in England (with London an interesting exception to this rule) have been fragmented and prone to the vagaries of policy changes over the past ten years" (p.4).

### Cultural-Economic Mediation Theory

**Detailed Explanation:** This theory explores how economic conditions shape cultural attitudes, which then influence political behavior. The authors build on Carreras' (2019) work showing that long-term economic decline in certain regions shaped cultural attitudes (anti-immigrant sentiment and Euroscepticism), which in turn drove support for Brexit. This mediation theory challenges simplistic economic or cultural explanations for populism by highlighting their interconnection. Long-term economic decline doesn't just create material hardship; it erodes community ties, local identity, and sense of security, generating cultural anxieties that manifest as anti-establishment or anti-immigrant sentiment. The theory suggests that economic and cultural factors aren't competing explanations for populism but interact in complex ways, with economic conditions often preceding and shaping cultural attitudes that directly influence voting behavior.

**Direct Quotes:** "The referendum result was seen as a reaction against globalisation and so-called 'metropolitan elites', although the decision is also widely seen to have been, in part, driven by xenophobia and fears related to immigration" (p.6).

"Conversations with respondents in Walsall North revealed how headline concerns around migration were actually rooted in deeper concerns around school provision" (p.14).

## Discussion

**Interpretation of Findings:**

The authors interpret their findings as evidence that the simple dichotomy between Remain and Leave voters conceals a more complex picture. They argue that both demographic factors and place-based experiences shape voting behavior, with governance arrangements playing a crucial mediating role between economic conditions and political responses.

The authors emphasize that communities are "left behind" not merely economically and culturally but also in terms of their perceptions of political agency. They interpret the contrasting voting patterns between Scotland and the West Midlands as evidence that devolved governance in Scotland provided citizens with alternative channels for expressing dissatisfaction with the status quo, while the fragmented and limited devolution in England left citizens feeling politically disempowered.

The interpretation recognizes the importance of "neighborhood effects" and socio-geographic networks in shaping political views. The authors suggest that highly educated voters in Leave-voting areas were more likely to vote Leave than similarly educated voters in Remain areas due to the influence of community and familial networks, highlighting the contextual nature of political decision-making.

The authors also interpret their findings as suggesting a need for meaningful devolution in England, arguing that the current mayoral model provides insufficient powers and accountability to address the sense of being politically "left behind."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Governance arrangements matter for populism:** The research demonstrates that similar economic grievances manifested in different voting patterns in Scotland versus the West Midlands, with Scotland's devolved government providing citizens with greater political agency and an alternative to populist voting. This suggests that institutional design is crucial in determining whether economic vulnerability translates into populist politics.
2. **Shared perceptions across political divides:** Despite divergent voting patterns, both Leave and Remain voters share perceptions of regional inequality and being "left behind" by metropolitan centers, suggesting common ground for addressing these issues. This finding highlights the importance of developing policies that address these shared concerns while avoiding divisive rhetoric.
3. **Complex interplay of economic and cultural factors:** The research supports the view that economic and cultural explanations for populism are intertwined rather than competing, with economic conditions often shaping cultural attitudes that directly influence voting behavior. This complexity requires nuanced policy responses that address both material needs and cultural anxieties.
4. **Spatial concentration of populism reflects governance failures:** The geographic concentration of populist voting patterns suggests that it's not just individual characteristics that determine political behavior but shared experiences of place, including perceptions of political representation. This spatial dimension highlights the need for place-based approaches to addressing populism.
5. **Appetite for meaningful devolution in England:** The research reveals frustration with limited and fragmented devolution in England and support for more substantial transfer of power and resources to the regional level. This suggests that addressing the governance deficit in English regions could be a crucial step in tackling the conditions that fuel populism.
