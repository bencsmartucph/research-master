Globalization and technological change have brought about a series of social and economic transformations in post-industrial societies ([Kriesi et al., 2006](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib32), [2008](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib33); [Im et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib24); [Kurer and Gallego 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib36); Oesch, 2013). One of the most important consequences of those transformations has been the growth of economic insecurity and labour market risks (see, among many others, [Emmenegger et al., 2012](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib12)). More flexible labour markets have paved the way for the rise of atypical forms of employment and labour market vulnerability, and reshaped political conflicts in Western democracies ([Häusermann 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib22)).

The political consequences of job insecurity have attracted increasing attention among analysts, practitioners and the overall public ([Johnson, 2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib69)), leading to a renewed interest in the topic within the academic community ([Sipma and Lubbers 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib66)). Insecurity, broadly understood, has been shown to be associated with a greater likelihood of non-voting, engaging in protest voting and supporting radical parties ([Emmenegger et al., 2015](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib13); [Rovny and Rovny 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib56); [Schwander 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib62); [Gomez and Ramiro 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib18), [2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib19)). However, research on the political consequences of job insecurity has found inconclusive, and sometimes even contradicting, results, due, in part, to the complexity of measuring this phenomenon.

Our findings contribute new evidence to the debate on the political effects of employment insecurity, but also lend support to the psychology of work literature, which has long highlighted the importance of focusing on how individuals perceive their work situation for understanding their emotional, attitudinal and behavioural responses ([Allan et al., 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib2)). The complexity of this phenomenon means that measures of individuals' objective contractual situation (labour market status) are often unable to capture this relationship. While job insecurity is far from being the main single predictor of support for radical parties, understanding how it could impact individuals' [political behaviour](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/social-sciences/political-behavior) is critical in contexts where atypical work arrangements have become much less ‘atypical’.

In the next pages, we first place our contribution in the context of the literature on the effects of job insecurity on party choice. We then present the hypotheses that we explore in this article. After that, we summarize our methodological decisions and data collection. Then we show and comment on our main analyses and, finally, conclude by synthesizing our findings and suggesting ways to proceed to further develop this field of research.

## 1. Job insecurity and vote: an analytical framework

### 1.1. Job insecurity and support for radical parties

The literature on the rise of new forms of [labour market](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/social-sciences/labor-market) risks and their diverse, multidimensional consequences is burgeoning - and justifiably so, given the importance of this phenomenon. Job insecurity (also called employment insecurity) is defined as an individual's uncertainty about the continuity of their current job relation ([Sverke and Hellgren 2002](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib68)). Job insecurity, and job precariousness more generally, is associated with multiple negative outcomes, including low [life satisfaction](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/social-sciences/life-satisfaction) ([Llosa et al., 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib39)) and psychological distress and anxiety ([Dollard and Bakker 2010](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib11)), and its relationship with political attitudes and behaviour has been subject to an increasing amount of research (see, among others, [Burgoon and Dekker 2010](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib8); [Bowyer and Vail 2011](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib5); [Kurer et al., 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib37); [Sipma et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib67)).

The idea that job insecurity is linked to diverse types of [political behaviour](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/social-sciences/political-behavior) and orientations is not new. Numerous studies on the economically and politically tumultuous 1930s in Europe establish a connection between the political events that marked that period and economic and employment insecurity (see, for example, [Bromhead et al., 2013](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib7); [Doerr et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib10); [Weitz 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib71)). The Great Depression and its dire socio-economic consequences have often been singled out as the main factor fostering the growth of the Nazi party during the Weimar Republic ([Siemens 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib65)). However, even for that specific experience of economic and [political crisis](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/social-sciences/political-crises), the competing importance of economic and other factors in explaining the rise of specific radical and extreme parties of opposing nature, and the social groups leading to their electoral growth, have been, and still are, disputed aspects of debate ([King et al., 2008](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib28); [Galofré-Vilà et al., 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib16)).[1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#fn1)

Recent studies on contemporary societies have empirically demonstrated that economic hardship and insecurity influence political orientations, perceptions, and policy preferences in several domains ([Margalit 2013](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib41), [2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib42); [Gidron and Hall 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib17); [Rooduijn and Burgoon 2018](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib55); [Kaihovaara and Im 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib27); [Marx 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib45); [Marx and Picot 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib47); [Vlandas 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib70)). One of the topics that has received the closest attention in the contemporary literature is the effect that job insecurity, and labour market risks more generally, have on individuals’ support for radical parties. The main focus has certainly been on the radical right (see, among others, [Betz 1994](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib4); [Kitschelt 1997](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib29); [Lubbers et al., 2002](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib40); [Rydgren 2005](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib59), [2007](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib60); [Coffé et al., 2007](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib9); [Kriesi et al., 2006](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib32), [2008](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib33); [Lindvall and Rueda 2014](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib38); [Abou-Chadi and Kurer 2021](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib1)); but some studies have also assessed the impact of related variables on support for the radical left (e.g. [Gomez and Ramiro 2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib18), [2022](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib19); [Sipma et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib67)).

The relationship between job insecurity and support for radical parties has been explained by different mechanisms. In the first place, job insecurity has been argued to be linked to negative attitudes towards political actors and institutions. Thus, for instance, [Zhirnov et al. (2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib72) argue that insecurity increases populist attitudes, which in turn lead to greater support for populist parties. In a similar vein, although not explicitly focusing on job insecurity, [Marx (2020)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib45) shows that situations of financial vulnerability can lead to anger, which is, in turn, associated with greater support for radical and populist parties.

Secondly, job instability has been shown to be associated with preferences for income redistribution and policies that protect individuals from market shocks and help them overcome situations of financial need ([Iversen and Soskice, 2001](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib84); [Rehm 2009](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib52); [Marx 2014](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib43); [Fernandez-Albertos and Manzano 2016](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib14)). As these are policies that are strongly advocated by radical-left parties, the general expectation is that job insecurity will be associated with stronger support for the latter ([Sipma et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib67)).

Finally, job insecurity might also increase negative reactions towards certain out-groups, such as immigrant workers, if these are blamed for one's precarious situation ([Sipma et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib67)). Current academic debates about the rise of RRPs in Western societies revolve around whether it is economic factors or cultural anxieties, or an interaction between both, that explain this phenomenon ([Healy and Lenz, 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib79); [Oesch and Rennwald 2018)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib51).[2](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#fn2) For example, the inter-relationship between economic hardship and cultural threat is central to the ‘globalization losers’ literature ([Kriesi et al., 2006](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib32), [2008](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib33)), which argues that an important part of the radical-right electorate in Western Europe is comprised by individuals who perceive their social status, economic and employment security, and their cultural/national identity to be threatened by greater international competition, market risks and immigration ([Betz 1994](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib4); [Kriesi et al., 2006](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib32), [2008](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib33), [2012](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib34); [Kitschelt 2013](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib30); [Rydgren 2013](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib61); [Kitschelt and Rehm 2014](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib31); [Rooduijn 2015](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib54)).

However, [Kurer (2020)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib35) argues that that researchers need to distinguish between status anxiety and material anxiety, both of which may affect individuals in precarious working conditions. Material anxiety can be prompted by the threat of losing one's job, receiving an irregular source of income or struggling to make ends meet, and is likely to lead individuals to support parties that advocate generous social policies and the expansion of the welfare state. In contrast, status anxiety is not necessarily related to people's material needs or job insecurity, but, rather, with the perception of having lost (or being at risk of losing) one's social status to other social groups, either individually or in comparison with previous generations. It is status anxiety, rather than the perception of job insecurity, that creates hostility towards other groups and can push individuals to oppose social and cultural change and, thus, to support the radical right (e.g., [Jost et al., 2003](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib75), [Küpper et al., 2010](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib87), [Riek et al., 2006](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib76); [Gidron and Hall 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib17)).

Empirical findings are mixed in the literature, although researchers have often focused on very different aspects of insecurity and attempted to measure them in different ways (see more on this in the next section). Nevertheless, several studies have found job insecurity to be associated with support for non-mainstream parties that propose more generous redistributive policies rather than the radical right. Thus, [Marx and Picot (2013)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib46) and [Marx (2016)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib44) find fixed-term workers to be particularly prone to support new left parties and the radical left, although not in every country. Their findings are consistent with [Lindvall and Rueda's (2014)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib38), who show that, when the Social Democrats lose support among ‘outsider’ workers, the latter tend to either vote for the radical left or abstain. Nevertheless, evidence that job precariousness might sometimes be associated with support for the radical right also exists. For instance, [Mughan et al. (2003)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib49) find subjective job insecurity to be associated with support the radical right in Australia. [Abou-Chadi and Kurer (2021)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib1), who focus on individuals who live with a partner, also find unemployment risks within the household (measured indirectly as the occupational unemployment rate of individuals within a household) to be associated with a higher probability of voting for RRPs. However, [Häusermann (2019)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib22) does not find workers in insecure jobs or in low-skill offshorable occupations to be more likely to support the radical right in eight West European countries. Other studies have, however, found less conclusive evidence. [Sipma et al. (2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib67), which we comment on in detail in the next section, reach different conclusions depending on whether they use objective or subjective indicators. [Antonucci et al. (2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib3), who focus on fears associated with job insecurity, find the latter to be related to greater support for the radical right in France, but not in the Netherlands (and not for the radical left in either country, although their classification includes parties like France's centre-left Radical Party of the Left, The [Ecologists](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/social-sciences/ecologist), and the Dutch Party for the Animals). Lastly, [Zhirnov et al. (2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib72) find a significant and positive relationship between ‘precarity of tenure’ (which comprises perceived risk of unemployment, subjective working-hours insecurity and anxiety about losing one's job) and voting for the populist right in Germany and the Netherlands, but a significant and negative relationship in Hungary and Poland, and a non-significant relationship with different signs in other countries. For the populist left, they find a positive association, but it is only statistically significant in Italy, where the party they classify as populist left had no representation. However, as [Zhirnov et al. (2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib72) only focus on populist parties (which, on the left, excludes Communist and other non-populist RLPs) and do not provide direct effects without controlling for variables such as individuals' satisfaction with different aspects of their job, their results are not fully comparable with those of other studies.

### 1.2. Objective or perceived job instability?

Something that stands out in our discussion of the literature is the diversity of ways in which job insecurity has been approached and measured. Research in this area has relied on either objective or subjective indicators, but little attention has been paid to the advantages and disadvantages of each approach.

All indicators of job insecurity have pros and cons. While objective indicators are less affected by potential endogeneity in studies of vote choice, they have several potential shortcomings. On their own, objective indicators do not allow researchers to establish if any connection between the former and vote choice stems from individuals’ reaction to the insecure nature of their employment or something else. For example, when researchers look at labour market position (employed or unemployed, employed with an open-ended or fixed-term job, employed full-time or part-time, etc.), they tend to use that as a proxy for job insecurity. However, besides being characterised by the risk of unemployment, many insecure jobs share other characteristics, such as the lack of social status, that can have potential political consequences. Fixed-term workers are more likely to be used to fill low-status positions in firms, to be viewed as outsiders by their co-workers, and to feel socially excluded ([Gundert and Hohendanner, 2014](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib20)). As discussed in the previous section, while status anxiety has been hypothesized to lead to greater support for the radical right, job insecurity is argued to lead to greater support for left-wing policies. On their own, objective indicators do not fully enable researchers to separate between those two phenomena.

A second, but related, potential shortcoming of objective indicators is that job insecurity is really difficult to observe in an objective manner, and even individuals who share the same labour market position may have a different unemployment risk depending on the firm, occupation, industry and labour-market context in which they find themselves.

This problem of measuring unemployment risks is ubiquitous in the insider-outsider literature, which is arguably one of the most influential strands of research dealing with the political consequences of job insecurity. Insider-outsider theory focuses on individuals' position in the labour market, and distinguishes between labour market ‘insiders’ (those with secure, protected and stable jobs) and ‘outsiders’ (those with flexible, insecure and less protected jobs) ([Rueda 2007](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib58)). However, these two groups have been operationalized in a variety of ways ([Vlandas 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib70)).

Given the difficulties of capturing the diverse range of factors leading to job instability, some studies have attempted to measure this phenomenon by focusing on indirect, albeit objective, measures of occupational-specific risks. This approach consists in grouping employed and unemployed individuals that share the same occupation and assigning them a given occupational risk, which is usually measured by the unemployment rate or the percentage of ‘offshorable’ jobs in their [occupational group](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/social-sciences/occupational-group) ([Rehm 2009](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib52), [2011](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib53); [Schwander and Häusermann, 2013](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib63); [Walter, 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib83)). However, measuring job insecurity in such an indirect way has several disadvantages. These include the risk of incurring in ecological fallacy, over-relying on occupational categories created for accounting purposes, and classifying as outsiders individuals in well-protected jobs who work in occupations with a high proportion of temporary jobs ([Marx and Picot 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib47)). Moreover, indirect measures could also capture other characteristics of occupations that might be correlated with occupational risks. Finally, although the main independent variable in these studies is measured at a higher level of aggregation than the individual (i.e. the occupational group), this is rarely taken into account in their models, which has long been known to increase downwards bias in standard errors ([Moulton 1990](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib48)).

An alternative, but more direct, approach within the insider-outsider literature consists in focusing on the specific situation of individuals, rather than derived measures, to separate labour market outsiders from insiders. While using direct measures is a more refined approach the problem, the difficulties of objectively capturing different situations of job insecurity have led to multiple definitions of ‘labour market outsiders’. For [Rueda (2005)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib57), this group comprises unemployed individuals and employees with low salaries, low protection, low rights, and/or low social security benefits. Others, however, have defined this group as comprising workers on part-time or fixed-term contracts as well as unemployed individuals ([Emmenegger, 2009](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib81)); employees in high-unemployment occupations ([Rehm 2009](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib52)); or individuals who might most likely experience a situation of vulnerable employment because they belong to an occupational class with high levels of unemployment or atypical employment, or because they are women or young ([Häusermann and Schwander, 2011](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib23); [Schwander and Häusermann, 2013](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib63)).

The different operationalizations of labour market outsiders and insiders have been argued to reflect different types of inequality that contribute to shaping individuals' preferences ([Vlandas 2020](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib70)), but they pose serious problems for analyzing the electoral consequences of dual markets and job insecurity. [Rovny and Rovny (2017)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib56) have shown that, despite the panoply of existing operationalizations, some conclusions are consistent across studies: labour market outsiders tend to vote less, and when they vote, they are less likely to vote for the mainstream right than labour market insiders. Nevertheless, as [Marx and Picot (2020)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib47) argue, focusing on individuals’ labour market status is not only the most transparent way of approaching this phenomenon, but it is also the one that comes closest to the original concept of outsider-insider divides. That said, most studies using labour market status have only found statistically significant effects after pooling a large number of countries and survey waves (or multiple survey waves for a small set of countries). And even so, results are often non-significant or very weakly significant for certain countries (see, e.g., [Marx and Picot, 2014](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib88)). This suggests that, if there is any association between labour market status and vote choice, this might be weak and far from universal.

Whilst most research on job insecurity and voting has focused on objective indicators, these have been contested by more recent studies (see, e.g., [Antonucci et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib3); [Zhirnov et al., 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib72)). Besides the disadvantages highlighted above, one recurrent argument is that job insecurity is a subjective concept that refers to individuals' uncertainty about losing their job ([Sverke and Hellgren 2002](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib68)). While some observed characteristics, such as labour market status, are associated with different underlying probabilities of losing one's job, they fall short of actually measuring individuals' degree of insecurity. As [Allan et al. (2021)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib73) argue, it is important to separate the individual experience of job insecurity from people's objective work situation, not only because subjective experiences tend to be better predictors of people's emotional and psychological responses, but also because insecurity can be found across a range of occupations and contractual situations. While open-ended contracts may put some individuals' minds at ease, they do not prevent them from perceiving unemployment risks. Conversely, individuals on temporary contracts may not always perceive a high risk of unemployment. For example, they might perceive their temporary status as a necessary stepping-stone towards a likely permanent job. Or they might consider it easy to find consecutive fixed-term jobs, either with the same employer[3](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#fn3) or with a different one, with no (or a very brief) transition period. Moreover, even though the insider-outsider literature has often only focused on employed individuals (but see [Jansen, 2017](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib25); [2019](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib26)), job insecurity also affects many self-employed people, including the growing sector of solo self-employed and dependent self-employed individuals.

Although indicators of subjective job insecurity allow us to directly tap into individuals’ actual perceived insecurity in a way that objective indicators of labour market status cannot, they are not free from problems. One of their main disadvantages is that, as with many other variables used to study vote choice (starting with issue positions and salience), they can be affected by endogeneity because respondents who vote for certain parties may have a gloomier view than others of their own unemployment prospects. In his study of preferences for redistribution, [Marx (2014)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib43) shows that the relationship between insecurity and policy preferences persists even after removing respondents with a strong party identification or ideological position. This is a demanding test but, of course, it does not completely eliminate the potential [endogeneity problem](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/social-sciences/endogeneity-problem) of subjective measures.

In light of this, [Marx and Picot (2020)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib47) recommend examining objective and subjective measures jointly, which is the approach adopted in this article. This strategy should enable researchers to better understand when insecurity works but labour market status does not make a difference, either because outsiders feel relatively secure or because insiders also feel insecure. Moreover, as discussed earlier, labour market status could also have a direct impact on [voting behaviour](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/social-sciences/voting-behavior) that is not mediated by perceived insecurity but by other variables (e.g. status anxiety).

Interestingly, there is still very little research examining the relationship between vote choice and perceived job insecurity, and virtually no studies attempting to assess whether subjective indicators perform better than objective indicators of labour market status as predictors of support for radical parties. [Antonucci et al. (2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib3) and [Zhirnov et al. (2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib72) represent one of the very few studies to look at subjective indicators in a comprehensive manner, but they do not include objective indicators of job insecurity in their models. Moreover, as mentioned earlier, [Zhirnov et al. One recent study that does examine objective and subjective indicators jointly is [Sipma et al. (2023)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#bib67). However, they only assess objective indicators in interaction with social class. Moreover, their dependent variable, a reported past vote measure, was collected anywhere between months and years after the relevant election took place. Despite the good quality of the datasets they employ, this analytical strategy is likely to contain a great amount of noise, as variables related to job insecurity are, by definition, very prone to change. Sipma et al.'s (2023) findings suggest that, while objective indicators are associated with support for the radical left among working-class voters, subjective perceptions of job insecurity are associated with support for the radical right (an outcome that they themselves qualify as surprising). They justify this seemingly contradictory finding by arguing that people who do not have an objectively insecure job but perceive their job situation to be insecure may not necessarily favour redistributive policies, as that would probably benefit other individuals more than it does them.[4](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0261379424000842#fn4) But this deserves further examination
