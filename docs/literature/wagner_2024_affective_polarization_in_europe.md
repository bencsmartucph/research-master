## Key Takeaways

1. **Affective polarization in multi-party systems requires different measurement approaches than in two-party systems:** "While in the USA two-party system, researchers can simply look at the gap between the in-party and the out-party to assess affective polarization, applying the concept to multi-party systems such as those predominant in Europe poses immediate challenges."
2. **The US is not uniquely high in affective polarization but shows a clearer increasing trend than many European countries:** "First, affective polarization in the USA is by no means unusually high... However, the USA is a clear example of increasing affective polarization, but this trend is not typical of all contexts."
3. **Partisan identities in Europe often form larger "affective blocs" rather than single-party identifications:** "While partisan divides in Europe are more diverse, studying patterns of affect reveals the existence of broader political camps: parties form 'affective blocs'... For example, parties on the left that often form coalitions together may not be seen as rivals but as a larger in-group ('the left')."
4. **Radical right parties generate uniquely strong negative affect in European systems:** "A party family that elicits particularly strong reactions are the radical right... Gidron, Adams and Horne (2023) demonstrate that radical-right dislike is even higher than their ideological distance and lack of government participation would predict."
5. **The relationship between affective polarization and democratic health is complex:** "Affective polarization has been found to correlate with democratic backsliding (Orhan, 2022), possibly because partisan identities and out-party hostility mean voters are less likely to sanction undemocratic behaviour by politicians they support... However, the research on the link between affective polarization and democratic stability is still in its infancy. Positive effects – such as on political engagement... and on exclusion of anti-democratic actors... still need to be explored more fully."

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### 1. Social Identity Theory in Partisan Contexts

**Detailed explanation:**
Social identity theory forms the backbone of research on affective polarization. This theory, originally developed by Henri Tajfel and John Turner, proposes that group membership creates in-group/out-group categorization that leads to favoritism toward one's own group and potential bias against perceived out-groups. In the context of affective polarization, political parties become significant social groups that shape identities. Wagner discusses how this identity-based approach has been applied to understand partisan affect in European contexts, where multiple parties and declining partisan identification present a more complex landscape than the US two-party system.

The paper argues that in Europe, partisan identities often cluster into larger ideological "camps" or "affective blocs," whereby parties that are ideologically similar or frequently cooperate in government are perceived as part of a broader in-group. This adaptation of social identity theory helps explain why, despite the fragmentation of European party systems, patterns of affective polarization still often reduce to a smaller number of distinct camps. The paper also connects partisan identity to other social identities and cleavages, suggesting that affective polarization is likely rooted in deeper social divisions.

**Direct quotes:**
"Moreover, as in the USA, positive partisanship in Europe is not merely instrumental, but also is often strongly expressive, relating to deeply held identities (Huddy et al., 2018)."

"Parties form 'affective blocs' (Kekkonen and Ylä-Anttila, 2021; Bantel, 2023). For example, parties on the left that often form coalitions together may not be seen as rivals but as a larger in-group ('the left')."

"Most importantly, research on affective polarization in Europe shows that partisan labels still mean a lot to voters: when people see or hear that someone supports or votes for a party, this creates strong in-group bias (Westwood et al., 2018; Helbling and Jungkunz, 2020), at least as strong as ethnic group favouritism."

**Associated literature:**
The paper cites foundational work by Huddy et al. (2015, 2018) on partisan social identity and its expressive (rather than purely instrumental) nature. It references adaptations of social identity measures for multi-party contexts by Bankert et al. (2017). The connection to the classic work on cleavages by Lipset and Rokkan (1967) demonstrates how current research on affective polarization connects to longstanding theories about group-based political conflict in Europe. The work of Mason (2016) on social sorting in the US is also referenced as an important comparison point.

**Application to research:**
This theoretical framework could be highly relevant to your research on far-right party support. The concept of "affective blocs" suggests examining not just individual far-right parties in isolation, but how they fit into broader political alignments. The finding that radical right parties face uniquely strong out-group hostility could help explain barriers to their electoral growth or coalition potential. The social identity perspective also suggests investigating how welfare or labor policies might be viewed through partisan identity lenses rather than just economic self-interest.

### 2. Negative Partisanship Framework

**Detailed explanation:**
The negative partisanship framework focuses specifically on out-party hostility rather than the full spectrum of affective polarization. This theoretical approach emphasizes that negative attitudes toward political opponents can be a stronger driver of political behavior than positive attachments to one's preferred party. Wagner notes that in European contexts, negative partisanship offers a useful lens that doesn't require the simultaneous presence of positive partisan identification, which is declining in many European democracies.

This framework is particularly relevant for understanding attitudes toward radical right parties in Europe, which often generate strong negative affect even among citizens who don't have strong positive attachments to other parties. The theory suggests that political identities can be formed around what one opposes as much as what one supports. Interestingly, Wagner notes that negative partisanship toward democratic challengers like radical right parties can actually have positive effects on democratic resilience, creating a normative complexity to evaluating affective polarization.

**Direct quotes:**
"Negative partisanship is largely a synonym of negative out-party affect, but can also encompass a negative party identity, where individuals define themselves by what they are not (Bankert, 2021). The concept therefore turns the spotlight away from polarization per se, that is, the spread of affect. Moreover, negative partisanship does not require the simultaneous presence of positive feelings or identification towards an in-party."

"However, a key finding is that negative partisanship in Europe is often towards the radical right (Meléndez and Kaltwasser, 2021; Bjånesøy, 2023), and in this case, negative partisanship can however be a bolster for democracy (Meléndez and Kaltwasser, 2021)."

**Associated literature:**
The paper references work by Bankert (2021) on measuring negative partisanship, and studies by Mayer (2017) and Meléndez and Kaltwasser (2021) applying the concept to European contexts. It connects to research by Ridge (2022) on how negative partisanship affects democratic satisfaction, and Bjånesøy (2023) on negative partisanship toward the radical right.

**Application to research:**
For research on far-right support, the negative partisanship framework offers a complementary perspective to traditional explanations focused on positive attraction to these parties. It suggests examining the "cordon sanitaire" phenomenon where other parties and voters actively oppose radical right parties. Understanding how welfare policies might be linked to negative partisanship (e.g., specific policies that create strong opposition) could provide insights into polarization dynamics around redistribution preferences.

### 3. Institutional Theory of Affective Polarization

**Detailed explanation:**
Wagner identifies an important theoretical perspective that links institutional arrangements to patterns of affective polarization. This framework suggests that the structure of political systems—including electoral rules, party system fragmentation, and coalition governance patterns—shapes how citizens experience partisan affect. The institutional theory moves beyond individual-level explanations to examine how macro-level factors condition the development and expression of affective polarization.

A key insight from this theoretical perspective is that coalition governance may reduce affective polarization by creating cross-cutting cooperation between parties. When parties govern together, their supporters develop more positive feelings toward each other, an effect that persists even after coalitions end. This suggests that institutions can moderate the tendency toward partisan hostility by creating incentives for elite cooperation. The comparative advantage of studying multiple European systems is that researchers can examine how different institutional configurations relate to varying levels and patterns of affective polarization.

**Direct quotes:**
"Moreover, one advantage of comparative research on affective polarization is the possibility of examining how it relates to macro-level factors. Studies on affective polarization in Europe thus highlight how institutional arrangements influence affective polarization: for instance, majoritarian political systems lead to contexts less conducive to elite cooperation, perhaps creating a better foundation for inter-party hostility (Gidron et al., 2020)."

"Institutional contexts shape elite behaviour, and how elites interact is clearly central for understanding affective polarization (McCoy and Somer, 2019; Bassan-Nygate and Weiss, 2022; Bäck et al., 2023). Thus, there is observational and experimental evidence that coalitions between parties can reduce the extent to which partisans dislike each other: supporters of parties that govern together feel more warmly towards each other, even controlling for ideological closeness (Horne et al., 2023). Interestingly, this interpartisan warmth persists even after the coalitions end: both current and past co-governance positively influence inter-party affect."

**Associated literature:**
The paper cites work by Gidron et al. (2020) comparing institutional effects across democracies, and research by Horne et al. (2023) on coalition governance and its impact on inter-party affect. It also references studies by McCoy and Somer (2019) and Bäck et al. (2023) on elite behavior and affective polarization, and experimental work by Praprotnik and Wagner (2023) on coalition signals.

**Application to research:**
This theoretical perspective offers valuable insights for research on far-right support and redistribution preferences. Examining how different welfare state institutions might moderate affective polarization around redistribution questions could yield important findings. Similarly, understanding how the institutional inclusion or exclusion of far-right parties affects affective polarization and support for these parties could inform research on radical right electoral dynamics.

### 4. Normative Theory of Democratic Implications

**Detailed explanation:**
The paper introduces an important theoretical discussion about the normative implications of affective polarization for democratic health. This framework grapples with whether affective polarization should be viewed as inherently problematic or whether it can sometimes serve positive democratic functions. Wagner challenges the common assumption that affective polarization is always harmful to democracy, suggesting a more nuanced view that considers both negative and positive potential effects.

On the negative side, the theory suggests that affective polarization may undermine democratic norms by reducing voters' willingness to sanction undemocratic behavior by politicians they support. It may also decrease social and institutional trust and exacerbate winner-loser gaps in democratic satisfaction. However, the normative theory also identifies potential positive effects, including increased political engagement and the democratic benefit of strong opposition to anti-democratic actors. Particularly in the European context, where radical right parties may pose challenges to liberal democratic values, negative affect toward these parties might actually be normatively desirable from a "militant democracy" perspective.

**Direct quotes:**
"One fear is that affective polarization may damage democracy, with McCoy and Somer (2019) and McCoy et al. (2018) even including affective polarization as part of a broader set of 'pernicious' developments that endanger democratic quality and stability. Affective polarization has been found to correlate with democratic backsliding (Orhan, 2022), possibly because partisan identities and out-party hostility mean voters are less likely to sanction undemocratic behaviour by politicians they support, partly because of the increased perceived stakes of electoral outcomes (Ward and Tavits, 2019)."

"For example, negative party identity towards the radical right is associated with strong support for democratic values (Meléndez and Kaltwasser, 2021). Here, it is important to relate affective polarization to the concept of militant democracy: some forms of negative affect are arguably legitimate when it comes to defending core democratic and liberal values (Capoccia, 2013)."

"This discussion becomes relevant when thinking about ways to reduce affective polarization. In the USA, research on ways to reduce affective polarization and partisan hostility has progressed a lot, allowing for robust inferences on what works and what does not (Hartman et al., 2022; Voelkel et al., 2023). Nevertheless, there is a need for a lot more research on this in multi-party systems. What is important is that research also focuses on the downstream consequences of affective polarization that should be prevented (Brauer, 2023). It is not clear, for example, that measures to reduce affective polarization improve democratic attitudes (Broockman et al., 2023; Voelkel et al., 2023), and it is perhaps those we should care about more than simple negative affect."

**Associated literature:**
The paper references work by McCoy and Somer (2019) and McCoy et al. (2018) on "pernicious polarization," and research by Orhan (2022) on democratic backsliding. It cites Meléndez and Kaltwasser (2021) on negative partisanship and democratic values, and Capoccia (2013) on militant democracy. Recent experimental work by Broockman et al. (2023) and Voelkel et al. (2023) on interventions to reduce affective polarization and their democratic effects is also discussed.

**Application to research:**
This normative framework has important implications for research on far-right support and redistribution preferences. It suggests examining not just the extent of polarization around welfare policies, but also the democratic consequences of such polarization. Research could investigate whether strong opposition to far-right welfare proposals has positive or negative effects on democratic attitudes and behavior. The normative complexity highlighted by Wagner suggests avoiding simplistic assumptions about whether reducing polarization should always be the goal.
