This paper investigates public attitudes toward "wage buyouts" - government programs that pay workers to stop working when their industries create negative externalities (such as environmental damage). The research examines competing theories explaining resistance to such programs: one viewing work primarily as income generation versus another seeing work as a source of identity and social recognition. Through three survey experiments with over 7,500 U.S. respondents, the author finds that while many workers resist being freed from work (even with full compensation), this resistance varies systematically. Program design matters significantly: respondents prefer compensation from employers over government and partial hour reductions over complete work cessation. Surprisingly, workers in threatened industries (from automation or offshoring) show lower job identification and greater openness to wage buyouts. The findings challenge assumptions about blue-collar workers' attachment to their jobs and reveal both partisan and Protestant religious influences on attitudes toward work cessation, with important implications for climate policy and technology-driven labor transitions.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Many workers resist being "freed" from work, even with full compensation**
    
    "Overall, a sizeable portion of workers are resistant to being freed from work. Strikingly, individuals are more willing to reduce their work hours by half than to cease work entirely, even when offered full compensation." This finding indicates work provides value beyond income, challenging purely economic views of labor.
    
2. **Program design matters significantly for acceptability of wage buyouts**
    
    "Program design matters: respondents are significantly more likely to accept compensation from their former employer than from the government." The source of compensation and whether programs propose complete versus partial work reduction greatly affects acceptance rates.
    
3. **Contrary to expectations, workers in threatened industries are more receptive to wage buyouts**
    
    "Those whose occupations are threatened by structural factors like offshoring or automation show lower identification with their jobs, and are accordingly more willing to accept government compensation for giving up those jobs." This challenges theories suggesting external threats strengthen identity attachment to occupations.
    
4. **Politics and religion influence attitudes toward wage buyouts in different ways**
    
    "While partisanship drives attitudes towards government handouts, it is not significantly associated with attitudes towards (non)work. On the other hand, Protestants show consistently greater resistance to non-work, even when this results from a lottery windfall." Protestantism appears to have a deeper influence on work ethic than political ideology.
    
5. **Demographic factors traditionally thought important (gender, race, rural/urban location) show minimal effects**
    
    "Finally, while the ethnographic literature highlights the relationship of men to their work, we find no consistent difference between men and women in their attitudes towards (non)work." This finding, along with others showing limited effects of race and geography, undermines common narratives about certain demographics having special attachment to work.
    

## Introduction

The introduction establishes the growing prevalence of wage buyout programs and positions them within contemporary policy challenges. It begins with a compelling case study of the Netherlands' program to pay dairy farmers to stop farming to reduce nitrogen emissions, highlighting the strong political backlash despite the economic rationality of such programs.

"In an effort to manage carbon emissions, nitrogen runoffs, and other externalities, governments are increasingly resorting to a drastic policy measure: paying workers to stop working. Though such initiatives may be, strictly speaking, welfare enhancing, they are increasingly politicized, and often meet with protests."

"The Dutch government learned this at its expense when its proposal was met with nation-wide protests. One of the affected workers neatly summed up the prevailing feeling among the protesters, declaring that 'I don't want a bag of money from the government. I just want to do my job.'"

"Meanwhile, an earlier poll suggested that a full 40% of Dutch farmers would in fact be willing to abandon their business if offered the right compensation."

The introduction further establishes the conceptual tension driving the research: do workers primarily view work as a means to earn income (making buyouts theoretically appealing), or do they see work as a source of identity and social recognition (making buyouts potentially offensive)? The author positions this question within broader contemporary issues including decarbonization, technology-driven labor displacement, and the political instrumentalization of work identity by right-wing parties.

## Theoretical Framework: Work and Non-Work

This section establishes the theoretical foundations by contrasting two ideal-type theories about the meaning of work and how they predict attitudes toward wage buyouts.

### Work as a Means

"Classical thinkers considered work as an unfortunate necessity that had to be endured. In Aristotle's Nichomachean Ethics, work is ideally avoided to allow for more worthwhile human ends like leisure and philosophy."

"In this respect, neoclassical economics shares the assumptions of classical philosophy. Most neoclassical labor supply models posit that individuals derive utility from consumption and leisure, while work is considered a disutility."

"Under this premise, if workers could receive the same income without having to work, they should always prefer maximal leisure."

The "work as means" theory draws from classical philosophical thought and neoclassical economics, both of which view work primarily as an instrumental activity for earning income. This theoretical approach predicts that workers would readily accept wage buyouts that offer equivalent compensation without requiring labor, as it would maximize their utility by removing the disutility of work while preserving its financial benefits. This view also treats income as fungible—the source of income (employer versus government) should be irrelevant to rational actors. The theory further suggests that workers in physically demanding occupations should be especially open to wage buyouts since they experience greater disutility from work.

### Work as an End

"A more recent literature, with roots in sociology and social psychology, offers a contrasting view that highlights the intrinsic benefits of work. In this telling, work is a means by which individuals form a personal identity. It shapes their social networks and it offers a sense of meaning within a community."

"In opposition to the economic literature, these intrinsic benefits are often said to come precisely from hard work—they arise not in spite of the difficulty of physically demanding and sometimes risky occupations, but precisely because of it."

"As Hochschild (2018) insists on describing the views of her Louisiana interview subjects, work is precisely not about the result: 'Hard' is the important idea. More than aptitude, reward, or consequence, hard work confers honor."

The "work as end" theory draws from sociological and social psychological research suggesting work provides intrinsic benefits beyond income: identity formation, social networks, meaning, and recognition. Under this view, the difficulty and physical demands of work can actually increase workers' attachment to it, as they derive dignity and honor from hard work. This theory predicts resistance to wage buyouts, especially among workers in physically demanding "blue collar" jobs, as they gain particular identity value from their occupations. It also predicts that the source of income would matter significantly, with government payments being viewed as less desirable than employer compensation.

### Attitudes to Work in the Face of Structural Threats

"One set of studies suggests that external threats lead to entrenchment: workers identify more strongly with their industry in ways that might make them more resistant to transitioning away from it."

"A competing set of studies leads to the opposite expectation. Threats to job security, in particular, have been shown to reduce industry attachment through an individual-level coping mechanism."

"This may be further suggestive evidence that as pressure on an industry grows—be it market pressure, regulatory pressure, or technological pressure—workers may grow more, rather than less open to exiting the workforce."

The author introduces two competing theories about how workers respond to structural threats to their industries. One perspective suggests that threats increase identification with threatened industries (entrenchment), while the alternative suggests threats weaken attachment as workers anticipate transitions. This theoretical debate has important implications for the timing of wage buyout programs - whether resistance would increase or decrease as externality awareness grows.

### The Politics of Wage Buyouts

"Right-wing populist leaders, in particular, are taking aim at measures which they claim are imposed by technocratic elites who fail to appreciate the value of a job to hard-working individuals."

"These populist leaders have taken up the narrative that work is an end in itself: they have capitalized on the sense among working class voters that the values they hold dear—among them an ethic of hard work, self-sacrifice, and contribution to one's local community—are being debased, in favor of cosmopolitan ideals held by liberal elites."

"It follows that the view of work as a source of significant intrinsic benefits, combined with the incentives of populist political entrepreneurs, does not bode well for wage buyout schemes."

This section examines how the meaning of work has become politically charged, with right-wing populist politicians leveraging narratives about work identity when opposing policies related to environmental protection and labor transition. These political dynamics shape perceptions of wage buyouts and potentially determine their viability in democratic settings. The author seeks to disentangle whether partisan differences reflect underlying values about work or simply opposition to government programs per se.

## Methodology

The research employs three pre-registered survey experiments on combined samples totaling over 7,500 U.S. respondents:

1. **Study 1** (n=2,450): Conducted in January 2022 through Respondi, focused on employed individuals.
2. **Study 2** (n=2,092): Conducted in August 2022 through Prolific, included both employed and unemployed respondents.
3. **Study 3** (n=3,145): Conducted in May 2023 through Bilendi, also included both employed and unemployed respondents.

All samples were selected to meet population quotas by age, gender, census region, and education.

### Treatment 1: Varying the Source of Compensation

"How willing would you be to stop working at your current job for the foreseeable future in exchange for a monthly check [from your former employer / government] equivalent to your full current salary? [1: Not willing at all — Very willing: 10]"

This experiment tests whether the source of compensation (employer versus government) affects workers' willingness to accept wage buyouts. The experiment included two treatment arms and a control group that received no information about the source.

### Treatment 2: Partial versus Full Redundancy

"Imagine a new government program that would continue paying your current salary and health insurance, but you have to [stop working / work half the hours you work currently] to claim these benefits."

This experiment tests whether workers prefer a partial reduction in hours versus complete work cessation, even when both options provide the same compensation.

### Treatment 3: Government Program versus Lottery Windfall

"[Lottery windfall] Imagine you have won the lottery. The amount of the prize is enough to maintain your exact current income, adjusted for inflation, for the rest of your life. Would you decide to stop working your current job?"

"[Government program] Imagine a government program that would pay you a monthly amount equivalent to your current income, adjusted for inflation, for the rest of your life. The government program requires you to stop working your current job. Would you agree to join this program?"

This experiment compares attitudes toward stopping work from a government program versus a lottery windfall to determine whether resistance stems from attitudes toward government programs specifically or attitudes toward non-work more generally.

### Key Variables

The study includes numerous variables to test theoretical predictions:

1. **Political ideology/partisanship**: Measured on a left-right scale (0-10) and through party identification
2. **Education**: As a proxy for skill level
3. **Income**: Reported personal income
4. **Gender**: Male/female
5. **Rural/urban settings**: Self-reported location type
6. **Manufacturing**: Industry indicator
7. **COVID-19 Impact**: Self-reported impact of the pandemic
8. **Automation/Offshoring Risk**: Both objective measures (Routine Task Intensity and Offshorability scores) and subjective assessments
9. **Subjective Social Standing**: Using the MacArthur Scale
10. **Occupational Identity**: Ranking of job importance relative to other identity traits
11. **Religious Affiliation**: Particularly Protestantism versus other denominations

## Findings and Analysis

### Baseline Attitudes Towards Wage Buyouts

"Most people appear at least somewhat ambivalent about giving up their work in exchange of a full paycheck. Just under 32% of respondents are fully willing to accept the compensation scheme, while over 8% are entirely unwilling, with the remainder of the sample distributed in between."

"One quarter of respondents would be more likely to refuse than to accept the scheme; 69% of respondents would be more likely to accept than to refuse it."

"At the top of the list is mining, with manufacturing not far behind. Arts, entertainment, and recreation, and Information industries are at the bottom—as are Agriculture, Forestry, Fishing and Hunting, from which draw the motivating example from the start of the article."

The distribution of responses shows most people are open to wage buyouts, yet significant resistance exists. Industry-level analysis reveals mining and manufacturing workers are most supportive of wage buyouts, while workers in arts, information, and agriculture are least supportive. However, variation appears primarily at the individual rather than industry level.

### Treatment 1 Results: Source of Compensation

"Being told that the check is coming from government significantly reduces favorability towards the scheme, with an average of 6.39 on the 0-10 scale. Being told that the check is coming from a former employer significantly increases approval, with an average approval of 7.07."

"The difference between both treatments amounts to a large substantive effect, equivalent to that of varying respondents' ideology from the sample mean (5) to its furthest extreme (10)."

"Conservatives are far less favorable to the scheme than liberals, and Republicans are far less favorable than Democrats. Yet interestingly, the magnitude of the treatment effects are not statistically different between the two groups."

The source of compensation matters significantly - respondents prefer employer-based compensation over government-based compensation. Political ideology is strongly associated with attitudes toward wage buyouts, but other demographic factors (race, gender, skill level) show minimal relationships. Manufacturing workers are actually more favorable to wage buyouts, contrary to theoretical expectations about blue-collar occupational identity.

### Vulnerability to Structural Forces

"Whether it is exposure to automation or offshoring, and whether it is measured objectively or subjectively, respondents who are more prone to job losses due to these forces are also more favorable to wage buyout schemes."

"Individuals who report higher subjective social standing are significantly less likely to accept a wage buyout scheme."

"Exposure to structural forces that make individuals more likely to be targeted by wage buyout schemes are associated with more, not less, openness to these schemes."

The findings contradict the entrenchment theory: workers more vulnerable to automation and offshoring are significantly more open to wage buyouts. Higher social standing correlates with resistance to wage buyouts, supporting the idea that economic precarity increases openness to labor transitions.

### Variation in Occupational Identity

"The two most consistent drivers associated with occupational identity are education and race: as per the implications of Lamont (2002) and Williams (2019), white respondents show greater identification with their work. The same is true for high-skilled respondents."

"As opposed to claims in some ethnographic accounts of work, men, and those living in rural areas, do not appear to identify more strongly with their work compared to other personal traits. Manufacturing workers, if anything, show a negative relation, though it does not cross the significance threshold."

"Vulnerability to structural forces like automation and offshoring decreases the extent to which individuals' identify with their work."

Contrary to prevalent narratives, education and race have stronger effects on occupational identity than gender, rurality, or manufacturing work. Economic vulnerability consistently weakens occupational identity, suggesting external pressure on industries may reduce resistance to wage buyouts over time.

### Treatment 2 Results: Partial vs. Full Redundancy

"Specifically, the average level of approval for decreasing working hours by half is 8.17 on the 0–10 scale, while the average approval for ceasing work entirely is 6.86."

"Insofar as workers are simply trying to maximize the ratio of compensation to working hours, one would expect that a maximum reduction of working hours, keeping compensation fixed, would be viewed most favorably. But if respondents attach importance to the social status that comes from holding a job, the conception of themselves as a working individual, and the sense of belonging they draw from workplace, then they might be willing to forfeit an additional reduction of working hours to preserve these values."

"These results lean strongly in favor of policy approaches to wage buyouts that focus on the intensive rather than the extensive margin, reducing working hours rather than removing individuals from their work environments entirely."

Respondents significantly prefer partial work hour reductions over complete work cessation, even with equivalent compensation. This finding strongly supports the "work as end" theoretical model that views work as providing social and identity benefits beyond income.

### Treatment 3 Results: Government Program vs. Lottery Windfall

"Republicans are significantly more wary of a government wage buyout; yet they appear no less open to ceasing work following a lottery windfall."

"By contrast, Protestantism is consistently associated with reluctance to cease work under both schemes. Protestants are also significantly more negatively disposed to the government scheme than to the lottery windfall, yet perhaps most striking is that Protestant religious affiliation appears as the strongest predictor of the decision to (not) cease work following a lottery windfall."

"Catholicism, by comparison, sees strictly no effect, suggesting this attachment to work is not driven by broad Christian values, but by specifically denominational ones."

The comparison reveals Republicans resist government programs rather than non-work per se, while Protestants consistently resist both work-cessation options. This finding highlights that religious values (particularly Protestant ones) may influence work ethic more deeply than political ideology in the U.S. context.

## Implications and Conclusions

"The first finding shows just how reluctant workers are to be 'freed' from the burden of work. Three survey studies conducted on a total of over 7,500 U.S. respondents suggest that many workers are ambivalent about giving up their work, even if they could continue drawing their full salary."

"Yet this baseline attitude to wage buyouts conceals considerable heterogeneity. First, while partisanship is associated with a strong aversion to wage buyouts, this seems driven more by attitudes against government programs, and less by attitudes to (non)work per se, suggesting that careful program design could help overcome the partisan divide over wage buyouts."

"In correcting for the orthodox economic view of jobs-as-income, the risk is that scholars and the media may both fall prone to a romanticization of the economic heartland, portraying blue collar and manufacturing workers as unresponsive to economic incentives, owing to an idiosyncratic attachment to work for work's sake."

The research has several key implications for policy and theory:

1. **Program design matters significantly**: Wage buyout programs structured through employers rather than government, and those focusing on reducing hours rather than eliminating jobs, will face less resistance.
2. **Partisan-based resistance is malleable**: Republican resistance to wage buyouts stems more from anti-government attitudes than opposition to non-work, suggesting alternative program designs could gain cross-partisan support.
3. **External pressure on industries may reduce resistance over time**: The finding that workers in threatened industries show lower occupational identity and greater openness to wage buyouts suggests resistance may decrease as externality awareness grows.
4. **Economic incentives remain powerful**: Despite sociological literature emphasizing working-class attachment to jobs, the findings indicate that economic considerations still matter significantly across all demographic groups.
5. **Future challenges may involve white-collar workers**: As AI increasingly threatens knowledge economy jobs, the higher occupational identity among these workers may create more resistance to necessary labor transitions.

## Relevance to Current Research Topics

### Support for Far-Right Parties and Redistribution Preferences

This paper offers valuable insights into how work identity influences political attitudes. The finding that right-wing voters primarily resist government-led labor transitions rather than non-work itself helps explain far-right mobilization around certain economic policies. The paper demonstrates how far-right parties instrumentalize narratives around work identity to oppose environmental and labor transition policies, even when those policies include compensation. This provides evidence that far-right supporters may not intrinsically oppose redistribution itself, but rather government intervention in the labor market.

### Welfare/Labor/Social Investment Policies

The research has direct implications for policy design in several domains:

1. **Climate policy implementation**: The findings suggest ways to structure decarbonization policies to minimize resistance, particularly focusing on partial work reduction and employer-mediated compensation.
2. **Technology transition policies**: As AI disrupts labor markets, the paper indicates that workers in threatened industries may be more receptive to transition assistance than commonly assumed.
3. **Program design principles**: The substantial difference in response to government versus employer-based compensation, and to partial versus full work cessation, provides concrete guidance for designing more acceptable labor transition policies.
4. **Religion and welfare attitudes**: The finding that Protestantism strongly influences work attitudes beyond partisan identification adds nuance to our understanding of welfare state preferences in different cultural contexts.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to understanding public attitudes toward labor policy in contexts of environmental transition and technological disruption. By challenging prevailing assumptions about working-class attachment to jobs while still acknowledging the non-economic value of work, it provides a more nuanced foundation for designing acceptable labor transition policies in democratic contexts.
