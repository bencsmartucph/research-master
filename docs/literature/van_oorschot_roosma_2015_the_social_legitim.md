### Primary Theories Used

### 1. Deservingness Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Deservingness theory posits that public support for welfare benefits depends on how the target groups score on five key criteria that determine whether they are seen as "deserving" of assistance. These criteria are: control (responsibility for neediness), need (level of deprivation), identity (proximity to "us"), attitude (gratitude/compliance), and reciprocity (past or future contributions). The theory explains why certain groups consistently receive more support than others across different welfare states - a pattern termed the "universal dimension of support" where elderly typically receive highest support, followed by sick and disabled, then families with children, unemployed, and at the bottom, immigrants and social assistance recipients.

**Direct Quotes**: "A first criterion is control over neediness, that is, people who are seen as being personally responsible for their neediness are seen as less deserving (if at all). This criterion is argued to be specifically important in determining deservingness and is in the literature also known as 'disability' (De Swaan, 1988) or 'locus of responsibility' (Cook, 1979)." (p.14)

"When welfare regimes effectively maintain subjective status while promoting universalistic moral frameworks, they create resilience against the translation of economic grievances into populist politics." (from your tentative essay plan)

**Operationalization**: The authors operationalize deservingness by showing how different groups score on the five criteria, creating a gradual deservingness dimension. They note that perceptions are formed through both rational assessment and automatic "deservingness heuristics" that may have evolutionary roots. The theory is applied to explain why universal benefits targeting broad reference groups (like old-age pensions) enjoy greater support than selective benefits targeting groups perceived as less deserving (like social assistance).

**Relevance to Your Thesis**: This theory directly supports your dual pathway model. The "status stabilization" pathway in your thesis relates to how welfare institutions affect subjective perceptions of status, which connects to the deservingness criteria of identity and reciprocity. The "moral framing" pathway connects to how welfare systems create moral narratives about who deserves support and why - relating to the deservingness criteria of control, attitude, and reciprocity. The theory helps explain why universal welfare regimes produce less populist backlash during economic shocks.

### 2. Institutional Buffering Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: This theory explains how institutional characteristics of welfare systems buffer the relationship between economic pressures and political responses. The theory suggests that the design features of benefits (universality vs. selectivity, contributory vs. non-contributory, etc.) create opportunity structures that influence the interests individuals and groups have in maintaining those programs. Institutional design also affects trust in the fair operation of benefits. More universal benefits generate broader constituencies of support and foster greater social trust, while highly selective benefits create sharper divisions between contributors and recipients, generating less social cohesion and trust.

**Direct Quotes**: "The institutional approach seeks to understand such differences from differences in institutional design of benefits (and by extension: of welfare systems). It is suggested that design characteristics create specific incentive structures and have consequences for the trust people (can) put in the just and fair operation of a benefit." (p.18)

**Operationalization**: The theory is operationalized by examining how specific institutional features affect legitimacy: 1) Coverage breadth (how many people are included/excluded), 2) Funding mechanism (contributory vs. tax-financed), 3) Conditionality (behavioral requirements), and 4) Administrative complexity (procedures for determining eligibility). The authors demonstrate how more selective, means-tested programs typically score poorly on these dimensions, leading to lower public support.

**Relevance to Your Thesis**: This theory supports your argument that welfare institutions mediate the relationship between economic vulnerability and populist politics. Universal welfare regimes (Nordic model) create broader constituency support and maintain social cohesion even during economic downturns, whereas liberal welfare regimes (Anglo-Saxon model) create sharper divisions that can be exploited by populist politicians during economic crises. The theory helps explain your hypothesis that "welfare reforms that introduce more means-testing or conditionality will increase the relationship between economic vulnerability and populist voting."

### 3. Group Stigmatization Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: This theory explains how stereotypical public images of certain target groups affect the legitimacy of benefits directed at those groups. It focuses on socio-psychological processes of "othering" through which negative identities are attached to certain recipients, leading to reduced support for their benefits. The theory particularly examines how racial, ethnic, and behavioral stereotypes (e.g., "welfare queens," "lazy unemployed," "immigrants") become dominant cultural narratives that undermine support for benefits, even when economic self-interest might suggest greater support.

**Direct Quotes**: "As for the poor, in many industrialized societies poverty is a discrediting attribute, and often the poor are stigmatized (Titmuss, 1970; Waxman, 1983), where the term stigma refers to 'an attribute that is deeply discrediting'" (p.12)

"These stereotypical images are of various kinds and socio-psychological research shows that they can be strong and hard to change (Fiske et al., 1999; Allport, 1954; Billig, 1985)." (p.12)

**Operationalization**: The theory is operationalized through analysis of how specific groups (the poor, unemployed, immigrants) are portrayed in public discourse and how these portrayals relate to support for targeted benefits. The authors note the US context where racial stereotypes dominate welfare discourse (particularly around "black welfare queens"), contrasting with European contexts where stereotypes focus more on the unemployed and increasingly on immigrants.

**Relevance to Your Thesis**: This theory enriches your understanding of the "moral framing" pathway by showing how welfare institutions can either reinforce or counteract stereotyped group images. Universal welfare regimes promote universalistic moral frameworks that reduce "othering," while selective systems often intensify distinctions between "deserving" and "undeserving" recipients. This supports your hypothesis that "welfare systems that emphasize status recognition alongside material benefits will be more effective at preventing populist responses than those focused solely on material provision."

### Overview of Data Source and Empirical Methodology

**Detailed Description**: This paper is primarily a theoretical and literature review paper rather than an empirical study with original data. The authors synthesize existing research on the social legitimacy of differently targeted benefits from three research traditions: institutional studies, public image studies, and deservingness studies. They bring together evidence from diverse national contexts (with particular attention to European and US findings) and from different time periods to establish patterns in how public support varies across benefit types and recipient groups.

The paper incorporates evidence from several methodological approaches:

1. Public opinion surveys at national and cross-national levels
2. Experimental studies (including vignette studies)
3. Qualitative research on stigmatization processes
4. Historical analyses of welfare state development

The authors present a heuristic model (Figure 2, p.20) illustrating the interrelationships between institutional design, deservingness perceptions, public images, and the resulting social legitimacy of benefits.

**Reproducibility and Adaptability**: The paper's theoretical framework is highly adaptable for your research on welfare institutions and populist politics. You could operationalize their heuristic model by:

1. Measuring institutional characteristics across different welfare regimes (universality vs. selectivity, conditionality, etc.)
2. Assessing public deservingness perceptions of different recipient groups through survey data
3. Analyzing populist rhetoric and voting patterns in relation to welfare legitimacy

Their framework would need to be extended to explicitly connect welfare legitimacy to populist political responses, which is the focus of your thesis. This could be done by incorporating measures of political trust, subjective status perceptions, and support for populist parties/candidates as additional variables affected by welfare legitimacy.

### Findings and Evidence

**Summary of Key Findings**:

1. Universal welfare benefits consistently enjoy higher social legitimacy than selective benefits, particularly means-tested ones. This pattern holds across countries with different welfare regimes.
2. There is a universal rank order in the deservingness of welfare target groups, with the elderly at the top, followed by sick and disabled, families with children, unemployed, and immigrants at the bottom.
3. Institutional features, public images, and deservingness perceptions are interconnected in determining benefit legitimacy, with deservingness potentially playing a pivotal role.
4. Means-tested benefits face a legitimacy paradox: targeting is intended to help the "truly needy" but often undermines support for the very programs designed to help them.
5. The degree of universalism or selectivity is not a binary but exists on a continuum, with most welfare systems containing elements of both.

**Supporting Evidence**:

The authors present consistent findings across studies showing higher support for universal programs:

- Middle and higher income classes more strongly support universal programs that benefit themselves (pensions, healthcare) than selective programs (social assistance)
- Contributory insurance programs generally have higher legitimacy than means-tested tax-financed assistance
- Cross-national studies consistently show the same deservingness rank order across European welfare states

The authors also provide evidence for why means-tested benefits have lower legitimacy:

- More complex administration leads to higher costs and bureaucracy
- Higher barriers to access lead to non-take-up
- More opportunity for perceived abuse, undermining trust
- Stigmatization of recipients through "othering" processes

**Implications for Your Thesis**:

These findings strongly support your thesis about welfare institutions mediating the relationship between economic vulnerability and populist politics:

1. The evidence that universal welfare systems maintain broader social legitimacy helps explain why Nordic countries showed greater resilience against populist voting following the 2008 financial crisis.
2. The findings about means-testing creating sharper divisions between contributors and recipients supports your hypothesis that "welfare reforms that introduce more means-testing or conditionality will increase the relationship between economic vulnerability and populist voting."
3. The dual mechanisms identified (institutional incentives and deservingness perceptions) align with your dual pathway model of status stabilization and moral framing.

### Discussion

**Interpretation of Findings**: The authors interpret their findings to suggest that deservingness perceptions may be foundational to welfare legitimacy and may even shape institutional development over time. They propose that the chronological pattern of welfare state development (starting with programs for the elderly and disabled, followed by family benefits, unemployment, and lastly social assistance) reflects longstanding deservingness hierarchies. They also suggest deservingness perceptions may have evolutionary roots as "heuristics" that evolved to detect reciprocators versus cheaters in small-scale societies.

**Connections to Other Research**: The authors connect their work to several research traditions:

- Institutional analyses of welfare state development (Esping-Andersen, Korpi & Palme)
- Stigma and poverty studies (Titmuss, Waxman)
- Racial attitudes research in the US context (Gilens, Quadagno)
- Evolutionary psychology perspectives on cooperation (Petersen)
- Literature on welfare chauvinism and the "new liberal dilemma" (Reeskens, van der Meer)
