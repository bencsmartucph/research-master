# 2025 paper by Van Hootegem Formative Personal Experiences: How Benefit Recipiency and Income Changes Shape Perceptions of System Abuse”  By demonstrating that becoming a welfare recipient reduces perceptions of benefit abuse through identification processes, while income increases can raise perceptions of tax evasion through "othering" processes (especially among those with low initial incomes), they reveal the complex ways in which citizens' views of welfare legitimacy are shaped by their own life trajectories.

    # **Introduction**
    
    Social protection is one of the core responsibilities of governments in contemporary Western democracies, but it is also one of its central areas of political contestation. Not only was the question of redistribution at the heart of the traditional class cleavage in politics (Lipset & Rokkan, [**1967**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0042); Roller, [**1995**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0059)) but welfare politics also remain contested and electorally salient (Bonoli & Natali, [**2012**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0004); Green-Pedersen & Jensen, [**2019**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0020); Kumlin & Goerres, [**2022**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0039)). Yet, beyond the supply side of politics, welfare state issues are pivotal to voters as well. Citizens often gain insight into the functioning of political systems through their interaction with the welfare state, which makes its legitimacy tightly coupled to broader political trust (Haugsgjerd & Kumlin, [**2020**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0025)) and satisfaction with democracy (Sirovátka et al., [**2019**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0065)). A critical aspect of this legitimacy, as well as part of current political debates, concerns questions about the extent to which others abuse or cheat the system (Andersen, [**1997**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0001); Roosma et al., [**2016b**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0061)). Besides being an integral part of the welfare populist agenda (De Koster et al., [**2013**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0009)), perceptions of system abuse are essential for belief in a system that delivers a just distribution of burdens where everyone contributes their fair share (Rothstein, [**1998**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0062)).
    
    However, despite the clear political relevance of these perceptions of system abuse, they are ill-understood and insight into their driving forces is lacking. While studies have highlighted the relevance of political narratives among, for instance, the radical right that pit hard-working citizens against unproductive ‘Others’ who avoid paying taxes or abuse the welfare system (De Koster et al., [**2013**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0009); Kochuyt et al., [**2023**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0035)), we know relatively little about what shapes citizens’ perceptions of system abuse beyond the supply side of politics. While some studies focus on how socio-economic status and ideology relate to these types of perceptions (Edlund, [**1999**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0012); Goossen et al., [**2021**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0019); Marriott, [**2017**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0047); McArthur, [**2021**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0050); Roosma et al., [**2016b**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0061)), other determinants are insufficiently explored. As a result, this paper puts forward an alternative explanatory framework for perceptions of system abuse that helps to understand how they arise and persist, namely: personal experiences. Although various scholars have turned to personal experiences in order to explain political sentiments and support for redistribution (Hacker et al., [**2013**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0023); Kumlin, [**2004**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0037); Margalit, [**2013**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0044); Naumann et al., [**2016**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0053)), they have rarely been called upon to dissect how people think about social groups (Danckert, [**2017**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0007)).
    
    It is crucial to understand whether formative personal experiences can shape these perceptions that are at the heart of debates on the legitimacy of the political system, as they tell us whether idiosyncratic experiences can lead citizens to update their views and attitudes or whether they are set in stone through social and political socialization (Mau, [**2004**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0048); Staerklé et al., [**2012**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0068)). Even though these experiences embody psychological, social, economic and administrative dimensions, understanding their collective effect provides the answer to whether such experiences can activate political learning and identification processes. This could incite enduring modifications in views that go beyond individuals’ immediate circumstances and could thus be determinative for people's perceptions of others’ behaviour (Margalit, [**2019**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0045)). In other words: can, beyond abstract representations or discourses, actual experiences and everyday realities of citizens be informative of their views (Patrick, [**2016**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0056); Shildrick & MacDonald, [**2013**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0064))?
    
    To study this in more detail, we look at how people's experiences that bring them closer to the groups often associated with fraud influence these individuals’ perceptions of system abuse conducted within these groups. As people undergo transforming conditions and experience a shift in their economic and social status, they could adjust their views on the system as well as on the behaviour of similar others. Based on theory, two different mechanisms could be at play in linking egotropic considerations to legitimacy views. On the one hand, individuals could start to identify more closely with the groups they approach, thereby reducing perceptions of system abuse through these formative personal experiences (Maassen & De Goede, [**1991**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0043); McArthur, [**2019**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0049); Roosma et al., [**2016b**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0061)). On the other hand, citizens could distance themselves from similar others who are deemed undeserving (i.e., ‘othering’), which could go hand in hand with increased perceptions of misconduct (McArthur, [**2019**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0049); Patrick, [**2016**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0056); Shildrick & MacDonald, [**2013**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0064)). To analyse which of these opposing theoretical propositions is most accurate, we formulate the following primary research question: *How do formative personal experiences inform perceptions of system abuse?*
    
    In studying this relationship, we extend available research in three ways. First, two dimensions of system abuse are studied: perceptions of benefit abuse and tax evasion. Current literature usually only focuses on the ‘recipiency side’ by diving into perceptions of welfare benefit over- and underuse (Andersen, [**1997**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0001); Goossen et al., [**2021**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0019); McArthur, [**2019**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0049); Roosma et al., [**2016b**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0061)), while disregarding misconduct on the ‘contribution side’ (Edlund, [**1999**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0012); Marriott, [**2017**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0047)). Yet, both are equally important for a perceived fair balance of burdens and benefits and thus the legitimacy of the political system (Rothstein, [**1998**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0062)). Second, instead of following the dominant focus on disadvantageous experiences, positive changes are also included in our analysis of perceptions of system abuse (Gugushvili, [**2016**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0021); Jaime-Castillo & Marqués-Perales, [**2019**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0031)). Positive and negative formative experiences could have distinctive impacts, and it is hence relevant to disentangle both. Last, the longitudinal nature of our panel survey data supplies an important contribution to the literature. In contrast to many existing studies that use cross-sectional data to solely study differences between groups, our analytic approach uses repeated measures from individuals via fixed effects models to also approximate the causal impact of formative personal experiences on perceptions of system abuse (Heise, [**1970**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0026); Keele, [**2015**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0034)).
    
    For our empirical analysis, three-wave panel data (2014–2017) from Norway are used (Kumlin et al., [**2017**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0038)), which directs attention to formative personal experiences in an encompassing and social democratic welfare state (Esping-Andersen, [**1990**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0014)). The role of experiences is usually tested in liberal states with a less encompassing social safety net, where they could hence have a larger influence (Jaeger, [**2006a**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0029); Margalit, [**2013**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0044); Rehm et al., [**2012**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0058)). In this sense, testing their role in a country with stronger social protection and higher expenditure rates might provide a more stringent test of the role of changes in the life situation (Naumann et al., [**2016**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0053)). Before elaborating on the data and methods, the theoretical framework is outlined in more detail.
    
    # **Theoretical framework**
    
    ### Perceptions of system abuse: Benefit abuse and tax evasion
    
    To study how formative personal experiences affect perceptions of system abuse, we look at two separate perceptions that cover the recipiency and contributory side of welfare, respectively. Our perceptions of interest represent dimensions that shed light on different aspects of how individuals or groups can cheat the system: welfare benefit abuse and tax evasion. Despite highlighting distinct characteristics, these two dimensions have in common that they are both financial crimes with similar victims, namely the state and society in general (Marriott, [**2017**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0047)). In addition, both perceptions are equally crucial in determining the legitimacy of the welfare state and political system more broadly (Roosma et al., [**2016a, 2016b**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0060%20#ejpr12685-bib-0061); Rothstein, [**1998**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0062)). Besides believing in the capacity of welfare policies to implement benefits efficiently and effectively without abuse in order to consider them as just, citizens should also consider a just distribution of burdens, whereby everyone contributes their fair share. This perception that others are not free-riding and contributing sufficiently is crucial for consenting to the tax system generally and for accepting one's own burden (Liebig & Mau, [**2007**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0041)).
    
    However, there are also a number of characteristics that are fundamentally different for the two types of perceptions of system abuse. To begin with, to study benefit abuse perceptions, we look particularly at perceptions of overuse, which imply that people take up benefits they do not deserve, instead of underuse, which encompasses the non-take-up of benefits (Roosma et al., [**2016b**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0061)). Given this focus on overuse, benefit abuse here is especially oriented at the ‘demand’ or ‘recipiency’ side of the welfare state, whereby questions arise as to whether certain groups are taking out too much in relation to what they contribute to the system. In this sense, those who commit benefit abuse are seen as ‘takers’ and the gaze is often oriented towards the ‘receiving side’ in society that benefits from redistribution. In addition, despite the common denominator, perceptions of tax evasion also have several distinct characteristics. Tax evasion is more tied to the ‘supply’ or ‘contribution’ side of the welfare state, as it implies not giving the money that you owe to the state instead of actively taking resources out of the system (Marriott, [**2017**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0047)). While resources are still illegitimately denied from society and the social system, tax evasion can be more strongly perceived as keeping the assets citizens have earned or worked for themselves. This is also inextricably bound up with a perception of tax evasion as a ‘white-collar crime’, which is seen as something that is mostly done by those who are on the contributing side of society (Croall, [**2001**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0006); Marriott, [**2017**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0047)).
    
    In sum, both dimensions of system abuse shed light on different types of social groups (recipients vs. contributors) that are important in relation to abuse perceptions and welfare legitimacy.
    
    ### Conceptualizing formative personal experiences
    
    To understand these perceptions, we introduce the concept of *formative personal experiences*, which can be defined as personal events that trigger a series of psychological, social and economic processes which may change an individual's outlook on the world around them. These experiences can be layered in the sense that they, for instance, can both encompass an administrative change as well as a psychological or economic shift in one's personal situation. In the present analysis, we are interested in whether a holistic personal experience – regardless of the multitude of layers involved – results in a new outlook on perceptions of system abuse. Understanding the role of these formative personal experiences is crucial to gain knowledge on whether citizens can still update their views on political issues based on their own relevant events or whether socializations in political narratives set unchangeable precedents.
    
    To study this, we mirror the divide in perceptions of system abuse between the demand and supply side of the welfare state in the operationalizations of formative personal experiences. Specifically, we argue that becoming a benefit recipient can alter perceptions of benefit abuse, and experiencing income change can affect views on tax evasion. Research has tried to disentangle how precariousness and experiences of disadvantage consolidate into various types of welfare and political attitudes (Danckert, [**2017**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0007); Hacker et al., [**2013**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0023); Naumann et al., [**2016**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0053); Rehm et al., [**2012**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0058); Soss, [**1999**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0067)). However, as an additional dimension of personal experiences, income increases are also examined as a positive formative personal experience that has the potential to shape perceptions of tax evasion. Although representing positive versus negative experiences that involve different ends of the welfare state – the *recipiency* versus *contribution* side – the two experiences also share certain characteristics. They both imply a change of material means and social status to varying degrees. However, they do differ in a number of points.
    
    The experience of dependence on welfare benefits places individuals within society's lower social strata, highlighting how adverse experiences or possible grievances can shape perceptions of benefit abuse. Despite the relief provided by high replacement rates of income which cushion dramatic material blows, welfare beneficiaries endure a demoted social status and associated stigma compared to the working population. This is largely due to their reliance on redistributive state mechanisms financed by contributors, and the fact that their deservingness is continuously evaluated (Laenen & Roosma, [**2022**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0040); van Oorschot, [**2000**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0070)). Moreover, becoming a benefit claimant unfolds as a particularly layered personal experience (which is less so in the case of income changes). While this research cannot delve deeply into this division due to empirical constraints, it is essential to recognize that becoming a benefit claimant involves an interplay between personal and administrative experiences. First, a grievance occurs for which the benefit is sought, such as becoming unemployed, disabled or sick – an experience that can be dramatic and hence profoundly formative in itself. Second, the administrative experience of claiming and retaining benefits can influence attitude formation. This is true both in terms of explicitly categorizing the claimant into a specific social group with similar experiences and in terms of the potential fairness or lack thereof in the administrative procedure (Danckert, [**2017**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0007); de Blok & Kumlin, [**2022**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0008)). Factors deemed impactful on procedural fairness outcomes, such as respectful treatment (Rothstein, [**2009**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0063)) and the ability to voice input into the process (Hirschman, [**1970**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0028)), may also sway how welfare beneficiaries see themselves or their group.
    
    Naturally, an experience of a positive income change is directly associated with ascending towards the welfare contributors in society, especially in progressive tax systems where income increases imply higher tax rates and hence larger contributions. This is quite the opposite of descending into welfare dependency, which encompasses becoming part of the recipiency side of state support. Moreover, experiencing a financial upturn is not typically entwined with extensive administrative procedures or interactions with policy enforcement. Although income increases could potentially affect taxation, the requirement to pay taxes applies practically to everyone. Nevertheless, increasing material means may kindle a sense of boosted social status, possibly instigating a conscious shift into a new social group adherence – that of the contributors to society. However, the overarching experience of a positive income change is associated with a more diffuse social grouping compared to the starker demarcation connected with transitioning into welfare dependency.
    
    ### The mechanisms of formative experiences: Identification or ‘othering’?
    
    In the literature, the role of experiences is usually framed by self-interest theory, which posits that citizens support the policies that maximize their personal benefits and material interests (Jaeger, [**2006b**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0030); Kangas, [**1997**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0033)). The argument is that support for redistribution and the political system is, for instance, higher among groups experiencing disadvantageous transitions, as they have a stronger interest in an encompassing state that protects them against social risks (Helgason & Rehm, [**2023**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0027); O'Grady, [**2019**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0055)). However, the literature does not provide a suitable theoretical framework for the link between formative personal experiences and perceptions of system abuse. Moreover, the commonly applied rational choice arguments do not adequately address the dependent variable. As a result, a distinct theoretical framework is required that can explain how personal experiences might impact people's perceptions of system abuse. Although other mechanisms are certainly viable as well, we focus on theories that can help explain the psychological processes through which benefit recipiency and income increases link to perceptions of system abuse. In this regard, two distinct mechanisms are possible: formative experiences could lead to stronger *identification* with groups who may commit abuse or could instil a sense of *‘othering’*.
    
    First, the identification perspective predicts that the closer people move towards the situation of the concerned groups, the less they will perceive abuse due to an increased capacity to put themselves in their shoes (Maassen & De Goede, [**1991**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0043); McArthur, [**2019**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0049); Roosma et al., [**2016b**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0061)). When people's objective positions make them belong to certain groups or at least occupy a more similar status, they can start to identify with these groups as a type of self-serving bias that gives their own situation more meaning and a sense of belonging. According to social identity theory, this identification then leads individuals to attribute more positive characteristics to the groups they belong to (Danckert, [**2017**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0007); Tajfel, [**1981**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0069)). The positive bias should lead to weaker perceptions of these groups cheating the system. This can be further strengthened by social learning processes, whereby the formative experience could help individuals understand more about the causes of their situation and recalibrate their own interests and ideas (Danckert, [**2017**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0007); Margalit, [**2013**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0044)). In this sense, stereotypical images of groups who are believed to cheat the system could be challenged when people start to belong more strongly to these respective groups.
    
    Alternatively, scholars have pointed to processes of ‘othering’ instead of identification. ‘Othering’, sometimes referred to as self-group distancing, encompasses a process whereby individuals increasingly differentiate and demarcate between groups, and distance themselves (‘the deserving’) from those with a similar social status who are considered to be immoral and undeserving of acquiring public resources (Patrick, [**2016**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0056)). As opposed to the identification perspective which is applied to establish a positive self-identity by praising one's group, ‘othering’ is exercised to do the same by introducing distance between oneself and the group one, on paper, belongs to (van Veelen et al., [**2020**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0071)). Derks et al. ([**2016**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0011)) argue that ‘othering’ takes place in three main ways, being (1) physical or psychological distancing from in-group members, (2) presenting oneself as someone not in their objective in-group and (3) endorsing stereotypical criticism of their own group and thus legitimizing the current inter-group hierarchy. Thus, in order to retain a positive self-image, formative personal experiences could also lead to stronger ‘us’ versus ‘them’ divides even within the in-group (Shildrick & MacDonald, [**2013**](https://ejpr.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/1475-6765.12685#ejpr12685-bib-0064)) and hence to stronger perceptions of system abuse due to ‘I'm not like the others’ attitude
    

## Introduction and Theoretical Context

This 2025 paper by Van Hootegem, Helgøy, and Nemčok examines how personal experiences shape perceptions of system abuse in welfare states. The authors investigate whether becoming a welfare recipient or experiencing income changes affects how people view welfare abuse and tax evasion. This research fills an important gap by exploring how individual experiences - rather than just political narratives or socialization - shape perceptions that are fundamental to welfare state legitimacy.

The authors position their work within broader debates about welfare state legitimacy, noting:

> "Perceptions that others will contribute their fair share are fundamental to the legitimacy of the political system. To better understand how these perceptions take shape beyond the influence of political narratives and socializations, this paper investigates the role of the formative personal experiences of benefit recipiency and income changes in explaining views on welfare abuse as well as tax evasion." (p. 72)
> 

The paper's theoretical framing connects to several important scholarly traditions:

1. **Welfare State Legitimacy**: Building on works by Rothstein (1998), Kumlin & Goerres (2022), and others, the authors argue that perceptions of system abuse are crucial to the perceived fairness and sustainability of welfare systems.
2. **Personal Experience Effects**: Drawing on research by Margalit (2013), Kumlin (2004), and Hacker et al. (2013), they explore how personal events shape political views.
3. **Group Identification vs. "Othering"**: The theoretical framework proposes two competing mechanisms for how experiences might affect perceptions.

A key theoretical contribution is their concept of "formative personal experiences," which they define as:

> "...personal events that trigger a series of psychological, social and economic processes which may change an individual's outlook on the world around them. These experiences can be layered in the sense that they, for instance, can both encompass an administrative change as well as a psychological or economic shift in one's personal situation." (p. 75)
> 

The authors explicitly identify the gap in existing literature:

> "...despite the clear political relevance of these perceptions of system abuse, they are ill-understood and insight into their driving forces is lacking. While studies have highlighted the relevance of political narratives among, for instance, the radical right that pit hard-working citizens against unproductive 'Others' who avoid paying taxes or abuse the welfare system (De Koster et al., 2013; Kochuyt et al., 2023), we know relatively little about what shapes citizens' perceptions of system abuse beyond the supply side of politics." (p. 72-73)
> 

## Theoretical Mechanisms

The authors propose two competing theoretical mechanisms that might explain how formative personal experiences affect perceptions of system abuse:

### 1. Identification Mechanism

This perspective predicts that as people move closer to a certain group (welfare recipients or higher-income contributors), they will identify more with that group and perceive less abuse within it:

> "First, the identification perspective predicts that the closer people move towards the situation of the concerned groups, the less they will perceive abuse due to an increased capacity to put themselves in their shoes (Maassen & De Goede, 1991; McArthur, 2019; Roosma et al., 2016b). When people's objective positions make them belong to certain groups or at least occupy a more similar status, they can start to identify with these groups as a type of self-serving bias that gives their own situation more meaning and a sense of belonging." (p. 76)
> 

The authors connect this to social identity theory (Tajfel, 1981), explaining that people attribute more positive characteristics to groups they belong to, which would lead to weaker perceptions of these groups cheating the system.

### 2. "Othering" Mechanism

Alternatively, the authors propose that people might distance themselves from similar others who are deemed undeserving:

> "'Othering', sometimes referred to as self-group distancing, encompasses a process whereby individuals increasingly differentiate and demarcate between groups, and distance themselves ('the deserving') from those with a similar social status who are considered to be immoral and undeserving of acquiring public resources (Patrick, 2016)." (p. 77)
> 

They elaborate:

> "Derks et al. (2016) argue that 'othering' takes place in three main ways, being (1) physical or psychological distancing from in-group members, (2) presenting oneself as someone not in their objective in-group and (3) endorsing stereotypical criticism of their own group and thus legitimizing the current inter-group hierarchy." (p. 77)
> 

This theoretical framework leads to competing hypotheses about both benefit recipiency and income changes:

**For benefit recipiency:**

- H1a: Becoming dependent on welfare benefits decreases perceptions of benefit abuse (Identification)
- H1b: Becoming dependent on welfare benefits increases perceptions of benefit abuse ("Othering")

**For income changes:**

- H2a: Positive income changes decrease perceptions of tax evasion (Identification)
- H2b: Positive income changes increase perceptions of tax evasion ("Othering")

## Norwegian Context

The authors conduct their study in Norway, which they characterize as a relatively stringent test case for their hypotheses due to:

1. A social democratic welfare state with predominantly universal access
2. Generous benefit levels and comprehensive taxation
3. A "culture of trust" with lower suspicions about benefit uptake and reduced stigma

> "In social democratic welfare states characterized by predominantly universal access to welfare services, there tends to be comparatively lower suspicion surrounding benefit uptake and reduced stigmatization compared to their liberal counterparts. This dynamic could generally make perceptions of abuse and changes thereof less likely." (p. 78)
> 

However, they note that despite these contextual factors:

> "...absolute levels of suspicion are still substantial – over half of the sample used in this study believes that benefit abuse and tax evasion are rather or very common in Norway." (p. 78)
> 

This makes Norway a conceptually interesting case that provides a "more stringent test of the role of changes in the life situation" compared to liberal welfare states where personal experiences might have larger effects.

## Results

### Descriptive Findings

The authors first established that perceptions of system abuse are relatively common in Norway:

> "Regarding welfare benefit abuse, only 6.3 per cent of respondents indicated that it is very uncommon, while more than half (53.0 per cent) indicated that it is either rather common or very common." (p. 81)
> 

Similar patterns were found for tax evasion, with 53.4% perceiving it as rather or very common.

### Cross-Sectional Analyses

The between-group analyses revealed:

1. **For benefit recipiency**: Individuals who received welfare benefits perceived approximately 0.1 point lower levels of benefit abuse compared to non-recipients (representing about a 3.3% effect on the 1-4 scale).

> "To begin with, individuals who received a welfare benefit tend to perceive approximately 0.1 point lower levels of benefit abuse, which translates to about a 3.3 per cent effect on a 1-to-4 point, compared to those who did not receive any support." (p. 82)
> 

This finding supports the identification hypothesis (H1a) rather than the "othering" hypothesis (H1b).

1. **For income changes**: Individuals who experienced income increases showed higher perceptions of tax evasion (by approximately 0.1 points, or 3.3%).

> "...individuals who have experienced an income increase exhibit higher rather than lower perceptions of tax evasion and financial misreporting compared to those with a stable income." (p. 84)
> 

This finding supports the "othering" hypothesis (H2b) rather than the identification hypothesis (H2a).

### Fixed Effects Within-Individual Analyses

The within-individual analyses showed:

1. **For benefit recipiency**: Becoming a welfare recipient decreased perceptions of benefit abuse by approximately 0.1 points (p < 0.05), consistent with the cross-sectional findings and supporting the identification hypothesis (H1a).
2. **For income changes**: The general effect of income change on perceptions of tax evasion was small and not statistically significant.

However, when examining heterogeneity by initial income level, the authors found:

> "...an income increase represents a substantially significant formative personal experience for the attitudes within the initially low-income group. Individuals in this group tend to express higher perceptions of tax evasion by approximately 0.8 points, signifying about a 2.7 per cent effect on the provided 1-to-4 point scale." (p. 85)
> 

This finding supports the "othering" hypothesis (H2b), but only for the low-income group.

### Robustness Checks

The authors conducted "flipped models" to test whether the effects extended to out-groups. They found some evidence that welfare recipients also developed weaker perceptions of tax evasion, suggesting a potential spillover effect:

> "It becomes apparent that individuals who start receiving a benefit tend to develop statistically significantly weaker perceptions of tax evasion. This suggests that not only do perceptions towards the group one start to belong to change but there might also be an influence on perceptions of system abuse more broadly." (p. 87)
> 

However, income changes did not significantly affect perceptions of benefit abuse, indicating that "the most consistent effects are observed when examining perceptions about the group one gets closer to" (p. 87).

## Discussion and Conclusions

The authors interpret their findings as supporting different mechanisms for different types of formative experiences:

1. **For welfare recipients**: The identification mechanism predominates, leading to lower perceptions of benefit abuse.
2. **For income increases**: The "othering" mechanism predominates (particularly among low-income individuals), leading to higher perceptions of tax evasion.

They explain this divergence through different attribution processes:

> "Those becoming dependent on welfare benefits tend to attribute their unfortunate circumstances, such as poverty or unemployment, to external factors rather than their individual actions (Furåker & Blomsterberg, 2003; Kallio & Niemelä, 2014). In this sense, benefit recipients may feel that they and similar others ended up in their precarious situation because of collective causes, thereby fostering a stronger sense of group belonging." (p. 88)
> 

In contrast:

> "...those who gain income often attribute this improvement to individualistic achievements and their own hard work, aligning closely with a meritocratic worldview (Mijs, 2019). Particularly among low-income groups, the shift in their opinions regarding tax evasion when experiencing an income increase might reflect a narrative often perpetuated by populist parties." (p. 88)
> 

The broader implications of these findings include:

1. Demonstrating that personal experiences can shape perceptions crucial to welfare state legitimacy beyond political narratives and socialization.
2. Showing that citizens can revise their views when encountering relevant experiences throughout their life course.
3. Revealing different psychological mechanisms at play in how people process different types of life changes.

The authors conclude with a summary of their contribution:

> "All in all, this paper highlights the significance of formative personal experiences as an explanatory framework for understanding perceptions crucial to the legitimacy of the social and political system. While the most consistent evidence points to changes in perceptions regarding the group one starts to belong to, there is also evidence that the formative personal experiences can influence opinions beyond one's own immediate group." (p. 88)
> 

## Theoretical Contributions and Connections

This paper makes several important theoretical contributions:

1. **Formative Personal Experiences Framework**: The authors introduce a framework for understanding how personal events can trigger psychological, social, and economic processes that reshape political perceptions.
2. **Dual Mechanisms**: The identification of both identification and "othering" as mechanisms linking experiences to perceptions enriches our understanding of how people process life changes.
3. **Recipiency-Contribution Distinction**: The paper demonstrates that different dynamics operate for experiences related to welfare recipiency versus income contribution.
4. **Starting Point Matters**: The finding that initial income levels moderate the effect of income changes on perceptions supports Helgason and Rehm's (2023) argument that "the relationship between income and attitudes is anchored by initial income but is incrementally updated as individuals learn more about their realized income group" (p. 77).

The paper connects to several important theoretical traditions:

1. **Social Identity Theory**: By showing how group identification shapes perceptions of similar others, it builds on Tajfel's (1981) work on in-group favoritism.
2. **Deservingness Theory**: The "othering" mechanism relates to literature on how people make judgments about who deserves welfare support (Laenen & Roosma, 2022; van Oorschot, 2000).
3. **Attribution Theory**: The explanation of divergent mechanisms draws on how people attribute causes of their circumstances (internal vs. external).
4. **Political Learning**: The paper supports theories of how citizens update their political views based on personal experiences (Kumlin, 2004; Margalit, 2019).

## Implications for Policy and Politics

While not the central focus, the paper has several implications for policy and politics:

1. **Welfare Design Effects**: The findings suggest that personal experiences within welfare systems shape perceptions of those systems, creating feedback effects that could influence political support.
2. **Populist Politics**: The authors note that findings about "othering" among those with income increases align with populist narratives that "pit hard-working individuals, deemed deserving due to their contributions, against others perceived as not contributing enough to the system" (p. 88).
3. **Trust Building**: If receiving benefits reduces perceptions of system abuse, policies that emphasize the collective nature of social risks might help build legitimacy for welfare systems.
4. **Cross-Cutting Effects**: The finding that benefit receipt can reduce perceptions of both benefit abuse and tax evasion suggests that personal experiences with one part of the welfare state might spill over to attitudes about other aspects.

## Conclusion

Van Hootegem, Helgøy, and Nemčok's paper makes a significant contribution to understanding how perceptions of system abuse develop and change through personal experiences. By demonstrating that becoming a welfare recipient reduces perceptions of benefit abuse through identification processes, while income increases can raise perceptions of tax evasion through "othering" processes (especially among those with low initial incomes), they reveal the complex ways in which citizens' views of welfare legitimacy are shaped by their own life trajectories. The longitudinal design allows for stronger causal claims than much existing research, while the theoretical framework connecting experiences to both identification and "othering" processes provides a nuanced understanding of how citizens update their political perceptions in response to life changes.

The research highlights that welfare state attitudes are not just products of political socialization or narratives but are continually reshaped by personal experiences throughout the life course. This dynamic perspective on attitude formation has important implications for understanding the politics of welfare state legitimacy in contemporary democracies.
