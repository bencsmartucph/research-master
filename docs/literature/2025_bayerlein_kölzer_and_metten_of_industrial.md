The authors note that "The regional decline of manufacturing employment is frequently linked to the rise of right-wing populism in many Western countries. Surprisingly, contributions that directly examine the relationship between industrial decline and electoral support for these parties, as well as its underlying mechanisms, remain scarce."

A key insight from the introduction is that regional industrial decline might also increase support for left-wing populism due to a greater demand for welfare compensation. This perspective broadens the conventional focus on right-wing populism as the sole political response to deindustrialization.

The researchers present their core findings early in the abstract: "Using novel historical data on regional manufacturing employment and geocoded survey data from Germany, we find that long-term actual industrial decline is associated with increased support for left-wing populism but not with support for right-wing populist parties. Rather, we find increased support for right-wing populism in places where manufacturing levels are historically high and industrial decline is looming."

This research makes an important contribution by distinguishing between "actual" and "looming" industrial decline, and by examining how each affects support for both right-wing and left-wing populist parties. The study focuses on Germany, examining the Alternative für Deutschland (AfD) as the right-wing populist party and Die Linke as the left-wing populist party.

## Theoretical Framework

### Geography of Industrial Decline and Populism

The paper begins by defining deindustrialization as "a decline in manufacturing production and employment, mostly resulting from globalisation and technological change since the 1970s." While this phenomenon affects national economies broadly, its impacts are geographically concentrated, often leading to localized unemployment and income loss.

The authors review existing literature on the "geography of discontent" thesis, which suggests that regions most affected by industrial decline become "left-behind places" and framed as major breeding grounds for right-wing populism. These areas continue to lag behind metropolitan areas that have benefited from the transition to service-oriented, knowledge-based economies.

However, the authors point out that "evidence of a direct link between long-term industrial decline and support for populist parties remains scarce." They review studies on Trump's support in the U.S. Midwest and Brexit support in the UK, noting that the evidence is mixed and often contradictory. Some studies suggest that support for right-wing populists like Trump is actually higher in areas where industrial employment remains prevalent, not where it has declined most severely.

### Two Types of Industrial Decline and Electoral Responses

The authors distinguish between two types of "losing out" from deindustrialization, drawing on Kurer's (2020) work:

1. **Actual decline**: Direct economic disadvantage where workers lose manufacturing jobs and either become unemployed or move to lower-paid service occupations.
2. **Looming decline**: A subjective sense of decline and status anxiety among those who remain employed in the diminished manufacturing sector.

"The first type of losing out primarily concerns a direct form of economic disadvantage, in which manufacturing employees, after losing their jobs, are now employed in lower-paid service occupations or have fallen directly into unemployment." This represents tangible material loss.

"The second type of losing out refers to a subjective sense of decline and status anxiety, describing blue-collar workers who managed to remain in the diminished manufacturing sector." These workers face an insecure position despite still being employed, primarily threatened by automation due to the routine nature of their manual occupations.

The authors theorize that these different types of loss lead to distinct electoral responses:

"On the one hand, material loss and objective economic disadvantage, such as low income and unemployment, are often associated with stronger support for left-wing parties. This can be primarily explained by an increased demand for egalitarian and redistributive policies following heightened economic hardship."

"On the other hand, surviving manufacturing workers are often concerned with subjective decline, status anxieties, and economic insecurities." These anxieties can trigger authoritarian and xenophobic attitudes, particularly when other social groups are perceived as competitors for scarce resources and societal recognition. The theoretical framework connects to labor market positions: remaining manufacturing employees as "insiders" who anticipate status decline may support right-wing populism, while those who have become "outsiders" after losing industrial jobs may support left-wing populism with its redistributive policies.

### The German Context

The paper examines Germany as a case study, noting that the AfD finds higher support among low- to medium-skilled production workers and the manual working class. This support is driven by "subjective status deprivations and unrealised status expectations." Conversely, Die Linke finds greater support among those experiencing economic hardship, the precariat class, and labor market outsiders.

"Germany's federal structure and socioeconomic institutions provide an important context for understanding these patterns. Unlike centralised political systems such as those in France or the United Kingdom, Germany's federal structure grants significant regional autonomy, allowing for tailored responses to regional economic challenges."

The authors highlight the heterogeneous development of manufacturing regions in Germany:

"Regions with higher manufacturing employment in the 1970s generally continue to be characterised by higher proportions of manufacturing employment today. In particular, these include regions specialized automotive industries (such as Wolfsburg and Ingolstadt) or mechanical industries (such as many regions in Baden-Württemberg), which have been able to retain their industrial production."

"In contrast, regions that relied on high levels of traditional heavy industrial production in the postwar decades—such as steel, textiles, and shipbuilding in Germany's coastal regions, the Ruhr, or Palatinate—have experienced a sharp decline in manufacturing employment since the 1970s and 1980s."

Critically, the authors note that regions with historically high manufacturing employment now face greater automation risks, while regions that already experienced major manufacturing job losses have fewer remaining jobs at risk of replacement.

## Hypotheses

Based on their theoretical framework, the authors develop two primary hypotheses:

1. **Hypothesis 1**: "The higher the decline in the industrial employment share, the higher the vote share of Die Linke."
2. **Hypothesis 2**: "The higher the historical industrial employment share, the higher the vote share of the AfD."

The first hypothesis connects actual industrial decline to support for left-wing populism, driven by demand for welfare state compensation. The second hypothesis links historical industrial employment (and looming decline) to support for right-wing populism, driven by status anxiety and fear of future economic decline.

## Methodology

### Data Sources and Variables

The authors use a comprehensive dataset covering industrial employment in Germany's 400 districts and district-free cities. Their two main independent variables are:

1. **Actual industrial decline**: Measured as the relative percentage change in industrial employment share from 1970 to 2021.
2. **Historical industrial employment share**: The share of industrial employment in 1970, representing areas where manufacturing was historically dominant.

"The year 1970 was selected as the starting point for the observation period because it marked the onset of severe deindustrialisation processes in many Western economies, particularly in the Federal Republic of Germany."

Data sources include:

- INKAR dataset for current regional manufacturing employment (2017, 2022)
- Schmitt et al. (1994) for industrial employment in 1970 in the Federal Republic of Germany
- Federal Statistical Office (1994) for districts in the former German Democratic Republic

For the dependent variables, they used:

- AfD and Die Linke vote shares in the 2021 federal election (second vote)

Control variables included:

- Regional unemployment levels
- Share of women
- Age structure
- Education level
- Spatial inequality and regional deprivation index (derived from percentage deviation of regional median income from nationwide median)
- A dummy variable for eastern German counties

### Analytical Approach

"In line with our hypotheses, we ran regression analyses at the regional level using a generalised linear model (GLM) as outlined by McCullagh and Nelder (1989). This approach was chosen because the GLM estimator accounts for the fact that the dependent variable in our regional-level model—i.e. the populist vote share in a given region—can only range from 0 to 1 (i.e. 0–100%)."

The authors specify their model as:

PopVote(a,l)(s,r) = α + β₁IndShare(s,r) + β₂IndDecline(s,r) + βⱼχⱼ(s,r) + ω(s)π(s) + ε

With the logit link function:
g(PopVote(r)) = log(PopVote(r)/(1-PopVote(r)))

Additionally, the authors use individual-level logistic regression with survey data from the German Socio-Economic Panel (SOEP) to explore potential mechanisms underlying the aggregate relationships. Key individual-level variables include:

- Automation risk (substitutability of occupation)
- Unemployment status
- Controls: gender, education, age, income satisfaction, East German residence

## Findings

### Regional-Level Results

The key findings at the regional level strongly support both hypotheses:

1. For the AfD (right-wing populism), they find a positive and statistically significant correlation with historical industrial employment share. The coefficient for industrial decline is negative and significant, meaning AfD support is lower in areas with higher actual industrial decline.
2. For Die Linke (left-wing populism), they find a positive and statistically significant correlation with industrial decline. The coefficient for historical industrial employment share is negative.

"According to the predicted marginal effects, moving across the range of the variable from 22.25% historical industrial employment to 72.5% increases the predicted AfD vote share by five percentage points for western German counties and nine percentage points in eastern Germany. This increase is quite substantial, considering the AfD's 2021 vote share of around 10%."

"Moving from an industrial employment decline of –25% (an actual increase) to a decline of 80%, the predicted Die Linke vote share in western German counties increases, ceteris paribus, from 2.6% to 3.8% and from 7.8% to 11.1% in eastern Germany."

The authors find that these patterns hold for both western and eastern Germany, though the correlations are stronger in eastern Germany due to higher vote shares for both parties in that region. The results are also consistent across the 2017 and 2021 federal elections.

### Individual-Level Mechanisms

The authors explore three potential mechanisms that might explain the regional-level associations:

1. **Individual labor market position**: Higher automation risk increases likelihood of AfD support, while unemployment increases likelihood of Die Linke support.
2. **Interaction between individual position and regional context**: The effect of historical industrial employment on AfD support might be stronger among those exposed to automation risk, and the effect of industrial decline on Die Linke support might be stronger among the unemployed.
3. **Sociotropic concerns and contextual effects**: Regional industrial decline might affect voting patterns regardless of individual labor market position.

Their findings on these mechanisms are less conclusive:

"For the AfD, both tables show a positive and statistically significant correlation between an individual's substitutability risk and AfD support, except for model 3 in Table 3, where statistical significance is absent. Focusing on Die Linke, we find that unemployment is not associated with support for the party in a statistically significant manner."

"Regarding the sociotropic mechanism, both tables indicate no significant association between individual vote choice for the AfD and the regional historical industrial employment share. However, the significant negative coefficient for industrial decline in Table 3 suggests that the likelihood of voting for the AfD decreases with higher actual industrial decline of a region."

"Regarding Die Linke, both tables report a positive and statistically significant correlation between regional industrial decline and the likelihood of voting for the party. This suggests that higher regional industrial decline exerts a contextual influence on higher individual support for Die Linke and lower support for the AfD."

Testing the interaction effects, they find: "For the AfD, we find no consistent statistically significant correlation across either election year. Only in 2017 do we observe a positive and statistically significant interaction between individual substitutability and historical industrial employment of a region."

Overall, their individual-level analysis suggests that sociotropic concerns may better explain voting patterns than personal economic circumstances.

## Discussion and Implications

The authors challenge the common narrative that right-wing populism finds greater support in "left-behind places" that have undergone severe deindustrialization. Instead, their findings suggest a more nuanced picture:

"Contrary to popular narratives, higher regional industrial decline is not a significant predictor of greater AfD support but is instead associated with higher support for Die Linke. In fact, the AfD seems to attract support in regions where long-term industrial decline was less severe. However, the AfD enjoys greater support in regions of Germany where manufacturing employment was historically high in the 1970s, reflecting anxieties associated with looming decline."

The regional-level findings strongly support both hypotheses, showing that actual industrial decline correlates with left-wing populist support, while historical industrial employment (and looming decline) correlates with right-wing populist support.

However, the individual-level mechanisms remain somewhat unclear: "The individual-level results show that greater risk of substitutability is indeed related to higher probability of voting for the AfD. However, we cannot uncover a robust correlation between regional-level historical industrial employment and individual-level support for the AfD, nor can we detect an effect through the interplay of the historical industrial employment share and individual-level substitutability risk."

The authors acknowledge several limitations:

1. The theoretical assumptions about the relationship at the aggregate level could only be tested to a limited extent.
2. It remains unclear exactly what attitudes and mechanisms drive greater support for Die Linke in areas that underwent stronger actual industrial decline.
3. The effect of industrial decline on vote choice may be moderated by further regional or individual characteristics not considered in their analysis.
4. The analysis did not account for internal migration, which is particularly relevant because it is often driven by labor market considerations.

## Theoretical Contributions and Connections

This research makes several important theoretical contributions:

1. **Distinction between types of industrial decline**: By differentiating between actual and looming decline, the authors provide a more nuanced understanding of how economic transformations affect political behavior. This builds on Kurer's (2020) "two notions of losing out" but applies it specifically to the context of populist voting.
2. **Challenge to the "left-behind places" narrative**: The findings question the prevalent assumption that right-wing populism thrives in areas most affected by deindustrialization. Instead, it suggests that different forms of industrial change produce different political responses.
3. **Integration of labor market insider-outsider theory**: The paper connects to theories about labor market dualization, showing how different positions in the labor market might lead to different political preferences. This builds on work by Burgoon et al. (2019), Kurer (2020), and Im et al. (2023).
4. **Contribution to understanding sociotropic voting**: While their individual-level analysis was inconclusive, their findings suggest that living in regions with particular economic characteristics affects political preferences regardless of individual circumstances. This connects to the growing literature on contextual effects and sociotropic voting (Enos 2017; McKay 2019; Rogers 2014).

The research highlights the complex interplay between economic change, status concerns, and political behavior. It suggests that the relationship between deindustrialization and populism is more complex than often portrayed, with different types of industrial change leading to different political responses.

## Social Psychological Mechanisms

The paper examines several social psychological mechanisms that might explain how industrial employment patterns affect support for populist parties:

1. **Status anxiety and subjective decline**: The authors argue that surviving manufacturing workers experience subjective status anxiety due to the declining prestige of their occupations and the threat of automation. "While employment as a routine blue-collar worker and physical labour provided societal recognition and status during times of industrial modernity, this is no longer the case in postmodern knowledge economies." These workers find right-wing populist messages appealing as they promise to restore their previously higher status.
2. **Group identity and threat perceptions**: "Ballard-Rosa et al. (2022, 2021) demonstrate that looming economic decline due to deindustrialisation is perceived as a threat to group status and collective identity among members of the U.S. white working class, enhancing authoritarian preferences." The paper connects to research showing how economic threats can activate authoritarian attitudes and xenophobia.
3. **Relative deprivation and social comparison**: The authors note that "male blue-collar workers from the white working class, particularly those without a university degree, increasingly feel marginalised in Western societies. Although their absolute material position has not objectively deteriorated significantly, they feel particularly threatened in their subjective and relative status by an increasingly multicultural society in which migrants, minorities, and female workers are gaining status." This highlights how perceptions of relative status loss, rather than absolute economic conditions, can drive political preferences.
4. **Sociotropic concerns**: The findings suggest that people's voting behavior is influenced by the economic conditions of their communities, not just their personal economic circumstances. "Numerous studies have shown that individuals often link perceptions of their personal economic situation to the economic conditions of their local community." This helps explain why regional industrial decline affects voting patterns even among those not directly affected by job loss.
5. **Divergent perceptions of deservingness**: The paper suggests that different attitudes toward welfare policy may explain divergent political preferences: "Positions of left-wing parties advocating a universal welfare state and unemployment benefits are likely to be unpopular among these voters, as they view beneficiaries of such policies, including the unemployed or immigrants, as 'undeserving and lazy recipients' of social security transfers." This connects to research on welfare chauvinism and perceptions of deservingness.

These social psychological mechanisms help explain the complex relationship between economic conditions and political preferences, showing how objective economic changes are filtered through subjective perceptions and social identities to produce different political responses.

## Methodological Strengths and Limitations

### Strengths:

1. **Novel historical data**: The authors compiled a comprehensive dataset on industrial employment in Germany from 1970 to 2021, allowing them to examine long-term trends across all 400 German districts.
2. **Multi-level analysis**: The study combines regional-level analysis with individual-level survey data, attempting to connect macro-level patterns with micro-level mechanisms.
3. **Appropriate statistical techniques**: The use of GLM with a logit link function properly accounts for the bounded nature of the dependent variable (vote shares).
4. **Robust controls**: The analysis includes relevant controls for regional characteristics that might affect voting patterns, such as unemployment, education, and demographic factors.
5. **Distinction between east and west Germany**: The authors account for the different historical trajectories of these regions and examine how the effects vary between them.

### Limitations:

1. **Inability to directly measure mechanisms**: The authors acknowledge that their individual-level data does not allow them to directly test their proposed mechanisms. "Unfortunately, the SOEP does not allow us to identify individuals who previously worked in the industrial sector and are now unemployed. Consequently, our measure cannot directly capture whether unemployment results from deindustrialisation."
2. **Cross-sectional analysis**: While the authors have historical data on industrial employment, their analysis of voting patterns is cross-sectional, making it difficult to establish causal relationships.
3. **No accounting for internal migration**: As the authors acknowledge, "our analysis did not account for internal migration, which is particularly relevant because internal migration is often driven by labour market considerations, especially among younger cohorts." This could confound the relationship between regional economic conditions and voting patterns.
4. **Limited exploration of alternative mechanisms**: The paper focuses primarily on economic and status-based explanations, with less attention to cultural factors or political supply-side factors that might influence populist voting.

Despite these limitations, the paper provides valuable insights into the complex relationship between industrial change and populist voting patterns, challenging simplistic narratives and offering a more nuanced understanding of how different forms of economic change affect political preferences.

## Conclusion

The paper makes a significant contribution to our understanding of the relationship between industrial employment, deindustrialization, and populist voting. By distinguishing between actual and looming industrial decline, the authors provide a more nuanced picture of how economic transformations affect political preferences.

Their findings challenge the common narrative that right-wing populism thrives in areas most affected by deindustrialization. Instead, they find that right-wing populism (AfD) finds greater support in regions where manufacturing employment was historically high and where the threat of future decline is most salient. Conversely, left-wing populism (Die Linke) finds greater support in regions that have already experienced significant industrial decline, where demands for welfare state compensation are stronger.

The research underscores the importance of distinguishing between different types of economic change and their political consequences. It suggests that economic insecurity can lead to different political responses depending on whether it manifests as realized material loss or anticipated status decline. This has important implications for understanding the rise of populism in advanced industrial democracies and for developing policy responses to address the underlying concerns of different voter groups.

While the paper's individual-level analysis was less conclusive, it points to the importance of sociotropic concerns and contextual effects in shaping political preferences. Living in a region with particular economic characteristics appears to affect voting behavior regardless of individual circumstances, suggesting that addressing regional inequalities may be an important component of responding to populist challenges.

Overall, this research provides a valuable contribution to our understanding of the complex relationship between economic change and political behavior, highlighting the need for nuanced approaches that consider both material conditions and subjective perceptions of status and security.
