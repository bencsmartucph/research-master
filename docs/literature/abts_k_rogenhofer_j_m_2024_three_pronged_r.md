Their central theoretical innovation lies in disaggregating resentment into three distinct but interrelated components—status insecurity, relative deprivation, and powerlessness—and empirically demonstrating how these psychological mechanisms mediate between social positions and two key populist attitudes: anti-elitism and demands for popular sovereignty. Using structural equation modeling on Belgian National Election Study data, they reveal that while socioeconomic disadvantage correlates with populist attitudes, this relationship operates primarily through group-based relative deprivation and feelings of powerlessness rather than individual-level economic anxiety.

### Key Theoretical Takeaways

**1. Resentment as Mediating Mechanism**
The study's most significant contribution is demonstrating that structural characteristics alone cannot explain populist attitudes. As the authors note, "there is almost no evidence that lower socioeconomic status has a direct effect on populist attitudes. Our results indicate that the inclusion of resentment as a mediating factor is crucial to understanding the populist appeal among the losers of globalization" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 14).

**2. Group-Level vs. Individual-Level Grievances**
The research reveals a crucial distinction between individual and collective experiences of disadvantage. "Individual-level status insecurities on populist attitudes is, however, not significant" while "group relative deprivation and feelings of powerlessness are important determinants of populist attitudes" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 12). This finding fundamentally challenges individualistic explanations of populist support.

**3. Politics of Resentment as Value Transformation**
Drawing on Nietzschean theory, the authors demonstrate how chronic powerlessness transforms into what they term a "politics of resentment": "Once resentment transforms into ressentiment, it turns compulsive, vindicative, and is in constant search for targets... Despite being characterized by powerlessness, ressentiment is 'creative' in seeking to alter a society's value system from within" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 6-7).

**4. Anti-Elitism vs. Popular Sovereignty as Distinct Phenomena**
The study reveals important differences between populist attitudes: "Anti-elitism is... strongly correlated with a generalized sense of injustice and impotence, whereas the call for popular sovereignty relates primarily to relative deprivation" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 12).

**5. Emotional Underpinnings Beyond Fear**
The research confirms that "anger is a more likely antecedent of populism than fear" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 17), with their analysis showing that revulsion (as a proxy for anger) significantly predicts anti-elitist attitudes while fear does not.

**6. Expansion of "Losers of Globalization" Thesis**
The authors demonstrate that populist attitudes "can be found across both low and intermediate socioeconomic positions" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 15), complicating simple class-based explanations of populist support.

## Introduction and Motivation Analysis

### Research Problem and Significance

The theoretical puzzle driving this research emerges from persistent inconsistencies in explaining populist attitudes through structural characteristics alone. As Abts and Rogenhofer note, "the alleged link between structural positions, resentment, and populist attitudes continues to spark a lively debate within the literature. The lack of conceptual clarity about resentment's constituent components and a lack of adequate survey measures compounds difficulties in making sense of populist attitudes prevalent among the losers of globalization" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 2).

The authors position their work within broader debates about the emotional foundations of political behavior, specifically challenging the predominant focus on discrete emotions in political psychology. They argue that "we focus less on discrete emotions than on the resentful mechanisms that mediate social transformations and changing intergroup relations, particularly for individuals experiencing a loss of status, a deteriorating sense of group position and an erosion of sociocultural hegemony" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 2).

This research addresses fundamental questions about political economy and democratic governance by examining how economic and social transformations translate into specific political attitudes. The authors situate their work within the "losers of globalization" literature, including Kriesi et al. (2006), Inglehart and Norris (2017), and Goodwin and Heath (2016), while building on qualitative insights from Cramer (2016), Gest (2016), and Hochschild (2016) about the role of resentment in American populism.

The broader significance extends to understanding democratic stability and legitimacy. As the authors observe, "Rather than being driven by a sense of subjective political efficacy (Rico et al., 2019), we hypothesize that populist attitudes are motivated by experiences of perceived injustice and powerlessness" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 2). This challenges conventional assumptions about democratic participation and suggests that populist attitudes may represent a fundamental critique of liberal democratic institutions rather than merely policy preferences.

### Theoretical Positioning and Innovation

The authors position their theoretical contribution at the intersection of political sociology and social psychology, building on but extending beyond existing work on populism and emotions. They explicitly distinguish their approach from the discrete emotions literature, noting that while they "agree with the elevation of anger over fear as a possible determinant of populism, our reasoning differs from that offered by the abovementioned scholars" (Marcus et al., 2019; Rico et al., 2017; Vasilopoulos et al., 2019; Vasilopoulos & Joost, 2020) (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 2).

Their key theoretical innovation lies in developing a sociologically grounded framework that moves beyond individual-level explanations. They argue that "powerlessness can inspire a value shift (Nietzsche, 1990[1887]; Scheler, 1994[1913]) in how resentful groups appraise democracy (Gidron & Hall, 2017)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 2). This represents a significant departure from political psychology approaches that focus on cognitive processing of emotions.

The authors challenge existing scholarship by arguing that "populist attitudes are not confined to the lowest segments of society but can equally affect members of the middle class, who fear a relegation in their social position" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 4). This challenges both Marxist class-based explanations and cultural backlash theories that focus primarily on declining groups.

## Literature Review and Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Major Theoretical Traditions Engaged

**The "Losers of Globalization" Literature**

The paper engages extensively with the structural transformation thesis developed by Kriesi et al. (2006) and others. This theoretical approach posits that "populist appeals are more likely to resonate with individuals disadvantaged by- and vulnerable to the profound social, cultural, and economic transformations brought about by globalization" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 4). The core mechanism assumes that "those who lack exit options and are unable to cope with these fundamental changes view globalization as threatening to their life chances, social status, group interests, and cultural identity" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 4).

Key contributors to this tradition include Betz (2020), who emphasizes cultural and status dimensions of globalization's impact, and Inglehart and Norris (2017), who distinguish between economic and cultural variants of the "left behind" thesis. The empirical evidence for this approach is mixed, as the authors note: "Although the working-class is a substantial voting block for the populist radical right, populist constituencies are heterogeneous and volatile" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 4).

The primary limitation of this literature is its tendency toward economic reductionism. As Abts and Rogenhofer observe, "there are good reasons to question whether economic hardship is a sufficient explanation of populist attitudes" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 4-5). This creates space for their resentment-based approach.

**Resentment and Ressentiment Theory**

The authors draw heavily on philosophical and sociological traditions examining resentment, particularly the work of Nietzsche (1990[1887]) and Scheler (1994[1913]) on ressentiment. This theoretical foundation provides crucial insights into how psychological states translate into political attitudes. They define resentment as "a blaming attitude that arises in response to injury, indifference, and injustice" and note that "at its core lies the belief that another intentionally and unjustly caused us harm, for instance, by depriving us of a scarce commodity that we believe is rightfully ours" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 5).

The distinction between resentment and ressentiment is crucial to their framework. While resentment represents "moral anger" that can motivate legitimate political action, ressentiment emerges through "chronic and persistent" experiences of powerlessness and "inspires a radical change in values" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 6). This theoretical distinction allows them to explain how individual grievances transform into systematic challenges to democratic norms.

Contemporary scholars building on this tradition include Salmela and von Scheve (2017), who examine the emotional roots of populism, and Cohen (2019), who analyzes populism as a politics of resentment. The authors position their work as extending this literature by providing empirical operationalization of theoretical concepts.

**Political Psychology and Affective Intelligence Theory**

The paper engages with the discrete emotions literature in political psychology, particularly work by Marcus et al. (2019), Rico et al. (2017), and Vasilopoulos et al. (2019) on the role of anger versus fear in populist attitudes. This literature applies affective intelligence theory to suggest that "anger, a close companion of resentment motivated by grievance and perceived injustice (Marcus, 2021), is a better predictor of right-wing populism than fear" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 2).

However, the authors critique this literature's focus on discrete emotions rather than underlying mechanisms, arguing that their sociological approach "complements important contributions in political psychology" while offering a different explanatory framework (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 2).

**Relative Deprivation Theory**

The study builds on relative deprivation theory, originally developed by Runciman (1966) and extended by Smith et al. (2012) and Pettigrew (2016). This theoretical tradition examines how "the judgment that one or one's ingroup is disadvantaged" when compared to relevant outgroups generates political attitudes (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 6). The authors note that "feelings of group-based relative deprivation are linked to populist attitudes in several quantitative studies (Abts, 2012; Spruyt et al., 2016; Urbanska & Guimond, 2018)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 6).

### Theoretical Synthesis and Framework

The authors develop an integrated framework that combines insights from these diverse theoretical traditions while addressing their individual limitations. Their synthesis rests on distinguishing three "constitutive experiences" of resentment: "status insecurity, relative deprivation, and powerlessness" that together create conditions for populist attitudes (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 5).

Their theoretical framework proposes a causal chain whereby structural disadvantage generates these three forms of resentment, which then "set in motion a populist politics of resentment that inspires citizens' disillusion with political elites and a radical embrace of popular sovereignty" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 5). The key theoretical innovation is showing how "chronic resentment and internalized powerlessness inspire a radical disillusion with the liberal dimension of our contemporary democratic imaginary" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 7).

The authors address tensions between individual and group-level explanations by arguing that while status insecurity operates at the individual level, relative deprivation "focuses on the relative position of that person's ingroup vis-à-vis relevant outgroups" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 6). This allows them to incorporate both psychological and sociological insights.

They resolve the tension between structural and cultural explanations by proposing that "experiences of status insecurity and relative deprivation can both give rise to resentment" while emphasizing that "resentment is often rooted in a perceived loss of status or anxiety that one's position within hierarchies of social prestige is threatened" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 5).

Their framework includes scope conditions, noting that this "form of redemptive politics, which seeks to bringing salvation via the promise of a better world, becomes a means of external blame attribution, transvaluation, and imaginary revenge that gives power to the people (popular sovereignty) and dethrones seemingly corrupt elites (anti-elitism)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 7).

### Hypotheses and Theoretical Predictions

The authors derive specific hypotheses from their theoretical framework that test both direct effects of resentment components and their mediating role between structural characteristics and populist attitudes.

For direct effects, they hypothesize that "individuals with pronounced economic insecurities are expected to express higher levels of anti-elitism (H2a) and a higher demand for popular sovereignty (H2b). Likewise, perceptions of relative group deprivation are expected to result in both inflated anti-elitism (H3a) and demands for popular sovereignty (H3b)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 7-8).

Their mediation hypotheses predict that "individuals at lower positions in the social structure are more resentful and consequently more likely to support populist attitudes than respondents in higher positions (H5a–H5d)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 8).

The authors also test competing emotional explanations, hypothesizing that "revulsion—the survey item which most closely resembles anger in our dataset—to have a direct effect on populist attitudes (H6a). Fear, another frequently cited driver of populist attitudes (Spruyt et al., 2016), is not expected to have a similar effect (H6b)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 8).

### Connections to Other Research Programs

This research has significant implications for understanding democratic backsliding and institutional legitimacy. The authors' finding that populist attitudes emerge from "a radical disillusion with the liberal dimension of our contemporary democratic imaginary" suggests that populism represents more than policy disagreement—it reflects fundamental challenges to democratic norms and procedures (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 7).

The study connects to research on political trust and institutional performance by showing how structural disadvantage translates into specific critiques of political elites. Their finding that "political elites are blamed for being unresponsive and not representing the increasingly valorized will of the ordinary people" provides insights into how economic grievances become political grievances (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 7).

The research also has implications for understanding social movements and collective action. Their emphasis on group-based relative deprivation rather than individual disadvantage suggests that populist mobilization follows patterns similar to other forms of collective action based on perceived group injustice.

The study bridges political psychology and political sociology by demonstrating how individual-level psychological states aggregate into collective political phenomena. Their finding that "populist attitudes relate—not to individual experiences—but to this sense of group-based injustice and a threatened group position" has implications for understanding how personal troubles become public issues (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 12).

## Methodology Overview

### Data Sources and Coverage

The study employs data from the 2014 Belgian National Election Study, representing "a register-based random probability (two-stage) sample of Belgians entitled to vote in the 2014 national elections" (Abts et al., 2015). The authors note that "data were gathered by means of computer-assisted personal interviews (response rate 47.5%)" with an additional "20-page drop-off questionnaire, containing specific questions on populist attitudes (completed by 74% of respondents)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 8).

The dataset provides comprehensive coverage of Belgian voters during a critical period when populist parties were gaining influence across Europe. The authors applied "weighting coefficients to the dataset, adjusting for gender, age, and education" and deleted "cases with incomplete information were deleted, resulting in total sample size of 1,348 respondents" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 8).

The Belgian context offers particular advantages for testing their theoretical framework because Belgium experienced significant economic pressures following the 2008 financial crisis while maintaining relatively stable democratic institutions. This allows examination of how structural changes translate into political attitudes without the confounding effects of major institutional disruption.

The temporal coverage captures populist attitudes during a period of European integration stress, providing insights into how global and European-level changes affect national political attitudes. However, the cross-sectional design limits ability to establish causal relationships definitively.

### Key Variables and Operationalization

**Dependent Variables**

The authors measure populist attitudes using two distinct latent constructs. Popular sovereignty is measured through five items including demands "that the people, not politicians should take political decisions (D22_1); that power should be returned to the people (D22_3); that political decisions are made under full popular control (D22_4), that the people are best represented by ordinary citizens (D22_2), and that ordinary people are better informed than politicians (D22_6)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 8-9).

Anti-elitism is measured using "statements that probe the respondent's assessment of politicians' opportunism (Q104_2), incompetence (Q104_3), and general immorality (Q104_4)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 9). The authors acknowledge that "the absence of a survey item that expressly measures respondents' conception of the people as homogenous is, nonetheless, a limitation of our study" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 9).

**Independent Variables - Resentment Components**

Status insecurity is operationalized using "two items eliciting respondents' concern with their personal near-term financial misfortune (Q98_1) and the ability to sustain their individual social status in the future (Q98_2)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 9). This captures individual-level anxiety about future position.

Powerlessness employs "three items capturing the sense that one's life is shaped by forces outside one's own control (Q60_3–Q60_5)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 9). This measures subjective political efficacy and control beliefs.

Group relative deprivation uses "three items (Q61_1–Q61_3) address the feeling of being disadvantaged or treated unfairly as a group, compared to a generalized other" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 9). This captures collective grievances rather than individual disadvantage.

**Structural Characteristics**

The study includes comprehensive measures of social position: "gender (0=female; 1=male), age (in years) and educational level (up to lower secondary degree—higher secondary degree—tertiary degree)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 9-10). Occupation follows "the Erikson–Goldthorpe–Portocarero social class schema" while "subjective income evaluation captures the respondent's current total disposable income" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 10).

The operationalization choices reflect the authors' theoretical framework by distinguishing between objective structural position and subjective evaluation of that position. This allows testing whether structural disadvantage operates through objective conditions or subjective perceptions.

### Analytical Model and Justification

The study employs structural equation modeling (SEM), which the authors justify because "it allows estimation of latent variables and corrects for measurement error in the model. This approach leads to a more accurate representation of constructs and relationships between them. In addition, it allows us to disentangle the mediation effects between structural characteristics and populist attitudes via resentment" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 10-11).

The analytical approach addresses key challenges in studying populist attitudes by accounting for measurement error in latent constructs and simultaneously testing direct and indirect effects. The authors note that "all analyses are performed using Mplus software version 7.4 (Muthén & Muthén, 2016) and weighted by age, gender, and educational attainment. Item nonresponse is addressed using full information maximum likelihood estimation" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 11).

Their identification strategy relies on the temporal logic of their theoretical framework, where structural characteristics are assumed to precede resentment, which in turn precedes populist attitudes. While this assumes causal ordering, the cross-sectional design limits definitive causal inference.

The model specification tests their core theoretical proposition through mediation analysis:

**Model 1 (Baseline):**
$$\text{Populist Attitudes} = f(\text{Structural Characteristics}, \text{Status Insecurity}, \text{Relative Deprivation}, \text{Powerlessness})$$

**Model 2 (Extended):**
$$\text{Populist Attitudes} = f(\text{Structural Characteristics}, \text{Resentment Components}, \text{Discrete Emotions})$$

The authors justify this approach by arguing that "by comparing the baseline model to this alternative model, we test whether the estimated effects of resentment are sensitive to the inclusion of some of the most common emotions referenced in the populism literature, whether they have an added explanatory value, and whether their effects are mediated by resentment" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 8).

## Empirical Findings and Theoretical Significance

### Core Empirical Results

The study's central finding confirms their theoretical prediction about the mediating role of resentment. The authors demonstrate that "there is almost no evidence that lower socioeconomic status has a direct effect on populist attitudes" while "the inclusion of resentment as a mediating factor is crucial to understanding the populist appeal among the losers of globalization" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 14).

Specifically, they find that "individuals who score highly on relative deprivation tend to harbor anti-elitist and people-centric attitudes. Those who feel their group is systematically deprived in comparison to other groups blame the political establishment for this injustice (β=.497; p<.001) and are more inclined to demand increased popular sovereignty (β=.274; p<.001)" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 12).

The differential effects on the two populist attitudes prove theoretically significant: "Anti-elitism is... strongly correlated with a generalized sense of injustice and impotence, whereas the call for popular sovereignty relates primarily to relative deprivation" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 12). This suggests that different psychological mechanisms drive different aspects of populist attitudes.

Crucially, "personal status insecurity, as the fear of loss in the near future, did not have a significant effect on anti-elitist and people-centric attitudes" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 12). This finding challenges individualistic explanations of populist support and supports their group-based theoretical framework.

### Theoretical Implications of Findings

**Group vs. Individual Grievances**

The study's most significant theoretical contribution lies in demonstrating that populist attitudes stem from collective rather than individual experiences of disadvantage. As the authors conclude, "populist attitudes relate—not to individual experiences—but to this sense of group-based injustice and a threatened group position" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 12).

This finding has profound implications for understanding populist mobilization. It suggests that populist appeals succeed not by addressing individual economic anxiety but by constructing narratives of group victimization and collective grievance. The authors note that "perceived egocentric status loss is less relevant for populist attitudes than sociotropic experiences of loss and deprivation concerning collective status, identity, and power" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 12).

**Mediation vs. Direct Effects**

The comprehensive mediation analysis reveals that structural disadvantage operates primarily through psychological mechanisms rather than directly determining political attitudes. For education, "this effect is completely mediated by relative deprivation (β=−.307; p<.001) and powerlessness (β=−.115; p<.01). Belief that their group is unfairly disadvantaged and a perceived lack of power, lead the less educated to blame political elites" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 12).

This finding suggests that economic inequality and social disadvantage do not automatically produce populist attitudes. Rather, these structural conditions must be interpreted through frameworks of group competition and perceived injustice to generate political consequences.

**Emotional Mechanisms**

The study's analysis of discrete emotions provides additional theoretical insights. Their robustness check confirms that "revulsion—compared to fear—is positively related to anti-establishment attitudes" while "revulsion had the strongest effect on anti-elitism, although there is no difference between revulsion and fear in their effect on people centrism" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 15).

Importantly, they find that "anger (revulsion) is often governed by a sense of grievance and injustice, as its effect on populist attitudes is partly mediated by relative deprivation" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 15). This supports their theoretical framework by showing how emotions operate through resentment mechanisms rather than as independent causal factors.

## Broader Implications for Democratic Theory and Practice

### Democratic Legitimacy and Institutional Trust

The study's findings have significant implications for understanding challenges to democratic legitimacy. The authors demonstrate that populist attitudes emerge from "a radical disillusion with the liberal dimension of our contemporary democratic imaginary" where "political elites are blamed for being unresponsive and not representing the increasingly valorized will of the ordinary people" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 7).

This suggests that populist challenges to democratic institutions stem not merely from policy disagreements but from fundamental questioning of representative democracy itself. The finding that powerlessness drives anti-elitism indicates that citizens experiencing low political efficacy may reject elite-mediated democracy in favor of more direct forms of popular control.

### Social Cohesion and Intergroup Relations

The emphasis on group relative deprivation as the primary driver of populist attitudes has important implications for social cohesion. The authors show that populist attitudes emerge when groups perceive their collective position as threatened by "relevant outgroups—distinguished by their ethnicity, power or status" who "are blamed for unfairly benefiting from globalization through an upward social mobility that the left behind seemingly lack" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 4).

This finding suggests that populist mobilization follows patterns of intergroup conflict theory, where perceived zero-sum competition between groups generates hostility and support for exclusionary policies. The authors note that "the loss of group status is perceived as an illegitimate intergroup injustice" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 4).

### Economic Policy and Political Attitudes

The study's findings challenge conventional assumptions about the relationship between economic policy and political attitudes. By showing that structural disadvantage operates through resentment mechanisms rather than directly, the research suggests that economic policies aimed at addressing objective disadvantage may be insufficient to reduce populist attitudes without also addressing perceptions of group status and relative deprivation.

The authors conclude that "people do not opt for populism because they feel personally deprived; they embrace populist attitudes if their disadvantage is perceived in collective terms and understood as the outcome of an unjust society, wherein they feel powerless to alter their own circumstances" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 16-17).

This has significant implications for policy responses to populist mobilization, suggesting that addressing populist attitudes requires attention to symbolic politics and group status as well as material conditions.

## Conclusion and Future Research Directions

This research makes substantial theoretical and empirical contributions to understanding the psychological foundations of populist attitudes. By disaggregating resentment into distinct components and demonstrating their differential effects on anti-elitism and popular sovereignty, Abts and Rogenhofer provide a sophisticated framework for understanding how structural disadvantage translates into political attitudes.

Their central theoretical insight—that populist attitudes emerge from group-based rather than individual experiences of disadvantage—challenges both economic and psychological explanations that focus on individual-level mechanisms. The finding that "collective, group-based interests and intergroup comparisons as well as the belief that one has little control over his or her own circumstances turn out to be far more relevant to the populist appeal" has profound implications for understanding contemporary democratic challenges (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 15).

The study opens several avenues for future research, including longitudinal analysis of how resentment develops over time, comparative analysis across different institutional and cultural contexts, and investigation of how different types of populist appeals interact with specific resentment mechanisms. The authors acknowledge limitations in their measurement of populist attitudes and suggest that "future attempts to measure populist attitudes would benefit from including items that tap explicitly into Manicheanism" (Abts & Rogenhofer, 2024, p. 17).

This research ultimately demonstrates that understanding populist attitudes requires attention to the complex interplay between structural conditions, psychological mechanisms, and group identities. Their three-pronged framework of resentment provides a valuable tool for analyzing how contemporary social and economic transformations translate into political challenges to democratic institutions.
