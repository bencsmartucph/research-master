## Comprehensive Overview

This paper makes two interrelated contributions to the comparative welfare state literature. First, it introduces an original dataset on the *workfare balance* — the relative weight of punitive versus enabling demands placed on the unemployed — across 16 OECD countries from 1980–2015. Second, and more theoretically significant, it tests and rejects several prominent explanations for why governments shift toward workfare, including power resources theory (left vs. right partisanship), third-way social democracy, and neoliberalism. In their place, the authors argue that *moral conservatism* — a commitment to mutual social obligations, paternalistic state authority, law and order, and traditional morality — is the robust driver of workfare legislation of both punitive and enabling kinds. The paper is significant for the welfare state literature because it disrupts the conventional wisdom equating workfare with neoliberalism, and for social policy theory because it shifts analytical attention from economic ideology to the moral economy as the foundation of activation politics.

---

## Key Takeaways

**1. The workfare trend is real but not uniformly punitive.**

The paper finds "an increasing number of legislative reforms affecting the workfare balance, somewhat tilted towards the punitive side; yet with the exception of the United Kingdom, we do not find a pronounced and broad overall shift towards punitive workfare." This is a direct challenge to scholarship that treats workfare as synonymous with punitive recommodification, and it matters enormously for your welfare/social investment research stream — the empirical picture is more heterogeneous than much of the literature assumes.

**2. Neoliberalism does not drive workfare — it suppresses it.**

The authors find that "ideas of free market allocation and non-intervention (by the state) lead to less enabling and punitive workfare reforms." This is the paper's most counterintuitive and theoretically significant finding: neoliberal market ideology is negatively associated with workfare activity of either kind, because workfare constitutes *state expansion*, not market retreat.

**3. Moral conservatism is the robust positive driver of workfare.**

The paper concludes that "moral conservatism explains workfare legislation of both the enabling and punitive kind" — with the effect amounting to a full standard deviation difference in workfare activity between low and high levels of moral conservatism. The mechanisms linking moral conservatism to activation politics — mutual obligations, paternalism, deservingness — connect directly to your interest in the social psychology of redistribution and far-right support.

**4. The left-right partisan divide is irrelevant for workfare.**

The results indicate that "scepticism regarding a clear left-right divide is indeed warranted" and that "we find no robust partisan effects (on punitive reforms, enabling reforms, or the total balance measure) associated with the share of left or right parties in government." This null finding has important implications: it suggests that the axis structuring workfare politics is not the classic redistributive left-right dimension but a moral-cultural one — relevant to your research on far-right parties and their welfare state positions.

**5. The UK is an outlier, not a model.**

The authors note that "the UK, clearly the country that has been at the centre of the workfare debate, is by no means typical or representative from a comparative perspective. No other country in our study has seen such a one-sided focus on punitive measures." This methodological warning is important for your seminar paper: over-generalizing from British workfare politics risks seriously distorting comparative conclusions.

## Section-by-Section Analysis

### Introduction

The paper opens by situating itself within the broad transformation of welfare states since the mid-1990s, emphasizing that the key change is not merely declining generosity but the growing conditionality of social rights. Governments have introduced both "'sticks' (punitive measures such as sanctions) and 'carrots' (enabling measures such as training and employment services), that are meant to ensure that the unemployed quickly find and accept a (new) job."

The central empirical puzzle motivating the paper is that the literature agrees on the general welfare-to-workfare trend but *disagrees sharply about the workfare balance* — whether punitive or enabling demands have come to dominate. This disagreement, the authors argue, is partly a function of conceptual conflation: researchers treating workfare as exclusively about sanctions and conditionality, and the social investment literature treating it as primarily about human capital upgrading.

The authors announce their intent: "We argue that the idea that neoliberalism is to blame for a shift to the punitive pole of the workfare balance is theoretically flawed and empirically unsupported." They position this as a challenge both to Peck-style workfare state theory and to the assumption embedded in much activation research that the ideological engine of welfare retrenchment and workfare are the same.

**Significance for your research:** The introduction establishes a framing distinction — between economic ideology (market liberalism) and moral ideology (conservatism, paternalism, desert) — that maps directly onto debates in far-right welfare politics. Far-right parties often combine hostility to redistribution toward out-groups with strong moral conditionality for in-group welfare recipients, a configuration that this paper's framework helps to theorize.

### Literature Review: Workfare vs. Social Investment

The paper's literature review is organized around two competing paradigmatic positions that have generated the empirical disagreement about the workfare balance.

### The Workfare/Recommodification Literature

The dominant strand of workfare scholarship follows Jessop (1993) and Peck (2001, 2003) in interpreting activation policies through the lens of the *Schumpeterian workfare state* — a post-Fordist regime that disciplines labor back into the market. Rueda (2015) is the paper's primary interlocutor in this tradition.

Rueda characterizes activation as follows: "activation is meant to push people into employment (although often this may turn out to be low-pay employment) by reducing the attractiveness of social benefits... attempts are made to develop or strengthen traditional active labor market policies so that benefit recipients are provided with the skills required to be successful when searching for a job."

For Rueda, both conditionality and activation amount to *recommodification* — making workers more dependent on the market. Knotz (2018a, 2018b) follows this framework, defining workfare in terms of tightened eligibility conditions and sanctions. Raffass (2017) introduces the concept of "demanding activation," focusing on intensified coercion. The common thread across these accounts is that they employ "a conceptual framework that highlights the 'intensification of behavioural conditionality in Western democracies' characterized by 'the increased severity of the sanctions to those whose conduct fails to conform to requirements'."

**Critical assessment by the authors:** This body of work is criticized for conflating the punitive *dimension* of workfare with workfare itself, thereby rendering invisible the enabling side. The operationalization of workfare as equivalent to sanctions or tightened conditionality pre-determines the finding that workfare is punitive — a conceptual circularity the paper's new dataset is designed to break.

**Relevance to your research:** The recommodification framework maps onto the "social rights contraction" component of welfare state change. For research on redistribution preferences and far-right support, the question of whether voters perceive workfare as legitimate conditionality or illegitimate punishment is crucial — the literature's disagreement about the workfare balance reflects a genuine ambiguity in how conditionality is experienced and supported by publics.

### The Social Investment Literature

The social investment perspective, associated primarily with Hemerijck (2013, 2017, 2018) and Bonoli (2013), offers a more optimistic reading of the same transformations. It emphasizes the shift from *ex-post compensation* to *ex-ante risk prevention* — investing in human capital to reduce unemployment risk rather than compensating it after the fact.

Active labour market policies in this tradition "remove obstacles to employment, upskill workers, or provide access to work experience" and comprise an "extremely diverse range of policies, going from vocational retraining to welfare-to-work or workfare schemes for social assistance beneficiaries."

However, the paper also engages the serious *critiques* of the social investment paradigm. Mertens (2017) shows that investment imperatives run up against austerity constraints. Bonoli et al. (2017) demonstrate a "Matthew Effect" — social investment tends to benefit middle- and higher-income groups at the expense of lower-income groups who bear the brunt of retrenched passive protection. Morel and Palme (2017) criticize the paradigm's productivist and gender-blind orientation.

Crucially, even Hemerijck (2018) concedes that despite the rhetorical embrace of social investment by the EU and OECD, the "'default' neoliberal paradigm of market liberalization, balanced budgets, hard currency and welfare retrenchment also remains with us."

**Relevance to your research:** The social investment critique — particularly the Matthew Effect and the dualization of social protection — is directly relevant to your interests in redistribution preferences and the political economy of welfare. The finding that social investment benefits the already-advantaged while disciplining the disadvantaged connects to explanations of why left-behind working-class voters may withdraw support from mainstream left parties and turn toward the far right, which often combines welfare chauvinism with support for conditionality.

### Theory Section 1: Left vs. Right Partisanship

### Theoretical Framework (200+ words)

The conventional power resources theory (Korpi 1983) holds that the balance of class power between labor and capital — mediated through party politics — determines welfare state outcomes. Social democratic and left parties, representing organized labor, favor enabling measures that invest in workers' human capital and protect social rights. Right and Christian democratic parties, representing capital and traditional hierarchies, are expected to favor punitive measures that restore work incentives and discipline the unemployed back into labor markets.

The paper takes two approaches to operationalizing partisanship. The first follows Castles and Mair (1984) using classic party family labels (left, center, right) weighted by portfolio share in government. The advantage is historical grounding and interpretive clarity; the disadvantage is that labels fail to capture ideological drift over time, particularly the Third Way transformation of social democracy in the 1990s. The second approach uses the dynamic Right-Left Index (RILE) from the Comparative Manifesto Project, which captures actual manifesto positions along a left-right continuum based on quantitative content analysis of party manifestos.

The authors acknowledge the broader challenge to power resources theory: after "decades of dealignment of core electoral groups from 'their' parties, few scholars still hold that it is instructive to conceive of the left as the political arm of the labour movement and the bourgeois parties as the representatives of capital." The Third Way literature (Green-Pedersen and van Kersbergen 2002) reinforces this skepticism — once social democratic parties embrace conditionality and activation, the partisan logic dissolves.

The theory section is therefore primarily *skeptical* in orientation: the authors predict null partisan effects, not because partisanship never matters, but because the old left-right divide is too blunt an instrument to capture the moral-ideological dynamics driving workfare. This prediction is confirmed empirically.

**Relevance to your research:** The dissolution of the traditional left-right axis for workfare politics is germane to far-right party literature. If workfare conditionality is driven by moral rather than economic ideology, then far-right parties — which often combine economic interventionism with strong moral conservatism and welfare chauvinism — may be more coherent workfare advocates than the partisan literature implies. The RILE measure's irrelevance also suggests that standard left-right party scales are inadequate for capturing the ideological content driving welfare conditionality reform.

### Theory Section 2: Neoliberalism vs. Moral Conservatism

### Neoliberalism as Market Ideology (200+ words)

The paper's most important theoretical contribution involves disaggregating what is commonly called "neoliberalism" into its constituent ideological components. In the comparative welfare state literature, neoliberalism is typically treated as the dominant ideological force behind welfare retrenchment, labor market deregulation, and the shift toward workfare. This view is widespread: the authors note that "the conviction that, since the 1980s, neoliberalism has been the political force that has driven welfare state retrenchment in general, and labour market deregulation and the contraction of unemployment protection in particular, is a topic of broad agreement in the literature."

The paper's critical intervention is to identify a fundamental theoretical problem with applying this framework to workfare specifically. Workfare — whether punitive or enabling — constitutes *state expansion*, not state retreat. Punitive workfare requires new bureaucracies to monitor compliance, implement activation plans, and administer sanctions. Enabling workfare requires public investment in training infrastructure and employment services. Both are fundamentally at odds with a genuine preference for minimal state intervention and market allocation.

The authors argue: "Supervising and implementing the job plans and activity requirements that underpin punitive workfare and penalties for non-compliance with activity plans entails new rules and more bureaucrats. This inevitably extends rather than limits the reach of the state, and thus goes against the instincts of a neoliberal understanding of the market-state nexus."

The authors operationalize *market ideology* using items from the Comparative Manifesto Project measuring emphasis on free enterprise, market mechanisms, and opposition to state economic intervention. They are careful to distinguish this from neoliberalism as a broader cultural or political project — the claim is specifically about the *economic non-interventionist* component of neoliberal thought.

**Relevance to your research:** This distinction between economic neoliberalism and moral conservatism is crucial for your welfare state research. Much far-right literature treats far-right parties as combining neoliberal economics with welfare chauvinism — but this paper's framework suggests the relevant dimension for workfare may be moral rather than economic. A far-right party that scores high on moral conservatism (law and order, traditional authority, national identity) but low on market ideology could be a strong workfare advocate even if not classically neoliberal. This has implications for how we code and theorize far-right welfare positions.

### Moral Conservatism (200+ words)

The paper's positive explanation for workfare centers on *moral conservatism* — a concept drawn from the "moral economy" tradition (Van Oorschot 2000; Petersen et al. 2011; Götz 2015; Taylor-Gooby et al. 2019) but applied here at the government and party level rather than the individual level.

Moral conservatism, as the paper uses it, rests on two related sets of convictions. First, a belief in *mutual social obligations* — that citizens have duties toward the state and community, not merely rights against it, and that the state has a legitimate paternalistic role in enforcing those duties. Second, a belief that the state has an obligation to uphold and reproduce *traditional moral order* — including traditional values around work, self-reliance, and social responsibility. Together, these create a disposition toward both punitive workfare (compelling the unemployed to fulfill their social obligations) and enabling workfare (equipping them to do so through training and activation).

This is why moral conservatism drives *both* poles of the workfare balance simultaneously, while neoliberalism suppresses both: the moral conservative wants an active, directive state that disciplines and enables; the neoliberal wants a minimal state that steps back. The authors state explicitly: "We expect that the more governments commit to this moral conservatism, the more they legislate both punitive and enabling workfare reforms."

Operationally, moral conservatism is measured using Manifesto Project items: traditional morality, political authority, and law and order. The authors note that results hold irrespective of the inclusion or exclusion of any single item, and are robust or stronger when the definition is extended to include "national way of life positive" and "multiculturalism negative" — items that connect directly to welfare chauvinism and the cultural politics of the far right.

**Relevance to your research:** This is the paper's most important theoretical contribution for your research agenda. The connection between moral conservatism and workfare creates a theoretical bridge between the welfare state political economy literature and the social psychology literature on redistribution preferences. Individual-level attitudes toward welfare recipients are strongly shaped by deservingness heuristics (Petersen et al. 2011; Jensen and Petersen 2017) — and moral conservatism at the party level may be the institutional counterpart to these individual moral judgments. Far-right parties' welfare chauvinism can be understood as a morally conservative position that selectively extends workfare obligations and conditionality to out-group members, while protecting the social rights of in-group "deserving" workers. The finding that "national way of life positive" and "multiculturalism negative" items strengthen the moral conservatism result is particularly telling in this regard.

### Methodology

**Unit of Analysis:** Country-year. The dataset covers 16 OECD countries from 1980–2015, embedded in the Comparative Political Dataset (Armingeon et al. 2019).

**Dependent Variables:**

- *Workfare balance (total/net)*: enabling changes minus punitive changes per year
- *Punitive changes*: coded negatively (−1 for new obligations, −2 for substantive new obligations)
- *Enabling changes*: coded positively (+1 for lowered activation requirements or qualification improvements, +2 for particularly substantive improvements)
- *Absolute workfare activity*: |punitive| + enabling, capturing overall legislative intensity regardless of direction

**Key Independent Variables:**

- *Left/right partisanship*: portfolio-weighted share of left and right parties in government, following Castles and Mair (1984) and Armingeon et al. (2019)
- *RILE (Right-Left Index)*: from the Comparative Manifesto Project; measures dynamic ideological positioning based on manifesto content analysis
- *Market ideology*: Manifesto Project items measuring preference for free enterprise, market mechanisms, and opposition to state intervention; operationalizes the non-interventionist dimension of neoliberal economic thought
- *Moral conservatism*: composite of Manifesto Project items — traditional morality, political authority, law and order; reflects government commitment to normative social obligations and traditional values
- *Controls*: spending for the unemployed, annual economic growth, unemployment rate (included and excluded to test robustness)

**Coding of Workfare Reforms:** Legislation was identified through international repositories (ILO, ISSA), national archives, and case study literature. Each punitive or enabling measure within a law was individually coded. Target scope (all unemployed vs. subgroups — youth, disabled, older workers, those without UI access, other) was also recorded. 390 reforms total were coded across the 16-country, 35-year period.

**Econometric Model:** Prais-Winsten regressions with panel-corrected standard errors (PCSE), addressing autoregression and heteroscedasticity in time series cross-sectional data. Country fixed effects are included throughout to control for unit heterogeneity and exclude stable country-specific confounds (Beck and Katz 1995). Dependent variable is lagged one year to reflect typical implementation delay between legislation and effect.

### Empirical Findings and Cross-Reference to Theory

**On partisanship:** The null result for both left/right party labels and RILE directly confirms the paper's theoretical prediction that the old partisan axis is irrelevant for workfare. The authors find "no robust partisan effects (on punitive reforms, enabling reforms, or the total balance measure) associated with the share of left or right parties in government... irrespective of the model specification and control variables." This confirms the theoretical argument that ideological transformations since the 1990s have rendered classic partisan heuristics insufficient.

**On market ideology:** Figures 3 (Panels B, C, D) show clearly that both punitive and enabling reforms are concentrated among cabinets with low or medium market ideology. For "governments with pronounced market ideology, predicted changes are much more modest and statistically indistinguishable from 0 (i.e., no change)." This confirms the theoretical claim that workfare is state expansion, not market retreat.

**On moral conservatism:** The mirror-image result appears in Figure 4. Panels B and C show "that morally conservative cabinets can be expected to legislate enabling and punitive reforms each year. By contrast, in the absence of moral conservatism, we would expect to observe little to no changes." The effect size is substantial — a full standard deviation difference in absolute workfare activity between low and high moral conservatism. This confirms the moral economy hypothesis and closes the explanatory loop opened by the null neoliberalism finding.

The cross-referential logic of the findings is particularly tight: market ideology and moral conservatism produce symmetric, opposite effects on the same outcomes, suggesting they are genuinely capturing distinct ideological dimensions rather than measuring the same underlying construct. Their effects cancel in the net workfare balance (Panel A) but are clearly visible in the absolute activity and component measures — an elegant empirical result that validates the paper's core theoretical disaggregation.

### Conclusions and Implications

The paper's conclusion distills the argument with precision: it is "too simple to see workfare as only punitive and as a product of neoliberalism in the sense it is commonly understood (i.e. economic orthodoxy prioritizing market allocation). Our findings indicate that laissez-faire beliefs are hard to reconcile with workfare and point to the moral foundations of workfare. Neoliberalism is much more likely to yield policy passivity (e.g., retrenchment), while moral conservatism pushes governments toward policy activity and the creation of new monitoring and control measures."

For your twin research interests, the paper's contributions can be summarized as follows. For the **political economy of welfare**: it demonstrates that workfare reform is not ideologically monolithic and that its primary driver is a moral-cultural dimension distinct from classic economic ideology — a finding with implications for how we theorize the ideological content of social investment and activation policy. For **far-right parties and redistribution**: the operationalization of moral conservatism (particularly items on national way of life and multiculturalism) suggests that the same moral economy that drives workfare can be racialized or nationalized into welfare chauvinism — conditioning social rights not just on compliance with activation requirements, but on membership in a morally valued national community. The social psychology literature on deservingness heuristics (cited throughout) provides the micro-foundations for this connection, and future research linking individual-level moral judgments about welfare recipients to the party-level moral conservatism measured here would directly advance your agenda.
