## Introduction and Theoretical Contribution

Nicole Wu's 2022 article published in *Political Science Research and Methods* explores a compelling paradox in contemporary political economy: why do workers blame globalization for their economic challenges when automation appears to be the more significant driver of labor market disruption? The paper makes several important theoretical contributions to our understanding of political attitudes toward globalization and technological change.

The research begins with a clear articulation of the problem: "Many, especially low-skilled workers, blame globalization for their economic woes. Robots and machines, which have led to job market polarization, rising income inequality, and labor displacement, are often viewed much more forgivingly." This sets up the central argument that citizens may be misattributing blame for their economic circumstances.

The paper positions itself within debates about the determinants of public opinion toward globalization. Wu challenges both dominant theoretical frameworks in this literature:

1. The self-interest approach: "The first posits that individual preferences should reflect economic self-interest. In studying attitudes toward trade, analysts typically derive predictions about individuals' material interests based on the Ricardo–Viner or Stolper–Samuelson model... In doing so, they assume that citizens understand and can accurately identify the sources of their financial stressors or fortunes."
2. The symbolic attitudes approach: "The second approach demonstrates that citizens are either disinterested or unable to engage in such reasoning. These researchers instead emphasize non-economic influence on preferences. Opponents of globalization are not necessarily economic losers. Economic ignorance, ethnocentrism, political framing, and other symbolic attitudes, rather than pocketbook concerns, determine attitudes toward globalization."

Wu proposes a synthesis that acknowledges both approaches have merit but are incomplete. She argues that "grievances against globalization have economic origins, but economic losers often misattribute blame for their anxieties, and consequently, demand policies that poorly advance their interests." This represents a sophisticated theoretical advance that bridges materialist and ideational approaches to public opinion.

The paper's central theoretical contribution is that automation anxiety is misdirected toward foreign entities through psychological and political processes. This is a significant insight for understanding the political consequences of technological change and has implications for broader theories of populism, protectionism, and political behavior.

## Context and Structural Economic Changes

The paper provides crucial context by examining structural economic changes in advanced industrialized countries, particularly the United States. Wu presents compelling evidence that while globalization receives disproportionate blame, automation has been a more significant driver of economic dislocation.

For instance, she notes: "Although globalization has important distributional effects, neither trade, immigration, nor offshoring explain the preponderance of affected workers' plight. The existing scholarship highlights technology's link to many of the structural economic changes witnessed in advanced industrialized countries."

The paper directly challenges common narratives about trade and immigration by citing extensive research showing their limited impacts on the labor market:

"Populists also point the finger at immigrants for displacing native workers and depressing their wages, but research in economics provides little support for their claims. Using a natural experiment, Card (1990) shows that the sudden influx of Cuban migrants had virtually no effect on wages or unemployment rates for low-skilled workers in Miami."

In contrast, Wu documents the substantial impact of technological change on labor markets:

"Although globalization has no doubt created winners and losers, neither international trade, immigration, nor offshoring explain the preponderance of the latter's plight. The literature on structural economic change emphasizes technology as a more important source of economic disruption."

She provides empirical evidence from manufacturing, showing that "Since the 1980s, a marked increase in labor productivity due to technological upgrades enabled real-manufacturing output to almost double despite a 30 percent decrease in employment." This directly challenges the narrative that manufacturing jobs were primarily lost to foreign competition.

The paper also explains the nature of technological change, noting that it has been skill-biased and routine-biased, with "post-1980 mechanization has been skill-biased and routine-biased, privileging skilled over unskilled workers, and non-routine over routine jobs." This has led to job polarization and growing inequality, particularly hurting middle-skill workers performing routine tasks.

## Theoretical Mechanisms of Blame Misattribution

The core theoretical contribution of the paper is its explication of why workers misattribute blame for economic dislocation toward globalization rather than automation. Wu identifies several key mechanisms:

### 1. Politically Motivated Framing by Elites

"The narrative that foreign imports and offshoring are killing the manufacturing sector became the dominant frame to understand job insecurity across much of the developed world... There are political motivations for elites to emphasize globalization over automation. First, the nature of globalization renders it convenient for elites to use group cues, a potent political tool. Outgroups, immigrants, and workers abroad, who are seemingly making considerable gains make clear and conceivable targets to blame."

This invokes theories of elite framing and cue-taking in political psychology, suggesting that political entrepreneurs strategically frame globalization as the culprit because it fits more easily into "us versus them" narratives. The paper connects this to research showing that "Political psychology research shows that group cues can trigger anxiety independently of the actual threat posed by the outgroup."

### 2. Predilection for Technology

"Technology also makes for a poor target for attack because of the convenience it brings. According to the World Values Survey, a majority of Americans believe that science and technology make our lives easier and more comfortable... Few would trade a personal computer for a shared typist, the ATM machine for visits to a teller, or automatic exchanges for manual telephone switchboards—not even if the sacrifice would save an occupation."

This mechanism draws on psychological theories about self-interest and cognitive biases. People are reluctant to blame technology because they personally benefit from it, creating a tension between their economic interests as workers and their interests as consumers of technology.

### 3. Media Attention and Overestimation of Globalization

"Although citizens downplay the risks of automation, journalists and the public tend to overstate the extent and downsides of globalization. The media sets the agenda and influences how issues are evaluated. Globalization is more heavily covered and scrutinized harder than automation."

This mechanism connects to theories of media effects, agenda-setting, and availability heuristics in cognitive psychology, suggesting that the greater salience of globalization in public discourse makes it a more cognitively available explanation for economic anxieties.

### 4. Psychological Biases and Defensive Attribution

The paper also discusses psychological mechanisms of attribution bias:

"Not only do individuals welcome the benefits of technological change, but they also have a tendency to downplay its risks. Automation is not on the minds of most... When directly prompted, two-thirds of American respondents expect machines 'to do much of the work currently done by humans within fifty years.' However, four-fifths of them think that their jobs will continue to exist in five decades."

This relates to theories of unrealistic optimism and attribution biases in social psychology, suggesting that people engage in defensive cognition to minimize personal threats while maximizing threats from outgroups.

Wu synthesizes these mechanisms into a coherent theory of blame displacement, arguing that "Due to the much higher salience of globalization and the public's predilection for technology, I expect that workers at risk of automation are more likely to attribute their economic anxieties to globalization, instead of technology."

### Policy Implications

The paper identifies three major policy implications of blame misattribution:

1. **Political consequences of misplaced blame**: "First, the misplacement of hostility toward globalization can lead to collective decisions that might be economically detrimental and difficult to be reversed, such as the fracturing of international political and economic unions (e.g., Brexit) and the election of political leaders who have protectionist agendas and the executive power to reverse trade liberalization."
2. **Suboptimal policy responses**: "Second, the misattribution of blame may lead to poor policy outcomes. If free trade and immigration are linked to overall welfare gains, responding to technological change with protectionism may hurt constituencies that derive key benefits from globalization. Protection may even hurt those who demand these policies in the first place if they misidentified the source of their economic problems."
3. **Inadequate preparation for future challenges**: "Finally, it is only through the understanding of the real challenge to work that citizens can better prepare themselves for the advent of large-scale job automation. Knowledge of the threat of automation will also motivate more informed discussions about appropriate adjustment and compensation measures for displaced labor."

These implications connect the paper's findings to broader debates about populism, protectionism, and policy responses to technological change. The paper suggests that addressing the real sources of economic anxiety may be more effective than protectionist policies targeting globalization.
