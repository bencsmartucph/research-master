## Comprehensive Summary

This research makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding populist support by introducing and validating the concept of "nostalgic deprivation" – the perceived discrepancy between individuals' current status and their idealized perceptions of past circumstances. The authors demonstrate that this subjective sense of decline operates consistently across diverse European contexts, providing a unifying framework that explains support for both left-wing and right-wing populist movements. The paper's core theoretical innovation lies in disaggregating nostalgic deprivation into three distinct dimensions (social, economic, and political power) while showing how each contributes to populist attitudes and voting behavior across ideological and geographic boundaries. This work challenges existing scholarship that treats left and right populism as fundamentally different phenomena driven by distinct grievances, instead proposing a common psychological foundation rooted in temporal comparisons and perceived status loss.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Unified Theory of Populist Support**: "Although cultural and economic grievances can be mutually reinforcing (Colantone & Stanig, 2019; Gidron & Hall, 2017), many analyses tend to link them with different political outcomes" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 1). The authors demonstrate that nostalgic deprivation provides a common foundation for both left and right populist support.
2. **Multidimensional Nature of Deprivation**: "However, deprivation can be understood through a variety of different lenses, including economic loss, power loss and a loss of social status (Gest et al., 2018)" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 3). The research reveals that while these dimensions are correlated, they remain conceptually distinct with different antecedents.
3. **Subjective vs. Objective Conditions**: "This suggests that deprivation may primarily be driven by subjective attitudes which shape how individuals perceive their own standing, rather than anchored in a decline in objective conditions" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 5). The findings emphasize the primacy of perceptions over material reality.
4. **Cross-Ideological Appeal**: "Moving from the minimum to the maximum on the deprivation scale is associated with an increase in the average agreement with populist attitudes by 0.48 points, on a 0 to 1 point scale" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 7). This effect operates consistently across left and right-wing respondents.
5. **Geographic Generalizability**: "While nostalgic deprivation has different origins and magnitudes, it has broad generalizability, predicting populist attitudes and voting for populist opposition parties across a variety of contexts" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 8).
6. **Incumbency Effects**: The research reveals that nostalgic deprivation's relationship with voting behavior varies based on whether populist parties are in government, suggesting different dynamics for opposition versus incumbent populist movements.

## Introduction and Motivation Analysis

### Research Problem and Significance

The paper addresses a fundamental puzzle in contemporary political science: how to explain the simultaneous rise of both left-wing and right-wing populist movements across Europe despite their apparently different appeals and constituencies. The authors identify a critical gap in existing scholarship, noting that "both sets of grievances are linked to the globalization of markets and human mobility (Rodrik, 2018). Thus, while left and right-wing populist platforms may ultimately highlight different targets and scapegoats, it remains possible that they appeal to voters with similar grievances" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 2).

The research problem is framed within broader theoretical debates about demand-side versus supply-side explanations for populist success. While supply-side theories focus on strategic party positioning, demand-side explanations emphasize voter responses to societal change. The authors position themselves within the demand-side tradition while challenging its fragmentation into separate explanations for left versus right populism.

The empirical puzzle motivating the research centers on observable patterns suggesting commonalities between populist movements despite surface-level differences. As the authors note: "Cultural grievances are often highlighted as the primary driver of support for populist right-wing parties (Margalit, 2019), whereas explanations for populist left-wing voting tend to revolve around economic grievances (March 2017; Marcos-Marne 2021; Salmela & von Scheve 2018)" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 1). This artificial separation obscures potential underlying commonalities.

The broader significance connects to fundamental questions about democratic stability and representation in advanced industrialized societies. The authors situate their work within debates about globalization's political consequences, arguing that understanding populist appeal requires moving beyond single-factor explanations toward more nuanced psychological and sociological frameworks.

### Theoretical Positioning and Innovation

The authors build upon existing work on relative deprivation theory while introducing crucial temporal dimensions. They reference Gest et al. (2018)'s foundational work on nostalgic deprivation in the US and UK contexts, arguing that "support for Donald Trump and the United Kingdom Independence Party (UKIP) can be explained by a concept they term 'nostalgic deprivation' – the discrepancy between voters' subjective understandings of their current status and their perceptions about past positionality" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 2).

The theoretical innovation lies in several key areas. First, the multidimensional operationalization of nostalgic deprivation represents a significant advance over single-measure approaches. Second, the cross-national validation extends theoretical insights beyond Anglo-American contexts. Third, the demonstration of cross-ideological effects challenges prevailing assumptions about left-right differences in populist support.

The authors position their contribution relative to competing theoretical traditions by arguing that "the resulting sense of downward mobility can unite a variety of disparate grievances under a common frame. Politically, it can be readily exploited by the supply-side appeals of left- and right-wing populist parties, which despite significant policy differences, share a messaging strategy which calls for a return to an idealized prior era when 'ordinary' people – rather than elites – were more central to society" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 2).

## Literature Review and Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Major Theoretical Traditions Engaged

### Relative Deprivation Theory

**Theoretical Foundation**: The paper builds extensively on relative deprivation theory, which posits that political behavior is driven by subjective evaluations of one's position relative to reference groups rather than absolute conditions. The core mechanism involves social comparison processes where individuals assess their circumstances against relevant others or temporal benchmarks.

**Key Scholars and Works**: The authors engage prominently with Gurr (1970)'s seminal work on relative deprivation and political violence, as well as more recent applications by Pettigrew et al. (2008) and Smith et al. (2012). They note that "relative deprivation – the subjective evaluation that one is worse off compared to a reference group (e.g., Gurr, 1970; Pettigrew et al., 2008; Smith et al., 2012)" provides the foundational framework for understanding grievance-based political behavior.

**Empirical Evidence**: Existing research demonstrates positive associations between measures of relative deprivation and various forms of political dissatisfaction and protest behavior. However, most studies focus on spatial rather than temporal comparisons, representing a significant limitation the current research addresses.

**Limitations and Debates**: Traditional relative deprivation research has been criticized for its focus on cross-sectional comparisons and limited attention to temporal dimensions. The authors note that "ethnographic research has revealed that many populist voters engage in explicit temporal comparisons; that is, they express a sense of loss and a desire to return to an idealized past (Cramer, 2016; Gest, 2016; Kuisz & Wigura, 2020)."

**Authors' Engagement**: The paper extends relative deprivation theory by incorporating temporal comparisons and developing multidimensional measures. The authors argue that traditional spatial comparisons are insufficient for understanding populist appeal, necessitating explicit attention to how individuals evaluate present circumstances against idealized past conditions.

### Status Anxiety and Social Identity Theory

**Theoretical Foundation**: The research engages with scholarship on status anxiety, particularly Gidron and Hall (2017)'s work on subjective social status and populist support. This tradition emphasizes how perceptions of group position influence political behavior, with declining status leading to compensatory political responses.

**Key Scholars and Works**: Beyond Gidron and Hall, the authors reference Bolet (2022) and Versteegen (2023) on social status anxiety and nostalgia. They note that "Gidron and Hall (2017) demonstrate that subjective social status anxiety predicts populist right-wing support across 15 European countries."

**Empirical Evidence**: Research consistently demonstrates correlations between perceived status decline and support for right-wing populist parties, though evidence for left-wing populism remains limited.

**Limitations and Debates**: Existing work primarily focuses on right-wing populism and often conflates different dimensions of status. The authors note that "the majority of studies have focused on support for far-right parties within a limited subset of countries within Western Europe."

**Authors' Engagement**: The paper extends status anxiety research by demonstrating its relevance for left-wing populism and developing more precise measures of different status dimensions (social, economic, political power).

### Economic Grievance Theory

**Theoretical Foundation**: This tradition emphasizes material conditions and economic inequality as drivers of populist support. Key mechanisms include labor market displacement, income stagnation, and regional economic decline.

**Key Scholars and Works**: The authors engage with extensive literature including Abou-Chadi & Kurer (2021), Anelli et al. (2021), Betz (1994), and others on economic determinants of populist voting. They note research showing that "lower-income voters are more likely to support far-right parties when their income growth lags behind other groups in society" (Burgoon et al., 2019).

**Empirical Evidence**: Substantial evidence links objective economic conditions to populist support, though effects vary considerably across contexts and operationalizations.

**Limitations and Debates**: Economic explanations struggle to account for populist support among economically secure individuals and fail to explain cross-ideological appeal. The authors note that "voters' subjective attitudes are arguably more proximate to their choices in the voting booth" than objective conditions.

**Authors' Engagement**: Rather than rejecting economic explanations, the authors integrate them within a broader framework emphasizing subjective perceptions of economic position over objective conditions.

### Theoretical Synthesis and Framework

The paper's theoretical contribution lies in synthesizing insights from relative deprivation theory, status anxiety research, and economic grievance models within a unified framework centered on nostalgic deprivation. The authors argue that "populist narratives, which generally centre around the negative influence of a globalized elite (Mudde, 2004), effectively unite citizens expressing different dimensions of deprivation" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 7).

The causal mechanism proposed involves several steps: (1) individuals make temporal comparisons between present circumstances and idealized past conditions; (2) perceived discrepancies generate feelings of nostalgic deprivation across multiple dimensions; (3) these feelings create receptivity to populist narratives emphasizing elite corruption and popular sovereignty; (4) receptivity translates into populist attitudes and potentially voting behavior.

The framework addresses tensions between different theoretical approaches by arguing that economic, cultural, and political grievances share common psychological foundations rooted in temporal comparison processes. The authors propose that "someone may feel economically stable but simultaneously outnumbered, socially discomforted or politically disempowered" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 3), suggesting that different dimensions of deprivation can operate independently while contributing to common political outcomes.

Scope conditions for the theoretical framework include the requirement for meaningful temporal benchmarks (hence the 25-year reference point) and the presence of populist parties capable of mobilizing nostalgic appeals. The authors also identify incumbency as a crucial moderating factor, noting that effects may reverse when populist parties hold power.

### Hypotheses and Theoretical Predictions

The research derives several key hypotheses from the theoretical framework:

1. **Main Effects Hypothesis**: Nostalgic deprivation across all three dimensions (social, economic, power) should positively predict populist attitudes and voting behavior.
2. **Cross-Ideological Hypothesis**: Effects should operate similarly for both left-wing and right-wing respondents, challenging traditional distinctions between populist movements.
3. **Cross-National Hypothesis**: The relationship should hold across diverse European contexts despite varying experiences of globalization and demographic change.
4. **Multidimensional Hypothesis**: Different dimensions of deprivation should have distinct antecedents while contributing to common political outcomes.

The authors differentiate their predictions from alternative explanations by emphasizing subjective perceptions over objective conditions and temporal rather than spatial comparisons. They note that the framework predicts "nostalgic deprivation predicts support for populist platforms among both left-wing and right-wing respondents, as well as across Eastern and Western Europe" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 1).

## Broader Theoretical Implications

### Connections to Other Research Programs

The paper's theoretical contributions extend well beyond populism studies to broader questions in political psychology, comparative politics, and political economy. The emphasis on temporal comparisons connects to research on retrospective voting and economic evaluations, suggesting that voters' temporal reference points may be more complex than typically assumed in retrospective voting models.

The multidimensional approach to deprivation has implications for social identity theory and intergroup contact research. By disaggregating social, economic, and political dimensions, the research suggests that group-based identities may be more fragmented and context-dependent than traditional approaches assume.

The findings also speak to broader debates about democratic representation and responsiveness. The authors suggest that "mainstream parties seeking to blunt the momentum of populist parties may need to address broader perceptions of downward mobility by challenging national myths (Smith, 1999), bolstering the dignity (Lamont, 2002) of those who see themselves as marginalized, or reducing the sense of threat (Gest, 2022) associated with ascending social groups" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 10).

The research connects to literature on political communication and framing by demonstrating how temporal frames can unite diverse grievances. This has implications for understanding how political entrepreneurs mobilize support and how counter-mobilization strategies might be developed.

## Methodology Overview

### Data Sources and Coverage

The research employs original survey data collected across 19 European countries in August 2020, representing one of the most comprehensive cross-national studies of nostalgic deprivation to date. The authors note that "initial samples of 1,000–1,200 European adults aged 18 and older were contacted in each country, drawing from the IPSOS online panel" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 3).

The dataset covers both Western European countries (Austria, Denmark, France, Germany, Italy, Netherlands, Spain, Sweden, United Kingdom) and Eastern European countries (Bulgaria, Czech Republic, Estonia, Hungary, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland, Romania, Slovakia, Slovenia), providing crucial variation in post-communist experiences and economic development levels.

The temporal coverage is particularly important given the theoretical focus on nostalgic deprivation. By surveying in 2020 and asking about conditions 25 years prior (1995), the research captures a meaningful generational comparison while ensuring that Eastern European respondents are not comparing to pre-communist periods.

The strengths of this data collection approach include the large sample size (19,296 respondents), representative sampling within countries, and standardized instrumentation across diverse contexts. Limitations include reliance on online panels (potentially excluding less digitally connected populations) and cross-sectional design (preventing causal inference).

### Key Variables and Operationalization

**Dependent Variables**: The research employs two key dependent variables measuring different aspects of populist support. For populist attitudes, the authors use "four questions derived from Akkerman et al. (2014), which probe the degree to which respondents are sceptical of elites, politicians and government institutions" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 3). For voting behavior, they measure "reported vote choice in the most recent national election" using the Rooduijn et al. (2019) classification of populist parties.

**Independent Variables**: The core theoretical variables involve three dimensions of nostalgic deprivation. For social deprivation, "respondents were asked to place themselves in a diagram which visualized how 'central and important you are to your society'" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 4). Economic deprivation involves assessments of how "financially well off are people like you compared to other people," while power deprivation measures "how much power do people like you have."

**Control Variables**: The research includes extensive controls for objective socio-demographic characteristics including "gender, age, income, education, marital status, occupational class and nativity" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 6). This allows the analysis to focus on subjective deprivation net of objective circumstances.

The operationalization choices reflect careful attention to theoretical requirements. The use of "people like you" phrasing allows younger respondents to make meaningful temporal comparisons even without direct experience of the comparison period. The 25-year timeframe ensures meaningful generational comparisons while avoiding confounding with regime changes in Eastern Europe.

### Analytical Model and Justification

The research employs both OLS and logistic regression models depending on the dependent variable, with robust standard errors clustered by country. The authors note that "since deprivation measures are correlated, we regress attitudinal and behavioural outcomes separately on economic, social and power deprivation" (Ferwerda et al., 2024, p. 6).

The identification strategy addresses several potential confounding factors. Country fixed effects control for unobserved national characteristics that might influence both deprivation and populist support. The inclusion of socio-demographic controls isolates subjective deprivation from objective circumstances. COVID-19 concern controls address potential contamination from pandemic-related anxieties.

For the core models, the specification can be represented as:

$$Y_{ic} = \alpha + \beta_1 Deprivation_{ic} + \beta_2 X_{ic} + \beta_3 COVID_{ic} + \gamma_c + \epsilon_{ic}$$

Where $Y_{ic}$ represents populist attitudes or voting for individual $i$ in country $c$, $Deprivation_{ic}$ captures nostalgic deprivation measures, $X_{ic}$ includes socio-demographic controls, $COVID_{ic}$ measures pandemic concerns, and $\gamma_c$ represents country fixed effects.

The analytical approach addresses endogeneity concerns through several mechanisms. The temporal structure of nostalgic deprivation (comparing present to 25 years ago) reduces reverse causality concerns. The inclusion of extensive controls addresses omitted variable bias. However, the authors acknowledge that "this analysis does not establish a causal relationship" and note that "these variables are likely mutually reinforcing."

The research demonstrates methodological sophistication in handling the complex structure of the data, with appropriate attention to clustering, weighting, and model specification. The separate analysis of different dimensions of deprivation addresses multicollinearity concerns while preserving theoretical distinctions. The robustness checks and alternative specifications (presented in supplementary materials) enhance confidence in the findings.
