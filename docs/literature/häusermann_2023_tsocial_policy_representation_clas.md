# Häusermann 2023 TSOCIAL POLICY REPRESENTATION Class and Social Policy Representation Authors: he chapter examines class-based differences in how citizens perceive their social policy preferences to be represented by political parties and the political system. While previous research has documented objective class biases in political representation, this study takes a "subjectivist turn" to analyze how voters themselves perceive representation across different social policy dimensions. Using original survey data from eight Western European countries, the authors find consistent class gradients in perceived representation, with higher social classes feeling better represented than lower classes, particularly regarding social consumption policies like pensions and unemployment benefits.

### Key Concepts

**Subjective Representation**:
The authors advocate for a "subjectivist turn" in studying representation:

> "Rennwald and Pontusson (2021) thus advocate a 'subjectivist turn' in the study of unequal representation, in order to better understand the extent, determinants and consequences of grievances caused by misrepresentation. We share their argument that voter perceptions of representation cannot simply be assumed, but rather need to be studied empirically." (p. 300)
> 

**Social Policy Priorities**:
Instead of general support for welfare, the authors focus on priorities across different policy fields:

> "Rather than in the level of support, citizens today differ more strongly in the type or field of social policy they prioritize: middle-class support is stronger for policies securing life cycle risks than for policies addressing labor-market risks; furthermore, while middle-class voters prioritize social investment, such as education and childcare much more strongly than voters in lower occupational and income classes, working-class voters prioritize income protection and social compensation policies." (p. 302)
> 

**Three Welfare Policy Dimensions**:

1. **Social Consumption Policies**: Traditional income transfers (pensions, unemployment benefits)
2. **Social Investment Policies**: Human capital formation (education, childcare, active labor market policies)
3. **Welfare Chauvinism**: Policy preferences regarding welfare benefits for migrants

**Types of Representation**:

1. **Party Representation**: Congruence between voters' preferences and their preferred party's priorities
2. **Systemic Representation**: Congruence between voters' preferences and the priorities of "politicians" or the party system in general

## 4. Key Findings and Evidence

### General Class Gradient in Perceived Representation

The authors find a clear class gradient in perceptions of systemic representation on social policy, with higher social classes perceiving better representation:

> "Figure 13.1 plots social classes' average evaluation of the extent to which 'political decision-makers share your (the respondent's) views about which reforms in social policy are the most important' (as measured on a 10-point scale). This figure corroborates one of the central tenets of unequal representation studies: the upper classes are indeed more likely to perceive politicians' (social policy) priorities as congruent with their own." (p. 304)
> 

The difference between upper-middle class and unskilled working class is approximately 0.5 points on a 10-point scale, which represents about a fourth of the standard deviation of this variable.

### Class Differences Across Policy Dimensions

The most striking finding is that class biases in perceived representation vary across different welfare policy dimensions:

1. **Social Consumption Policies** (pensions, unemployment benefits):
    - Strongest class gradient in perceived representation
    - Working classes feel significantly less represented than upper-middle classes
    - On consumption policies, "the distance to voters' preferred party is 3.4 points greater for the working class than for the upper-middle class" (p. 314)
2. **Social Investment Policies** (education, childcare, active labor market):
    - Moderate class gradient, but less pronounced than for consumption policies
    - Working classes feel parties prioritize investment more than they do
    - "On social investment policies, the distance to their own party is about 1.7 points larger for the unskilled working class than for the upper-middle class." (p. 315)
3. **Benefits for Migrants**:
    - Similar pattern to social investment, but with smaller differences
    - Working classes perceive parties as prioritizing migrant benefits more than they do

When looking at specific policy fields, the largest class differences appear in:

- Pensions
- Unemployment benefits
- Higher education

> "The strongest class gradient appears for two social consumption policies that are largely salient across countries and strongly demanded by lower-class voters: pensions and unemployment benefits." (p. 315-316)
> 

### Representation by Preferred Party vs. Party System

A surprising finding is that class biases persist even when looking at voters' preferred parties:

> "The second interesting finding stemming from these analyses is that the two measures of class inequalities in subjective representation display a greater similarity than we might have expected. In other words, class differences in perceptions of proximity are similar, whether gauged against respondents' preferred party or the main parties in the system." (p. 315)
> 

This suggests that even when voters choose parties that should represent their interests, these parties don't fully eliminate class biases in representation.

### Effects of Party System Configuration

Against expectations, the presence of strong challenger parties does not consistently mitigate class biases in representation:

> "The analyses summarized in Figure 13.4 return two interesting results. First, they indicate that, overall, the trends in unequal congruence identified in Figures 13.2 and 13.3 are persistent across most configurations of the partisan supply. Working-class voters perceive their own party and the system as less congruent with their consumption priorities (in comparison to the upper-middle class) in most of the countries under study." (p. 318)
> 

The primary exception is Spain, where a strong left-wing challenger party (Podemos) exists but no strong right-wing challenger:

> "In this country, in which the partisan supply is characterized by the presence of a strong left-wing challenger (Podemos), but the absence of one on the right, there are no class differences in perceptions of proximity, either by voters' preferred party or the system overall." (p. 318)
> 

However, the authors are cautious about generalizing from this single case.

## 5. Policy Implications and Mechanisms

### Challenges for Policy Reform

The findings suggest significant challenges for implementing major welfare reforms:

> "These results indicate that it might be hard to redress some of these inequalities in congruence. The lower-grade classes prioritize further expansion in those policy areas that represent a bigger portion of social spending (most notably, pensions). It appears very unlikely that parties' policy platforms will adopt a strongly expansionary pension agenda, moving the system's position closer to the working class demands." (p. 321)
> 

The perceived lack of representation may undermine the legitimacy needed for difficult policy reforms:

> "Faced with important demographic and socioeconomic transformations, worse perceptions of congruence might hinder the possibilities for governments to introduce welfare reforms. Responsiveness plays an important role in building a 'reservoir of goodwill' on which governments can capitalize to survive difficult periods of more 'responsible' but less-responsive decisions. The absence of such a reservoir might undermine the capability for parties to make hard choices." (p. 321-322)
> 

### Policy Salience

The authors find that class biases in perceived representation are strongest for policies that are most salient to voters:

> "Class differences in perceived party proximity are greater on those policies that are generally more salient to voters." (p. 316)
> 

This suggests that addressing unequal representation might require particular attention to highly salient policy areas like pensions and unemployment benefits.

## 6. Connections to Political Behavior

### Implications for Political Trust and Legitimacy

The findings have implications for political trust and legitimacy, particularly among working-class voters who consistently perceive worse representation:

> "In terms of implications and consequences, research on class-biased unequal representation has not only documented representation deficits, but also demonstrated detrimental effects of nonrepresentation, for example, in terms of political participation and alienation." (p. 300)
> 

### Voting Behavior and Party Choice

The authors examine whether voters choose parties that minimize distance on social policy priorities:

> "Additional analyses suggest that voters indeed tend to elect parties that are close to them on social policies, but also that this is not the only consideration, since some of them do vote for parties that are not minimizing distances on this issue. For about 25 percent of respondents, there would indeed be another party (different to the one they voted for) significantly closer to them (10 or more points closer on the 100-point scale)." (p. 322)
> 

Interestingly, there are no apparent class differences in whether voters select the party closest to their social policy preferences, suggesting other factors influence party choice.
