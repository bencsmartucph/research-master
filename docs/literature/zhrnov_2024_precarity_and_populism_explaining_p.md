# ⭐Zhrnov (2024) Precarity and populism: explaining populist outlook and populist voting in Europe through subjective financial and work-related insecurity" The paper explores the relationship between precarity (subjective financial and work-related insecurity) and populism across 10 European countries. It proposes a theoretical framework linking precarity to both populist outlook (attitudes such as people-centrism and anti-elitism) and populist voting behavior. The authors argue that precarity creates both a symbolic link (affecting populist attitudes) and an instrumental link (influencing voting for populist parties)

## Primary Theories Used

### >1. Ideational Theory of Populism

### Detailed Explanation

The paper adopts Mudde's (2004) ideational approach to populism, which conceptualizes it as a set of ideas based on the antagonistic relationship between "the people" and "the elite." This approach understands populism as a political mindset characterized by people-centrism, anti-elitism, and a Manichaean worldview. On the voter level, populism manifests as attitudes that are activated by external factors like economic insecurity. The ideational approach allows the authors to separate populist attitudes (outlook) from voting behavior, recognizing that people might hold populist views without necessarily voting for populist parties, depending on contextual factors.

### Direct Quotes

> "Following the ideational definition of populism, we interpret individual-level populism as a set of populist attitudes, as a particular political mindset, which, when activated, manifests as electoral support for populist parties and politicians" (p.706)
> 

> "The ideational definition of populism conceptualizes populism in minimal terms, as a unique set of ideas based on the antagonistic relationship between 'the people' and 'the elites' whereby populists seek to restore popular sovereignty (or the 'will of the people') for both parties and voters" (p.706)
> 

### Operationalization and Application

The authors operationalize populist outlook through three dimensions: people-centrism (viewing the will of the people as the primary source of political authority), anti-elitism (negative attitudes toward political and other elites), and Manichaean outlook (seeing politics as a conflict between two antagonistic groups). They measure these dimensions through survey questions validated in previous research. For populist voting, they use the PopuList database to classify parties as populist or non-populist and further categorize them as left, center, or right.

### Relevance to My Thesis

This theoretical approach connects to my thesis by providing a framework for understanding how economic insecurity might translate into populist politics through attitudinal changes. The distinction between populist outlook and voting behavior is particularly relevant, as it helps explain why economic vulnerability might create populist attitudes that don't always manifest in voting for populist parties.

## 2. Symbolic and Instrumental Link Theory

### Detailed Explanation

Building on Gidron and Hall (2017), the authors propose a dual-pathway model connecting precarity to populism. The symbolic link suggests that precarious individuals are drawn to populist views because these views enhance their representation by offering a narrative in which "the people," in contrast to secure elites, are affected by shared experiences of insecurity. The instrumental link postulates that populist attitudes can be activated into voting behavior when voters perceive populist parties as responding to their insecurity concerns. This framework explains how precarity can influence both attitudes and behavior through separate but related mechanisms.

### Direct Quotes

"Building on Gidron and Hall (2017), our theoretical framework postulates a symbolic and instrumental link between work-related (precarity of tenure and precarity at work), financial insecurity (broadly referred to as 'precarity'), and populism." (p.706-707)

"In our framework, we postulate that populist ideas can be activated into voting through an instrumental pull-in process according to which the voter perceives RPR and RPL agendas as responding to their subjective work and financial insecurity" (p.707)

### Operationalization and Application

The authors test this theoretical framework by examining the relationship between precarity measures and populist outlook first, then testing whether these relationships translate into voting behavior. They conduct separate analyses for the symbolic link (precarity → populist attitudes) and the instrumental link (precarity → voting for populist parties). The analysis accounts for contextual factors like incumbency status that might affect the instrumental link.

### Relevance to My Thesis

This dual-pathway model directly relates to my interest in how welfare institutions might mediate between economic vulnerability and political outcomes. The symbolic link helps explain how economic insecurity can foster anti-elite attitudes regardless of welfare context, while the instrumental link highlights how institutional factors (like whether populist parties are in government) can affect whether these attitudes translate into votes.

### 3. Precarity Theory

### Detailed Explanation

The paper draws on Standing's (2011) conceptualization of precarity as "a generalized set of social conditions and an associated sense of insecurity." It expands this concept beyond the traditional focus on employment contract insecurity to include multiple dimensions: precarity of tenure (job security), precarity at work (working conditions including autonomy, work-life balance, and income adequacy), and financial precarity (inability to meet basic financial needs). This multi-dimensional approach recognizes that insecurity can stem from various aspects of work and financial life, affecting people across the socioeconomic spectrum.

### Direct Quotes

"Expanding on this, we have operationalized the sociological notion of precarity that was popularized by Standing (2011) and is now widely used in sociological research (Parfitt and Barnes, 2020) to examine the potential effect of precarity on populist voting, where precarity refers to 'a generalized set of social conditions and an associated sense of insecurity'" (p.705)

"While this concept originates in the field of the sociology of work, its relevance and implications are far broader given the intersections between work and financial insecurity that individuals face in current times" (p.705)

### Operationalization and Application

The authors operationalize precarity through three distinct measures: (1) financial precarity, measured through questions about financial insecurity; (2) precarity of tenure, focused on job security concerns; and (3) precarity at work, centered on working conditions like autonomy and work-life balance. They conduct factor analyses to validate these measures and examine their relationships with populist attitudes and voting behavior separately and in combination.

### Relevance to My Thesis

This multidimensional conceptualization of precarity connects to my thesis by providing a more comprehensive framework for understanding the forms of economic vulnerability that might influence political behavior. The distinction between different types of precarity helps explain why certain welfare institutions might address some forms of insecurity but not others, potentially affecting their efficacy in preventing populist politics.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Multidimensional Precarity and Populism**: Both financial insecurity and poor working conditions significantly influence populist attitudes and voting, while job tenure insecurity shows weaker effects. This suggests that addressing the quality of work and financial security, not just employment stability, is crucial for mitigating populist tendencies.
2. **Symbolic vs. Instrumental Links**: Precarity has a more consistent relationship with populist attitudes (symbolic link) than with populist voting (instrumental link), which is more contingent on political context. This helps explain why economic vulnerability doesn't always translate directly into populist voting and highlights the importance of examining both attitudes and behavior.
3. **Cross-National Variations**: The relationship between precarity and populist voting varies across countries, with particularly strong effects in Germany, France, and Sweden, and negative relationships in countries where populist parties are in government (Poland and Hungary). This demonstrates the importance of political context in mediating between economic vulnerability and political behavior.
4. **Importance of Financial Insecurity**: Financial precarity shows the strongest and most consistent relationship with populist attitudes across countries, suggesting that welfare institutions addressing financial vulnerability might be particularly important for preventing populist backlash.
5. **Beyond Labor Market Dualism**: The findings challenge the focus on labor market insiders versus outsiders in explaining populist support, showing that insecurity affects broader segments of the population and operates through multiple dimensions. This suggests that comprehensive welfare approaches addressing various forms of precarity might be more effective than targeted programs for specific vulnerable groups.
