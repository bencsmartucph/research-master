## Primary Theories Used

### 1. Universalism-Particularism Framework

**Explanation:**
The universalism-particularism framework represents a political-sociological perspective on how socio-structural transformations in knowledge societies relate to inequality perceptions. Rather than viewing economic and cultural conflicts as separate dimensions that trade off against each other, this theory posits that attitudes toward inequalities align along a single dimension. On one end are universalists (typically new left voters) who oppose hierarchies and inequalities of all types, whether socioeconomic or sociocultural. On the other end are particularists (typically right-wing voters) who are more accepting of social stratification and hierarchies across multiple dimensions.

**Direct quotes:**
"A first, more rational-choice-based perspective expects a trade-off, with new left voters "privileged" by economic transformations emphasizing sociocultural inequalities over socioeconomic ones–and vice versa for "disadvantaged" far right voters. A second, more sociological perspective, predicts attitudes on inequalities to be aligned along a single dimension from new left "universalists" being inequality-averse to right-wing "particularists" being more inequality-tolerant."

"This literature shares a strong focus on structural changes in the economy, but theorizes these in a broader sense, beyond increasing income and wealth inequality, including the socio-structural implications of the educational expansion, changing occupational structures and the ensuing expansion of the educated middle classes."

"In such a perspective, the emergence of sociocultural dimensions of inequality on the political agenda has not necessarily crowded out economic inequality, but has extended the 'egalitarian' programme of the 'new left' to novel constituencies and beneficiaries."

**Application:**
The authors operationalize this framework by measuring respondents' perceptions of both the importance and problematization of six different inequalities (three socioeconomic: income, education, social class origin; and three sociocultural: gender, sexual orientation, migration background). They test whether new left/Green voters show higher levels of inequality aversion across all dimensions compared to radical right voters.

**Historical context:**
This framework builds on work by Kitschelt (1994), Bornschier (2010), Häusermann (2010), and Beramendi et al. (2015), who have documented the blurring of traditional "economic" and "cultural" dimensions of political conflict in Western democracies. The term "universalism-particularism" specifically acknowledges this amalgamation of previously separate dimensions into a more coherent cleavage with roots in socioeconomic change.

### 2. Trade-Off Hypothesis

**Explanation:**
The trade-off hypothesis represents a more rational choice-based perspective on how different electoral constituencies might prioritize certain types of inequalities over others. This theory suggests that new left voters (who tend to be more educated, middle-class, and economically advantaged) emphasize sociocultural inequalities while downplaying economic ones, while radical right voters (who tend to be less educated, working class, and economically disadvantaged) prioritize economic inequalities while dismissing sociocultural ones. This perspective implies that focusing on sociocultural inequalities comes at the expense of addressing economic inequalities, potentially explaining why lower-educated workers have shifted to the radical right.

**Direct quotes:**
"The absence of renewed, large-scale left-right class conflict at the level of partisan politics has been explained mainly with elite-driven factors. These authors argue that the focus of the New Left on inequalities other than economic ones (such as gender, migration or sexual orientation) has distracted left parties and left voters from the fight against economic inequality, and - moreover - has relegated lower educated voters to those parties contesting such a focus on sociocultural inequalities, in particular parties of the Radical Right."

"A crowing-out of economic inequalities by sociocultural ones may, in principle, be collateral or intentional. The rather rational choice-based perspective that follows from this literature suggests that, as voters of the New Left on average tend to belong to the winners of the knowledge society, they have an explicit or implicit interest in downplaying matters of economic distribution politically."

**Application:**
The authors test this hypothesis by examining whether new left/Green voters problematize sociocultural inequalities more strongly than socioeconomic ones, and whether the reverse pattern holds for radical right voters. They also analyze whether knowledge economy "winners" (highly educated, higher income, sociocultural professionals) are less concerned about economic inequalities than "losers" (less educated, lower income, production workers).

**Historical context:**
This perspective draws on work by scholars like Piketty (2020), Mudge (2018), Berman (2006, 2019), and Manow (2018), who have highlighted the role of elite-driven factors in the leftward shift of cultural politics and the rightward shift of economic politics. It represents a critique of how identity politics may have diverted attention from class-based economic concerns.

### 3. Knowledge Economy Transformation Theory

**Explanation:**
The knowledge economy transformation theory explains how shifts toward service-based, knowledge-intensive economies have reshaped social structures, political preferences, and perceptions of inequality. This theory posits that increasing demand for high-skilled labor in growing service sectors has led to educational expansion, occupational changes, and the growth of socioeconomic inequalities. These structural transformations have created new "winners" (highly educated professionals) and "losers" (less educated workers), reshaping partisan alignments and attitudes toward inequality.

**Direct quotes:**
"Opposition between the far right and the new left – primarily over sociocultural conflicts, matters of identity, and the boundaries of community – has transformed the politics of advanced democracies. The divide mobilized by these parties is often viewed as having displaced more traditional conflict over economic inequality."

"There is ample and still growing evidence that the rise of this political divide is linked to socio-structural electoral constituencies that mirror the development of opportunities and threats in the knowledge society: education is one of the strongest predictors of new left versus radical right voting."

"Political economic studies on the 'great U-turn' in the development of inequalities (i.e. the reversal of the declining trend to a growth of inequality after the 1970s) have launched a massive research agenda on the re-emergence of income, wealth, occupational, educational and risk inequalities in Western economies since the 1990s."

**Application:**
The authors operationalize this theory by examining how different structural characteristics associated with knowledge economy transformations (education level, field of education, occupational class, income) relate to perceptions of different types of inequality. They use multiple operationalizations to capture various aspects of being "winners" or "losers" in the knowledge economy.

**Historical context:**
This theory builds on work by scholars like Iversen and Soskice (2019), Oesch (2006, 2008), Gingrich and Häusermann (2015), and Kriesi et al. (2008), who have documented how socioeconomic transformations have reshaped electoral constituencies and political conflicts in advanced democracies.

## Key Findings

### 1. New Left voters are more inequality-averse across all dimensions

"Our results show that new left voters are generally more aware of differences due to socioeconomic and sociocultural factors and think of these as more problematic than their far right-party leaning counterparts."

"On all considered inequality dimensions, without exception, the Greens have higher values (perception of importance times problematization) than AfD supporters."

"Our findings mostly confirm the expectations of the universalist-particularist framework suggesting the alignment of both socioeconomic and sociocultural inequality concerns: progressive, left voters are more concerned about all types of inequality than conservative and especially radical-right constituencies."

**Relevance to your research:**
This finding challenges the idea that welfare policies would only appeal to the economic interests of far-right voters. It suggests that radical right supporters may be generally less concerned about inequalities, even economic ones, compared to left-wing voters. Your research on how welfare/labor/social investment policies might moderate support for far-right parties should consider that the appeal of such policies may not be primarily about addressing economic inequalities as such, but perhaps about other aspects like social status, recognition, or cultural identity.

### 2. Higher education is associated with greater inequality aversion, not less

"Across the six different types of inequality, including education, respondents educated in more cultural/communicative fields are more inequality averse."

"The highly educated 'winners of the knowledge economy' indeed think of sociocultural inequalities as more problematic than the less educated."

"While income inequality aversion of high and low educated respondents does not differ significantly, (dis)advantages due to education are the only inequality that is significantly more acceptable to highly educated knowledge economy winners than to their less educated counterparts."

**Relevance to your research:**
This finding suggests that educational disparities may be central to understanding support for far-right parties. For your research on welfare/labor policies, it implies that educational policies and ensuring equal educational opportunities may be particularly important for reducing political polarization. It also suggests that the correlation between education and political preferences may be partly due to socialization experiences rather than just economic self-interest.

### 3. Occupation and field of education strongly shape inequality perceptions

"The two occupational classes exemplary of the electoral realignment in knowledge economies, production workers and sociocultural professionals, mainly differ in their problematization of sociocultural inequalities and (dis)advantages due to one's social origin."

"Whereas production workers are more accepting of inequalities due to social origin, gender, sexual orientation and migration background, they display the same extent of acceptance with regards to (dis)advantages due to education or income."

"Socio-cultural professional, educated in fields requiring more cultural-communicative skills, core constituents of the new left/Green parties, are more inequality-averse than production workers or those with an education background in more technical-economic fields."

**Relevance to your research:**
This finding highlights the importance of occupational structures in shaping political preferences. For your research on welfare/labor policies and far-right support, it suggests that policies targeted at specific occupational groups may be more effective than universal approaches. It also indicates that workplace socialization experiences may be as important as material interests in shaping political attitudes.

### 4. Radical right voters show little concern for economic inequalities

"Our results point to no evidence that far right voters care strongly about economic inequalities, despite this party family's disproportionate success among lower-educated workers. This suggests that a particularist far right is highly unlikely to emerge as a champion for inequality, even on economic dimensions narrowly defined."

"The lower problematization and divisiveness of economic inequalities may entail a certain crowding-out of economic inequalities at the benefit of sociocultural inequalities at the systemic level of the party system."

**Relevance to your research:**
This finding challenges the economic anxiety thesis that often underlies research on welfare policies and far-right support. It suggests that welfare policies alone may not be sufficient to reduce support for far-right parties if they aren't accompanied by elements that address cultural concerns or status anxiety. Your research should consider how economic policies interact with cultural and status dynamics.

## Discussion and Relevance to Your Research

This paper has several important implications for your research on how welfare/labor/social investment policies might moderate support for far-right parties:

1. **Beyond economic self-interest:** The findings suggest that support for far-right parties is not primarily driven by economic concerns or self-interest, but by broader value orientations (particularism) that make these voters more accepting of various forms of inequality. This challenges the assumption that welfare policies targeting economic insecurity would automatically reduce far-right support.
2. **Importance of educational policies:** The strong association between education (both level and field) and inequality perceptions suggests that educational policies may be particularly important for reducing support for far-right parties. Your research should consider how educational investments and reforms could complement other welfare policies.
3. **Occupational targeting:** The differences in inequality perceptions between occupational groups suggest that welfare policies targeted at specific occupational sectors might be more effective than universal approaches. For example, policies supporting production workers specifically might be more effective than general income support.
4. **Framing matters:** The paper suggests that how policies are framed and communicated could be crucial. Far-right voters appear less responsive to appeals based on reducing inequality, suggesting that policies might need to be framed in terms of other values to appeal to these voters.

"If the Left in particular were to bring back economic inequalities to the forefront of electoral politics (for ideological, historical or normative reasons), a framing strategy that ties them to a joint agenda of universalism, involving both sociocultural and socioeconomic inequalities, might thus be more promising than a sole focus on economic inequalities."

1. **Status and recognition:** The paper highlights that status concerns may be more important than material inequalities for far-right voters. Your research should consider how welfare policies might address not just material needs but also status concerns and recognition.

## Key Takeaways

1. **New left voters are more inequality-averse across all dimensions than radical right voters**, challenging the narrative that they have abandoned economic concerns in favor of identity politics.

"Our findings mostly confirm the expectations of the universalist-particularist framework suggesting the alignment of both socioeconomic and sociocultural inequality concerns: progressive, left voters are more concerned about all types of inequality than conservative and especially radical-right constituencies."

1. **Education and occupational structures strongly shape inequality perceptions**, with highly educated individuals in socio-cultural fields being most inequality-averse.

"In terms of individual-level mechanisms, explanations for why new left voters might be generally inequality averse and receptive to universalist political appeals lie largely in their socialization experiences, especially through education and at the work place."

1. **Far-right voters show little concern for economic inequalities**, suggesting economic policies alone may not reduce their support.

"Our results point to no evidence that far right voters care strongly about economic inequalities, despite this party family's disproportionate success among lower-educated workers."

1. **Universalism-particularism provides a better framework than trade-off models** for understanding how inequality perceptions relate to political preferences.

"Overall, these structural factors relate to the problematization of inequalities largely as we would expect based on a more sociological (rather than instrumental) understanding of how people come to interpret the world, developing values through socialization experiences and embeddedness in certain social milieus."

1. **Political mobilization strategies need to consider both economic and cultural dimensions** rather than prioritizing one over the other.

"If the Left in particular were to bring back economic inequalities to the forefront of electoral politics (for ideological, historical or normative reasons), a framing strategy that ties them to a joint agenda of universalism, involving both sociocultural and socioeconomic inequalities, might thus be more promising than a sole focus on economic inequalities."
