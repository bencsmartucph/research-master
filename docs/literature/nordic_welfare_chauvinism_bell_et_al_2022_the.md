# ⭐Nordic Welfare Chauvinism (Bell et al., 2022) The paper examines welfare chauvinistic attitudes in Sweden, Norway, and Finland, arguing that while strict exclusionary welfare chauvinism (completely denying immigrants access to benefits) is virtually non-existent in Nordic countries, moderate welfare chauvinism (requiring citizenship before granting equal rights to benefits) is surprisingly prevalent. The authors contend this contradicts the Nordic welfare states' reputation for universalism and egalitarianism. Their central thesis is that welfare chauvinistic attitudes in Nordic countries are primarily explained by negative attitudes toward benefits in general and anti-immigrant sentiments, particularly toward culturally dissimilar immigrants, rather than by socioeconomic factors or dissatisfaction with society.

The argument is supported by robust empirical evidence using European Social Survey data, demonstrating that Finland, Norway, and Sweden rank high in Europe for citizenship-contingent welfare attitudes despite their reputation for tolerance. The paper's strength lies in its comparative approach both within Nordic countries and across Europe, providing nuanced insights into different forms of welfare chauvinism. The authors effectively distinguish between strict exclusion (denying benefits entirely) and moderate exclusion (requiring citizenship), highlighting how moderate positions may still undermine universalist principles.

The paper implicitly assumes a causal relationship between attitudes toward benefits, anti-immigrant sentiment, and welfare chauvinism, though the cross-sectional design cannot definitively establish causality. It also draws on theories of intergroup threat and scapegoating without extensively engaging with their theoretical foundations.

## Primary Theories Used

### Intergroup Threat Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Intergroup threat theory posits that anti-immigrant attitudes emerge from perceived threats that immigrants pose to the majority ethnic group. Drawing from Blumer's (1958) group position theory and later developed by scholars like Stephan et al. (2016), this framework distinguishes between symbolic threats (to values, culture) and realistic threats (to material resources). The theory suggests members of dominant groups feel they have proprietary claims to privileges and fear subordinate groups may threaten their position. In welfare contexts, this manifests as natives perceiving immigrants as competitors for limited resources like jobs, benefits, and housing. The theory holds that economic competition is especially salient for individuals with lower socioeconomic status who occupy similar economic positions as immigrants, leading them to view welfare distribution as a zero-sum game where immigrant gains mean native losses.

**Direct Quotes**:
"According to Blumer (1958), members of the dominant group feel that they have a proprietary claim to certain areas of privilege, and they fear that the subordinate group will threaten their position."

"We are primarily interested in the realistic threat perceptions, as they are related to concerns of the loss of material resources (Stephan et al., 2016). Here, it is posited that natives may perceive immigrants as competitors for jobs, benefits, affordable housing and so on."

**Operationalization and Application**: The authors operationalize intergroup threat theory through socioeconomic variables (education, income satisfaction, and unemployment status) as predictors of welfare chauvinism. They hypothesize that individuals with lower socioeconomic status would perceive greater economic threat from immigrants and thus display stronger welfare chauvinistic attitudes. This is tested in a linear regression model (Model 1) that examines associations between these socioeconomic factors and attitudes about when immigrants should receive equal welfare rights.

**Relevance to My Research**: This theory directly relates to my research on how welfare institutions mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics. It highlights how economic insecurity can shape status perceptions and create competition-based frameworks that may fuel populist responses. The paper's findings that socioeconomic factors have limited explanatory power in Nordic welfare states suggests that welfare institutions may be effectively buffering against direct economic threat perceptions, supporting my hypothesis about welfare institutions' role in status stabilization.

### Scapegoat Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Scapegoat theory, originally developed by Allport (1954), explains how prejudice and hostility toward outgroups can arise when individuals attribute societal problems to these groups rather than acknowledging systemic causes. This displacement of blame allows the ingroup to externalize responsibility for societal difficulties. In contemporary contexts, immigrants often become convenient scapegoats for economic downturns, austerity measures, or declining public services. The theory suggests that when people experience deteriorating social conditions, they seek simple explanations and targets for their frustration. The association between immigration and welfare decline in public discourse creates a cognitive framework where immigrants are blamed for straining welfare systems, regardless of empirical reality. This process has been exacerbated in recent decades by neoliberal policies coinciding with increased immigration, creating what Bello (2017) calls a perfect storm for scapegoating immigrants.

**Direct Quotes**:
"The implication is that some outgroups innocently attract the aggression generated by the frustrations suffered by members of the ingroup (Allport, 1954). It is not 'we' who are responsible for the misfortunes; it must be someone else's fault."

"Bello (2017) argues that decreased public expenditure in several OECD countries in the 1990s and 2000s combined with an increase in immigration made immigrants an easy scapegoat. She asserts that vulnerable groups saw deteriorating living standards combined with a rising immigrant population in their neighbourhoods and concluded it had to be the fault of the immigrants that living standards deteriorated."

**Operationalization and Application**: The authors test scapegoat theory by examining whether dissatisfaction with society predicts welfare chauvinism. They include two variables in Model 2 of their regression analysis: satisfaction with the economy and satisfaction with health services. The hypothesis is that individuals less satisfied with these aspects of society would be more likely to support welfare chauvinistic policies, blaming immigrants for perceived deterioration of services.

**Relevance to My Research**: Scapegoat theory provides valuable insight into my research question about how welfare institutions shape moral frameworks around economic vulnerability. When welfare institutions fail to maintain status stability during economic transitions, scapegoating of outgroups may increase, facilitating populist narratives. The paper's finding that satisfaction with society has limited explanatory power in Nordic contexts suggests strong welfare institutions may be preventing scapegoating dynamics, supporting my hypothesis about welfare institutions' role in shaping attributional processes and moral frameworks.

### Deservingness Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Deservingness theory examines how people make moral judgments about who deserves welfare benefits based on perceived characteristics and behaviors of recipient groups. This theoretical framework, developed in welfare research by scholars like Van Oorschot and Roosma (2017), suggests that public support for welfare distribution depends on assessments of recipients' control, need, identity, attitude, and reciprocity. In welfare chauvinism contexts, deservingness judgments often operate along ethnic and cultural lines, with majority populations perceiving certain immigrant groups as less deserving than others. The theory posits that cultural distance plays a crucial role in these assessments, with culturally dissimilar immigrants facing stricter deservingness criteria than culturally similar ones. These judgments create hierarchies of deservingness that impact policy preferences and may undermine universalist welfare principles by introducing conditional, identity-based criteria for benefit access.

**Direct Quotes**:
"Reeskens and Van der Meer (2017) find in their Dutch sample that individuals feel that certain nationalities 'deserve' welfare benefits more than other nationalities; for example, a Surinamese immigrant is considered more deserving of welfare benefits than an Afghan or a Moroccan immigrant."

"More culturally dissimilar immigrants are also often the target of welfare chauvinistic rhetoric (Edwards et al., 2021)."

**Operationalization and Application**: The authors operationalize deservingness theory by examining attitudes toward different immigrant groups as predictors of welfare chauvinism. They include two variables that distinguish between attitudes toward culturally similar immigrants and culturally dissimilar immigrants (measured by willingness to allow immigrants of same race/ethnicity versus immigrants from poor countries outside Europe). Model 4 of their regression analysis tests whether more negative attitudes toward either group predict stronger welfare chauvinistic attitudes.

**Relevance to My Research**: Deservingness theory directly connects to my research on how welfare institutions shape moral frameworks that mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics. It helps explain how welfare regimes can either reinforce or counteract particularistic moral frameworks that distinguish between deserving and undeserving recipients based on group identity. The strong association found between attitudes toward culturally dissimilar immigrants and welfare chauvinism in all three Nordic countries suggests that even universal welfare institutions may not fully eliminate identity-based moral frameworks, a crucial insight for my thesis about the dual status-moral pathways.

## Data Source and Empirical Methodology

The study uses data from the European Social Survey (ESS) round 8, collected in 2016-2017 across European countries, with particular focus on Norway (n=1,282), Sweden (n=1,131), and Finland (n=1,753). The total sample size is 4,366 native-born respondents, with foreign-born individuals excluded from analysis. The research employs linear regression models with welfare chauvinism as the dependent variable, measured through a question asking when immigrants should obtain equal rights to social benefits (from "immediately on arrival" to "they should never get the same rights").

The methodological approach uses nested models to test four explanations for welfare chauvinism: socioeconomic factors (Model 1), satisfaction with society (Model 2), attitudes toward benefits (Model 3), and anti-immigrant attitudes (Model 4). This progressive nesting allows the authors to assess the incremental explanatory power of each theoretical framework. Post-stratification weights are applied to ensure representativeness.

The methodology's strength lies in its comparative design, examining patterns both within Nordic countries and contextualizing findings within broader European trends. However, its cross-sectional nature limits causal inference, and the self-reported attitude measures may be subject to social desirability bias. Additionally, the operationalization of "culturally similar" versus "culturally dissimilar" immigrants relies on somewhat crude proxy measures.

This methodology could be adapted for my research by incorporating additional welfare regime variables and expanding to include non-Nordic countries with different welfare systems, allowing for more direct testing of how institutional configurations mediate economic vulnerability and political attitudes. Adding longitudinal dimensions would strengthen causal claims about welfare institutions' mediating effects.

## Findings and Evidence

1. **Strict welfare chauvinism is rare in Nordic countries, but moderate welfare chauvinism is common**
The study finds that extremely exclusionary attitudes (believing immigrants should never receive equal welfare rights) are rare in Nordic countries (Norway: 1.96%, Sweden: 0.76%, Finland: 2.52%). However, moderate welfare chauvinism (requiring citizenship before granting equal rights) is surprisingly prevalent: 36.77% in Norway, 29.65% in Sweden, and 42.39% in Finland. The evidence comes from descriptive statistics and comparisons with other European countries, showing that despite their reputation for tolerance, Nordic countries rank high in Europe for citizenship-contingent welfare attitudes. This finding suggests that citizenship-based conditioning of welfare rights could be a mechanism through which economic vulnerability translates into populist politics even in generous welfare states, supporting my hypothesis that welfare program design can affect social status independent of material benefits.
2. **Socioeconomic factors and satisfaction with society have limited explanatory power**
Unlike theorized, socioeconomic factors (education, income satisfaction, unemployment) and satisfaction with society have minimal impact on welfare chauvinistic attitudes in Nordic countries. The evidence comes from regression Models 1 and 2, where these variables explain only small proportions of variance in welfare chauvinism (e.g., only 1.5% in Finland). Even education, typically a robust predictor of attitudes toward immigrants, only significantly reduces welfare chauvinism in Sweden when controlling for all other factors. This finding challenges conventional economic insecurity explanations for populist attitudes and suggests welfare institutions may be successfully decoupling economic position from status perceptions, relevant to my research on how welfare institutions mediate the relationship between economic vulnerability and populist politics.
3. **Negative attitudes toward welfare benefits predict welfare chauvinism**
The study demonstrates a significant relationship between general skepticism toward welfare benefits and welfare chauvinistic attitudes. In the regression analysis, this factor has similar effects in Norway (β=0.244, p<0.001) and Sweden (β=0.236, p<0.001), though weaker in Finland (β=0.095, p<0.01). Model 3 shows inclusion of this variable substantially increases explanatory power (R² rises to 11% in Norway and Sweden). This finding supports the theoretical link between neoliberal values and welfare chauvinism, suggesting that welfare institutions shape broader moral frameworks about deservingness and responsibility. This directly connects to my research on how welfare institutions influence moral framing mechanisms that mediate economic vulnerability and political responses.
4. **Cultural dissimilarity strongly predicts welfare chauvinistic attitudes**
The strongest predictor of welfare chauvinism in all three countries is negative attitudes toward culturally dissimilar immigrants. Regression coefficients are substantial: Norway (β=-0.382, p<0.001), Sweden (β=-0.288, p<0.001), and Finland (β=-0.327, p<0.001). In Norway and Sweden, attitudes toward culturally similar immigrants have no significant effect, while in Finland they have a much weaker effect (β=-0.077, p<0.05) than attitudes toward culturally dissimilar immigrants. Model 4 shows the addition of these variables dramatically increases explanatory power (R² reaches 16% in Norway and Sweden, 12% in Finland). This finding indicates that welfare chauvinism operates through cultural and identity-based mechanisms rather than purely economic ones, supporting my hypothesis about the importance of moral frameworks in mediating between economic vulnerability and populist politics.
5. **Differences in immigrant population size and composition explain country variations**
The study identifies differences in welfare chauvinism patterns between the three Nordic countries, particularly Finland's higher levels of moderate welfare chauvinism (42.39%) and the significant effect of attitudes toward culturally similar immigrants there. These variations correspond to differences in immigrant population size (Finland: 5.8%, Norway: 14.4%, Sweden: 16.4%) and composition (Finland's large Russian population perceived as an economic threat). This contextual evidence suggests that welfare institution effects are moderated by demographic realities and the visibility of different immigrant groups, an important consideration for my research on how welfare institutions interact with local contexts to shape political responses to economic vulnerability.

## Discussion

The authors interpret their findings as evidence that welfare chauvinism in Nordic countries has distinct characteristics from other European contexts. They emphasize that the "moderate" form of welfare chauvinism (requiring citizenship before granting equal rights) represents a significant contradiction to Nordic universalist welfare principles, despite appearing less extreme than complete exclusion. The authors argue this indicates potential future shifts in Nordic social policy toward more restrictive approaches for non-citizens, effectively creating a two-tier welfare system that undermines universalism. They connect their findings to broader literature on how welfare chauvinistic attitudes can influence mainstream political parties to adopt more restrictive policies, even in generous welfare states.

The paper highlights the novel empirical connection between neoliberal attitudes toward welfare benefits and welfare chauvinism, suggesting this link may transcend welfare regime types. The authors also emphasize the importance of distinguishing between attitudes toward different immigrant groups, noting that welfare chauvinism primarily targets culturally dissimilar immigrants across all three countries despite varying immigrant population compositions.

For my research on welfare institutions mediating between economic vulnerability and populist politics, these interpretations provide crucial insights. The findings suggest welfare institutions shape moral frameworks around deservingness in ways that may facilitate or inhibit populist politics. Even in universal welfare regimes known for egalitarianism, identity-based moral frameworks persist that distinguish between deserving and undeserving recipients based on citizenship and cultural similarity. This supports my hypothesis that welfare institutions operate through dual pathways of status stabilization and moral framing. The prevalence of moderate welfare chauvinism in Nordic countries indicates that universalist welfare institutions may successfully prevent extreme exclusionary attitudes while still allowing for conditional, identity-based welfare preferences that could be mobilized by populist politics. The paper demonstrates how welfare deservingness frameworks can create openings for populist mobilization even in contexts where economic insecurity is effectively buffered by generous welfare provisions.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Welfare universalism does not eliminate identity-based moral frameworks**
Despite Nordic welfare states' reputation for universalism and egalitarianism, substantial proportions of their populations support conditioning welfare rights on citizenship status. This reveals that even the most universalist welfare regimes do not fully eliminate identity-based moral frameworks around deservingness. For my research, this suggests that welfare institutions may effectively stabilize economic status while still allowing for particularistic moral frameworks that condition belonging on citizenship or cultural similarity. This dual effect helps explain why economic shocks might translate into populist politics even in generous welfare states, as identity-based frameworks provide moral legitimation for exclusionary politics despite material security.
2. **Moderate welfare chauvinism represents a significant threat to universalist principles**
The paper demonstrates that moderate welfare chauvinism (requiring citizenship for benefit access) is surprisingly common in Nordic countries and represents a significant departure from universalist principles without appearing as extreme as complete exclusion. For my research, this highlights how welfare institutions can experience gradual normative erosion through seemingly moderate positions that nevertheless undermine core universalist principles. This suggests a mechanism through which populist politics might gradually transform welfare institutions—not through complete dismantling but through incremental introduction of conditionality and deservingness criteria that shift moral frameworks from universalistic to particularistic orientations.
3. **Cultural dissimilarity trumps economic factors in welfare chauvinism**
The strongest predictor of welfare chauvinism across all three Nordic countries is attitudes toward culturally dissimilar immigrants, while socioeconomic factors have minimal explanatory power. For my research, this finding suggests that welfare institutions' ability to prevent populist responses to economic vulnerability depends not just on material provision but on their capacity to promote moral frameworks that counteract cultural othering. This supports my thesis about the dual pathways model—welfare institutions must both stabilize status and promote universalistic moral frameworks to effectively prevent economic grievances from translating into populist politics.
4. **Neoliberal attitudes toward welfare benefits correlate with welfare chauvinism**
The paper identifies a significant relationship between general skepticism toward welfare benefits and welfare chauvinistic attitudes, supporting a theoretical link between neoliberal values and welfare chauvinism. For my research, this connection reveals how broader ideological frameworks about state responsibility and individualism interact with welfare institutions to shape responses to economic vulnerability. It suggests welfare institutions not only provide material benefits but also cultivate normative frameworks about collective responsibility and deservingness that can either facilitate or inhibit populist politics during economic downturns.
5. **Universalist welfare systems may prevent extreme exclusionary attitudes**
Despite surprisingly high levels of moderate welfare chauvinism, extremely exclusionary attitudes (believing immigrants should never get equal rights) remain rare in Nordic countries compared to other European nations. For my research, this suggests universalist welfare institutions may create a floor effect for exclusionary attitudes, preventing the most extreme forms while still allowing conditional forms of welfare chauvinism. This partial buffering effect helps explain cross-national variation in populist responses to economic shocks—universal welfare states may not eliminate populist tendencies but might moderate their expression and limit their most extreme manifestations, creating greater resilience against extreme right-wing populism during economic crises.
