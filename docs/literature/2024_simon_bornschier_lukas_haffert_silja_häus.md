## Comprehensive Summary

This groundbreaking study presents a sophisticated theoretical framework for understanding contemporary European electoral politics through the lens of cleavage formation. The authors argue that beneath the apparent chaos and fragmentation of modern party systems lies a structured transformation centered on the emergence of a new universalism-particularism cleavage. Their core theoretical contribution positions group identities as crucial mediators between social structure and political behavior, bridging the gap between structural determinism and political agency.

The research demonstrates that "Western Europe is experiencing growing levels of political polarization between parties of the New Left and the Far Right" (Bornschier et al., 2024, p. abstract), which reflects not chaos but the crystallization of new forms of political organization. The authors' central insight is that focusing on ideological party blocks rather than individual parties reveals underlying stability and cross-national similarities that observers focused on fragmentation have overlooked. Their work fundamentally challenges both dealignment theorists who emphasize electoral volatility and purely structural accounts that ignore the role of identity formation in political mobilization.

## Key Takeaways

1. **New Cleavage Emergence**: The authors identify a universalism-particularism cleavage that has emerged across Western European democracies, structuring competition between New Left and Far Right party blocks in remarkably similar ways across different national contexts.
2. **Identity Mediation**: Group identities serve as crucial intermediaries between social structural positions and political behavior, explaining how "the links between social structure and political parties are fostered and perpetuated in a world in which parties' ideological appeals address broad segments of the electorate" (p. 5).
3. **Constrained Volatility**: Rather than experiencing random electoral chaos, European democracies exhibit "constrained volatility" where voters switch between parties within ideological blocks but rarely cross block boundaries, suggesting underlying structural stability.
4. **Knowledge Economy Foundations**: The structural basis of the new cleavage lies in the transformation toward knowledge-based economies, creating new patterns of winners and losers that are "materially, but also more broadly in terms of status, outlook, or the rise and demise of worldviews and ways of life" (p. 16).
5. **Cross-National Consistency**: Despite different institutional contexts and timing of realignment, the authors find "strikingly consistent patterns across countries" (p. 34) in how social identities relate to political alignments.
6. **Agency Within Structure**: Political entrepreneurs operate within constraints set by structural transformations and identity potentials, explaining both similarities and variations in how the new cleavage manifests across countries.

## Introduction and Motivation Analysis

### Research Problem and Significance

The paper addresses a fundamental empirical puzzle in contemporary European politics: how to reconcile the apparent contradiction between increasing electoral volatility and fragmentation on one hand, and evidence of persistent socio-structural influences on voting behavior on the other. The authors frame this as a debate between two competing interpretations of electoral change.

The dealignment perspective, exemplified by scholars like De Vries and Hobolt, portrays contemporary voters as "dealigned" and susceptible to strategic appeals by political entrepreneurs who "creatively unite heterogeneous electoral coalitions based on the issues of the day, galvanized by populist or emotional appeals" (p. 1). This view suggests that traditional social anchors of political behavior have dissolved, creating a fluid and unpredictable electoral environment.

Conversely, the realignment perspective, drawing on comparative political economy and cleavage theory, emphasizes "the role of long-term structural changes of the economy and society at large that give rise to fundamentally new conflicts across advanced democracies" (p. 1). This tradition includes influential works by scholars like Kitschelt, Kriesi, Beramendi, Häusermann, and Hooghe and Marks, who argue for continuing structural foundations of political competition.

The authors position their work as resolving this theoretical tension by demonstrating that "politics remains anchored in social divisions does not imply that Boris Johnson, Marine Le Pen, or Emanuel Macron do not matter, but rather, that their leeway in rallying coalitions of social groups is limited by the extent to which these groups share fundamental conceptions of who they are and what they want" (p. 1).

This approach addresses broader questions about the resilience of democratic representation in knowledge economies, the conditions under which structural change produces political realignment versus dealignment, and the mechanisms through which new forms of political conflict emerge and stabilize in post-industrial societies.

### Theoretical Positioning and Innovation

The authors build upon but significantly extend classical cleavage theory by introducing two key innovations. First, they emphasize group identities as "the intermediate level connecting social structure and the organizational expression of cleavages" (p. 2). This addresses a crucial gap in existing literature, as they note that "those who insist that cleavages continue to matter have not devoted much attention to answering the question of what constitutes the 'glue' linking social groups and political parties" (p. 7).

Second, they advocate studying "alignments between identity-laden social groups and ideological blocks, rather than individual parties" (p. 2). This analytical innovation allows them to distinguish between surface-level party competition and deeper patterns of electoral alignment that transcend specific organizational forms.

The authors challenge existing scholarship by arguing that purely structural approaches cannot explain why certain appeals resonate with specific groups while others fail. They contend that "appeals only resonate with individuals when they fall on 'fertile soil,' that is, when individuals share a collective identity, or at least frameworks of understanding and worldviews that can provide the basis for one" (p. 6-7).

Their theoretical framework also addresses the transformation of party organization, noting that parties have evolved "from being the political expression of social groups to becoming brokers that build coalitions between social groups on ideological grounds" (Katz and Mair, 2018, cited on p. 6). This organizational change necessitates new theoretical tools for understanding contemporary voter-party linkages.

## Literature Review and Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Major Theoretical Traditions Engaged

### Cleavage Theory and Electoral Realignment

The paper extensively engages with the classical cleavage tradition initiated by Lipset and Rokkan (1967) and developed by scholars like Bartolini and Mair (1990). The authors note that traditional cleavage theory emphasized "the role that collective identities played in this translation of structural disruption into political competition: A sense of shared identity is central to collective action, and it mediates the nonobvious step from objective group belonging to political mobilization" (p. 15).

However, they critique existing cleavage scholarship for neglecting the identity component in contemporary analysis. They observe that "Over the past decades, researchers have studied how the 'old' cleavage structures have profoundly loosened, focusing on both structural and political change, but largely neglecting the question of how voters perceive their place in this transformed landscape" (p. 15-16).

The realignment literature, including work by Inglehart (1984), Kitschelt (1994), and Kriesi et al. (2008), provides the foundation for understanding how "value change, educational expansion, and economic modernization are reconfiguring the links between voters and parties, rather than disrupting them" (p. 3). The authors build on this tradition while addressing its limitations in explaining the mechanisms of identity formation and political mobilization.

### Comparative Political Economy

The paper draws heavily on comparative political economy scholarship that documents how structural transformations create new patterns of inequality and opportunity. They reference work by Beramendi et al. (2015), Häusermann and Kriesi (2015), and Iversen and Soskice (2019) that demonstrates how "class, educational background, and the relative position of social groups in the knowledge economy continue to shape individual preferences and policy outcomes, though in new ways" (p. 4).

This literature provides crucial insights into the material foundations of political realignment, showing how the transition to knowledge economies creates "new inequalities [that] have contributed to raising the saliency of questions related to cultural liberalism and changing gender roles, immigration and multiculturalism, or the boundaries of communities" (p. 26-27).

### Social Psychology and Identity Theory

The authors innovatively integrate social psychological insights from social identity theory, drawing on work by Tajfel and Turner (1979), Huddy (2001), and Mason (2018). They explain that "Work building on social identity theory documents people's innate tendency to simplify the social world by categorizing and stereotyping into 'us' and 'them.' A psychological desire to positively distinguish one's own group fosters in-group bias and the derogation of out-groups" (p. 18).

This integration allows them to explain why certain group identifications become politically salient while others remain dormant. They note that social psychology "especially highlights the affective component of identity antagonisms, which has recently entered issue-based/spatial accounts of political conflict via the concept of 'affective polarization'" (p. 19).

### Cultural Sociology

The paper also draws on cultural sociology, particularly Bourdieusian approaches to understanding symbolic boundaries. They reference work by Lamont (2000) and others showing how "groups monopolize privileges and resources by constructing 'symbolic boundaries' of who belongs and who does not" (p. 18). This perspective helps explain how material interests translate into cultural identities and political alignments.

### Theoretical Synthesis and Framework

The authors develop an integrated framework that positions group identities as mediators between social structure and political behavior. They argue that "cleavages become consolidated at the identity level as conflict potentials arising from social structure become activated by political actors" (p. 16).

Their theoretical model addresses the question of "how durable links between social constituencies and party blocks emerge along this divide" (p. 2) by emphasizing three key processes:

First, **identity salience** increases as individuals interact with members of similar groups, creating "similarities in terms of the raw materials for a new cleavage" (p. 10) across countries. As they explain, "Because our personal interactions are patterned by social structural position – chiefly in terms of class, education, and urban-rural residence – identity salience is not entirely voluntaristic" (p. 10).

Second, **frame alignment** occurs when political parties' appeals resonate with existing group identities and grievances. The authors note that framing "draw[s] on the cultural stock of images of what is an injustice, of what is a violation of what ought to be" (Zald, 1996, cited on p. 10).

Third, **block formation** emerges as parties compete within ideological spaces rather than completely restructuring them. They argue that "politicians and 'issue entrepreneurs' for the most part move within the ideological space without fundamentally altering its basic contours or the group divisions underlying competitive dimensions" (p. 12).

The theoretical framework also addresses scope conditions, noting that "the mobilization markets for different identities are constrained and that the constraints emanate from the fact that identities and the structural potentials that allow these identities to resonate are intertwined" (p. 71).

### Hypotheses and Theoretical Predictions

The authors derive several key hypotheses from their theoretical framework:

**Structural Consistency Hypothesis**: "We start here from the insight that each individual holds multiple identities with the potential of being politically relevant... the relative salience of these identities should determine which of them will shape political alignments" (p. 10). This leads to the prediction that similar structural transformations across countries will produce similar identity potentials.

**Frame Convergence Hypothesis**: Political mobilization frames should converge across contexts because they "draw on the cultural stock of images" available in similar knowledge economies. They specifically predict that "the Far Right has converged on a particularistic frame that emphasizes the preservation of traditional (national) communities and status hierarchies" (p. 10).

**Block Alignment Hypothesis**: Voters should align with ideological blocks rather than individual parties, creating "constrained volatility" where movement occurs within blocks but rarely between them. They predict that "voters seldom switch between ideological party blocks" (p. 2).

**Identity Mediation Hypothesis**: Group identities should mediate the relationship between structural position and political behavior, with "the most important in-groups and out-groups of each electorate constitut[ing] massively important predictors" (p. 52) of vote choice.

## Broader Theoretical Implications

### Connections to Other Research Programs

The paper's theoretical contributions extend beyond electoral behavior to broader questions in political science and related disciplines. The authors' framework for understanding identity formation connects to research on social movements, noting that "the literature on social movements that highlights how collective action frames point to injustices and combine them with a definition of the group or social category in question" (Gamson, 1992, cited on p. 9).

Their work also bridges political sociology and political psychology by showing how structural transformations create psychological motivations for group identification. They explain that "the quest for maintaining dignity and status" can motivate boundary-drawing, where "pride in national or rural communities, identification with hard work, or adherence to traditional, conservative, more patriarchal moral standards provide a path to positive identity even for objective 'losers' of economic and social change" (p. 18).

The framework has implications for understanding democratic representation and governance, as the authors note that "increased party fragmentation makes it ever more difficult to create governments or find majorities in legislatures" (p. 74), while new forms of identity-based polarization may threaten liberal democratic norms.

## Methodology Overview

### Data Sources and Coverage

The authors employ original survey data from "France, Germany, Switzerland, and the UK" collected between "November 2020 and January 2021" (p. 19). The surveys recruited "2,000 participants each in France and Germany and 3,000 participants each in Switzerland and the UK" (p. 19), with samples that are "population-representative in terms of education, age, and gender" (p. 20).

The case selection strategy reflects theoretical considerations about electoral realignment timing. The authors explain that "France and Switzerland are representative of countries that experienced early and strong realignment, Germany and the UK are cases of late and less consolidated realignment" (p. 20). This variation allows them to test whether similar structural foundations produce similar identity patterns despite different political trajectories.

The temporal scope is significant, as the authors note they "conducted our surveys in the aftermath of several major events, including the COVID-19 pandemic in all our countries, and Brexit in one of them" (p. 73-74). If their framework holds under these stressful conditions, it suggests considerable robustness.

### Key Variables and Operationalization

**Group Identity Measures**: The centerpiece of their measurement strategy involves asking respondents about "perceived closeness to different social groups" using the question "Of the following groups, how close do you feel towards them? By 'close' we mean people who are most like you in terms of their ideas, interests, and feelings" (p. 20) on a ten-point scale.

The seventeen groups measured span multiple dimensions: education (higher, medium, lower education), class (wealthy people, humble means, hard workers, creative workers, social/education workers), residence (urban, rural), national identity (conationals, people with migration background), cultural orientation (cosmopolitans, down-to-earth people, culturally interested people), gender (feminists), and religion (Christian-Western values).

**Party Block Classification**: Rather than relying on traditional party family classifications, the authors derive party blocks empirically using "mixture models fitted to the 2019 Chapel Hill Expert Survey (CHES) data" (p. 23). This produces three main blocks: New Left (combining green left and traditional left), Traditional Right, and Far Right, with an additional Right Liberal category for France.

**Structural Variables**: The authors measure education as "lower (primary and lower secondary education), middle (upper- and post-secondary education and professional training), and higher levels of education degrees (tertiary education)" (p. 27). They also include measures of educational field (CECT ratios), occupational class (using Oesch's framework), and residence (urban-rural distinctions calibrated by country).

### Analytical Model and Justification

The authors employ multiple analytical strategies appropriate to their theoretical questions. For validating the structural foundations of identities, they use "linear regression models, pooled across countries (with data weighted by education, gender, and age), predicting closeness to key identity groups" (p. 30).

To study the political mobilization of identities, they employ discriminant analysis that "seeks to determine which combination of group identities most powerfully predicts vote choice for the New Left, Right, and Far Right party blocks" (p. 49). This technique allows them to identify the most important identity distinctions while handling the complexity of multiple group memberships.

For examining cross-national variation, they use comparative analysis of group-party associations, asking respondents "Which party would you associate most closely with each of the following groups?" (p. 58). This approach allows them to test whether voters have accurate mental maps of their party systems and whether these maps reflect the theoretical expectations about cleavage formation.

The authors also employ conjoint experimental methods where "respondents to indicate which person they feel closer to" (p. 36) based on randomized profiles, allowing them to assess the relative importance of different identity dimensions in driving feelings of closeness.

The analytical strategy is designed to address potential confounding by using multivariate models that control for objective social characteristics while testing the independent effects of subjective identities. As they note, "controlling for voters' social structural characteristics, their central in-groups and out-groups are massively important predictors of vote choice" (p. 52).

The authors' mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative survey analysis with experimental components and cross-national comparison, provides multiple forms of evidence for their theoretical claims about cleavage formation through identity mediation.
