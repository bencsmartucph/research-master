## **Radical right voters in two-dimensional welfare politics**

For many decades, the working class has been the core constituency of the left while upper and middle classes have lent their support predominantly to conservative, liberal or Christian–democratic parties. However, socio-structural transformations in post-industrial societies, the emergence of new party families, and the increasing salience of issues such as immigration have led to the emergence of new ties between parties and classes. Most notably, the working class has become the backbone of support for the radical right (Oesch [Citation2008](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#); Rydgren [Citation2012](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)). In contrast, the well-educated, new middle class, especially professionals working in health, education, welfare, or the media sector – the so-called socio-cultural professionals – have become the preserve of Left parties in most West European countries (Oesch and Rennwald [Citation2018](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)). However, more traditional sectors of the middle class, most prominently small business owners, are a ‘contested stronghold’ of the centre-right due to their right-wing economic preferences; however, mostly due to their scepticism of immigration and integration, they are also attracted to the radical right. Nevertheless, the proletarisation of the radical right (Bornschier [Citation2010](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)) has resulted in their largest vote potential lying within the working class.

This proletarisation has implications for RRPs’ positioning on the economic dimension. The increasing share of working-class voters has led them to move towards the centre or towards ‘blurring’ their stances on economic issues and welfare state generosity. In order to please their working-class voters’ demand for protection without jeopardising their traditional middle-class voters’ aversion to state-intervention, RRPs are expected to strategically blur their position (Rovny [Citation2013](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#); Rovny and Polk [Citation2020](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)).

We argue in this paper that the situation of RRPs is completely different for the second dimension of welfare politics. Rather than focussing on the size or generosity of the welfare state, conflict in this dimension is about how the welfare state should be recalibrated, whose needs it should cater to, and what goals it should pursue. As discussed in detail below, the literature suggests that the radical right electorate occupies a predominantly consumption-oriented position due to a) their working-class voters’ material self-interest, b) a connection between consumption support and particularistic socio-cultural attitudes, and c) trust considerations.

The aforementioned proletarisation of RRPs shifts the median partisan’s placement towards prioritising consumption over social investment because, for the working-class constituency, it may be clearer whether and to what degree these benefits pay off. First, consumption policies materialise immediately whereas investments usually only pay off in the future. Second, willingness to invest in the future may depend on the economic outlook, which might be considered grimmer among working-class voters (Häusermann *et al.* [Citation2021](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)). Third, it has been shown that social investment policies suffer from ‘Matthew effects’, where the lower classes seem to have less knowledge about how to utilise investing policies such as childcare and labour market reintegration measures (Bonoli and Liechti [Citation2018](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#); Pavolini and Van Lancker [Citation2018](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)).

Moreover, even beyond self-interest, there is a link between support for the radical right and a preference for consumption over investment. Beramendi *et al.* ([Citation2015](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)) have postulated the existence of a nexus between the second, non-economic dimension of political conflict and emphasis on investment and consumption because of an inherent logical connection between universalism and social investment, and particularism and consumption. There are good reasons why prioritising consumption over investment fits with RRPs’ and their voters’ particularistic positions. First, the stabilising character of consumption-oriented social policies (e.g. pensions and contribution-based unemployment benefits) that promote rather than challenge traditional gender roles and the male breadwinner model should find an echo in culturally conservative attitudes. In contrast, many social investment policies enhance gender equality which is connotated to universalistic values (Busemeyer *et al.* [Citation2021](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)).

Second, consumption policies are more easily targetable towards specific groups that are perceived as being the most deserving of welfare benefits. Pension systems, for example, can be arranged so that they reward ‘hard-working’ native men but exclude labour-market outsiders and immigrants. Meanwhile, social investment policies such as education or childcare have the explicit goal of increasing equality of opportunity. Therefore, social investment policies tend to benefit groups such as atypical workers and immigrants as well. However, these groups the radical right would like to exclude or at least reduce in terms of their presence in the pool of welfare recipients (Ennser-Jedenastik [Citation2018](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#); Fenger [Citation2018](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)).[Footnote2](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)

Lastly, research has highlighted the importance of trust in government and political institutions as a vital factor in predicting support for social investment. Since social investment measures can be expected to pay off only in the long-term, are fraught with considerably more uncertainties than known, existing consumption policies, and depend on effective implementation, trust in political agents is essential for supporting (social) investment measures (Garritzmann, Neimanns, *et al.* [Citation2018](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#); Jacobs and Matthews [Citation2017](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)). RRPs, which usually have a strong populist component, however, frequently campaign on an anti-establishment platform that subverts citizens’ trust in politics and political elites. Concomitant with that, RRPs are especially successful in mobilising and attracting voters that have a low level of trust in politics, politicians and political institutions (Bélanger and Aarts [Citation2006](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#); Söderlund and Kestilä‐Kekkonen [Citation2009](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)). It follows that RRP voters are less likely to embrace social investment.

To summarise, the literature points to several mechanisms that account for a relationship between radical right support and the prioritisation of consumption over investment. This link is largely confirmed in empirical analyses (Busemeyer *et al.* [Citation2021](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#); Fossati and Häusermann [Citation2014](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#); Garritzmann, Busemeyer, *et al.* [Citation2018](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#); Häusermann, Pinggera, *et al.* [Citation2020](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#)). Moreover, our own attempt replicates this finding (see [Figure A1 in online appendix](https://doi.org/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115)): radical right voters constitute the clear, exclusive pole in favour of prioritising consumption such as pensions over policies such as childcare or education[Footnote3](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01402382.2021.1902115#). Their preference for consumption policies is statistically distinct from the preferences of all other electorates. If RRP voters want any social policy at all, they clearly prefer traditional, insurance policies.

## Introduction and Overview

The 2022 article by Matthias Enggist and Michael Pinggera in West European Politics challenges prevailing academic understanding of radical right parties' (RRPs) positions on welfare state policies. While previous research characterized RRPs' economic positions as deliberately "blurry," moderate, or inconsistent, Enggist and Pinggera demonstrate that this blurriness is limited to only one dimension of welfare politics - welfare state generosity. On a second dimension - the prioritization of consumption-oriented policies (like pensions) versus social investment policies (like education) - RRPs actually take clear positions favoring traditional welfare state consumption policies.

The paper makes several significant contributions to understanding party competition and welfare politics in Western Europe:

> "Recent literature shows that radical right parties (RRPs) present moderate or blurry economic stances. However, this paper argues that this blurriness is restricted to only one of the two main conflicts of contemporary welfare politics, namely questions centring on welfare generosity. In contrast, when it comes to the goals and principles the welfare state should meet, RRPs take a clear stance favouring consumption policies such as old age pensions over social investment, in accordance with their voters' preferences." (p. 102)
> 

The authors analyze party manifestos from seven European countries and original survey data on voters' perceptions of party positions. Their findings demonstrate that while RRPs deliberately de-emphasize questions about welfare state size, they consistently and clearly advocate for traditional consumption-oriented welfare policies over social investment policies.

> "RRPs de-emphasise how much welfare state they want while consistently and clearly defending the traditional welfare state's consumptive focus against recalibration proposals." (p. 102)
> 

This finding represents a significant nuancing of our understanding of radical right economic positioning, showing that RRPs are not merely "blurry" on all economic issues, but rather strategically ambiguous on some dimensions while clear on others.

> "These findings have important implications for party competition and welfare politics." (p. 102)
> 

## Theoretical Framework

### Radical Right Parties and Economic Positioning

The authors begin by situating their research within the literature on radical right economic positioning. They acknowledge that RRPs have primarily mobilized voters on socio-cultural issues, particularly immigration, while their economic positions have received less scholarly attention. They trace how academic understanding has evolved from Kitschelt and McGann's (1997) "winning formula" that paired authoritarianism with neoliberalism, to more recent research showing RRPs adopting more centrist economic positions.

> "This newer research has shown that against the expectations of the 'winning formula', RRPs no longer present distinctly right-wing economic positions and argued that RRPs have very good reasons to refrain from advocating staunchly welfare-critical stances. On the contrary, a range of studies have placed RRPs around the centre of the economic dimension (Afonso and Rennwald 2018; de Lange 2007; Kitschelt 2004) or have at least observed them moving to the left (Eger and Valdez 2015, 2019; Lefkofridi and Michel 2014; Rovny and Polk 2020)." (p. 105)
> 

The authors pay particular attention to Rovny's (2013) thesis that RRPs deliberately blur their economic positions due to their electorally diverse constituency:

> "According to Rovny, parties are well-advised to take a more pronounced stance on issues that are shared unequivocally by a party's core constituency while opting to blur their positions on issues where they face a divided electorate – as RRPs do most prominently concerning the economy and the welfare state." (p. 105)
> 

The blurring concept is crucial to the paper's argument. It refers to a strategic choice by parties to either avoid taking clear positions or to present vague, contradictory or ambiguous positions on issues where their electorate has heterogeneous preferences.

### Two-Dimensional Welfare Politics

The paper's key theoretical innovation is to identify and analyze two distinct dimensions of welfare politics, rather than treating welfare as a single dimension. The authors argue that welfare politics has fundamentally transformed:

> "Welfare state politics, which is traditionally seen as one of the main issues of the economic dimension, has fundamentally transformed over the last decades. Structural changes have had lasting effects on both citizens' demand for social protection and elites' leeway for providing the demanded coverage." (p. 106)
> 

They identify a second dimension of welfare state conflict that centers on recalibration - what the welfare state should do, rather than how large it should be:

> "The most established way of thinking about the conflict concerning what the welfare state should do is the social investment paradigm (Beramendi et al. 2015; Esping-Andersen et al. 2002; Hemerijck 2013; Morel et al. 2012). The logic of social investment policies differs from that of 'passive' or 'consumptive' social policies in that the former aims at 'creating, mobilising, or preserving skills' (Garritzmann et al. 2017: 37) in order to support citizens' earnings capacity." (p. 106-107)
> 

This distinction between consumption policies (like pensions and unemployment benefits) and social investment policies (like education and childcare) forms the backbone of their analysis.

> "The conflict over the recalibration of the welfare state is masked if we focus only on general support for the welfare state. Conflict over the size of the welfare state is different from conflict over social investment vs. consumption priorities." (p. 107)
> 

### Radical Right Voters and Two-Dimensional Welfare Politics

The authors then explain how the socio-structural composition of radical right electorates shapes their welfare preferences. They note that RRPs increasingly draw support from working-class voters (the "proletarisation" of the radical right), while also maintaining support from segments of the traditional middle class, particularly small business owners.

They argue that while this diverse electoral coalition creates heterogeneous preferences regarding welfare state generosity (working-class voters preferring more generous welfare, middle-class voters less), RRP voters are united in their preference for consumption over investment policies. The authors provide three mechanisms to explain this:

1. Material self-interest of working-class voters:
    
    > "First, consumption policies materialise immediately whereas investments usually only pay off in the future. Second, willingness to invest in the future may depend on the economic outlook, which might be considered grimmer among working-class voters (Häusermann et al. 2021). Third, it has been shown that social investment policies suffer from 'Matthew effects', where the lower classes seem to have less knowledge about how to utilise investing policies such as childcare and labour market reintegration measures (Bonoli and Liechti 2018; Pavolini and Van Lancker 2018)." (p. 108-109)
    > 
2. Connection between consumption support and particularistic socio-cultural attitudes:
    
    > "First, the stabilising character of consumption-oriented social policies (e.g. pensions and contribution-based unemployment benefits) that promote rather than challenge traditional gender roles and the male breadwinner model should find an echo in culturally conservative attitudes. In contrast, many social investment policies enhance gender equality which is connotated to universalistic values (Busemeyer et al. 2021)." (p. 109)
    > 
3. Trust considerations:
    
    > "Research has highlighted the importance of trust in government and political institutions as a vital factor in predicting support for social investment. Since social investment measures can be expected to pay off only in the long-term, are fraught with considerably more uncertainties than known, existing consumption policies, and depend on effective implementation, trust in political agents is essential for supporting (social) investment measures (Garritzmann, Neimanns, et al. 2018; Jacobs and Matthews 2017)." (p. 109)
    > 

## Hypotheses

Based on this theoretical framework, the authors develop three hypotheses:

1. Regarding welfare state size:
    
    > "Hypothesis 1: Radical right parties exhibit a blurry position on the welfare state size dimension by de-emphasising social policy." (p. 110)
    > 
2. Regarding recalibration priorities:
    
    > "Hypothesis 2: Radical right parties take a clear pro-consumption stance on the recalibration dimension." (p. 111)
    > 
3. Regarding public perception:
    
    > "Hypothesis 3: Radical right parties' clear consumption stance resonates with the public's party perceptions." (p. 111)
    > 

## Data and Methodology

### Case Selection

The paper analyzes seven Western European countries: Austria, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Norway, Sweden, and the United Kingdom. The RRPs included are:

- Austrian Freedom Party (FPÖ)
- Alternative for Germany (AFD)
- The League (LN, Italy)
- Party for Freedom (PVV, Netherlands)
- Progress Party (FrP, Norway)
- Sweden Democrats (SD)
- United Kingdom Independence Party (UKIP)

This represents a diverse set of RRPs from different welfare regimes and with different historical legacies.

### Data Sources

The authors use two main data sources:

1. **Party manifesto data**: They refine data from the Manifesto Research on Political Representation (MARPOR) corpus, which provides access to parties' election manifestos broken down into "quasi-sentences" assigned to broad policy categories.
2. **Survey data on voter perceptions**: Original survey data collected between October and December 2018 involving 12,500 respondents across eight Western European countries.

### Measurement and Operationalization

For the welfare state size dimension, the authors measure:

> "We take a manifesto's share of positive social policy statements as an indicator of emphasis on welfare issues." (p. 113)
> 

For the recalibration dimension, they develop a sophisticated coding scheme:

> "We ask whether a statement is about social policy. We are not interested in statements that only address revenue and not expenditures (e.g. taxation). Hence, mentioning or implying social policy and addressing the expenditure side are the two necessary conditions for a statement to receive further consideration in our coding." (p. 112)
> 

They classify social policy statements into specific policy fields and categorize them as either consumption or investment:

> "Statements that do not refer to one of these policy fields are coded as 'other' (e.g. housing or disability related measures). Dependent on these fields, all social policy statements are then classified as either social investment (including all claims directed to ECEC, education, tertiary education, or ALMP) or consumption (including all claims directed to pension, unemployment benefits, social assistance, (passive) family policy, or healthcare)." (p. 112)
> 

For measuring public perceptions, they use survey data where respondents distributed 100 points across different policy fields according to how they thought parties would prioritize spending:

> "Respondents were asked how they think a given party X would prioritise social policy spending in different policy fields. To answer this question, they were given 100 points to distribute across six social policy fields in the way they would expect party X to prioritise these expenditures." (p. 113)
> 

## Empirical Findings

### Findings on Welfare State Size (Hypothesis 1)

The analysis confirms that RRPs de-emphasize social policy issues in their manifestos:

> "Of all party families, RRPs devote the least attention to the welfare state in their election manifestos, with manifesto space that is devoted to welfare expansion amounting to around 3 percentage points less than average. Even parties with constituencies that are less dispersed and are overall more sceptical of social policy expansion, such as the liberals or the conservatives, put more emphasis on social policy." (p. 115)
> 

This supports Hypothesis 1 and aligns with Rovny's position-blurring thesis, suggesting that RRPs strategically downplay welfare generosity issues to avoid alienating segments of their diverse electorate.

### Findings on Recalibration (Hypothesis 2)

The analysis strongly supports Hypothesis 2, showing that RRPs take a clear pro-consumption stance on the recalibration dimension:

> "By far, RRPs reveal the highest share of statements in favour of consumption (relative to social investment). In fact, this share is more than 20 percentage points higher than the average. Hence, even though the salience of social policy issues is (strategically) kept low, RRPs present themselves as the strongest preservers of a traditional, consumptive welfare state and the fiercest opponents of welfare state recalibration." (p. 115-116)
> 

This is a key finding that nuances our understanding of RRP economic positioning. Country-specific analysis reinforces this finding:

> "In every country, it is the RRP that constitutes the consumptive pole. Only around a third or less of their positive statements are directed towards social investment." (p. 116)
> 

### Findings on Public Perception (Hypothesis 3)

The analysis also confirms Hypothesis 3, showing that the public accurately perceives RRPs' pro-consumption stance:

> "Figure 4 shows how party families are perceived by all voters, and it appears that public perceptions of party positions conform surprisingly well to parties' communication in their respective manifestos." (p. 117)
> 

> "Most importantly for our purposes, however, voters ascribe the consumption pole on the recalibration dimension to the RRP family." (p. 117)
> 

This is particularly significant as it shows that despite RRPs' strategy of de-emphasizing welfare issues overall, their clear pro-consumption stance does reach voters.

> "...despite differences in the size, historical origin, and institutional embeddedness of the party system, all RRPs under scrutiny are clearly seen as opposing the modernisation of the welfare state from consumption to social investment policies." (p. 118)
> 

When analyzing specific policy fields, the authors note:

> "...the perception of RRPs as supporters of consumption is strongly driven by them being perceived as fervent defenders of pensions. Moreover, an extraordinarily strong dislike of active labour market policies is attributed to the radical right." (p. 118)
> 

## Discussion and Implications

The authors argue that their findings have several important implications:

1. **For party competition**: RRPs' strategic situation is less uncomfortable than previously assumed, as they can take clear positions on the recalibration dimension where their electorate has unified preferences.
    
    > "First, our finding contributes to party competition literature by implying that welfare issues' salience in the political debate is not inevitably problematic for RRPs. Their strategic situation is less uncomfortable than previously assumed since their electorate has unclear preferences only regarding one welfare dimension but not the other." (p. 120)
    > 
2. **For understanding voting behavior**: The clear positioning of RRPs on the recalibration dimension suggests that economic motivations may play a more important role in radical right voting than previously thought.
    
    > "Second, our findings call into question the prevalent opinion among researchers, that the (working-class) vote for the radical right is based exclusively on socio-cultural rather than economic motivations." (p. 120)
    > 
3. **For welfare policy-making**: RRPs' clear pro-consumption stance has implications for their role in coalitions and policy-making.
    
    > "Third, the clear positioning of RRPs on what kind of a welfare state they will pursue casts a different light on their role in welfare policy making, especially considering their government participation or their role of kingmaker in some countries. RRPs might therefore help the left expand or at least stabilise consumption policies such as pensions. At the same time, they can be expected to be the most formidable opposition to expanding social investment policies such as childcare or tertiary education." (p. 120)
    > 
4. **For understanding political dimensions of conflict**: The paper suggests an overlap between the recalibration dimension and the second, non-economic dimension of political conflict.
    
    > "Lastly, our paper corroborates and extends the expectation that Beramendi et al. (2015) expressed with regard to the remarkable similarity between the conflict over social investment vs. consumption and what is often termed the second, non-economic dimension. There seems to be an overlap between not only demand side preferences but also the supply side conflict structure, with green and socially liberal parties at the universalist/social investment pole being opposed to RRPs at the particularistic/consumption pole and social democratic parties getting trapped in the middle." (p. 121)
    > 

## Social Psychological Mechanisms

The paper identifies several social psychological mechanisms that explain RRP voters' preference for consumption over investment policies, which in turn shapes RRP positioning. These mechanisms help us understand how welfare/labor/social investment policies moderate support for far-right parties.

### 1. Immediacy and Uncertainty

The authors argue that working-class voters (who increasingly support RRPs) prefer consumption policies because:

> "First, consumption policies materialise immediately whereas investments usually only pay off in the future. Second, willingness to invest in the future may depend on the economic outlook, which might be considered grimmer among working-class voters (Häusermann et al. 2021)." (p. 108-109)
> 

This reflects psychological tendencies toward immediate gratification and risk aversion, especially among economically vulnerable populations. Social investment policies require trusting in delayed benefits under conditions of uncertainty, which is psychologically more demanding.

### 2. Deserving In-Group vs. Undeserving Out-Group Dynamics

The authors highlight how consumption policies align with the particularistic tendencies of RRP voters:

> "Second, consumption policies are more easily targetable towards specific groups that are perceived as being the most deserving of welfare benefits. Pension systems, for example, can be arranged so that they reward 'hard-working' native men but exclude labour-market outsiders and immigrants. Meanwhile, social investment policies such as education or childcare have the explicit goal of increasing equality of opportunity." (p. 109)
> 

This reflects social psychological processes of in-group favoritism and out-group derogation. RRP voters, with their strong nativist tendencies, prefer policies that can be targeted at the "deserving" native population rather than universalistic policies that also benefit immigrants or other out-groups.

### 3. Trust in Political Institutions

The authors emphasize the role of trust in explaining support for social investment:

> "Lastly, research has highlighted the importance of trust in government and political institutions as a vital factor in predicting support for social investment. Since social investment measures can be expected to pay off only in the long-term, are fraught with considerably more uncertainties than known, existing consumption policies, and depend on effective implementation, trust in political agents is essential for supporting (social) investment measures (Garritzmann, Neimanns, et al. 2018; Jacobs and Matthews 2017)." (p. 109)
> 

RRP voters, who typically have low trust in political institutions, are therefore less likely to support social investment policies. The authors note:

> "RRPs, which usually have a strong populist component, however, frequently campaign on an anti-establishment platform that subverts citizens' trust in politics and political elites. Concomitant with that, RRPs are especially successful in mobilising and attracting voters that have a low level of trust in politics, politicians and political institutions (Bélanger and Aarts 2006; Söderlund and Kestilä-Kekkonen 2009)." (p. 109)
> 

### 4. Cultural Conservatism and Gender Roles

The authors identify a connection between cultural conservatism and support for consumption policies:

> "First, the stabilising character of consumption-oriented social policies (e.g. pensions and contribution-based unemployment benefits) that promote rather than challenge traditional gender roles and the male breadwinner model should find an echo in culturally conservative attitudes. In contrast, many social investment policies enhance gender equality which is connotated to universalistic values (Busemeyer et al. 2021)." (p. 109)
> 

This suggests that support for consumption policies is partly driven by a psychological preference for maintaining traditional social structures and gender roles, which aligns with the cultural conservatism of RRP voters.

## Conclusion

Enggist and Pinggera's paper makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of RRP positioning on welfare issues. By distinguishing between the welfare state size dimension and the recalibration dimension, they show that RRPs' economic positions are not uniformly blurry but rather strategically differentiated. RRPs de-emphasize issues of welfare state generosity where their electorate has heterogeneous preferences but take clear pro-consumption stances on issues of recalibration where their electorate is united.

This finding has important implications for party competition, voting behavior, welfare policy-making, and our understanding of political conflict dimensions. The paper also illuminates the social psychological mechanisms that link welfare preferences to support for far-right parties, highlighting how material self-interest, in-group/out-group dynamics, trust in institutions, and cultural conservatism shape preferences for consumption over investment policies.

The research suggests that welfare politics plays a more important role in radical right success than previously recognized, not through positions on welfare state generosity but through clear opposition to welfare state recalibration toward social investment. This nuanced understanding helps explain how RRPs have successfully mobilized working-class voters despite the apparent contradiction between these voters' economic interests and traditional right-wing economic policies.
