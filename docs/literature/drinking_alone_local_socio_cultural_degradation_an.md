## Paper Summary

This paper by Diane Bolet introduces the "Local Socio-Cultural Degradation Theory" to explain radical right support in Britain. Using an original dataset on British community pub closures, Bolet argues that the disappearance of these local institutions increases radical right (UKIP) support by triggering social isolation and symbolizing the decline of working-class identity. Combining district-level data with UK panel data (2013-2016), the study demonstrates that individuals in districts experiencing one additional community pub closure relative to total pubs are 4.3 percentage points more likely to support UKIP. This effect is amplified under conditions of material deprivation but is not moderated by immigration levels. The research contributes to our understanding of radical right support by highlighting how the degradation of socio-cultural spaces creates fertile ground for radical right parties by fostering both social disconnection and cultural identity loss among affected communities.

## Primary Theories Used

### 1. Local Socio-Cultural Degradation Theory

**Explanation:**
The Local Socio-Cultural Degradation Theory developed by Bolet argues that the decline of local socio-cultural hubs increases support for radical right parties by triggering two interconnected processes: social isolation and cultural identity loss. This theory builds upon and improves the social capital/social isolation theories by adding a spatial dimension and connecting the loss of community spaces to status anxiety among specific social groups.

The theory identifies the closure of community-based social hubs as a key mechanism through which individuals develop feelings of marginalization and cultural loss. Unlike previous social capital theories, Bolet's framework explicitly connects these feelings to radical right support by explaining how these parties' nostalgic, restoration-focused rhetoric appeals directly to people experiencing such degradation.

Bolet explains: "I advance what I call the 'Local Socio-Cultural Degradation Theory', which improves upon social capital/social isolation theories. The latter posit a link between people's lack of connectedness in their community and radical right support at the individual level, but they do not adequately capture the distinctive reasons why people would vote for radical right parties (as opposed to radical left voting or abstention)."

The theory is distinctive in that it:

1. "advances that the closure of social places that are at the heart of a local community sparks a sense of social marginality among the people who used to frequent these places, causing them to question their place in their society."
2. Notes that "for many such people, these closures also mark the disappearance of their cultural heritage, and they experience a loss of cultural identity as a result."
3. Connects this directly to radical right voting: "This process ultimately leads these individuals to vote for radical right parties, whose rhetoric and ideology tap into precisely such social and cultural grievances."

The author applies this theory specifically to community pubs in Britain, arguing they are ideal socio-cultural hubs to study because:

Community pubs are a focal point of local communities in Britain, through which social connections are established and sustained. My study focuses on community pubs because they embody the traditional 'working-class white' identity, meaning their decline is likely to foster the processes of social isolation and cultural identity loss that eventually translate into radical right votes.

**Historical context and theoretical landscape:**
The theory builds on several theoretical traditions:

1. Social capital theories (Putnam, Hall, Leonard) - focused on how social networks and community involvement build trust and civic engagement
2. Status anxiety/decline theories (Gest, Gidron & Hall) - examining how previously dominant social groups react to perceived loss of social status
3. Local sociotropic accounts (Enos) - studying how people's immediate environments shape their political attitudes

As Bolet notes: "Drawing on recent work on local sociotropic influences and status decline, this hypothesis improves on social capital/social isolation theories by better linking adverse social conditions to radical right voting (as opposed to radical left support or abstention)."

A key contribution is that the theory provides a more precise explanation for why social isolation leads specifically to radical right support rather than other political reactions:

The Local Socio-Cultural Degradation Theory improves upon the social capital/social isolation by better linking adverse social conditions to voting (as opposed to abstaining) for a radical right party (as opposed to a radical left party). It does not predict that individuals who experience this kind of socio-cultural marginalization will abstain from voting. Rather, unlike the social capital/social isolation theory, it distinguishes between those who have always felt isolated and those who have become isolated after experiencing a decline of activity in their local area.

### 2. Status Anxiety/Decline Theory

**Explanation:**
The paper incorporates elements of status anxiety theory, which focuses on how the white working class perceives its declining position in society and how this perception drives support for radical right parties. This theory helps explain why particular social groups are especially affected by the closure of specific types of social spaces.

According to Bolet: "The status anxiety literature suggests reasons why a decline of certain kinds of socio-cultural hubs might have an effect on a particular segment of the population, namely the white working class. Gest et al. (2018) and Gidron and Hall (2017) stress how this group has perceived they are shifting to the periphery of their society relative to a new dominant white class of professional elites, amid rising immigration and economic crises."

The theory emphasizes how economic transformation has pushed certain groups to society's margins:

The transformation of the economy has contributed to the gradual disappearance of low-skilled, decent and secure jobs in manufacturing sectors and the rising demand for highly skilled employees with higher education (Gidron and Hall, 2017). It has relegated low-skilled white workers to the fringes of the social order. Low levels of status inspire a diffuse cultural resentment against the loss of their social group's previously established dominance in the social hierarchy.

This status resentment connects directly to radical right support because:

This resentment is translated into a radical right vote because radical right parties evoke a sense of nostalgia and pledge to return to an idealized image of the past characterized by its social cohesion and cultural homogeneity.

The application of status anxiety theory in this paper helps explain why community pub closures affect political behavior by showing how they symbolize broader status loss for specific social groups.

### 3. Interaction of Material and Cultural Factors

**Explanation:**
Bolet develops a theoretical framework that explores how material deprivation interacts with socio-cultural degradation to amplify radical right support. Rather than positioning economic and cultural explanations as competing, the paper advances our understanding of how they work together:

I argue that the impact of local socio-cultural degradation on radical right support can be amplified under certain contextual conditions. Individuals correctly perceive local conditions (Newman et al., 2015) so changes in their local environment can influence their voting behavior. Recent studies have emphasized the role that real economic contexts have in conditioning individuals' predispositions about political attitudes and voting behavior (McKay, 2019; Carreras et al., 2019; Ansell and Adler, 2019; Colantone and Stanig, 2018b).

The theory proposes different mechanisms for how economic deprivation versus immigration might moderate the effects of socio-cultural degradation:

Those who live in economically depressed areas are more likely to perceive these economic difficulties and translate them in socio-cultural terms. Worsening economic conditions may directly impact the financial viability of socio-cultural hubs and may degrade the social conditions of local residents, as unemployment may reinforce social isolation and feelings of political powerlessness. Local economic deprivation, which is perceived by residents, amplifies their actual and perceived sense of socio-cultural decline.

In contrast, the theory posits that:

The same cannot be said about immigration. The relation between immigration and socio-cultural degradation is not as straightforward as it is for economic deprivation. Local socio-cultural degradation specifically looks at dysfunctional local conditions that translate an internal threat caused by national structural changes (i.e. higher business rates, taxation and governmental regulation due to austerity measures) rather than an external threat caused by the arrival of newcomers.

This contributes to our understanding of how different contextual factors may moderate political behavior through different causal pathways.

### Social Psychological Mechanisms

The paper identifies several social psychological mechanisms that explain support for radical right parties, which align with your research focus:

1. **Social Isolation**: The paper demonstrates how the loss of social spaces creates isolation that makes people receptive to radical right messaging:

Following these theoretical accounts, the Local Socio-Cultural Degradation Theory proposes that it is the deterioration of white working class socio-cultural places that plays a role in shaping radical right support. Being exposed to a community in decline, manifested by the gradual disappearance of everyday socio-cultural experiences, can contribute to people's sense of social isolation and low social status because people typically compare their experience to that which they have had in the past—and have come to expect—but now see 'others' as having.

1. **Loss of Cultural Identity**: The paper highlights how the closure of community spaces represents a threat to cultural identity which radical right parties exploit:

The disappointment of their expectations, in turn, leaves them resentful and open to distinctively radical right appeals that invite reactionary nostalgia for earlier times (e.g. "Make America Great Again" in the US and "Take Back Control" in the UK) (Norris, 2005; Gest et al., 2018).

1. **Relative Deprivation**: The study emphasizes the importance of perceived relative loss rather than absolute deprivation:

People who have always felt isolated, as assumed in the social isolation theory, could just as well respond with a radical right vote and an abstention. The relative loss of social interactions compared to the expectations is what leads to radical right support.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Socio-cultural spaces matter politically**: The closure of community-rooted social spaces has measurable effects on political behavior, particularly support for radical right parties.

The presence of community pub closures is associated with the propensity for an individual to support a radical right party. One additional community pub closure relative to the number of pubs in the district increases an individual's likelihood to support UKIP by around 4,3 percentage points.

1. **Material and cultural factors interact**: Economic deprivation amplifies the effect of socio-cultural degradation on radical right support, demonstrating the importance of examining these factors together rather than separately.

This supports the notion that the interaction of material and cultural factors shapes radical right voting, thereby providing a more nuanced understanding of this electoral support than the arbitrary "economic" versus "cultural" explanations.

1. **Social isolation has specific political effects**: The paper demonstrates that social isolation doesn't simply lead to political disengagement but can foster support for radical right parties that offer a sense of identity and belonging.

The diminished role of community structures—families and local voluntary or professional organizations—they argue, presents an opportunity for charismatic leaders to lure atomized individuals. A thriving radical right movement or party then provides local loyal 'quasi-communities', which fill the void left by decaying identities and prevents individuals from responding to isolation through political disengagement.

1. **Place-based experiences shape political behavior**: The local experience of socio-cultural degradation provides a powerful explanatory frame for understanding radical right support.

The decline in these social places matters because they are not only functional enablers of social interaction; they also convey historical, geographical and social meanings that their inhabitants internalize and re-enact in their daily interactions within such spaces (Agnew, 2011). They develop a distinctive character, and become associated with distinctive traditions, for a specific social group, which has been described in sociology as a 'communal ethos' (McQuarrie, 2017).

1. **Policy implications for addressing radical right support**: The findings suggest that policies to maintain local social infrastructure could potentially reduce radical right support.

Highlighting the political dangers associated with the decline of local social activity lends urgency to state policies to improve social cohesiveness in economically deprived localities.
