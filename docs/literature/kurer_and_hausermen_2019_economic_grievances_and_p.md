## 2. Theoretical Framework and Contribution

The paper makes two major theoretical contributions that aim to reconcile contradictory findings in prior research on economic hardship and political participation.

### 2.1 Conceptualizing Different Types of Economic Grievances

The first contribution is a more nuanced conceptualization of economic grievances, distinguishing between two fundamentally different types of economic hardship:

1. **Relative status**: Economic hardship compared to others in society (societal reference point)
2. **Deprivation**: Economic hardship compared to one's previous situation (temporal reference point)

As the authors explain:

> "Social reference points highlight relative differences in economic hardship between different subgroups of society. The general pattern of vertical, longstanding and systematic discrepancies in resource endowment between individuals or groups remains relatively stable over time" (p. 871).
> 

Contrasting this with the second type:

> "Intertemporal comparisons, on the other hand, deliver a very different kind of reference point. Deprivation over time can happen on any level of absolute economic hardship, is much more volatile and potentially affects a much broader and more diverse subset of society. Its correlation with socioeconomic status may therefore be weak" (p. 871).
> 

This distinction is crucial because the authors theorize these different types of grievances affect political behavior through different psychological mechanisms:

> "The experience of deteriorating economic conditions over time is not so strongly related to resources, the first pillar of political participation as famously proposed by Verba et al. (1995), but rather to the second pillar: motivation or psychological engagement. In contrast to structural and relatively stable differences in the vertical distribution of resources, deprivation over time is expected to produce a kind of dissatisfaction, frustration or even anger beneficial to political activity" (p. 872).
> 

### 2.2 Political Mobilization as a Critical Moderator

The second major theoretical contribution addresses the role of political context in moderating the relationship between economic grievances and protest behavior. The authors argue that:

> "Economic strain does not in and of itself trigger or mediate protest (Kitschelt 1986: 59). Rather, people's grievances must be politicised. Collective actors – political entrepreneurs – play a crucial role in this process, since they use and provide political opportunities in terms of mobilisation" (p. 872).
> 

The paper identifies two specific mechanisms through which political mobilization moderates the grievance-protest relationship:

1. **Organizational capacity mechanism**: "The financial and organizational power and strength of these entrepreneurs varies strongly between different national contexts and helps explain the occurrence of protest activity" (p. 872).
2. **Individual signaling mechanism**: "Increased visibility of ongoing protest sends signals to individuals and encourages them to participate in protest activities and publicly voice their dissatisfaction" (p. 872-873).

This signaling mechanism operates through two specific channels:

> "At the level of an individual, mobilisation occurs through two different channels: a demonstration effect and an attribution effect. The former refers to 'threshold models' of political protest (Granovetter 1978; Kuran 1991), meaning that citizens' beliefs in the value of participating in protests may change when the intensity of existing protest passes a certain threshold" (p. 873).
> 

And regarding the attribution effect:

> "The second channel through which citizens may be mobilised to protest has less to do with informational cues emanating from existing protest but with the attribution of grievances. Particularly in the area of economic grievances (i.e., the experience of deprivation such as unemployment or income loss), the reaction of an individual to this experience depends on whether a person attributes the grievance to her own fault or whether she blames outside factors for this grievance" (p. 873).
> 

Based on this theoretical framework, the paper develops two main hypotheses:

1. Structural economic disadvantage (relative status) will demobilize political participation, while deterioration of economic conditions (deprivation) will mobilize participation.
2. Political mobilization will moderate these relationships, with higher levels of mobilization reducing the demobilizing effect of relative status and enhancing the mobilizing effect of deprivation.

## 3. Data and Methodology

### 3.1 Data Sources

The authors employed three distinct data sources to create a comprehensive dataset capturing their three core concepts:

1. **European Social Survey (ESS)**: Used for measuring individual protest behavior and demographic control variables. The authors combined waves ESS3-6 (2006-2012) to capture the period before and during the Great Recession.
2. **European Union Statistics on Income and Living Conditions (EU-SILC)**: Used to construct measures of economic grievances based on unemployment risk for different occupational groups.
3. **Original protest event data**: Collected by research teams at the University of Zurich and the European University Institute, covering approximately 32,000 protest events reported in international news agencies between 2000-2015 in 28 European countries.

As the authors explain:

> "We draw on three different data sources in order to compile a dataset that provides an accurate measure for each of the three core concepts (protest, grievances and mobilisation) of this article" (p. 873).
> 

### 3.2 Operationalization of Variables

### 3.2.1 Dependent Variable: Protest Participation

The dependent variable was constructed from four ESS questions about non-electoral political participation:

> "Respondents are asked whether during the year preceding the survey they took part in a demonstration, signed a petition, wore a protest badge or boycotted certain products. For the dependent variable, we created a dummy variable coded as 1 if a respondent answered one of these four questions affirmatively" (p. 874).
> 

### 3.2.2 Independent Variables: Economic Grievances

The authors operationalized their two concepts of economic grievances using unemployment risk by occupational group:

1. **Relative status**: Group-specific unemployment rate compared to the country average.
2. **Deprivation**: Change in group-specific unemployment rate compared to the previous year.

These were calculated using the following formulas:

> "relative status = grievance<sub>i,t</sub> − grievance<sub>j,t</sub>"
> 
> 
> "deprivation = grievance<sub>i,t</sub> − grievance<sub>i,t−1</sub>"
> 

Where i denotes the occupational group and j the entire country (p. 874).

The authors used Oesch's (2006) eight-class schema for defining occupational groups:

> "We rely on Oesch's (2006) class scheme with the following eight occupational groups: large employers and liberal (self-employed) professionals; technical (semi-) professions; (associate) managers in business and public sector; sociocultural (semi-) professions; petit bourgeoisie and small business owners; production workers; office clerks; and service workers" (p. 888, note 3).
> 

### 3.2.3 Moderator Variable: Political Mobilization

The authors operationalized political mobilization in terms of change in visible protest activity:

> "For our moderator variable, we are particularly interested in the change of number of protests, since the different levels of protest activity are relatively stable between countries. Therefore, we also added up the number of protests in the period from t-2 to t-1 (24 to 13 months before the ESS survey) in order to calculate the change in the absolute number of protests in each country during the relevant period in which respondents' protest activity was asked for (t-1 to t0)" (p. 876).
> 

The authors explain why they focus on change rather than absolute levels:

> "This is a more valid indicator of a vibrant and mobilising atmosphere of collective action and engagement than the mere level of activity, because people in countries with an active protest culture are quite likely to get used to a 'baseline level' of non-electoral opposition" (p. 876).
> 

### 3.3 Control Variables

The authors included controls at both the individual and country level:

Individual level: "Education, gender, age, employment status, union/organisational membership and being in a relationship" (p. 877).

Country level: "Trade union density per country and year in order to separate the impact of financial and organisational capacity of protest movements from our signaling mechanism. Furthermore, we control for the party in government as well as for the official unemployment rate at the time of the survey" (p. 877).

### 3.4 Statistical Analysis

The authors employed hierarchical logistic mixed-effects models to account for the nested structure of their data (individuals within countries in different years). As they explain:

> "Due to the nested structure of the data (individuals in countries in different years) and the binary dependent variable, we run hierarchical logistic mixed-effects models" (p. 877).
> 

The final dataset included over 200,000 observations across 28 European countries, though the analytical sample for most models was smaller due to availability of all variables.

## 4. Main Findings

### 4.1 Direct Effects of Economic Grievances on Protest Participation

The first key finding is that the two types of economic grievances have opposite effects on protest participation:

> "While structural economic disadvantage (i.e., the level of grievances) unambiguously de-mobilises individuals, the deterioration of economic prospects (i.e., a negative change in economic grievances) instead increases political activity" (p. 868).
> 

This is shown clearly in Table 1 (p. 878) where:

1. **Relative status** has a strong negative coefficient of -2.941 (p < 0.001) in Model 2 and -3.013 (p < 0.001) in Model 4, indicating that higher relative unemployment risk compared to the country average reduces the likelihood of protest participation.
2. **Deprivation** has a strong positive coefficient of 2.153 (p < 0.001) in Model 3 and 2.190 (p < 0.001) in Model 5, indicating that experiencing an increase in unemployment risk over time increases the likelihood of protest participation.

The authors note:

> "The difference between the demobilising effect of relative status and the mobilising effect of over-time deprivation is an interesting finding, which highlights the importance of conceptualisation and measurement, and quite likely helps explain some of the inconclusive evidence reviewed in the theory section" (p. 879).
> 

When both measures are included in the same model (Model 5), both effects remain significant and in opposite directions, confirming that they represent distinct processes rather than capturing the same underlying phenomenon.

### 4.2 Moderating Effect of Political Mobilization

The second key finding is that political mobilization significantly moderates the relationship between economic grievances and protest participation:

> "Both interaction terms are positive and statistically highly significant. An increase in mobilisation and opportunity indeed seems to activate citizens confronted with economic hardship" (p. 879).
> 

In Table 2 (p. 880-881), the interaction terms show:

1. **Relative status × Mobilisation**: Positive coefficient of 0.017 (p < 0.001) in Model 3, indicating that higher levels of political mobilization reduce the demobilizing effect of relative status.
2. **Deprivation × Mobilisation**: Positive coefficient of 0.025 (p < 0.05) in Model 4, indicating that higher levels of political mobilization enhance the mobilizing effect of deprivation.

Figure 1 (p. 881) visualizes these interaction effects, showing:

> "The left plot shows the logged odds ratio of our first grievance measure – relative status – conditional on the level of political mobilisation... As we would expect from the results in Table 2, lower relative status generally depresses political activity: the (logged) odds ratio for this variable usually remains in the negative domain below the horizontal zero-line. However, this negative effect is clearly mitigated in a more active protest environment, which provides more opportunities to voice dissatisfaction, and in fact fully disappears in circumstances with an exceptional surge in mobilisation" (p. 879).
> 

For deprivation, the authors observe:

> "The plot on the right hand displays the same conditional logged odds ratio for the case of deprivation. Again, this plot corroborates our findings from Table 2. On average, a deterioration of their situation has a mobilising effect on citizens – and this effect is clearly reinforced in a mobilised protest context that offers ample opportunity to voice dissatisfaction and join existing protest movements" (p. 879-881).
> 

### 4.3 Control Variables and Their Effects

The analysis also reveals several notable patterns in the control variables:

1. **Education** has a strong positive effect on protest participation (coefficient around 0.25-0.28, p < 0.001 across all models).
2. **Union membership** and **other association membership** both strongly predict protest participation (coefficients 0.28-0.31 and 1.25-1.29 respectively, p < 0.001).
3. **Being female** has a significant positive association with protest participation in all models (coefficient around 0.14-0.16, p < 0.001).
4. **Age** has a small but significant negative effect (coefficient around -0.003 to -0.004, p < 0.001).
5. **Government party** also matters, with predominantly left hegemonic governments associated with higher protest levels (coefficient around 0.12, p < 0.001) compared to other government compositions.

## 6. Theoretical Implications and Significance

### 6.1 Reconciling Contradictory Findings

One of the most significant contributions of this study is reconciling contradictory findings in previous research on economic hardship and political participation. By distinguishing between different types of economic grievances, the authors provide a theoretical framework that explains why some studies find mobilizing effects while others find demobilizing effects:

> "Revealing these two countervailing effects provides an important clarification that helps reconcile many seemingly conflicting findings in the existing literature, and to reassess the value of objective measures of economic grievances as compared to subjective measures, which have become more widespread in social movement research" (p. 868).
> 

### 6.2 Implications for Democratic Theory

The findings have important implications for democratic theory, particularly regarding concerns about inequality leading to democratic erosion:

> "If the presence of organised and visible political action is a decisive signal for citizens that conditions the micro-level link between economic grievances and protest, then democracy itself – that is, organised collective action – can help sustain political equality and prevent the vicious circle of democratic erosion" (p. 868-869).
> 

This challenges fatalistic views about the inevitable demobilizing effects of inequality:

> "Our results imply that increasing economic inequality does not inevitably result in ever-increasing political inequality. While the danger of self-reinforcing economic and political inequality is certainly real, political actors within the democratic system have both the means and the power to break or at least mitigate this vicious circle" (p. 885).
> 

### 6.3 Bridging Political Economy and Social Movement Research

The study demonstrates the value of integrating insights from political economy and social movement literature:

> "In this article, we aim to explain – and to some extent reconcile – some of the contradictory findings by bridging two strands of research that have all too often been treated in isolation: the social movement and the political economy literature" (p. 867).
> 

Specifically:

> "Political economy pays attention to actors' material interest and thus helps to counter the 'strange disappearance of capitalism in social movement studies' (Hetland & Goodwin 2013), which is especially peculiar in the light of widespread anti-austerity protests around the globe (Della Porta 2015; see also Stanley & Goodwin 2013)" (p. 867).
> 

## 7. Methodological Strengths and Limitations

### 7.1 Strengths

The study has several notable methodological strengths:

1. **Multiple data sources**: The use of three distinct data sources allows for precise measurement of each core concept without common method bias.
2. **Objective measures of grievances**: By using objective unemployment risk rather than subjective perceptions, the authors avoid potential endogeneity between perceptions and protest behavior.
3. **Temporal coverage**: Analyzing the period before and during the Great Recession provides a natural experiment with increased variance in both independent and dependent variables.
4. **Comprehensive controls**: The models include both individual-level and country-level controls to account for alternative explanations.
5. **Extensive robustness checks**: Multiple alternative specifications confirm the stability of the findings.

### 7.2 Limitations

The authors acknowledge several limitations to their approach:

1. **Cross-classification of data**: The complex structure of the data creates challenges for multilevel modeling:

> "We refrain from analysing the data as a three-level structure as suggested by Schmidt-Catran and Fairbrother (2016) because of the specific construction of our measures of economic grievances. While the variable is technically situated at the lowest level, it entails information from the country dimension of the cross-classification at a specific time point" (p. 877).
> 
1. **Protest event data**: While the authors collect extensive protest data, they acknowledge potential selection bias in media reporting:

> "Selection bias, finally, surely exists to some extent (Hutter 2014: 147). However, a systematic comparison of our own media data with two alternative datasets, one quantitative and one qualitative, comes to the conclusion that our data-generation process 'does not bias the data in important ways' (Wüest & Lorenzini 2017: 18)" (p. 888, note 4).
> 
1. **Causal inference**: While the study identifies strong correlations, establishing definitive causal relationships remains challenging due to the observational nature of the data.

## 8. Future Research Directions

The paper opens several promising avenues for future research:

1. **Subjective vs. objective grievances**: Further exploration of how objective economic conditions translate into subjective perceptions and political behavior.
2. **Specific forms of protest**: More detailed analysis of how different types of grievances affect different forms of political participation.
3. **Political entrepreneurs**: Deeper investigation of how specific political entrepreneurs mobilize economic grievances.
4. **Long-term effects**: Examining whether the mobilizing effects of temporal deprivation persist over longer periods or fade as new baseline expectations are established.
5. **Electoral consequences**: Extending the analysis to electoral participation and voting behavior to understand the broader political consequences of economic grievances.

## 9. Conclusion

This paper makes significant contributions to our understanding of how economic hardship affects political participation, particularly protest behavior. By distinguishing between different types of economic grievances and highlighting the moderating role of political mobilization, the authors provide a more nuanced framework that helps resolve contradictions in existing research.

The key takeaways are:

1. Economic hardship has differential effects on political participation depending on whether it represents stable socioeconomic disadvantage (relative status) or deteriorating conditions over time (deprivation).
2. Political mobilization significantly moderates these relationships, with greater mobilization reducing the demobilizing effect of relative status and enhancing the mobilizing effect of deprivation.
3. These findings suggest that democratic systems contain within them the capacity to mitigate potential vicious cycles of economic and political inequality through collective action and mobilization.

As the authors conclude:

> "While there remains a large gap between the ideal and the reality of democracy, achieving more equal political voice is at least a first important step in opposing the detrimental influence of economic inequality on the functioning of contemporary democratic politics" (p. 885-886).
> 

This research contributes significantly to our understanding of how democracies respond to economic challenges and provides cautious optimism about the capacity of democratic systems to sustain political equality despite rising economic inequality.
