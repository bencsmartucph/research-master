# Research Notes: Partisan Divides on Welfare Chauvinism and Welfare Populism This research by Enggist and Häusermann examines whether welfare chauvinism and welfare populism—characteristic features of radical right parties' welfare positions—attract voters beyond the radical right electorate. Using data from eight Western European countries, they found:  Contrary to widespread assumptions, welfare chauvinism and welfare populism divide the right more than the left

- **Mainstream right voters** show only moderate support for these positions

## Conceptual Framework

### Two Types of Welfare Chauvinism

1. **Welfare Protection**: Expansive welfare chauvinism focused on protecting natives
    - "The focus here is on protecting the welfare provision for native citizens rather than disentitling immigrants" (p.3)
    - Emphasizes "the generous rights of natives" (p.3)
    - Theoretically could appeal to left voters
2. **Welfare Exclusion**: Restrictive welfare chauvinism focused on cutting benefits for immigrants
    - "The focus of welfare exclusion is on the immigrants, but in a fiscally restrictive way" (p.4)
    - "The underlying reform direction is one of welfare state retrenchment, notably at the expense of immigrants" (p.4)
    - Could appeal to mainstream right voters

### Welfare Populism

- Defined as criticism of welfare that "does not stem from a fundamental opposition to redistribution... but is based on the idea that the current administration of the welfare state does not benefit the 'common people'" (p.4)
- Two components:
    - Perception that welfare "wastes money on 'lazy, undeserving welfare scroungers'" (p.4)
    - Critique of "'corrupt elite' seemingly responsible for misdirecting and mismanaging welfare benefits" (p.4)

## Key Empirical Findings

### Partisan Divides on Welfare Exclusion

- **Radical right voters**: Strongly supportive (1.9 on 0-3 scale)
- **Conservative/liberal voters**: Moderate position (1.5)
- **Left voters**: Clearly opposed (below average citizens)
- "Green electorates represent the pole in opposition towards welfare exclusion" (p.8)

### Partisan Divides on Welfare Protection

- **Radical right voters**: Strongly endorse expanding welfare for natives exclusively
- **Conservative voters**: Average support
- **Liberal voters**: Below average support
- **Left voters**: "Remain significantly more opposed to welfare protection than the average citizen" (p.10)
- "This is remarkable and anything but obvious given that the statement used to operationalize support for welfare protection asks about welfare expansion for a large share of the population" (p.10)

### Partisan Divides on Welfare Populism

- **Radical right voters**: Most welfare populist
- **Radical left voters**: More welfare populist than mainstream left, similar to mainstream right
- **Social democratic voters**: Least welfare populist
- "Despite small differences on the left, the real dividing line concerning welfare populism runs between voters of the radical right and all other party families, leading again primarily to a divided right" (p.11)

## Class-Based Analysis

The research examined class-based divides within party electorates:

- **Working-class vs. middle-class divide**: Support for welfare exclusion, protection, and populism is strongest among production workers, followed by service workers
- **Social democratic and radical left parties**: Still have substantial working-class voters (34.8% and 40.0% respectively)
- **Working-class voters within left parties**: More supportive of welfare chauvinism than middle-class left voters, but still less supportive than radical right voters
- "Even the working-class constituency of the left is at most averagely supportive of these socially conservative critiques of the welfare state" (p.13)

## Implications

- "If welfare chauvinist reforms were to be viable, they would rather be the outcome of coalitions between radical right and some mainstream right parties" (p.15)
- "Welfare exclusion might be the more viable of the two welfare chauvinist reform strategies" (p.15)
- For left parties, "the strategic situation... at the immigration–welfare nexus today is less complicated than often perceived" (p.14)
- "To be congruent with their current electorates, left parties would take a stand for immigrants' welfare rights" (p.14)

## Methodological Approach

- Data collected from eight Western European countries: Sweden, Denmark, Germany, Netherlands, UK, Ireland, Italy, Spain
- Approximately 1500 respondents per country (12,129 total)
- Survey measured welfare exclusion through agreement with "government should reduce social assistance benefits only for non-nationals"
- Welfare protection measured through "government should expand social assistance benefits for country nationals only"
- Welfare populism measured on a 10-point scale from "money is used efficiently" to "a lot of money is wasted"

This research challenges common assumptions about the potential for welfare chauvinism to divide the left, showing instead that the major divides exist within the right between radical right and mainstream right voters.
