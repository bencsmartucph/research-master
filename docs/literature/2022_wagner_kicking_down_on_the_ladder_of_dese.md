The paper examines how welfare reforms since the 1990s, which have increasingly emphasized "activation" and conditionality for welfare recipients, influence those recipients' attitudes toward other welfare-receiving groups. Wagner argues that there exists a feedback mechanism between welfare policy experiences and support for welfare solidarity across different recipient groups. The central thesis posits two opposing mechanisms: First, welfare recipients who experience cuts or increased conditionality often develop more exclusionary attitudes toward those perceived as less deserving ("kicking down" effect), ultimately contributing to welfare chauvinism. Second, recipients who experience enabling "social investment" policies that successfully facilitate social inclusion may develop more inclusive welfare attitudes, even toward outgroups.

### Kicking down the ladder of deservingness

**1. Introduction**

The question of who should be included in or excluded from solidarity provision by the welfare state is a salient dimension of conflict in advanced capitalist European democracies.  Citizens' perceptions about the deservingness of specific welfare recipient groups are a salient driver of social policy preferences (van Oorschot 2000; van Oorschot et al. 2017; Roosma et al. 2016) and of voting behavior: while overall positive perceptions of welfare recipients are related to support for new liberal left-wing parties, negative perceptions towards them are related to populist radical right-wing party (PRRP) support, contributing to the salience of the "new politics" cleavage (Attewell 2021).  Welfare recipient groups whose deservingness is subject to salient public contestation include pensioners, who are being called upon to retire at an ever later age (Häusermann 2010; Palier 2021).  They include unemployed persons and minimum-security recipients who are being called upon to "activate" and get back into employment (Soss/Schram 2007; Bonoli 2010).  A group whose entitlements are subject to particularly polarized public debate, finally, are immigrants.  This is because PRRPs have popularized welfare chauvinism, the demand to exclude immigrants from the national welfare system, notably to mobilize "native" working-class electorates (Andersen/Björklund 1990; Mews/Mau 2014; Carreja/Harris 2022).

A barely answered question is how the welfare reforms of recent decades have affected welfare deservingness perceptions among the public and in how far they have contributed to the salience of this dimension of conflict.  It is a long-standing insight that advanced capitalist welfare politics take place in a context of "permanent austerity" (Pierson 1994; 2001): the total amount of budgetary spending is held limited due to the perception that further expansion of the state budget is fiscally unsustainable.  It has often been suggested that this situation is prone to produce zero-sum conflicts: as overall spending is constrained, conflict shifts towards questions on which groups deserve to be treated with priority (Häusermann 2010; Enggist 2019; Bremer/Bürgisser 2021).  The precise mechanisms that link this shift in welfare policy making to the formation of salient "exclusivist" or "inclusivist" deservingness-attitudes in the population, however, are still underexplored.  Existing evidence suggests that "activation"-oriented reforms of labor market policy conducted since the 1990s in Europe and the US have not much changed the perception of recipients, namely of unemployed persons, among the general public (Soss/Schram 2007; Buss/Ebbinghaus/Nauman 2017).

What, however, if these reforms have altered recipients' own perceptions of other recipient groups?  This question gains in relevance when we consider further policy fields such as public retirement pension policy, whose recipients amount to a popular majority in European welfare states and which has undergone significant reforms since the 1990s.  To address this gap, I formulate hypotheses on how experiences with those types of welfare reforms that have dominated the scene since the 1990s affect recipients' support for solidarity with groups other than their own.  I propose that on the one hand, welfare cuts (Pierson 1994) and increases in welfare conditionality as they typically comes with "activation" strategies (Bonoli 2010; Dwyer et al. 2022) may have spurred fears of entitlement loss and of social exclusion among recipients.  In reaction, many tend to harshen their views on entitlements for those who are seen as even less deserving than themselves in what we could call a "downward spiral" of solidarity erosion.  Following the "universal rank order" of deservingness proposed by van Oorschot (2000), this means the elderly point their finger at the young, those who work point at those who don't work, and "natives" point at immigrants, always arguing, "exclude them - not us" (a behavior called "deflection", Bolton et al. 2022).

On the other hand, social investment policies (Morel et al. 2012) provide active resources enabling recipients to improve their human capital, labor market position, and generally their capacity to socially participate, what can lead to experiences of social inclusion (Mettler 2002).  In reaction, I hypothesize that there is a tendency to perceive that "others should be offered the same opportunity", showcasing the formation of more inclusive welfare attitudes even towards outgroups in an "upward spiral" of solidarity bonds.

In this paper, I focus on welfare recipients' lived experiences with labor market and pension policies and on the mechanisms that lead from these experiences to the formation of normative attitudes.  I test the above-formulated hypotheses on a data set of 51 qualitative interviews with labor market and pension policy recipients conducted in Austria and (East-)Germany between 2018 and 2020.  These countries have highly similar institutional systems;  however, Germany has gone considerably farther than Austria in terms of "cuts" and "conditionality"-oriented labor market and retirement reforms.  The sample consists of women and men and of various age groups, but of only a single occupational category: blue-collar workers.  Manual workers' employment trajectories are particularly sensible to "old social risks" (Häusermann 2010), namely those of ageing, unemployment, sickness, and accident.  This is why they can be expected to be particularly heavily concerned by the types of welfare reforms at stake.

At the same time, they hold more rigorous visions of welfare deservingness than other groups.  This presents favorable conditions for a test of the "exclusion"-hypothesis ("kicking down"), but rather difficult conditions for a test of the "inclusion"-hypotheses ("solidarization").  A serious drawback of the dataset is that it does (almost) not include any workers with first- or second-generation immigration background, because it has been originally collected with the aim of analyzing the formation of populist-radical-right-wing political attitudes (which are more common among individuals who saliently identify as "natives" to their country of residence).

In this dataset, I try to identify links between policy experiences and deservingness attitudes by focusing on two elements.  This is (1) cognitive links explicitly established by interviewees between their own welfare experiences and their attitudes towards others' entitlements.  It is (2) cognitive links implicitly visible between the two by the use of the same deservingness criteria to interpret both issues.  Within the given dataset I find consistent evidence for the mechanism of "kicking down".  Many policy recipients who themselves make experiences of exclusion or misrecognition with labor market or pension policies tend to justify their own deservingness (to be included, to be treated better) by blaming (even) weaker groups in the sense of "exclude them - rather than me".  In so doing, they apply the same deservingness norms that the state (hurtfully) applies to them to those who stand below themselves on the "ladder".

The "exclusion" and "misrecognition" experiences that are associated to these behaviors can be rather clearly classified.  All of them share similar characteristics: urgent problem pressure (high stakes) meets perceived misrecognition of one's needs and capacities by the state as well as a perception of double standards: the official goals and justifications of the policy do not seem to correspond to its effects on the ground.  Among unemployed workers' experiences with the employment office, this includes being forced to write numerous applications without hope of success (but under threat of sanctions) and being forced to participate in training when expecting it will not increase labor market chances and/or not having had a say in the choice of training.  Among prospective retirees, it includes suffering deductions from one's pension benefit when feeling that one is not responsible for missed contribution years, but rather, unemployment has incurred due to termination by the last employer, unviable conditions at the last workplace, labor market situation, or health status.  Finally, among both groups, perceived misrecognition of health status by public institutions (employment office, pension insurance, health/ social insurance authorities) that affects labor market status counts among these experiences.  While pressures external to the welfare state (labor market, employers) play a big role, most of these experiences are attributable to welfare reforms or reform processes of the recent decades that can be categorized as "cuts" or, even more so, as "conditionalities".  These create systematic conflicts notably for workers who are labor market outsiders (Emmenegger et al. 2012).

There naturally is heterogeneity among reactions to this type of welfare policy experience.  While "kicking down" is the most common reaction among the manual workers in the data set, some interviewees react inversely by challenging the norms enacted by the state and proposing categorically different norms, including solidaristic and universalistic visions of welfare access.  Interviewees who do so, however, typically have access to political, cultural, or educative spheres, in which alternative visions of welfare circulate.  Motions of "challenging" of interviewees who do not dispose of such ideational resources are mainly limited to spontaneous and particularistic demands of an exception from the rule, while the general validity of rigorous deservingness-norms and their application to "weaker" groups are not being questioned.

On the other side, I find mixed evidence for the mechanism of solidarization (the mirroring of experienced inclusion in inclusive attitudes) in so far as it applies only to a very small number of cases in the data set.  In these cases, however, highly "visible" experiences of social inclusion enabled by social investment-oriented welfare policies go with decidedly inclusivist welfare attitudes, and links between the two are being explicitly established by interviewees.  While these findings need further validation with different types of data and methods, the presented evidence does suggest two things.  Firstly, welfare reforms of the recent decades that have focused on cuts and conditionalities may have led to processes of (de-)solidarization between welfare recipient groups, contributing to salient public issues such as the blaming of the unemployed and welfare chauvinism.  Secondly, however, reforms that emphasize the social inclusion of recipients by means of the provision of active resources ("social investment") may, where they result in visible successes, on the contrary contribute to inclusive attitudes among recipients and solidarization among welfare recipient groups, and this even among social groups who on average hold different attitudinal dispositions such as manual workers.

**2. Literature review and theory**

Public attitudes on welfare access: how steep is the "ladder" of deservingness?

European publics' perceptions of welfare recipients' deservingness follow a relatively stable "rank order": there is a hierarchy of social groups who are on average seen to be "more" or "less" deserving to benefit from public solidarity (van Oorschot 2000; Laenen/Meuleman 2017; Meuleman et al. 2020).  The underlying logic of who stands where on the ladder of deservingness has been explained by five "deservingness criteria", summarized by v. Oorschot (2000) in the "CARIN"-model.  These are:

(1) Control: to what degree is a recipient themselves responsible for being in a situation of need - as opposed to, are the reasons outside their control?

(2) Attitude: is a recipient thankful for the solidarity received?  Do they make an effort to change their situation?  Are they being "honest"?

(3) Reciprocity: what has a recipient themselves contributed to the community of solidarity?

(4) Identity: does a recipient "belong" to the community, the polity?

(5) Need: how grave is the recipient's situation of need?

In the reality of (continental) European welfare states (Esping-Andersen 1990s), the question of socio-economic contribution by means of work (employment) and of social contribution payments is the main object of this dominant logic of deservingness.  In this logic, pensioners - the elderly who have contributed to the community during all of their life by means of their work and their social contribution payments, and whose situation of "ageing" will occur to everyone are the most deserving recipient group, followed by those who are sick and hence unable to work for a reason that is out of their control.  Unemployed persons tend to be seen as more deserving when the reason for their occupational inactivity is considered to lie outside their sphere of influence, and as less deserving when unemployment is considered to be their own fault or choice.  Immigrants who are not in employment tend to be seen as the least deserving group of all: they have no track record of contribution in their current country of residence whatsoever;  and in addition, they do not "belong" to the in-group (Reeskens/van der Meer 2019).

Even if who stands where on the "ladder" of deservingness is relatively stable across time and across countries (Laenen/Meuleman 2017), the relative steepness of this ladder is something that can vary, and notably so among subgroups.  More inclusivist visions of welfare access figuratively speaking translate to flatter steps on the ladder: the above-named deservingness criteria are being applied, but not in overly rigorous manner.  Differences are being made between recipient groups, but these are not extremely large.  In this situation, recipients of any group can be confident that their step on the ladder carries them firmly;  and there is not much object to conflicts, as (moral) differences are not very large.  Peak inclusivism occurs where steps are level and all recipient groups are seen to be equally deserving of solidarity: this attitudinal pattern has been called "welfare universalism".  As Laenen/Meuleman (2017) show, there is a group of varying size in every country that holds "universalist" visions of welfare deservingness.

On the other hand, we can imagine exclusivist visions of welfare access as the same "ladder" standing steeply upright: deservingness criteria remain the same as before, but they are very rigorously applied  and differences between recipient groups are large.  On a steep ladder, recipients of any group may struggle to keep grip of their respective step.  This can lead to conflict on the ladder, i.e. between welfare recipient groups, with those above kicking down on those below them.  The salience of deservingness-criteria can be expected to rise under these circumstances.

## Primary Theories Used:

### 1. Deservingness Theory (van Oorschot's CARIN Model)

**Detailed Explanation**: The paper builds centrally on van Oorschot's (2000) theory of welfare deservingness, particularly the "CARIN" model that identifies five key criteria determining who is perceived as deserving of welfare support. These criteria are: Control (responsibility for one's situation), Attitude (gratitude and compliance), Reciprocity (past contributions), Identity (belonging to the in-group), and Need (severity of situation). Wagner employs this theoretical framework to analyze how recipients interpret their welfare experiences and how they judge other recipient groups. Crucially, the paper extends van Oorschot's theory by conceptualizing deservingness not just as a rank order of groups but as a "ladder" that can be steeper or flatter, representing more exclusivist or inclusivist welfare attitudes respectively. This allows Wagner to theorize that welfare reforms can alter the steepness of this ladder, even while maintaining the same general hierarchy. The theory is particularly useful for understanding how welfare recipients might internalize these criteria from their policy experiences and then "mirror" them when judging others. The deservingness framework provides coherent explanatory power for understanding why certain welfare recipients might engage in "kicking down" behaviors when they feel their own deservingness is threatened.

### 2. Policy Feedback Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Wagner draws on policy feedback theory, which examines how policies shape subsequent politics through their effects on citizens. This theoretical approach, associated with scholars like Pierson (1994, 2001) and Mettler (2002), suggests that policies create experiences that influence citizens' political attitudes, capacity for action, and sense of civic inclusion. Wagner extends this framework to examine how activation-oriented welfare reforms specifically shape recipients' perceptions of deservingness and solidarity. The paper builds a nuanced theoretical model where different types of policy experiences (inclusion vs. exclusion, recognition vs. misrecognition) lead to different attitudinal outcomes. Of particular theoretical importance is Wagner's distinction between two types of activation policies—"conditionality" (obligations and sanctions) versus "social investment" (enabling resources)—and their differing impacts on recipients' sense of inclusion and solidarity. The policy feedback framework allows Wagner to analyze how policies not only distribute resources but also communicate norms about deservingness and worth that recipients then apply to their judgments of others. This represents an important theoretical contribution regarding the potential unintended consequences of welfare reform.

**Direct Quotes**:
"What feedback does this have on the welfare attitudes of target populations? I draw on qualitative interviews with Austrian and German labor market and pension policy recipients, showing there is a general tendency to internalize deservingness criteria and to 'mirror' them onto others." (p.1)

"Mettler (2002) describes how the US-American GI-Bill has led to 'inclusion effects' among its recipients, namely to increased levels of social and public participation." (p.8)

"My basic theory is that welfare recipients learn social norms that govern welfare access during their own welfare experiences and subsequently mirror these norms in their attitudes towards other recipient groups." (p.11)

### 3. Labor Market Dualization Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: The paper incorporates labor market dualization theory, which examines the division between labor market "insiders" (those with stable employment trajectories) and "outsiders" (those with interrupted or atypical employment). This theoretical framework, developed by scholars like Emmenegger et al. (2012), helps Wagner analyze how different groups within the working class experience welfare reforms differently. Wagner extends this theory to explain why certain groups (particularly labor market outsiders) are more likely to experience exclusionary effects from conditionality-oriented welfare reforms. The paper develops a theory of how labor market status interacts with policy type to produce different welfare experiences and subsequent attitudinal responses. Wagner argues that labor market outsiders are especially vulnerable to both negative policy experiences and exclusionary deservingness attitudes. This theoretical perspective is crucial for understanding the stratified impacts of welfare reform and why certain segments of the working class might be more susceptible to developing welfare chauvinist attitudes. The paper's nuanced approach to labor market status, incorporating both current and biographical definitions, represents an important methodological advancement in applying dualization theory.

**Direct Quotes**:
"Individuals with stable employment trajectories, no unemployment experience, and jobs covered by collective contracts that provide them comprehensive access to generous welfare entitlements have been called labor market insiders (Ebbinghaus et al. 2012)." (p.10)

"On the contrary, labor market outsiders are individuals with interrupted employment trajectories, periods of unemployment, who work part-time or whose jobs are not covered by contracts that provide them full access to welfare entitlements (ibid). I expect this group to concentrate salient, and notably negative, experiences with labor market and pension policies." (p.10)

"These create systematic conflicts notably for workers who are labor market outsiders (Emmenegger et al. 2012)." (p.3)

## Findings and Evidence:

### Summary of Key Findings:

The study finds strong evidence for the "kicking down" mechanism, where welfare recipients who experience exclusion or misrecognition often justify their own deservingness by blaming groups perceived as less deserving ("exclude them – rather than me"). These recipients apply the same deservingness criteria to others that they feel the state has unfairly applied to them.

The exclusion experiences associated with this behavior include: being forced to write numerous job applications without realistic prospects but under threat of sanctions; being compelled to participate in training perceived as unhelpful; suffering pension deductions for missed contribution years despite factors beyond their control; and perceived misrecognition of health status by public institutions.

Labor market insiders (those with stable employment trajectories) typically internalize deservingness norms emphasizing reciprocity and control, applying these moderately to other welfare recipient groups. Labor market outsiders who experience exclusion either "kick down" (most common) or "categorically challenge" the deservingness framework (less common, typically among those with access to alternative political ideologies).

Evidence for the "solidarization" mechanism is limited but present in a small number of cases, where recipients who had positive experiences with social investment policies expressed more inclusive welfare attitudes and explicitly connected their positive experiences to their support for others receiving similar opportunities.

### Supporting Evidence:

Wagner provides numerous interview excerpts to illustrate these findings. For instance, a 60-year-old Austrian man who became unemployed shortly before retirement age describes his experience with the employment office:

"Yes, the AMS [Austrian employment office]. I have written 100s of applications. One. One has sent back a read confirmation, and another one answered that the job was already taken. And everyone else – disappeared into thin air. Not even an answer. And then the people from AMS needle you [to appear regularly for meetings] … so they can see that you're still alive, but nothing more. [...] This is solely a chicanery, nothing else." (p.19)

Regarding the "kicking down" phenomenon, a 61-year-old German woman who will receive a minimum pension after an interrupted work history states that immigrants "are given preference. You hear that all the time." (p.20)

For the positive "solidarization" mechanism, a German man in his 30s who benefited from an internship abroad through the employment office states: "[They should] leave unemployment benefits as they are but do more to get people into companies [...] like that internship. Things like that, we need more." (p.23)

### Implications for My Thesis:

These findings offer several important implications for your research on welfare states, status politics, and populism:

1. The paper provides empirical support for examining welfare policy not just as material redistribution but as communicating messages about social worth and status. This aligns with your interest in how status concerns might predict political attitudes.
2. The "kicking down" mechanism offers a micro-level explanation for the link between welfare reform and populist attitudes, suggesting that activation policies might inadvertently foster welfare chauvinism among certain groups.
3. The distinction between "conditionality" and "social investment" approaches to activation provides a framework for analyzing how different policy designs might produce different status effects and political responses.
4. The finding that labor market outsiders are particularly susceptible to exclusion experiences and subsequent welfare chauvinism connects to your interest in how economic insecurity shapes political attitudes.
5. The potential for social investment policies to foster more inclusive attitudes, even among groups predisposed to exclusionary views, offers a hopeful direction for policy development that could mitigate populist tendencies.

These findings suggest that examining the subjective experience of policy recipients—particularly their perceptions of status recognition—might be crucial for understanding the connection between welfare policy and populist politics. The paper's evidence that welfare reforms can create feedback effects on solidarity between recipient groups provides a mechanism that could help explain why economic insecurity sometimes manifests as populist attitudes rather than class-based solidarity.

## Discussion:

### Interpretation of Findings:

Wagner interprets the findings as suggesting that welfare reforms since the 1990s focused on cuts and conditionalities may have contributed to processes of de-solidarization between welfare recipient groups. The author argues that recipients' exposure to strict deservingness criteria through activation policies often leads them to apply similar criteria to other welfare recipients, creating a cascade of blame directed at increasingly vulnerable groups.

The paper proposes that this mechanism may help explain the rise of welfare chauvinism and support for populist radical right-wing parties among segments of the working class. However, Wagner also presents the possibility that social investment approaches emphasizing the social inclusion of recipients through active resource provision may counteract this trend and foster more inclusive attitudes.

The author acknowledges that there is heterogeneity in responses to exclusion experiences. While "kicking down" is the most common reaction among manual workers in the dataset, some interviewees react by challenging the norms enacted by the state and proposing alternative visions of welfare, particularly those with access to political, cultural, or educational spheres where alternative visions circulate.

### Connections to Other Research:

Wagner connects the findings to several streams of literature:

1. Research on welfare deservingness perceptions (van Oorschot 2000; Laenen/Meuleman 2017), demonstrating how deservingness criteria are applied in practice and can be intensified through policy experiences.
2. Studies on welfare reform and activation (Bonoli 2010; Pierson 2001), extending this literature by examining the attitudinal consequences of these reforms for recipients.
3. Work on labor market dualization (Emmenegger et al. 2012), showing how insider/outsider divisions affect welfare experiences and attitudes.
4. Research on welfare chauvinism and populist radical right support (Attewell 2021; Careja/Harris 2022), providing micro-level mechanisms that could explain these political trends.

The author identifies areas for future research, including validation with different types of data and methods, and further exploration of how social investment policies might foster inclusive attitudes even among groups predisposed to exclusionary views.

### Relevance to My Research:

This paper offers several valuable conceptual and methodological insights for your research on welfare states, status politics, and populism:

1. The conceptualization of deservingness as a "ladder" with varying steepness provides a useful framework for analyzing how different welfare regimes might affect status politics and solidarity.
2. The focus on status recognition in welfare policy experiences aligns with your interest in how status concerns (rather than purely material concerns) might explain political behaviors and attitudes toward welfare programs.
3. The paper offers a mechanism connecting economic precarity to populism through the mediating role of welfare policy experiences, which could help explain the sometimes puzzling relationship between economic insecurity and political attitudes.
4. The distinction between "work-quality precarity" and "financial precarity" mentioned in your research interests could be integrated with Wagner's framework, examining how different forms of precarity might produce different welfare experiences and political responses.
5. The methodology combining biographical interviews with theoretical coding offers a model for examining how individual life trajectories interact with policy structures to produce political attitudes.

The paper's exploration of how welfare policies might inadvertently foster divisions between recipient groups provides a crucial link between institutional structures and individual political attitudes that could enrich your analysis of the "Dignity in Decline" thesis. The findings suggest that attention to the normative messages communicated by welfare policies, and how these are interpreted by recipients, may be essential for understanding the complex relationship between economic conditions and political behaviors.
