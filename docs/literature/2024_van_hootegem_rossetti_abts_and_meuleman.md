## Introduction and Research Motivation

This paper by Van Hootegem, Rossetti, Abts, and Meuleman (2024) examines the ideological underpinnings of public support for demanding active labor market policies (ALMPs) in Belgium. The paper moves beyond traditional explanations based on self-interest or generic left-right ideology to explore how specific ideological elements of the activation policy paradigm shape public attitudes.

The authors argue that existing research on welfare state attitudes has overlooked the importance of specific ideological frameworks that constitute the activation paradigm:

> "Research either focused on self-interest or left–right ideology to explain support for demanding active labour market policies (ALMPs). This article focuses instead on how attitudes towards these policies are rooted in the underlying policy paradigm."
> 

The central research motivation stems from the observation that ALMPs represent a significant transformation of European welfare states, shifting from passive compensation schemes to more activating measures that emphasize individual responsibility and market participation:

> "In the last decades, European welfare states have moved from 'passive' social policies to more 'activating' measures (Cox, 1998; Graziano, 2012) that aim to increase labour participation among people who are dependent on social security or social assistance benefits (Boland, 2016; Bruttel & Sol, 2006; van Berkel & Borghi, 2008, p. 332)."
> 

The authors highlight that ALMPs represent a fundamental shift in how welfare states address unemployment:

> "As ALMPs are amongst the most prominent 'new' types of social policies that declare a break with traditional compensation policies and openly call into question deeply entrenched institutions that handle unemployment (Häusermann, 2012; Maron & Helman, 2017, p. 406), understanding their support base is primordial to grasp the legitimacy of contemporary welfare systems and political feasibility of future reforms."
> 

## Literature Review and Theoretical Framework

### The Policy Paradigm of Demanding Activation

The authors develop a theoretical framework centered on the concept of a "policy paradigm," which they define as an interpretive framework consisting of a set of ideas about organizing principles and the nature of problems that policies address. They draw on a rich literature about policy paradigms:

> "To capture the ideological roots of support for demanding ALMPs, our study investigates how its underlying policy paradigm informs public preferences. We assume that the paradigmatic ideas on the organizing principles and the problems behind a specific policy (Béland, 2005, 2016; Daigneault, 2014; Hall, 1993) are especially relevant to dissect ideological support for ALMPs."
> 

The authors distinguish between two types of ALMPs:

1. **Enabling ALMPs**: Focused on investments in human capital and employability.
2. **Demanding ALMPs**: Emphasizing coercive elements like benefit cuts, obligatory training, and sanctions for non-compliance.

Their analysis focuses primarily on demanding ALMPs, which represent a particularly significant break with traditional welfare state paradigms:

> "Indeed, demanding ALMPs are not neutral or traditional policy measures, but entail a break with the traditional vision on the desired organization of unemployment benefit distribution as well as on the causes behind welfare dependency (Romano, 2018)."
> 

The paradigm of demanding activation is characterized by two main elements:

1. A shift in the balance between universality and conditionality:

> "Concerning the trade-off between universality and conditionality, the activation turn implies a renewed focus on selective distribution that aims to cut unemployment benefits, or at least match welfare rights with obligations (Seikel & Spannagel, 2018). Demanding ALMPs reinsert the market principle into government policy and promote a re-commodification of labour (Boland, 2016, p. 335)."
> 
1. A transformation from state to individual responsibility:

> "With regard to the responsibility of state vs. individual, the paradigm of demanding activation entails a transformed outlook on the very nature and causes of social risks. The activation turn emphasizes individual responsibility and labels welfare dependency as a 'personal failing' (Watts & Fitzpatrick, 2018)."
> 

### Distributive Justice and Activation Support

The first key ideological element that the authors identify is distributive justice principles. Drawing on the distributive justice literature, they distinguish three principles:

1. **Equality**: Distributing equally to all citizens regardless of additional criteria.
2. **Equity**: Making distribution conditional on past contributions (proportional to taxes paid, welfare contributions, etc.).
3. **Need**: Selective distribution oriented only toward those highest in need.

The authors argue that the activation paradigm represents a shift from equality-based to more conditional or selective welfare provision:

> "The activation turn implies a shift from more equal and universal unemployment benefit distributions to more conditional (cf., equity) or selective (cf., need) welfare provision, boiling down to a fundamental alteration of the underlying conceptions of distributive justice (Buchanan, 1990; Ervik et al., 2015)."
> 

They hypothesize that preferences for equity and need principles will be associated with greater support for demanding activation:

> "Hypothesis 1. Individuals who prefer the principle of equity over equality will express more support for demanding ALMPs."
> 

> "Hypothesis 2. Individuals who prefer the principle of need over equality will express more support for demanding ALMPs."
> 

### Unemployment Attributions and Activation Support

The second key ideological element is attributions of unemployment—the perceived causes of why people become unemployed. The authors identify four types of attributions along two axes:

1. Individual-social axis: Whether factors internal or external to the individual cause unemployment.
2. Blame-fate axis: Whether unemployment is attributed to controllable versus inevitable events.

This yields four attribution types:

- Individual blame: Attributing unemployment to laziness or unwillingness to find a job
- Individual fate: Seeing unemployment as personal misfortune
- Social blame: Attributing unemployment to social injustice and structural exclusion
- Social fate: Seeing unemployment as caused by unavoidable societal processes

The authors argue that the activation paradigm emphasizes individual blame attributions:

> "According to the paradigm underlying the punitive approach to activation, individual failings and a weak work ethic are the main causes of unemployment (Boland, 2016; Daguerre, 2007), and sanctions and benefit cuts are seen as effective means to force passive welfare dependents back into employment (Dwyer, 2000)."
> 

They hypothesize:

> "Hypothesis 3. Individuals who blame unemployment on the individual rather than attributing it to social blame or social fate will express more support for demanding ALMPs."
> 

> "Hypothesis 4. Individuals who ascribe unemployment to individual fate rather than attributing it to social blame or social fate will express more support for demanding ALMPs."
> 

### Conventional Explanatory Mechanisms

The authors also acknowledge the importance of traditional explanatory frameworks from the welfare attitudes literature:

1. **Self-interest theory**: Suggesting that individuals in precarious economic situations (potential beneficiaries of unemployment benefits) would oppose demanding ALMPs:

> "Following a rational-choice logic that is based on cost–benefit calculations, actual or potential welfare beneficiaries might thus be more negative towards ALMPs that potentially limit benefit access or levels (Achterberg et al., 2014; Carriero & Filandri, 2018; Kootstra & Roosma, 2018)."
> 
1. **Political ideology**: Suggesting that right-wing and authoritarian individuals would be more supportive of demanding ALMPs:

> "In line with this theory, right-wing-oriented individuals with strong authoritarian values and economically liberal beliefs are found to be more supportive of demanding ALMPs (Fossati, 2018; Kootstra & Roosma, 2018; Laenen & Meuleman, 2019; Roosma & Jeene, 2017)."
> 

However, they argue that these traditional frameworks may be insufficient to fully understand activation support, particularly given that activation is not aligned with traditional left-right divides:

> "As ALMPs are being pursued by parties across the ideological spectrum and cut across traditional distributive conflicts between capital and labour (Cronert, 2020; Deeming, 2015; Häusermann, 2012), social positions and more conventional abstract ideological beliefs could be insufficient in themselves to fully grasp the reasons for supporting these policies."
> 

## Methodology

### Data and Context

The study uses data from the Belgian National Election Study 2014, collected among Belgians eligible to vote. The authors explain their choice of Belgium as a research site:

> "Despite some peculiarities of its unemployment benefit system, Belgium is a well-suited context to examine support for demanding ALMPs. After a long period of reluctance and fragmented policy momentum (Hemerijck & van Kersbergen, 2019), activation measures have been especially implemented since 2004 with a strong boost from 2012 onwards (van Lancker et al., 2015)."
> 

The survey used two-stage random probability sampling and was conducted through Computer-Assisted Personal Interviewing, resulting in a sample of 1,901 individuals with a response rate of 47.5%. Post-stratification weights based on age, gender, and education were applied.

### Indicators

1. **Dependent variable**: Support for demanding ALMPs was operationalized using six Likert-type items measuring support for:
    - Limiting unemployment benefits to 2 years
    - Requiring minimum income recipients to do community work
    - Obliging long-term unemployed to accept any job
    - Obliging long-term unemployed to re-educate or lose benefits
    - Stricter government control on job-seeking behavior
    - Harsher punishment for those who don't fulfill obligations
2. **Independent variables**:
    - **Distributive justice preferences**: Measured by a direct question asking how government should organize unemployment benefits allocation (equality, equity, or need)
    - **Unemployment attributions**: Measured by asking respondents what they think is the most important reason for unemployment (individual blame, individual fate, social blame, or social fate)
    - **Ideological dimensions**: Left-right placement (11-point scale), authoritarianism (3 items), and economic liberalism (2 items)
    - **Social-structural variables**: Occupational class, income, education, welfare dependency, unemployment status, union membership, gender, age, and region

### Statistical Modeling

The authors used Structural Equation Modeling (SEM) to analyze support for demanding ALMPs:

> "To analyse support for demanding ALMPs, we employ SEM, which allows us to assess structural pathways between constructs, while taking random measurement error into account through the use of latent variables."
> 

They estimated three stepwise models:

1. A model including only social-structural and control variables
2. A model adding distributive justice preferences and unemployment attributions
3. A model adding ideological predictors (left-right, authoritarianism, economic liberalism)

## Empirical Findings

### Descriptive Results

The descriptive analysis revealed strong public support for demanding activation measures in Belgium:

> "The proportions of respondents agreeing with each item, as displayed in Table 1, reveal that there is a strong degree of public support for demanding activation measures (see also: Carriero & Filandri, 2018; Houtman, 1997; Kootstra & Roosma, 2018). Although agreement is slightly lower for limiting unemployment benefits over time, the other demanding ALMPs measures are supported by half to more than two-thirds of the respondents."
> 

Regarding distributive justice preferences, the majority (52.2%) preferred equality-based distribution of unemployment benefits, while 29% preferred equity-based, and 18.8% preferred need-based distribution.

For unemployment attributions, most respondents (35.6%) identified a general lack of jobs (social blame) as the main cause of unemployment, while about 30% attributed unemployment to individual blame.

### Determinants of Support for Demanding Activation

The regression analysis revealed several key findings:

1. **Self-interest variables** showed some expected patterns:

> "Persons in higher income quartiles score higher on public support for ALMPs than those in the lowest quartile, which is in line with their more limited interest in unconditional welfare provisions. [...] In addition, the coefficients for unemployment and welfare dependency show that the unemployed and benefit recipients score much lower on support for demanding ALMPs than the employed and those not receiving benefits."
> 
1. **Distributive justice preferences** were significant predictors:

> "Support for demanding ALMPs is 0.28 standard deviations higher among those preferring equity compared to individuals selecting equality as the preferred principle, which is a substantial difference. [...] Moreover, also respondents who prefer the need principle favour demanding ALMPs more strongly than those who prefer equality-based distribution (thus confirming Hypothesis 2)."
> 

Notably, the effect of the need principle was even stronger than that of equity:

> "The effect parameter for need is considerably and significantly larger than the one for the principle of equity (b = 0.28 vs. b = 0.55; p-value difference test = 0.00)."
> 
1. **Unemployment attributions** showed strong effects:

> "Individual blame attribution significantly reinforces preferences for demanding activation: Respondents who see laziness or a lack of motivation as the primary cause of unemployment are more likely to support ALMPs, confirming its close connection with moralizing unemployment and welfare sanctioning as measures to combat laziness and structural dependency (Dwyer, 2000; Fossati, 2018)."
> 

However, contrary to Hypothesis 4:

> "Contrary to Hypothesis 4, however, respondents who attribute unemployment to the misfortune of individuals (individual fate) do not support ALMPs more than individuals who see social fate or social blame as its primary cause."
> 
1. **Traditional ideological predictors** were also significant:

> "Right-wing individuals are more likely being more supportive of demanding ALMPs. [...] Moreover, the coefficient is moderate to rather weak in size (b = 0.12), indicating that left–right conflicts are not the most decisive determinant of public opinions on demanding ALMPs. Authoritarianism nevertheless has a very strong impact, with a standardized coefficient of 0.47."
> 

The combined explanatory power of all variables was substantial (R² = 49.1%), with the policy paradigm variables (distributive justice and unemployment attributions) adding 15% to the explained variance beyond social-structural variables.

## Discussion and Theoretical Implications

### The Importance of Policy Paradigms

The authors' findings strongly support their central thesis that support for demanding ALMPs is rooted in the ideological elements of the activation paradigm:

> "The results indicate that support for the forced reintegration of the jobless in the labour market is related to preferences regarding distributive justice and views on the primary causes of unemployment."
> 

They highlight the particularly strong effects of the need principle and individual blame attributions:

> "The relationship to the principle of need is even stronger than that of equity, which indicates that a focus on self-help, individual responsibility and minimal welfare distribution are important elements in the justification of the sanctioning of undeserving benefit claimants. Moreover, attitudes towards demanding policies are shown to be strongly connected to the idea that the unemployed are to blame personally for their neediness."
> 

### Beyond Traditional Explanatory Frameworks

The authors argue that their findings demonstrate the added value of examining specific ideological elements of the policy paradigm rather than relying solely on general left-right ideology:

> "Although other reasons also occur for people to support demanding ALMPs, as for instance to some extent self-interest, these findings evidence that the ideological paradigms on which social policies are grounded (Béland, 2005, 2016; Cox, 1998; Daigneault, 2014; Hall, 1993) are important to understand their legitimacy and support base."
> 

They also note that self-interest cannot fully explain support patterns:

> "However, the effects of other social structural predictors are insignificant or have a sign that contradicts self-interest theory. [...] Clearly, social structural predictors that reflect self-interest cannot fully explain how support for demanding ALMPs takes shape."
> 

### Policy and Societal Implications

The authors discuss important implications of their findings for understanding the broader societal significance of activation policies:

> "The strong connection of support for activation with the principle of need and individual blaming has important implications. As the principle of need justifies the replacement of universal and reciprocal welfare policies with means-tested programs, support for demanding ALMPs seems to be linked to the adherence to a liberal view on the welfare state (Clasen & van Oorschot, 2002)."
> 

They warn about potential negative consequences of the stereotyping associated with demanding activation:

> "In addition, as attributions evoke and reinforce stereotypes about target groups, ALMPs are embedded in stereotypical images of the unemployed as being lazy and dependent. The reliance of both support for ALMPs and the activation paradigm itself (Daguerre, 2007) on these stereotypes may have important repercussions, as policies can restrict the opportunities and resources of stigmatized groups, and worsen stigma-related problems (Link & Hatzenbuehler, 2016)."
> 

## Connections to Broader Literature on Welfare Attitudes and Far-Right Politics

While the paper does not directly address far-right politics, its findings have important implications for understanding the appeal of right-wing populist parties. The connection between demanding activation support and attributions of individual blame for unemployment aligns with narratives often employed by far-right parties that distinguish between "deserving" and "undeserving" recipients.

The authors note that support for demanding activation is connected to a "producerism" worldview:

> "This policy orientation is in line with a need-based distribution that implies more selective and residual welfare state provision (Clasen & van Oorschot, 2002)."
> 

This producerism, which divides society into "productive" and "unproductive" groups, is a central feature of many right-wing populist narratives. The individualization of social risks implied by the activation paradigm resonates with the anti-welfare rhetoric of some far-right movements.

The paper's findings on the importance of individual blame attributions also connect to the literature on welfare chauvinism, where welfare access is conditioned not only on citizenship or ethnic boundaries but also on perceived deservingness. Welfare chauvinism often overlaps with the producerism identified in this study, creating a nexus between activation support and potential support for far-right parties.

Additionally, the strong effects of authoritarianism (β = 0.47) on support for demanding activation suggest overlapping psychological dispositions between demanding activation supporters and potential far-right voters, as authoritarianism is often identified as a key predictor of far-right support.

## Methodological Strengths and Limitations

### Strengths:

1. **Theoretical innovation**: The paper moves beyond traditional explanatory frameworks to examine specific ideological elements of the activation paradigm.
2. **Comprehensive modeling**: The SEM approach allows the authors to assess direct and indirect effects while accounting for measurement error.
3. **Stepwise model building**: By adding variables in stages, the authors can assess the added explanatory value of policy paradigm variables beyond traditional predictors.

### Limitations:

1. **Cross-sectional data**: The authors acknowledge that they cannot make causal claims about the relationship between policy paradigms and public support:

> "Claims about causality can nevertheless not be made here, as it is well possible that policymakers appeal to already existing sentiments and feedback effects between institutions and public opinions might exist (Kumlin & Stadelmann-Steffen, 2014)."
> 
1. **Single-country focus**: The study is limited to Belgium, potentially limiting generalizability:

> "Despite the inability to disentangle the link between policy paradigms and public sentiments, it seems most likely that interpretation effects are at play, whereby policies and political discourses—that is, the demanding activation paradigm—provide the public with ways to interpret the goals and problems of unemployment policies (Pierson, 1993)."
> 
1. **Measurement of justice principles and attributions**: The use of single-item measures for these key variables might not capture the full complexity of these constructs.

## Conclusion

This paper makes several important contributions to our understanding of welfare attitudes:

1. It demonstrates that support for demanding ALMPs is firmly rooted in specific ideological elements of the activation paradigm, particularly preferences for need-based and equity-based distributive justice and individual blame attributions of unemployment.
2. It shows that these policy paradigm variables explain a substantial portion of variance in activation support beyond traditional explanatory variables like self-interest and general left-right ideology.
3. It highlights how activation policies may reinforce stereotypical views of the unemployed and contribute to the stigmatization of welfare recipients.

The paper provides valuable insights into the ideological foundations of contemporary welfare state transformations and the public legitimacy of activation reforms. It also connects to broader debates about the individualization of social risks, the retrenchment of universal welfare provision, and the political consequences of welfare state restructuring.

The findings suggest that the activation paradigm has become embedded not only in policy discourse but also in public attitudes, potentially contributing to a fundamental reshaping of the normative foundations of European welfare states.
