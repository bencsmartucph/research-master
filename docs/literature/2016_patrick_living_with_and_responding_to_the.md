## Primary Theoretical Frameworks

### 1. Stigma and Shame Theory (Goffman, Walker)

Patrick builds her analysis primarily on Erving Goffman's classic conceptualization of stigma and Robert Walker's cross-national research on shame and poverty. This theoretical framework helps explain how benefit recipients experience and manage their stigmatized identities.

**Explanation and Application:**

Goffman's theory defines stigma as a process where individuals with discredited attributes are rejected by society, damaging their "normal" identities. Patrick applies this to benefit recipients, whose non-work status and benefits receipt are treated as problematic "attributes" that mark them as deviant:

> "In his classic work, Goffman defines stigma as: 'The phenomenon whereby an individual with an attribute which is deeply discredited by his/her society is rejected as a result of the attribute. Stigma is a process by which the reaction of others spoils normal identity' (1990)."
> 

> "Goffman's theoretical account of stigma recognises how people with deviant or discredited identities and behaviours (what he terms attributes) can be rejected and excluded from society. It attends to and focuses on the relational, and the consequences of having particular characteristics that set one apart from the 'normal'. The process of stigma 'spoils' affected individuals' identities, and casts them as deviant and 'other'. This is of clear relevance for out-of-work benefit claimants whose non-work and out-of-work benefits receipt are so often seen as problematic 'attributes'."
> 

Building on Goffman, Patrick incorporates Baumberg's (2016) categorization of benefit-related stigma into three dimensions:

> "Baumberg (2016) describes three categories of benefit-related stigma: personal stigma, claims stigma and stigmatisation. Personal stigma is a person's 'own feeling that claiming benefits is shameful' (Baumberg et al, 2012, 5), a category that attends more to stigma as internalised and applied to oneself rather than traditional theoretical accounts of stigma which are inevitably about societal reaction and responses (see Goffman, 1990). Stigmatisation is the view that others see claiming benefits as shameful, while 'claims stigma' refers to the shame and stigma attached to the processes of claiming benefits (Baumberg, 2016)."
> 

Walker's research on shame and poverty complements Goffman's framework by highlighting how categories of "them" and "us" create social hierarchies that lead to shaming processes:

> "In their cross-country study of shame and poverty, Walker (2014; 2013) describe how categories and distinctions drawn between 'them' and 'us' operate to determine who has lower and higher social status, and then feed into a shaming process that has negative implications for those judged to sit at the bottom of the social hierarchy. For Walker (2014), shaming that is backed by the power of the state is best described as 'stigmatisation'."
> 

Patrick emphasizes that stigmatization involves both internal and external processes, with individuals internalizing negative identities while also experiencing external exclusion:

> "What is important is that stigmatisation and what Chase and Walker (2013) term the 'co-construction of shame' are processes that have both internal and external constituents; individuals feel and take on a shamed and stigmatised identity, just as they also respond to and sense external forces of stigmatisation and exclusion."
> 

**Historical Context and Scholarly Landscape:**

This theoretical approach emerged from Goffman's pioneering work on stigma in the 1960s, which has been widely applied to understand various forms of social exclusion. Walker's more recent cross-national research on shame and poverty (2013-2014) updated Goffman's concepts specifically for poverty contexts. Baumberg's (2016) categorization of benefit stigma further refined these concepts for contemporary welfare settings. Together, these theories form a robust framework for understanding how negative societal narratives about benefits receipt become internalized and managed by affected individuals.

**Relevance to Your Research:**

This theoretical framework is crucial for understanding how welfare policies and public discourse about benefits can affect recipients' self-perception and behavior. For your research on social psychological mechanisms affecting far-right support, Patrick's application of stigma theory illustrates how welfare policies that increase stigmatization might create alienation, resentment, and division among the working class—potentially driving some toward far-right populism that promises to restore dignity and recognition.

### 2. Citizenship Engagement and Claim-making

Patrick interprets the "othering" responses observed among benefit recipients as a form of defensive citizenship engagement, drawing on Ellison's (2000) conceptualization of citizenship engagement forms.

**Explanation and Application:**

Patrick suggests that when benefit recipients engage in "othering" (criticizing other supposedly less deserving claimants), they are actually engaging in a defensive form of citizenship claim-making:

> "Arguably, this 'othering' is best understood as an admittedly defensive form of citizenship engagement (Ellison, 2000) and claim-making, which sees claimants make use of the dominant narrative as a discursive resource (Fohrbeck et al, 2014) that can be deployed to shore up their own deservingness and entitlement to social welfare."
> 

The theory distinguishes between defensive and proactive forms of citizenship engagement:

> "Defensive forms of citizenship engagement encompasses activities that seek to defend the real or perceived erosion of social rights (Ellison, 2000; Cook et al, 2012). They can be contrasted with more proactive forms of citizenship engagement, where individuals and groups feel able to mobilise in efforts to extend their entitlement, and even to make demands for new rights (Ellison, 2000; Cook et al, 2012)."
> 

Patrick argues that the othering behavior is a strategic response to a hostile welfare environment, serving as recipients' attempt to assert their legitimate citizenship rights:

> "Othering as a form of defensive citizenship engagement and claim-making is inevitably negative in tone, and can of course have repercussions for claimants' own citizenship inclusion given that it serves to reanimate and strengthen the dominant, problematising narrative. However, when set against a context in which many mainstream politicians, much of the media and wider public seem united in their critique of the behaviours and dependencies of out-of-work benefit claimants, it is a perhaps unsurprising response. Understood in this light, 'othering' becomes an active effort by claimants to assert their own deservingness and lay claim to their citizenship rights, at a time when these are repeatedly threatened and undermined both by a discursive and stigmatising narrative, but also by policy reforms that retrench and residualise social welfare provision (Patrick, 2014)."
> 

**Historical Context and Scholarly Landscape:**

This theoretical approach draws on Ellison's (2000) work on citizenship in changing welfare states and relates to broader literature on how marginalized groups navigate their citizenship status. It connects to scholarship on welfare deservingness (van Oorschot), social exclusion (Levitas), and how disadvantaged groups engage in boundary-making (Lamont). The framework helps explain defensive responses to welfare stigma as citizenship practices rather than merely internalized oppression.

**Relevance to Your Research:**

This theoretical perspective offers valuable insights for your research by explaining how welfare policies can affect political behavior through citizenship identity and recognition. When welfare systems create experiences of disrespect or contested citizenship, beneficiaries may develop political responses that vary from defensive othering to seeking political alternatives that promise restored dignity and recognition—including far-right parties that offer cultural recognition to those feeling marginalized. The framework helps explain how welfare policy design can either foster social solidarity or division.

### 3. "Othering" as a Social-Psychological Response to Stigma

Patrick dedicates significant attention to the concept of "othering" as a key social-psychological mechanism through which stigmatized benefit recipients manage their identities.

**Explanation and Application:**

Othering refers to the process whereby individuals distance themselves from a stigmatized group by emphasizing differences between themselves and supposedly less deserving others. Patrick notes this was the most common response to benefit stigma among her participants:

> "While there were some signs of resistance to the dominant 'scrounger' narrative, what was most notable in this research was the 'othering' in which so many of the participants engaged, where they shored up their own deservingness to benefits via a critique of some 'other' deemed less deserving and whose behaviour was characterised as more problematic."
> 

She contextualizes this within the growing literature on othering among people in poverty:

> "There is a growing body of literature illustrating the 'othering' in which people in poverty engage, whereby they emphasise the non-deservingness of some 'other' while – very often – simultaneously defending their own entitlement to benefits (Walker, 2014; Chase and Walker, 2013; Garthwaite, 2014; Batty and Flint, 2013; Shildrick and MacDonald, 2013)."
> 

Patrick documents how participants created distinctions between deserving and undeserving benefit recipients, often based on characteristics they themselves possessed:

> "There were clear correlations between individual participants' own benefit claiming identity, and those whom they identified as being undeserving, with evidence that participants were implicitly seeking to emphasise their own deservingness by critiquing and 'othering' those who were unable to display the same characteristics as themselves, something also highlighted in Chase and Walker's research (2013)."
> 

Participants frequently targeted immigrants, those with substance issues, fraudulent disability claimants, and those with no previous employment experience:

> "Participants frequently spoke about the undeservingness of immigrants, those with substance misuse issues, fraudulent disability benefit claimants, and those with no previous employment experience."
> 

This othering reinforces rather than challenges the dominant negative narrative:

> "Through their engagement in 'othering', itself a response to the 'othering' they experience as members of a stigmatised and presumed deficit population, out-of-work benefit claimants are themselves re-circulating and extending the reach of the 'scrounger' narrative. As the 'othered' also 'other', the framing consensus on 'welfare' is further embedded and strengthened, in ways that reduce scope for an alternative narrative or solidaristic challenge to the status quo (Chase and Walker, 2013)."
> 

**Historical Context and Scholarly Landscape:**

This theoretical approach builds on social identity theory (Tajfel & Turner) and boundary work literature (Lamont). It connects to research on how marginalized groups often respond to stigma by creating moral boundaries that distinguish themselves from others in similar circumstances. In welfare scholarship, this connects to van Oorschot's work on deservingness criteria and has been documented across welfare regimes in studies by Shildrick & MacDonald, Chase & Walker, and others.

**Relevance to Your Research:**

The othering mechanism provides crucial insight for your research by showing how welfare systems that emphasize differentiation between deserving and undeserving recipients might inadvertently foster social division rather than solidarity. This relates directly to how welfare policy design might influence support for far-right politics—policies that create visible "deserving vs. undeserving" distinctions might activate identity-based politics and undermine class solidarity, creating fertile ground for far-right appeal. Conversely, more universalistic welfare approaches might reduce such divisive othering.

## Key Takeaways and Relevance to Your Research

### 1. Benefits Stigma is Multi-Dimensional and Pervasive

Patrick's research reveals how benefits stigma operates at multiple levels—personal, procedural, and social—affecting claimants' self-perception, dignity, and social connections:

> "Overall, then, the participants in this study demonstrated the extent to which the stigma associated with benefits receipt is affecting how people see themselves, imagine they are seen by others, and experience the processes associated with benefits receipt. These experiences of stigma can be profoundly damaging for individuals' self-esteem and sense of self-worth and so may actually make the government's efforts to see people transition from benefits and into paid employment less rather than more likely."
> 

**Relevance**: This finding suggests that stigmatizing welfare approaches may backfire in terms of policy effectiveness while also creating psychological conditions that make recipients more susceptible to political messages offering dignity restoration—whether from the left or right. Your research might explore how different welfare framing and implementation approaches affect recipients' sense of social inclusion/exclusion and subsequent political attitudes.

### 2. The Benefits System Itself Can Entrench Stigma

Patrick found that the actual processes of claiming benefits were often experienced as stigmatizing and dehumanizing:

> "In this study, there was widespread evidence of stigma being attached to the process of claiming benefits (claims stigma), with 12 of the longitudinal sample of 15 recounting experiences of this form of stigma. Participants frequently described processes and interactions which were dehumanising and alienating, findings which are reinforced by other research (Chase and Walker, 2013; Baumberg et al, 2012)."
> 

> "'You're just another number, you're not a person. That's how I feel about it [being on benefits].' (Young Jobseeker (YJS), W3)"
> 

**Relevance**: This suggests that the administrative design and implementation of welfare policies matter significantly for recipients' experiences and potential political responses. Your research might examine how administrative processes in welfare systems (respectful vs. punitive, dignified vs. stigmatizing) correlate with political attitudes, particularly toward far-right parties that often promise to restore dignity to "forgotten" citizens.

### 3. "Othering" as a Political Mechanism

Patrick's research shows how stigmatization can lead to division among welfare recipients through othering processes:

> "In differentiating between the undeserving 'them' and the deserving 'us' it was common to make a broad distinction between those who 'choose' benefits, or who were to 'blame' for their own situation, and those who were reliant on benefits due to factors beyond their control such as impairments, unemployment and caring responsibilities."
> 

**Relevance**: This mechanism directly connects to your research on how welfare policies might moderate far-right support. Welfare systems that emphasize categorical distinctions between "deserving" and "undeserving" recipients may facilitate the kind of othering that creates fertile ground for far-right messaging, which often leverages similar us/them distinctions but along ethno-nationalist lines. Conversely, more universalistic welfare approaches might reduce such divisions.

### 4. Welfare Reform Can Intensify Stigma and Shame

Patrick observed that welfare reforms often intensified experiences of stigma among recipients:

> "What is important is that processes of welfare reform, and changes to benefit entitlement were sometimes experienced as deepening and extending the reach of this claims stigma, particularly around the questioning of eligibility that the reforms so often entail."
> 

**Relevance**: This suggests that welfare reform periods may be particularly high-risk moments for political alienation and potential far-right support. Your research might examine whether support for populist or far-right parties increases during periods of welfare reform, particularly when those reforms increase conditionality or scrutiny of recipients.

### 5. Opportunities for Resistance and Alternative Approaches

Despite the predominance of othering, Patrick documented instances of resistance to the dominant narrative:

> "There were also some attempts to highlight the 'deservingness' of most, if not all, benefit claimants, and to emphasise that fraudulent and undeserving 'behaviours' were limited to a minority of cases. This could be seen as an explicit challenge to the dominant narrative, and was most notable in the accounts of Cath and Isobella. Both highlighted how there are 'undeserving' people in every walk of life, a more inclusive approach than the more common 'them' and 'us' dichotomy."
> 

**Relevance**: This finding suggests that alternative welfare narratives and designs might foster more inclusive social attitudes. Your research could explore whether welfare systems that emphasize solidarity and shared risk (rather than individual deservingness) correlate with lower far-right support. It also indicates potential for counter-narratives to reduce divisive othering.

## Conclusion and Implications

Patrick's research provides valuable insights into the social psychological mechanisms through which welfare policies and associated narratives affect benefit recipients' identities, social relationships, and citizenship practices. The study reveals how stigmatizing welfare approaches can damage self-esteem, foster division among recipients through othering processes, and potentially undermine policy effectiveness by creating psychological barriers to employment transitions.

For your research on welfare policies and far-right support, this paper suggests several important connections:

1. **Welfare system design matters**: Systems that emphasize individual deservingness and create visible distinctions between "deserving" and "undeserving" recipients may inadvertently foster social division and othering that mirrors far-right rhetoric.
2. **Administrative processes affect political responses**: Degrading or dehumanizing welfare administration may create feelings of disrespect that make recipients more receptive to political messaging promising restored dignity and recognition.
3. **Welfare reform periods are high-risk moments**: Reforms that increase scrutiny or conditionality may intensify stigma and alienation, potentially creating windows of opportunity for far-right mobilization.
4. **Alternative approaches are possible**: More universalistic welfare designs and narratives emphasizing shared risk and solidarity might reduce divisive othering and potentially moderate support for far-right politics.

To strengthen your research, you might consider incorporating measures of perceived stigma, dignity, and respect in welfare interactions as mediating variables between welfare policy exposure and political attitudes. Patrick's findings suggest these psychological experiences may be critical mechanisms connecting welfare systems to political behavior.
