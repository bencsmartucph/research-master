## Primary Theories Used

### 1. Affective Polarization Theory

**Explanation:**
Affective polarization represents a shift from conceptualizing polarization primarily in ideological terms to understanding it as a social-psychological phenomenon. The authors explain that this concept, introduced by Iyengar, Sood, and Lelkes (2012), refers to "the tendency to evaluate in-party members positively and out-party members negatively" (p.1). The theory posits that partisan identities now function as social identities, creating an emotional divide between partisans that extends beyond policy disagreements.

The authors note that despite the prominence of this concept, its measurement has been inconsistent and often relies on simple indices like warmth bias (the difference between in-party and out-party feeling thermometer ratings). This methodological limitation has potentially obscured the complex psychological processes underlying partisan animus, contributing to mixed findings about the relationship between affective polarization and democratic attitudes.

**Direct quotes:**
"In political science, much attention has been paid to the problem of partisan polarization. Early work on polarization largely focused on the ideological and issue differences between partisans in the political elite and the mass public. More recently though, scholars have explored the social divide between partisans. Most notably, in a foundational paper, Iyengar, Sood, and Lelkes (2012) introduced the concept of affective polarization, defined as the tendency to evaluate in-party members positively and out-party members negatively." (p.1)

"Researchers have become particularly interested in the consequences of affective polarization for the health of American democracy. Among other things, scholars have suggested that affective polarization may lead to a breakdown of democratic norms, insofar as it makes it more difficult for citizens to accept democratic outcomes that favor an increasingly-hated out-party. However, empirical evidence has not provided clear evidence that affective polarization encourages citizens to abandon democratic norms." (pp.1-2)

"Given concerns over the relevancy of affective polarization in explaining partisan animus, we believe that both the conceptualization and measurement of affective polarization require an update." (p.2)

**Application in the paper:**
The authors use affective polarization as their foundational theoretical framework but argue for reconceptualizing it as multidimensional rather than unidimensional. They critique the limitations of existing measures (particularly warmth bias) and develop a more nuanced model that captures different psychological mechanisms underlying partisan animosity. This reconceptualization allows them to explore how different components of affective polarization relate differently to anti-democratic attitudes and political violence.

**Historical context and key scholars:**
The concept of affective polarization emerged from the broader literature on partisan polarization in American politics, shifting focus from elite-level ideological polarization (e.g., McCarty, Poole, and Rosenthal, 2006) to mass-level social division. Key scholars include Shanto Iyengar, Gaurav Sood, Yphtach Lelkes, Sean Westwood, and Lilliana Mason. The theory draws heavily on social identity theory from social psychology, applying intergroup dynamics to partisan identities.

### 2. Social Identity Theory

**Explanation:**
Social identity theory serves as the psychological foundation for understanding affective polarization. Developed by Henri Tajfel and John Turner, this theory proposes that group membership is a significant source of pride and self-esteem, with individuals seeking to enhance their self-image by favoring their ingroup and discriminating against outgroups. In the context of partisan politics, this manifests as individuals viewing their partisan identity as a core social identity, leading to ingroup favoritism and outgroup derogation.

The authors explain that this theoretical framework helps explain why partisans develop animosity toward opposing partisans even in the absence of substantive policy disagreements. Social identity theory posits that the mere categorization of people into groups can trigger intergroup bias, which is amplified when the group identity becomes salient and important to the individual.

**Direct quotes:**
"This conceptualization is rooted in social identity theory, which argues that identification with a social group (such as a political party) is sufficient to generate favoritism for that group. According to social identity theory, people want to feel positively about the groups they belong to, which leads them to want the ingroup to do better than or compare favorably to outgroups." (p.2)

"This follows from the notion that affective polarization is rooted in perceived differences in social identity rather than substantive disagreement in terms of ideology and issues." (p.3)

**Application in the paper:**
The authors use social identity theory to conceptualize the three components of their affective polarization model. Othering reflects the tendency to exaggerate ingroup/outgroup differences; aversion captures the negative evaluations and behavioral avoidance of outgroups; and moralization connects to how individuals tie their ingroup identity to moral convictions. By grounding their measure in social identity theory, the authors provide a psychological explanation for why and how partisan animosity develops.

**Historical context and key scholars:**
Social identity theory emerged from the work of Henri Tajfel and John Turner in the 1970s and 1980s, originally focused on explaining intergroup conflict and discrimination. In political science, scholars like Leonie Huddy, Lilliana Mason, and Alexa Bankert have applied this theory to understand partisan identities as social identities rather than merely ideological preferences. This application has been critical for understanding the emotional and identity-based nature of contemporary partisan conflict.

### 3. Political Sectarianism Framework

**Explanation:**
The authors significantly draw on the concept of political sectarianism introduced by Finkel et al. (2020), which analogizes partisan polarization to religious sectarianism. This framework identifies three psychological processes that underlie interpartisan conflict: othering, aversion, and moralization. The authors adapt and modify this framework to develop their own multidimensional model of affective polarization.

Political sectarianism represents a specific manifestation of affective polarization characterized by the confluence of these three psychological processes. This theoretical framework emphasizes that partisan animosity is not merely about negative feelings toward outgroups but involves complex psychological processes related to identity, moral conviction, and perceptions of fundamental difference.

**Direct quotes:**
"In an insightful review, Finkel et al. (2020) analogize polarization to religious sectarianism, and argue that three processes undergird interpartisan conflict: othering, aversion, and moralization. They define othering as the tendency to view opposing partisans as essentially different or alien to oneself, aversion as the tendency to dislike and distrust opposing partisans, and moralization as the tendency to view opposing partisans as iniquitous." (p.3)

"Though affective polarization could conceivably consist of a wide range of perceptions, this tripartite distinction narrows things down by focusing on core psychological aspects of intergroup differentiation frequently identified by political psychologists: the tendency to exaggerate ingroup/outgroup differences, the tendency to like outgroups less, and the tendency to see one's own orientations as being rooted in moral conviction." (p.3)

"We believe that this tripartite reconceptualization of affective polarization provides a richer depiction of the ensemble of processes that account for interpartisan animosity, and we build on key aspects of it in the development of our own model." (p.3)

**Application in the paper:**
While the authors build on the political sectarianism framework, they modify it in two important ways. First, they define each dimension more broadly as beliefs about the out-party, the in-party, and the relation between them, rather than solely as negative attitudes toward outgroups. Second, they relax the assumption that the three dimensions must co-occur to predict partisan hostility, instead treating them as related but distinct components that may independently predict various outcomes. These modifications allow the authors to develop a more nuanced understanding of how different aspects of affective polarization relate to democratic attitudes.

**Historical context and key scholars:**
The political sectarianism framework is relatively recent, introduced by Eli Finkel and colleagues in 2020. It represents an attempt to synthesize various strands of research on partisan polarization and integrate insights from social psychology, political psychology, and religious studies. This framework has quickly gained attention as a way to understand the increasingly tribal nature of American politics.

### 4. Moral Conviction Theory

**Explanation:**
The authors incorporate moral conviction theory, particularly in their conceptualization of the moralization dimension of affective polarization. This theory, developed by Linda Skitka and colleagues, proposes that attitudes held with moral conviction have unique psychological properties and behavioral consequences distinct from non-moral attitudes of similar strength. When people view their attitudes as reflective of core moral beliefs about right and wrong, they become more resistant to compromise, more intolerant of those who disagree, and more willing to reject the legitimacy of authorities who make decisions counter to those moral convictions.

The authors specifically adapt this theory to understand how partisans may view their partisan identity as reflective of moral convictions, which has important implications for democratic attitudes and behaviors. This theoretical perspective helps explain why some forms of affective polarization may be more strongly associated with anti-democratic attitudes than others.

**Direct quotes:**
"We adopt this focus based on extensive psychological research suggesting that a subjective belief that one's own convictions are rooted in morality is a critical and essential basis for several polarization-related inclinations: intolerance of and a desire to be apart from those with opposed views, heightened political engagement, refusal to compromise, and an unwillingness to defer to or accord legitimacy to institutions that produce outcomes inconsistent with one's convictions." (p.4)

"Moral conviction imparts a sense of universality and obligation to one's preferences, thereby inherently motivating a denial of the morality of opponents' positions. In this vein, past work has explored how moralized attitudes toward specific issues or policies have unique consequences for political behavior, but partisan identification itself may be heavily moralized in the sense that individuals believe that their partisan identity reflects fundamental ideas about the difference between right and wrong." (p.4)

"When partisans believe their party identity is inextricably linked to their deepest moral convictions, negotiation and compromise become equivalent to dereliction of moral duty, and partisan intolerance and abandonment of democratic processes may be seen as more justifiable." (p.5)

**Application in the paper:**
The authors use moral conviction theory to develop their moralization subscale, which measures the extent to which individuals believe their partisan identity reflects fundamental moral beliefs. Interestingly, their findings reveal that moralization has a complex relationship with democratic attitudes. While it positively relates to support for an anti-democratic inparty candidate and partisan spite, it also correlates with greater support for democratic norms in some contexts. This suggests that moralized partisan identities may have both positive and negative implications for democratic functioning.

**Historical context and key scholars:**
Moral conviction theory emerged from the work of Linda Skitka and colleagues in the early 2000s, building on earlier research on moral psychology and political attitudes. Key scholars include Linda Skitka, Christopher Bauman, and more recently, Timothy Ryan who has applied these insights to understand political compromise and polarization. This theoretical perspective has become increasingly important for understanding the moral dimensions of political conflict.

### Theoretical Insights for Understanding Far-Right Support

The multidimensional model of affective polarization presented in this paper offers several important insights for my research on how welfare/labor/social investment policies might moderate support for far-right parties.

**Othering and Far-Right Appeal:** The concept of othering—perceiving fundamental differences between groups—is particularly relevant to understanding the appeal of far-right parties. These parties often exploit perceptions of fundamental differences between "native" populations and immigrants or minority groups. The authors' finding that othering is associated with anti-democratic attitudes, albeit weakly, suggests that the psychological mechanism of perceiving fundamental group differences may contribute to the appeal of far-right rhetoric.

In my research, I could explore whether welfare policies that promote social cohesion and reduce perceptions of fundamental differences between social groups might moderate the appeal of far-right parties by reducing othering tendencies. The authors' othering subscale could be adapted to measure perceptions of fundamental differences between ethnic or national groups rather than partisan groups.

**Aversion and Support for Extreme Policies:** The finding that aversion—disliking and avoiding outgroup members—is most strongly associated with anti-democratic attitudes and support for political violence has important implications for understanding support for far-right parties. Far-right parties often propose extreme policies targeting outgroups, and aversion may be the psychological mechanism that most strongly predicts support for such policies.

For my research, this suggests exploring whether welfare/labor policies that promote positive intergroup contact could reduce aversion toward minority groups and consequently reduce support for far-right parties. The authors' aversion subscale could be adapted to measure avoidance of and negative attitudes toward immigrant or minority groups.

**Moralization and Policy Preferences:** The complex relationship between moralization and democratic attitudes shown in this study offers nuanced insights for my research. The authors find that moralization is associated with stronger political identities but has mixed relationships with anti-democratic attitudes. This suggests that the moral framing of policy issues could have both positive and negative effects on democratic attitudes.

In my research, I could explore how the moral framing of welfare policies influences their effectiveness in moderating far-right support. Policies framed as upholding moral values like fairness and care might resonate differently with voters compared to policies framed in purely economic terms. The moralization subscale could be adapted to measure the extent to which individuals view social welfare policies as reflective of moral convictions.

### Methodological Relevance

The authors' methodological approach offers valuable lessons for my research. Their development of a multidimensional scale through a rigorous process of item generation, factor analysis, and validation provides a template for developing measures of psychological mechanisms relevant to far-right support. Their finding that different subdimensions have distinct relationships with outcome variables underscores the importance of using nuanced, multidimensional measures rather than simple unidimensional indices.

For my research, this suggests developing multidimensional measures of attitudes toward welfare policies and far-right support, recognizing that different aspects of these attitudes may have distinct relationships with outcome variables. Additionally, the authors' use of experimental methods to examine heterogeneous treatment effects provides a model for testing how welfare policies might differently affect individuals with varying psychological predispositions.

### Potential Integration into My Research Framework

The authors' finding that partisanship often moderates the relationship between affective polarization and anti-democratic attitudes suggests that the effectiveness of welfare policies in reducing far-right support may similarly vary across different population segments. My research could examine whether the effectiveness of welfare policies differs based on individuals' levels of othering, aversion, and moralization toward outgroups.

Moreover, the authors' observation that "those high in moralization hold stronger political identifications and more knowledge about national politics, whereas individuals high in aversion mainly hold stronger ideological identifications yet less political knowledge" (p.15) suggests that different types of welfare policies might appeal to different psychological profiles. Policies that appeal to moral values might be more effective for individuals high in moralization, while more straightforward economic policies might better address the concerns of those high in aversion.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Affective polarization is multidimensional, not unitary:** "Our data suggest that affective polarization is not a unitary superordinate construct. Rather its subdimensions represent three distinct but related forms of affective polarization that vary in their relationship with political outcomes." (p.16) This suggests that attitudes toward outgroups (whether partisan, ethnic, or national) should be conceptualized as multidimensional rather than unidimensional.
2. **Aversion most strongly predicts anti-democratic attitudes:** "Aversion was by far the most consistent predictor of our various measures of anti-democratic attitudes, and those high in aversion were even more likely to support softer sentences for those convicted of the most violent of crimes." (p.15) This finding suggests that reducing aversion toward outgroups might be particularly important for reducing support for extreme policies.
3. **Moralization has complex, sometimes positive, relationships with democratic attitudes:** "Those high in moralization may be resistant to anti-democratic sentiments not framed specifically in terms of the achievement of partisan advantage because viewing partisan identification through a strongly moralized lens is an inherent affirmation of the current political establishment." (p.16) This suggests that moral framing of policies might sometimes promote rather than undermine democratic attitudes.
4. **The relationship between polarization and anti-democratic attitudes is not consistently moderated by partisanship:** "In most cases, the effects of the full APS and the subscales were similar among Republicans and Democrats. When there were differences, Republicans high in affective polarization tended to be more inclined to endorse anti-democratic attitudes." (p.12) This finding challenges simplistic narratives about partisan asymmetry in anti-democratic attitudes.
5. **Different dimensions of polarization relate differently to political knowledge:** "Othering was unrelated to knowledge in either sample, aversion was negatively associated with knowledge in both samples, and moralization was positively associated with knowledge in both samples." (p.9) This suggests that interventions targeting different psychological mechanisms might need to be tailored to individuals with different levels of political knowledge.
