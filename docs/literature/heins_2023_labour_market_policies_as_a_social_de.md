## 2. Theoretical Framework: Pathways Between Labor Market Policies and Wellbeing

### 2.2 Material Pathways

Material pathways focus on the economic impacts of policies and how material circumstances directly affect health and wellbeing:

> "Material pathways focus on the economic impact of policies and the direct effect of material circumstances, such as income loss, poverty, job (in)security and other positive or adverse employment conditions, on individual health and wellbeing." (p. 509)
> 

These pathways are directly related to the manifest functions of employment, particularly income and other material benefits derived from employment contracts.

### 2.3 Psychosocial Pathways

Psychosocial pathways examine how social stressors and lived experiences affect physiological changes that influence mental health and wellbeing:

> "Psychosocial pathways refer to the links between social stressors – people's lived experiences, such as their perceived social status, autonomy, control, social isolation, stigma, feelings of self-worth and shame – and physiological changes which in turn have an influence on wellbeing and mental health." (p. 509)
> 

The author connects these pathways to influential theories of unemployment:

1. **Jahoda's Latent Deprivation Model**: This model suggests that unemployment negatively impacts wellbeing not only through loss of income (manifest function) but also through the loss of five latent functions of employment: time structure, social contacts, collective goals, status/identity, and regular activity.
2. **Fryer's Agency Restriction Model**: This model challenges Jahoda's view of unemployed individuals as passive actors, arguing that unemployment harms wellbeing by constraining personal agency, autonomy, and planning abilities.
3. **Ezzy's Concept of Status Passage**: This approach emphasizes how the subjective interpretations people assign to unemployment affect wellbeing. Unemployment is socially constructed as negative through the meanings attached to it and the low status conferred upon unemployed individuals.

> "Unemployment as such is not inherently unpleasant but it is socially constructed as negative by the meanings attached to it and the low status conferred to the unemployed." (p. 509)
> 

The author notes that in advanced democracies, psychosocial explanations focusing on relative deprivation may be more important than material explanations centered on absolute deprivation, though both remain relevant.

## 3. Theoretical Connections Between Labor Market Policies and Wellbeing

The paper hypothesizes several mechanisms linking specific labor market policies to wellbeing outcomes:

### 3.1 Unemployment Benefits

**Material pathway**: Unemployment benefits compensate for income loss during unemployment, potentially easing financial worries depending on their generosity.

**Psychosocial pathway**: Benefit receipt, particularly means-tested benefits, can be stigmatizing, leading to feelings of shame, loss of status, and deterioration of mental health.

> "Unemployment benefits compensate for some of the income loss in case of involuntary unemployment and thereby somewhat ease financial worries, depending on their generosity. They are thus assumed to alleviate the economic impact of job loss via material pathways. However, benefit receipt, particularly of means-tested benefits, is often stigmatising for recipients, potentially leading to feelings of shame, loss of status, worthlessness and a deterioration of mental health and might thus exert a negative impact on wellbeing via psychosocial factors." (p. 510)
> 

### 3.2 Active Labor Market Programs (ALMPs)

ALMPs aim to reintegrate unemployed individuals into the labor market and are expected to positively impact wellbeing by:

- Shortening unemployment duration
- Replacing employment as a source of social contacts
- Providing purpose and routine
- Offering opportunities to use and improve skills
- Preventing social isolation

> "ALMPs can furthermore replace employment as a source of social contacts and could accordingly play a mediating role in addressing psychosocial factors of unemployment by giving some purpose and routine to job seekers, offering the chance to utilise and improve skills and avoiding social isolation." (p. 510)
> 

The author distinguishes between different types of ALMPs:

- **"Enabling" interventions**: Focused on upskilling or re-skilling through education and training, hypothesized to positively impact wellbeing by increasing human and social capital.
- **"Workfare" measures**: Including benefit sanctions, expected to diminish beneficial effects and potentially worsen mental health by reducing control and autonomy.

### 3.3 Employment Protection Legislation (EPL)

**Weak EPL**: Likely to increase objective and subjective job insecurity, leading to precarious forms of employment with adverse effects on wellbeing through:

- More frequent experiences of unemployment
- Greater uncertainty about future work and income
- Lower control over work processes
- Higher stress levels
- Exclusion from workplace entitlements, potentially conferring lower social status

**Strong EPL**: Should reduce both objective risks and subjective feelings of job insecurity, benefiting mental health and wellbeing.

> "Strict EPL should reduce both objective risks and subjective feelings of job insecurity, and thus be beneficial for mental health and wellbeing." (p. 511)
> 

However, the author notes a potential complication when high EPL levels lead to labor market segmentation or dualization, where "insiders" enjoy high protection while "outsiders" experience high unemployment risks and insecure employment.

## 4. Empirical Evidence: Unemployment Benefits and Wellbeing

### 4.1 Life Satisfaction Outcomes

The majority of research confirms that unemployment benefits buffer the negative impact of unemployment on wellbeing measures:

- A meta-analysis found that in countries with low or medium unemployment protection, the negative effect of unemployment was significantly larger than in countries with high protection levels (Paul and Moser, 2009).
- Ochsen and Welsch (2012) found that higher unemployment benefit replacement rates increased life satisfaction not only for unemployed individuals but also for the general population, indicating spillover effects.
- Wulfgramm (2014) and Voßemer et al. (2018) found that generous unemployment benefits moderate the negative effect of unemployment on life satisfaction.

> "Ochsen and Welsch (2012), based on individual-level data from ten countries for the period 1975–2002, found that a higher unemployment benefit replacement rate increased the life satisfaction of the average citizen, i.e. unemployment benefit generosity is not only positive for the unemployed but has also spillover effects for employed individuals." (p. 511)
> 

However, not all studies have confirmed these positive effects:

- Eichhorn (2014) concluded that unemployment benefit generosity did not significantly reduce the negative impact of unemployment on life satisfaction.
- Nordheim and Martinussen (2020) found that while some social expenditure (health, incapacity, families, housing) increased life satisfaction, spending on unemployment reduced life satisfaction when controlling for unemployment levels.

### 4.2 Suicide Outcomes

Several studies have examined the relationship between unemployment benefits and suicide rates:

- Breuer and Rottmann (2014) estimated that a 10% increase in unemployment benefit generosity decreased male suicide rates by more than 2%.
- Cylus et al. (2014) found that generous unemployment benefits offset the impact of unemployment rates on suicide, though effects were small.
- Studies examining the Great Recession yielded mixed results: while Reeves et al. (2015) found no moderating effect, Norström and Grönqvist (2015) did find such an effect across 30 countries.

> "Cylus et al. (2014), using US state-level data, suggested that the impact of unemployment rates on suicide is offset by the presence of generous unemployment benefits, though estimated effects were small in magnitude." (p. 512)
> 

The author notes that methodological differences may explain the varied findings, including different control variables, country samples, measurement approaches, and aggregation levels.

## 5. Empirical Evidence: Benefit Sanctions and Conditionality

The evidence on benefit sanctions and welfare conditionality consistently indicates negative effects on wellbeing:

- Williams (2021a) demonstrated that unemployment benefit sanctions led to increases in self-reported anxiety and depression using English panel data from 2010-2014.
- Williams (2021b) found that increased benefit sanctions correlated with increased anti-depressant prescriptions.
- A longitudinal qualitative study showed that benefit claimants with pre-existing mental health conditions experienced worsening mental health when benefit conditionality and sanctions were imposed (Dwyer et al., 2020).

> "Williams (2021a) provided robust evidence using English panel data for the period 2010–2014 (during which the frequency and severity of benefit sanctions intensified markedly in the United Kingdom) that unemployment benefit sanctions lead to increases in self-reported anxiety and depression." (p. 512)
> 

These negative effects were particularly pronounced for vulnerable groups:

- Single parents experienced especially detrimental wellbeing effects (Davis, 2019; Katikireddi, 2018).
- Barr et al. (2016) found that the UK Work Capability Assessment program was associated with increased suicides, self-reported mental health problems, and antidepressant prescriptions, with the greatest increases in the most deprived areas.

> "Barr et al. (2016) observed that the UK Work Capability Assessment programme (introduced in 2008 to assess eligibility for a benefit paid to people who have an illness, health condition or disability) was associated with an increase in suicides, self-reported mental health problems and antidepressant prescriptions. The greatest increases in these adverse health outcomes were found in the most deprived areas of England, thus widening health inequalities and challenging the claim that work is the best form of social inclusion for all types of out-of-work claimants." (p. 513)
> 

## 6. Empirical Evidence: Active Labor Market Programs (ALMPs)

Research on ALMPs shows mixed effects on health and wellbeing indicators:

### 6.1 Positive Impacts

Reviews by Coutts (2009) and Haw et al. (2015) identified several positive health impacts of ALMPs:

- Reductions in psychological distress, depression, and anxiety
- Increased subjective wellbeing
- Higher levels of control
- Improved motivation and self-esteem
- Reduced stigma and improved support

> "The positive health impacts identified in randomised control trials included reductions in psychological distress; depression and anxiety; increased subjective wellbeing; higher levels of control; improvements in motivation and self-esteem through feeling needed; having a meaningful activity; and experiencing less stigma and improved support." (p. 513)
> 

These positive effects were stronger when programs were oriented toward training and skills enhancement.

### 6.2 Negative Impacts

Studies also reported negative outcomes when:

- Programs were perceived as inadequate alternatives to regular work
- Participants felt exploited or experienced reduced control
- Low-paid and insecure employment exacerbated financial strain

### 6.3 Program Design Matters

The effectiveness of ALMPs depends on program design and participants' perceptions:

- Wulfgramm (2011) found that participants in Germany's "One-Euro-Jobs" program reported higher life satisfaction compared to unemployed welfare recipients, particularly when the measure matched their skills and seemed to increase future employment chances. This effect disappeared when participants perceived the program as degrading or only participated to avoid benefit cuts.
- Rose (2019) found that different types of ALMPs had varying impacts: wage subsidies and subsidized self-employment (providing actual work experience) significantly enhanced wellbeing, while training programs showed smaller increases.

> "Recipients of wage subsidies and those in subsidised self-employment – who are thus gaining actual work experience – show significantly enhanced wellbeing while those in training programmes – which in theory should increase future job prospects but in practice might not – show a smaller increase." (p. 514)
> 
- Sage (2015a, 2015b) found that work-oriented ALMPs focusing on work experience or training were more effective than employment-assistance ALMPs centered on intensified jobseeker advice.
- Wang et al. (2021) found that the benefits of ALMPs varied by gender, with unemployed women deriving similar mental health benefits from ALMPs compared to employment, while unemployed men benefited more from formal employment, especially high-quality jobs.

## 7. Empirical Evidence: Employment Protection Legislation (EPL)

Research on EPL and wellbeing shows mixed results:

### 7.1 Job Security and Mental Health

- Butterworth et al. (2013) found that work of poor psychosocial quality (characterized by low control, high demands, insecurity, low esteem) had similarly detrimental effects on common mental health disorders as unemployment.
- However, Caroli and Godard (2016) did not confirm the health-damaging effect of job insecurity on common mental health problems when controlling for potential biases.

> "Butterworth et al. (2013) found that work of poor psychosocial quality (characterised by low control, high demands, insecurity, low esteem) has similarly detrimental effects on common mental health disorders as unemployment." (p. 514-515)
> 

### 7.2 EPL and Subjective Wellbeing

- Ochsen and Welsch (2012) found that higher levels of employment protection increased life satisfaction of the average citizen.
- Voßemer et al. (2018) showed that in countries with high EPL for regular workers, easing restrictions on temporary employment increased the negative effects of unemployment on both life satisfaction and health.
- Russell et al. (2020) concluded that conventional EPL indicators had little influence on young people's wellbeing, but subjective job insecurity across the workforce was associated with reduced life satisfaction for both youths and adults.

### 7.3 EPL and Suicide

- Kim and Cho (2017) showed that decreased EPL was associated with increased suicide rates in 20 OECD countries (1994-2010).
- Kim et al. (2017) found that perceived job insecurity was associated with suicide ideation in Korea (2012-2015).
- Antonakakis and Collins (2015) found that high EPL for regular workers in Southern Eurozone periphery countries mitigated the effects of austerity on suicide mortality, particularly among older men.
- In contrast, Breuer and Rottmann (2014) found that strict EPL was positively associated with suicide mortality, potentially due to adverse effects on labor market outsiders.

> "In contrast, Breuer and Rottmann (2014) found that relatively strict EPL was positively associated with suicide mortality, potentially due to adverse effects on labour market outsiders who typically struggle to find a job in highly regulated labour markets which offer little flexibility for employers to hire and dismiss staff." (p. 515)
> 

## 8. Discussion: Key Insights and Policy Implications

### 8.1 Methodological Limitations

The author acknowledges several methodological limitations in the existing research:

- Labor market policies (benefit generosity, ALMP, EPL) are often operationalized crudely.
- Findings vary by age group, gender, and region.
- Individual-level studies with robust control group designs often have low participant numbers.
- Ecological studies correlating country-level policies with wellbeing outcomes cannot establish causality at the individual level.

> "Further methodological limitations include that studies using individual-level data and employing a more robust control group design were often hampered by low participant numbers while ecological studies correlating wellbeing outcomes in countries with varying social protection spending and other macro-level labour market features cannot establish if outcomes at the individual level were ultimately caused by the features of labour market policies in the respective country or were due to potentially unobserved confounding factors." (p. 515)
> 

### 8.2 Unemployment Benefits: Mixed Evidence and Entitlement Principles

While generous unemployment benefits often mitigate the negative impacts of unemployment on wellbeing (for both unemployed and employed individuals), the evidence is not conclusive. The author suggests that the entitlement principles underlying benefits might explain the divergent findings:

> "Given the known detrimental impact of stigmatisation on wellbeing, we can assume that benefits based on need and subject to potentially humiliating means-testing will have more negative effects on wellbeing via psychosocial pathways even though benefits may alleviate financial worries somewhat. In contrast, entitlement rights to unemployment benefits derived from social insurance contributions should have a much less stigmatising effect." (p. 516)
> 

This represents an important area for future research.

### 8.3 ALMPs: Program Design is Crucial

The effects of ALMPs on wellbeing are mixed, but the author identifies a pattern:

- "Enabling" types of ALMP focused on human capital development and programs that emulate regular employment tend to have wellbeing-enhancing effects.
- "Employment assistance" types of ALMP or programs that don't account for personal circumstances often have no or negative wellbeing effects.

> "While 'enabling' types of ALMP focusing on human capital development and programmes that emulate regular employment seem to have mainly wellbeing-enhancing effects, 'employment assistance' types of ALMP or those that do not take into account personal circumstances such as lone parenthood have no or even negative wellbeing effects." (p. 516)
> 

### 8.4 Benefit Sanctions and Welfare Conditionality: Consistent Negative Effects

The evidence on benefit sanctions and welfare conditionality is more unanimous, showing clear negative effects on wellbeing:

> "The increased stigmatisation of the unemployed in a punitive activation context might be creating a 'culture of self-blame' that could lead to even more stress in the life of ALMP participants." (p. 516)
> 

The author criticizes approaches that interpret unemployment as a result of having the "wrong attitude" while ignoring structural factors:

> "Friedli and Stearn (2015) criticised the focus on 'positive psychology' approaches in many 'work first' programmes which interpret unemployment as a result of having the 'wrong attitude' towards work while ignoring the structural context of depressed labour markets." (p. 516)
> 

### 8.5 EPL and Labor Market Dualization

The evidence on EPL is inconclusive, which the author attributes to the phenomenon of labor market dualization:

> "This might be explained by the fact that high EPL can lead to dualised labour markets (Emmenegger et al., 2012) in which so-called labour market 'insiders' enjoy high levels of protection, but those at the fringes of the labour market suffer from high levels of unemployment and insecure employment, which are both bad for health." (p. 517)
> 

### 8.6 Welfare State Regimes and Flexicurity

The author discusses how different welfare state types impact wellbeing outcomes:

- Liberal welfare states (combining flexible labor markets with modest benefits) generally lead to poor wellbeing outcomes.
- Social democratic welfare states show smaller relationships between work stress and depressive symptoms.

The Danish "flexicurity" model—combining flexible labor markets with generous unemployment benefits and supported by ALMP—may diminish adverse health effects of job loss:

> "The flexicurity principle may diminish adverse health effects of job loss since relatively generous UB are supplemented by skills training to promote employability and mobility." (p. 518)
> 

However, flexicurity is "far from a 'magic bullet'" as it appears to fail low-educated people with longstanding illnesses. Instead, generous unemployment benefits combined with ALMPs and strong EPL yield better outcomes for vulnerable groups.

## 9. Conclusions and Policy Recommendations

### 9.1 Quality of Employment Matters

The author emphasizes that the quality of employment, not just quantity, is crucial for wellbeing:

> "The report concluded that '[g]etting people off benefits and into low-paid, insecure and health-damaging work is not a desirable option' (Marmot, 2010)." (p. 518)
> 

### 9.2 Policy Recommendations

Based on the reviewed evidence, the author makes several policy recommendations:

1. **Adequate unemployment benefits** that enable healthy living.
2. **Enabling and skill-enhancing ALMPs** that lead to sustainable, good-quality employment rather than serving merely as conditions for benefit receipt.
3. **Consideration of job quality** alongside job quantity.
4. **Avoiding stigmatization** of benefit claimants and the discursive presentation of unemployment as a "lifestyle" or individual failure.
5. **Increased variety and choice** for unemployed individuals, recognizing that not any type of job or ALMP program benefits wellbeing.

> "The findings from research on the wellbeing effects of ALMP indicate that enabling and skill-enhancing programmes leading to sustainable good quality employment, rather than serving as a condition for benefit receipt without any prospect of proper labour market integration are the way forward for a progressive labour market policy that takes wellbeing into account." (p. 518)
> 

In conclusion, this paper provides valuable insights into how labor market policies affect wellbeing through both material and psychosocial pathways. It highlights the importance of considering wellbeing outcomes alongside traditional economic metrics when designing and evaluating these policies, particularly for vulnerable populations. Future research should focus on clarifying causal mechanisms, examining policy interactions, and exploring how these relationships might evolve in changing economic and social contexts.
