## Introduction and Overview

This paper by Arno Van Hootegem, Koen Abts, and Bart Meuleman (2021) published in Acta Sociologica addresses a paradoxical finding in welfare state research: socioeconomically vulnerable groups, who objectively benefit most from welfare state arrangements, often express stronger criticism of these same welfare institutions. The authors seek to resolve this paradox by introducing a social psychological framework centered on the concept of resentment, arguing that welfare criticism among lower socioeconomic groups stems from deeper experiences of relative group deprivation and social distrust rather than mere self-interest or ideological inconsistency.

The authors frame this criticism within a "welfare populist" perspective that pits "hard-working people against the deceitful elite and welfare abusers." This framework helps explain the seemingly contradictory finding that lower socioeconomic groups simultaneously support redistribution while criticizing welfare state performance.

As the authors state:

> "This article aims to explain the paradoxical finding that socio-economically vulnerable groups express more economic, moral and social criticism of the welfare state. As these groups generally benefit more from the welfare state and hold more egalitarian world views, their stronger criticism cannot be explained by the traditional frameworks of self-interest and ideology." (p. 125)
> 

The study uses structural equation modeling on data from the Belgian National Election Study 2014 to test whether social experiences of resentment mediate the relationship between socioeconomic position and welfare state criticism.

## Theoretical Framework

### The Paradox of Welfare Criticism

The authors begin by establishing the paradox that motivates their research: individuals with lower socioeconomic positions tend to be more critical of welfare state performance despite benefiting more from it and generally holding more egalitarian views. This cannot be adequately explained by traditional frameworks of self-interest or ideology.

> "Multiple studies report that while individuals in socio-economically vulnerable positions are more supportive of social redistribution (Achterberg and Houtman, 2009; Achterberg et al., 2011; Jaeger, 2006), they appear at the same time less convinced that the welfare state is capable of achieving just economic, moral and social outcomes (van Oorschot, 2010; van Oorschot et al., 2012)." (p. 126)
> 

The authors argue that existing theoretical frameworks fall short in explaining this paradox:

> "The two most recurring explanations for welfare attitudes – self-interest and ideology (Jaeger, 2006; Staerklé et al., 2012; van Oorschot et al., 2012) – expect groups in lower positions to be less critical, as these groups tend to have a greater interest in social protection and traditionally adhere to more leftist worldviews. From these theoretical vantage points, their welfare criticism appears to be irrational or ideologically inconsistent (cf. Converse, 2006; Middendorp, 1978)." (p. 126)
> 

### Types of Welfare State Criticism

The authors identify three distinct forms of welfare state criticism:

1. **Economic criticism**: Focuses on the economic inefficiency and fiscal burden of the welfare state from a neoliberal and anti-establishment perspective.

> "The alleged economic consequences correspond to a neoliberal and anti-establishment criticism of the welfare state, which focuses on how taxpayer money is misused and how redistribution distorts the functioning of the free market (cf. McCluskey, 2003)." (p. 127)
> 
1. **Moral criticism**: Centers on the alleged moral failures of the welfare state from a conservative perspective, especially regarding dependency and work ethic.

> "Second, moral critics adopt a conservative perspective that directs attention to the so-called 'moral hazards or failures' or the welfare state, which blames the institution of establishing a dependency culture and undermining the work ethic (cf. Crepaz, 2008; Likki and Staerklé 2015; Murray 1984)." (p. 127)
> 
1. **Social criticism**: Questions the welfare state's ability to achieve equal and fair outcomes, such as reducing poverty and guaranteeing citizens' well-being.

> "Third, the criticism in the social domain questions the ability of the welfare state to achieve equal and fair outcomes, such as reducing poverty and guaranteeing the well-being of all citizens. In this regard, the welfare state has not realized its potential for abolishing inequalities and social problems, as it is argued to be ineffective, inefficient and wasteful (Dwyer, 2000)." (p. 127)
> 

### Welfare Populism

The authors introduce the concept of "welfare populism" as a framework to understand the criticism of the welfare state by lower socioeconomic groups. Welfare populism is characterized by:

1. A critique that pits the "hard-working common people" against elites and welfare abusers
2. A belief that the welfare state has abandoned deserving groups while benefiting undeserving ones
3. Anti-establishment rhetoric coupled with moral arguments about distributive justice

> "By emphasizing the unintended dysfunctions and purported unfairness of the distributive system, welfare populism accuses the welfare state of abandoning the 'common people' who are in real need. In line with the social welfare criticism, the lower socio-economic groups blame the welfare state, in the aftermath of the breakdown of pre-modern stratified orders, of not guaranteeing the social inclusion and participation of all citizens into society (Bommes, 2012, p. 85)." (p. 128)
> 

Importantly, the authors stress that welfare populism does not entail opposition to redistribution itself:

> "As such, the concept of welfare populism stresses the common denominator of economic, moral and social criticisms, rather than treating them as completely isolated dimensions. Importantly, the welfare populist criticism on the welfare state does not imply less support for redistribution (Achterberg et al., 2011; Houtman et al., 2008). On the contrary, welfare populists criticize the welfare state precisely because it has not been able to achieve equality and rightly targeted redistribution." (p. 128)
> 

### Modernization Losers Theory

To explain why certain groups are more attracted to welfare populism, the authors draw on the "losers of modernization" thesis. This theory posits that processes of modernization (globalization and individualization) have fundamentally reshuffled social group positions, creating winners and losers.

> "The losers of modernization thesis offer levers to understand why particular groups are more attracted to welfare populism. This theory argues that processes of modernization – i.e. globalization and individualization – have reshuffled the status positions of social groups profoundly over the last decades (Betz, 1994). While the winners of modernization with high economic and cultural capital profit from flexibility and globalization, lower positioned groups see their life chances reduced by the effects of globalization and face new existential and economic insecurities (Kriesi et al., 2006; Teney et al., 2014)." (p. 128)
> 

The authors argue that these "losers of globalization" become attracted to populist discourses:

> "The so-called losers of globalization, such as the lower educated, blue collar workers and low-income groups, feel deprived of opportunities, and become attracted to a populist rhetoric (Betz, 1994; Kriesi et al., 2006). These populist discourses are appealing because they provide meaning to disadvantaged positions by blaming others – both the responsible establishment and the guilty deviants – for increasing social malaise (cf. Derks, 2004; Abts, 2012)." (p. 128-129)
> 

### Social Experiences of Resentment

The authors identify three types of social experiences of resentment that may explain the welfare criticism of modernization losers:

1. **Economic status insecurity**: The fear of losing established living standards and position within economic hierarchy

> "First, we conceptualize economic status insecurity as the fear of losing established living standards and the position within the hierarchy of economic prestige (Castel, 2003). In contrast to economic precarity as such, which might in itself especially stimulate support for government intervention, status insecurity is part of a larger sense of loss of hope and solidarity that goes hand in hand with frustration and bitterness towards the social and political system (Hoggett et al., 2013)." (p. 129)
> 
1. **Group relative deprivation**: The perception that one's ingroup is disadvantaged compared to other groups

> "Second, group relative deprivation is anticipated to play a key role in understanding welfare criticism. Group relative deprivation entails a judgement that one's ingroup is disadvantaged compared to other groups in society, which results in experiences of disadvantageous group positions and eventually in radical frustration and anger (Smith et al., 2012)." (p. 129)
> 
1. **Social distrust**: A general lack of trust in others' intentions and belief that others are guided by self-interest

> "A third relevant experience of resentment is generalized distrust towards others. The concept of social distrust refers to the general belief that others have bad intentions and are guided by self-interest, making deception and abuse plausible (Baier, 1994; Govier, 1997; Luhmann, 1979)." (p. 129)
> 

The authors posit that these experiences mediate the relationship between socioeconomic position and welfare criticism, leading to the following hypotheses:

> "We anticipate that the experience of economic status insecurity (Hypothesis 1), group relative deprivation (Hypothesis 2) and social distrust (Hypothesis 3) are positively related to economic, moral and social criticism of the welfare state. Furthermore, the negative relationship between socio-economic position and welfare state criticism is expected to be mediated by economic status insecurity, group relative deprivation and social distrust (Hypothesis 4)." (p. 130)
> 

## Methodology

### Data Source

The study uses data from the Belgian National Elections Study 2014, with a sample of 1,824 Belgian voters (after excluding cases with missing values). The sample had a response rate of 47.5%, and post-stratification weights for gender, age, and education were applied to adjust for non-response bias.

### Measurement of Variables

### Dependent Variables: Welfare Criticism

Three forms of welfare state criticism are measured using multiple five-point Likert-type items:

1. **Economic criticism**: Three items assessing opinions on the cost of the welfare state, its impact on economic productivity, and the free market as an alternative
2. **Moral criticism**: Three items examining attitudes toward the effect of the welfare state on work ethic, responsibility, and welfare dependency
3. **Social criticism**: Three items measuring whether the welfare state is believed to guarantee welfare for citizens, decrease poverty, and grant equal access to healthcare

The authors used Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) to establish the measurement quality and dimensionality of this instrument, confirming a three-factor model.

### Independent Variables: Socioeconomic Status

Socioeconomic status is measured through four variables:

- Education (three categories: lower/secondary, higher secondary, tertiary)
- Occupation (coded according to Erikson-Goldthrope-Portocarero class scheme)
- Income (net equivalized household income, divided into quartiles)
- Welfare dependency (whether the respondent or household member received welfare benefits)

Additionally, the authors control for:

- Political ideology (left-right placement on a scale from 0 to 10)
- Age
- Gender
- Region (French-speaking Belgium vs. Flanders)

### Mediating Variables: Social Experiences

The three social experiences are operationalized as latent variables measured by multiple five-point agree-disagree statements:

1. **Economic status insecurity**: Three items about future economic worries
2. **Group relative deprivation**: Three items evaluating whether individuals feel their group is treated unfairly compared to others
3. **Social distrust**: Two items referring to potential bad intentions of others

### Analytical Strategy

The authors use structural equation modeling (SEM) to estimate the mediation effects between social structural variables and welfare state criticism. They employ bootstrapping (1000 replications) to ensure valid statistical inference for direct and indirect effects. The fit indices for the structural model show adequate fit (SRMR = 0.023; RMSEA = 0.030).

## Results

### Social Structure and Resentment

The results confirm that individuals with lower socioeconomic status report higher levels of resentment across all three dimensions:

> "The lower the level of education, the more experiences of economic status insecurity as well as group relative deprivation, and the stronger the social distrust. Those in the highest income quartile score significantly lower on all three dimensions compared to those in the lowest income quartile." (p. 134)
> 

Occupational differences showed that:

> "The coefficients for the occupational classes reveal that blue collar workers and the self-employed are less trusting and feel more relatively deprived than white collar workers, the service class and inactive individuals." (p. 134)
> 

The authors also found that benefit recipients experience higher levels of all three forms of resentment:

> "Benefit recipients perceive that they are relatively disadvantaged, expect difficulties maintaining their economic status and are less trustful compared to individuals who do not receive benefits." (p. 134)
> 

### Resentment and Welfare Criticism

The analysis revealed group relative deprivation as the most consistent predictor of all forms of welfare criticism:

> "For each of the forms of welfare criticism, group relative deprivation is a strong and consistent predictor. In accordance with hypothesis 2, those who feel that their group is systematically disadvantaged in comparison to other groups score higher on economic, moral as well as social criticism." (p. 134)
> 

The impact of social distrust was limited to moral criticism:

> "Social distrust, finally, impacts moral criticism (b = 0.15) but has no significant effect on economic and social criticism. A lack of trust in the generalized other heightens the moral concerns that the welfare state erodes individual responsibility and stimulates passivity but does not affect economic and social criticisms (thus confirming hypothesis 3 only partly)." (p. 134)
> 

Contrary to expectations, economic status insecurity did not significantly impact welfare criticism:

> "Individual economic status insecurities do not have a significant impact on the three forms of welfare state criticism (which disconfirms hypothesis 1). Overall, economic status insecurity, as a fear of loss in the near future, does not necessarily lead to a more critical stance towards the welfare state." (p. 134)
> 

### Mediation Effects

The authors found significant mediation effects, particularly through group relative deprivation:

> "The lower educated are, through their experience of group relative deprivation, indirectly more critical about economic, moral and social aspects of the welfare state. The belief that they are structurally disadvantaged and that principles of fairness have been violated, leads the lower educated to criticize the welfare state and disregard its social merits." (p. 136)
> 

Similar indirect effects were found for occupation, income, and welfare dependency:

> "Blue collar workers, those with a low income and benefit claimants are all more critical of the welfare state through their senses of relative injustice at the group level." (p. 136)
> 

This mediating role of group experiences supports the authors' theoretical framework:

> "These findings confirm the importance of one particular social experience – group relative deprivation - in explaining the welfare state criticism of the losers of modernization." (p. 136)
> 

### Direct vs. Indirect Effects

The authors found an interesting pattern of opposing direct and indirect effects:

> "The duality between direct effects of self-interest and indirect effects of relative deprivation is crucial in understanding welfare state criticism of lower social groups. When only total effects are investigated, misleading conclusions could be drawn, as they do not provide insight into these patterns." (p. 136)
> 

Individuals in vulnerable positions showed indirect effects of greater criticism through resentment, but direct effects of less criticism due to self-interest:

> "These results indicate that while individuals in a vulnerable position are indirectly more critical of the welfare state, they are directly often more positive, as they benefit from extensive redistribution and a strong welfare state." (p. 136)
> 

## Discussion and Conclusions

The authors successfully demonstrate that the welfare criticism of lower socioeconomic groups is not irrational or ideologically inconsistent but can be explained through a framework of social experiences of resentment:

> "Instead of reducing attitudes that do not fit in traditional frameworks to irrationality, value incoherence or ideological inconsistency (Converse, 2006; Middendorp, 1978), our research stresses the need to uncover the underlying structures of these paradoxical relationships." (p. 137)
> 

Group-based deprivation emerged as the most important factor:

> "Group-based deprivation turns out to be far more relevant to understand welfare criticism, as it systematically strengthens economic, moral and social criticisms. Group comparisons and the resulting perceived disadvantage instigate views that the welfare state harms the economy, creates a dependency culture and fails to eliminate social inequalities." (p. 137)
> 

The authors conclude that welfare criticism from lower socioeconomic groups should be understood within a welfare populist framework:

> "Instead, this paper argued that their welfare criticism takes shape within a wider welfare populist framework that opposes the hard-working 'common people' to the establishment and the unproductive, immoral and undeserving classes of society. In accordance with the 'politics of resentment', we argued that a welfare populist discourse is instigated by social experiences of resentment, which arise through a restructuring of relative group positions in the wake of modernization." (p. 137)
> 

## Theoretical Implications and Contributions

### Reconceptualizing Welfare Attitudes

This paper makes several important theoretical contributions to welfare attitude research:

1. It challenges dominant explanatory frameworks of self-interest and ideology by introducing social psychological mechanisms of resentment
2. It explains a paradoxical finding that previously appeared as irrational or inconsistent behavior
3. It bridges welfare attitude research with theoretical perspectives on populism and modernization, offering a more comprehensive understanding of welfare criticism
4. It emphasizes the importance of group-based experiences over individual self-interest, suggesting welfare attitudes are fundamentally intergroup phenomena

### Variables, Measurement, and Operationalization

The study's operationalization of variables demonstrates several strengths:

1. **Multidimensional welfare criticism**: By measuring economic, moral, and social criticisms separately, the authors capture the complex and sometimes contradictory nature of welfare attitudes.
2. **Multiple indicators of socioeconomic position**: Rather than relying on a single measure of socioeconomic status, the authors use education, occupation, income, and welfare dependency, providing a more comprehensive picture.
3. **Latent variables for social experiences**: The use of multiple indicators for each type of resentment helps ensure these abstract concepts are measured reliably.
4. **Confirmatory approach**: The use of CFA to validate measurement models adds rigor to the operationalization of key concepts.

### Connections to Broader Theoretical Frameworks

The paper connects to several broader theoretical frameworks:

1. **Modernization theory**: The paper draws on theories of modernization, particularly how globalization creates winners and losers, linking individual attitudes to macro-level social change.
2. **Populism research**: The study connects welfare criticism to populist politics, showing how resentment can fuel populist rhetoric that pits "the people" against both elites and "undeserving" groups.
3. **Social psychological theories of intergroup relations**: By emphasizing group relative deprivation, the paper connects to social identity theory and theories of intergroup conflict.
4. **Political psychology**: The findings relate to work on how emotions and perceptions of fairness influence political attitudes and behavior.
5. **Welfare state research**: The study contributes to understanding welfare state legitimacy and the social psychological foundations of welfare attitudes.

## Social Psychological Mechanisms Explaining Welfare Policy Support

The paper offers several important insights into the social psychological mechanisms that might explain support for or criticism of welfare/labor policies:

### Group Relative Deprivation as a Key Mechanism

The strongest finding of the study is that group relative deprivation mediates the relationship between socioeconomic position and welfare criticism:

> "Group comparisons and the resulting perceived disadvantage instigate views that the welfare state harms the economy, creates a dependency culture and fails to eliminate social inequalities. Feelings of deprivation at the group level apparently instil the idea that the welfare state has let the hard-working people down and instead operates in advantage of the immigrants, 'welfare scroungers' and the corrupt elite, who serve as points of comparisons." (p. 134)
> 

This suggests that when people perceive their group as disadvantaged compared to others, they may develop resentment toward welfare institutions they see as maintaining or exacerbating these inequalities. This is particularly relevant for understanding how welfare policies might affect support for far-right parties, as these parties often frame themselves as defenders of the "forgotten" working class against elites and immigrants.

### The Dual Effect of Self-Interest and Group-Based Resentment

A fascinating finding is the dual effect of socioeconomic position on welfare criticism:

> "For multiple variables these direct effects ran counter to the indirect effects, illustrating the distinct importance of self-interest, ideology and social experiences. Directly, lower social groups are thus often less critical, but indirectly lower social groups express more criticism through their group deprivation and distrust." (p. 136)
> 

This suggests welfare policies face a complex landscape of attitudes: policies that appeal to self-interest (e.g., increased benefits) might simultaneously trigger resentment if perceived as unfairly benefiting other groups. This helps explain why welfare reforms can sometimes increase rather than decrease political polarization.

### The Role of Trust in Welfare State Legitimacy

The study finds that social distrust specifically affects moral criticism of the welfare state:

> "A lack of trust in the generalized other heightens the moral concerns that the welfare state erodes individual responsibility and stimulates passivity but does not affect economic and social criticisms." (p. 134)
> 

This suggests that policy interventions aimed at increasing social trust might help reduce moral criticism of the welfare state. Welfare policies that emphasize reciprocity and monitoring might address concerns about moral hazard and reduce welfare criticism based on social distrust.

### Economic Insecurity vs. Group-Based Concerns

Interestingly, individual economic insecurity did not significantly impact welfare criticism:

> "That economic status insecurities do not predict welfare criticism implies that welfare state criticism should not be understood in terms of purely egocentric considerations. Instead, it is a fundamentally group-based phenomenon." (p. 134)
> 

This finding challenges assumptions that economic insecurity directly drives political attitudes and suggests that how people interpret their economic circumstances in a group context may be more important than their individual economic concerns.

### Welfare Criticism Without Opposing Redistribution

Perhaps most importantly for understanding the relationship between welfare policies and political preferences, the authors emphasize that welfare criticism does not imply opposition to redistribution:

> "Importantly, the welfare populist criticism on the welfare state does not imply less support for redistribution (Achterberg et al., 2011; Houtman et al., 2008). On the contrary, welfare populists criticize the welfare state precisely because it has not been able to achieve equality and rightly targeted redistribution." (p. 128)
> 

This suggests that supporters of far-right parties may not be opposed to welfare spending per se, but rather to how welfare resources are allocated. Welfare policies that are perceived as benefiting the "right" recipients might therefore reduce support for far-right parties, while those perceived as benefiting "undeserving" groups might increase such support.

## Conclusion

This comprehensive analysis of "The welfare state criticism of the losers of modernization" reveals a sophisticated theoretical framework that helps explain the paradoxical finding that socioeconomically vulnerable groups often express stronger criticism of the welfare state despite benefiting from it. By introducing social psychological mechanisms of resentment, particularly group relative deprivation, the authors provide a compelling explanation for what previously appeared as irrational or inconsistent attitudes.

The paper makes significant contributions to our understanding of welfare attitudes, populism, and the political consequences of modernization. It demonstrates that welfare criticism among lower socioeconomic groups is not simply a matter of self-interest or ideology but is rooted in experiences of group-based resentment that arise from perceived unfairness in the distribution of resources and opportunities. This has important implications for understanding support for far-right parties and designing welfare policies that are perceived as fair and legitimate across social groups.
