## Primary Theories Used

### Welfare Deservingness Framework (CARIN criteria)

**Detailed Explanation**: The welfare deservingness framework, as developed by van Oorschot and colleagues, proposes that citizens use five criteria (CARIN) when judging who deserves welfare benefits: Control (personal responsibility for neediness), Attitude (gratitude/compliance), Reciprocity (prior/future contributions), Identity (belonging to in-group), and Need (financial/health necessity). These criteria were initially deduced from literature and subsequently tested through survey research. The framework posits that variation in public support for different welfare programs and target groups can be explained by how these groups are perceived on each criterion. The theory has been primarily tested through quantitative survey research and vignette experiments, with researchers selecting which criteria to measure and how to operationalize them.

**Direct Quotes**:

> "The core claim of the welfare deservingness model, as depicted in Figure 1, is that public support for social benefits and services is to a large extent contingent on the perceived deservingness of their respective target groups." (p.3)
> 

> "A target group's overall welfare deservingness, in turn, depends on how citizens evaluate that group on five crucial deservingness criteria and the importance they attach to such criteria when forming an opinion." (p.3)
> 

**Operationalization and Application**: In this study, the authors use focus groups to allow participants to freely discuss deservingness criteria rather than imposing predetermined measures. They analyze transcripts using directed content analysis with five predefined codes from deservingness theory (control, attitude, reciprocity, identity, need) while allowing new codes to emerge. They specifically examine which criteria participants spontaneously reference when justifying welfare distribution preferences, how these criteria are applied, and what meanings participants attribute to them.

### Welfare Regime Theory (Esping-Andersen)

**Detailed Explanation**: Welfare regime theory, developed by Esping-Andersen (1990), identifies three distinct ideal types of welfare states based on their institutional arrangements, degree of decommodification, and stratification effects: liberal (emphasizing means-testing and minimal state intervention), corporatist-conservative (emphasizing contribution-based social insurance), and social-democratic (emphasizing universalism and equality). This theory challenges the notion that welfare states differ merely in spending levels, arguing instead that they represent qualitatively different approaches to welfare provision with distinct underlying normative principles. Liberal regimes (like the UK) focus on providing for the poor through means-tested programs; corporatist-conservative regimes (like Germany) emphasize social insurance linked to employment; social-democratic regimes (like Denmark) emphasize universal programs and equality.

**Direct Quotes**:

> "[O]ur findings suggest the existence of an institutional logic to welfare preferences, as the focus group participants to a large extent echoed the normative criteria that are most strongly embedded in the institutional structure of their country's welfare regime." (p.1)
> 

> "In Esping-Andersen's view, the 'liberal' regime type (present mostly in Anglophone countries, as, for example, the United Kingdom) is characterized by a focus on means-tested welfare provision, based on liberal conceptions of limited state influence and citizen's personal responsibilities." (p.15)
> 

**Operationalization and Application**: The authors use welfare regime theory as an interpretive framework to understand cross-national differences in deservingness judgments. They selected three countries representing different welfare regimes (UK/liberal, Germany/corporatist-conservative, Denmark/social-democratic) and examine whether participants' deservingness criteria align with the institutional logic of their welfare systems. They analyze how normative criteria used in focus group discussions relate to the principles embedded in different welfare regime types.

**Relevance to My Research**: Welfare regime theory provides a crucial framework for understanding how different institutional arrangements might mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics. My research hypothesizes that universalistic welfare regimes better prevent populist responses to economic shocks. This paper's finding that deservingness judgments align with welfare regime types supports my thesis that institutions shape the moral frameworks through which economic conditions are interpreted.

### Institutional Logic of Welfare Attitudes (Larsen)

**Detailed Explanation**: This theory, developed by Larsen (2006), proposes that citizens' welfare attitudes are shaped by the institutional design of their welfare states, creating a link between welfare regime types and public opinion. It suggests that welfare institutions socialize citizens into particular normative frameworks that align with institutional principles. Different welfare regimes emphasize different distributive principles: liberal regimes emphasize need and targeting welfare to the poor, corporatist regimes emphasize reciprocity and insurance principles, and social-democratic regimes emphasize universalism and equality. The theory proposes that institutions and attitudes reinforce each other over time through recursive processes. Larsen argues that institutional arrangements influence citizens' perceptions of welfare recipients, definition of target groups, and criteria for determining deservingness.

**Direct Quotes**:

> "Our findings bring more nuance to the debate, by suggesting that the importance attached to deservingness criteria depends to some extent on the institutional regime context in which a person lives." (p.18)
> 

> "Overall therefore, there does seem to be—as Larsen (2006) suggests—some kind of 'institutional logic' to popular welfare attitudes." (p.16)
> 

**Operationalization and Application**: The authors use this theory to interpret cross-national differences in deservingness judgments. They analyze how participants in different welfare regimes prioritize different normative criteria and examine whether these align with the principles embedded in their welfare systems. They evaluate whether need dominates in liberal UK, reciprocity in corporatist Germany, and universalism/equality in social-democratic Denmark.

**Relevance to My Research**: This theory directly supports my hypothesis that welfare institutions shape political responses to economic vulnerability by influencing moral frameworks. The finding that citizens' normative criteria align with welfare regime types suggests institutional arrangements play a key role in mediating between economic conditions and political responses. This helps explain cross-national variation in populist politics despite similar economic pressures.

## Findings and Evidence

1. **Citizens use only some CARIN criteria while adding others**: Participants across all three countries made explicit reference to control, reciprocity, and need, but not to attitude and identity. Additionally, they employed context-related criteria not included in the deservingness framework: equality/universalism, cost awareness, and social investment (in Denmark only). This finding is supported by extensive evidence from focus group transcripts showing how participants justified welfare preferences, with direct quotes demonstrating each criterion's use. This suggests my research should consider both deservingness criteria and broader context-related criteria when examining how welfare institutions mediate economic vulnerability and populist politics.
2. **Different welfare regimes emphasize different normative criteria**: In the UK (liberal regime), financial need dominated discussions, with participants focusing on means-testing and detailed financial assessments. In Germany (corporatist-conservative), reciprocity (especially past contributions) was the dominant criterion, with welfare rights tied to prior payments. In Denmark (social-democratic), no single criterion dominated, with participants balancing need, reciprocity, and equality/universalism. This finding is supported by systematic country-by-country analysis of transcripts. For my research, this suggests welfare institutions shape the interpretive frameworks through which economic conditions are understood, potentially explaining cross-national variation in populist responses.
3. **Deservingness criteria have distinct meanings within each criterion**: Control primarily related to personal responsibility for employment but also encompassed health responsibility (UK only) and children as innocent third parties. Reciprocity included both past contributions (dominant in Germany and UK) and future contributions (more prominent in Germany and Denmark). Need primarily meant financial need but also included health needs (except in Germany). These nuanced interpretations emerged from participants' discussions. This finding highlights how economic vulnerability might be interpreted differently depending on the dominant moral frameworks shaped by welfare institutions.
4. **Equality/universalism criterion manifests differently across regimes**: In Denmark and the UK, participants emphasized universal healthcare access, while German participants focused more on equal pension levels. The UK emphasized equal access but not equal benefit levels. Danish participants showed stronger attachment to equality principles, initially resisting ranking exercises as contrary to welfare state principles. This finding derived from cross-national comparison of transcripts. For my research, this suggests welfare institutions that emphasize universalism may create different moral frameworks that influence how economic grievances are interpreted politically.
5. **Institutional socialization shapes welfare attitudes**: The observed alignment between participants' normative criteria and welfare regime types suggests institutional socialization of citizens into particular normative frameworks. The authors note this may reflect bi-directional influence, with both institutions shaping attitudes and attitudes influencing institutional development over time. This interpretation is supported by the systematic cross-national differences in deservingness criteria. This finding directly supports my thesis that welfare institutions can shape moral frameworks, potentially mediating between economic vulnerability and populist politics.

## Discussion

The authors interpret their findings as evidence of an "institutional logic" to welfare preferences, where citizens' normative criteria align with principles embedded in their welfare regimes. They suggest this alignment could result from lifelong socialization into institutional norms (normative institutionalism) or reflect a bottom-up process where public preferences shape institutional design through democratic processes. They suggest a "continuous covariation" where public policy and public opinion reinforce each other over time. While careful not to claim their findings as a definitive test of regime theory, they interpret the cross-national differences in deservingness judgments as supporting Larsen's (2006) thesis about institutional influences on welfare attitudes.

The authors position their findings as filling important gaps in the deservingness literature by demonstrating which criteria citizens actually use (only three of five CARIN criteria plus context-related criteria) and revealing the concrete meanings people attribute to abstract criteria. They emphasize that qualitative research provides insights that survey-based approaches miss, particularly regarding the spontaneous application of criteria and their nuanced interpretations.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Welfare institutions shape the normative criteria citizens use to evaluate deservingness**: The paper demonstrates that citizens in different welfare regimes emphasize different criteria when judging welfare deservingness (need in liberal UK, reciprocity in corporatist Germany, mixed criteria in social-democratic Denmark). This directly supports my thesis that welfare institutions mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics by shaping how economic conditions are interpreted. If universal welfare systems emphasize equality and shared citizenship, they may create moral frameworks that resist populist appeals based on in-group/out-group distinctions, even during economic downturns.
2. **Context-related criteria extend beyond individual deservingness**: The paper identifies criteria (equality/universalism, cost awareness, social investment) that extend beyond individual deservingness to broader societal concerns. This suggests welfare institutions shape not only judgments about individuals but also conceptions of social solidarity and collective welfare. For my research, this indicates that welfare institutions may influence populist politics not just by addressing material insecurity but by fostering particular conceptions of citizenship and social belonging that either reinforce or counteract populist frames.
3. **Reciprocity has both backward-looking and forward-looking dimensions**: Participants interpreted reciprocity both as past contributions (justifying benefits for those who have paid in) and future contributions (justifying investment in those who will contribute later). This distinction is crucial for my research because it suggests welfare systems emphasizing future reciprocity might better accommodate economic transitions by justifying support for those displaced by economic change based on their future potential rather than past contributions, potentially reducing vulnerability to populist appeals.
4. **Universal welfare systems foster resistance to purely need-based distribution**: Danish participants initially resisted ranking exercises as contrary to welfare state principles, suggesting universalism creates strong normative resistance to purely need-based distribution. This supports my hypothesis that universal welfare regimes create moral frameworks emphasizing shared citizenship rather than distinctions between deserving and undeserving groups. This may explain why universal welfare states show greater resilience against identity-based populist appeals during economic downturns.
5. **Welfare attitudes and institutions likely co-evolve over time**: The authors suggest a "continuous covariation" where institutions shape attitudes and attitudes influence institutional development. This bi-directional relationship has important implications for my research on welfare reform and populist politics. It suggests that neoliberal welfare reforms that introduce more means-testing and conditionality may gradually shift moral frameworks toward individualistic explanations for economic outcomes, potentially increasing vulnerability to populist politics that attribute economic problems to out-groups rather than structural factors.
