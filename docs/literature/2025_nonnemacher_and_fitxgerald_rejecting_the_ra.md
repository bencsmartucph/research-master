## Executive Summary

This 2025 paper by Jeffrey Nonnemacher and Jennifer Fitzgerald examines how economic inequality affects support for populist radical right parties, specifically testing whether increased awareness of inequality helps or hurts such parties. Through a survey experiment in Sweden, the researchers find that when respondents are prompted to consider economic inequality, particularly at the local level, they become significantly less likely to support the Sweden Democrats (SD), a radical right populist party. The effect is strongest among those who identify as ideologically right-wing and those who express concerns about law and order. The paper challenges the prevailing narrative that economic inequality fuels radical right support, instead finding evidence for the competing theory that inequality undermines radical right parties by shifting voter attention to economic issues where these parties are not considered competent.

## 1. Introduction and Research Motivation

The paper enters an ongoing debate about the relationship between economic inequality and support for radical right populist parties. The authors note that while economic factors clearly influence voting behavior, "how they matter is a subject of continued debate."

They identify two competing theoretical perspectives:

1. **Status threat perspective**: Inequality benefits radical right parties by generating economic grievances and fears that align with radical right messaging.
2. **Issue ownership perspective**: Inequality weakens radical right parties because they are not seen as competent on this issue, which is traditionally "owned" by left-wing parties.

The authors' novel contribution is examining how the geographic scope of inequality affects its impact on voting behavior:

> "Our project further departs from existing studies by distinguishing between national-level inequality and municipal-level inequality. By prompting some survey participants to reflect on inequality in their own community while others encounter information on national-level inequality, we can unpack how voter behavior may differ in more proximal versus remote contexts."
> 

Key findings from the introduction:

> "When prompted to think about economic inequities in society, respondents on average become less inclined to support the Sweden Democrats, a radical right populist party. These effects are particularly robust and sizable when the matter at hand is local—rather than national—inequality."
> 

The authors argue that local inequality has stronger political effects because:

> "We reason that people tend to conceptualize their local communities differently than they do their national ones, and we contend that inequality in the close, quotidian arena heightens concerns about daily life and local harmony. This then pushes citizens to favor centrist parties that are expected to offer pragmatic solutions in lieu of rhetorical intensity."
> 

## 2. Theoretical Framework and Literature Review

### 2.1 Status Threat and the Radical Right

The paper thoroughly reviews existing literature suggesting that inequality benefits radical right parties. This perspective posits that those who perceive themselves as economically disadvantaged or threatened by inequality are more likely to support radical right parties.

Key mechanisms identified:

1. **Relative deprivation**: Those experiencing economic hardship or perceiving themselves on the "losing end" of inequality gravitate toward radical right rhetoric.

> "Rooduijn and Burgoon (2018) similarly find that experiencing economic hardship fosters support for the radical right, especially when aggregate economic conditions are good. Similarly, those who consider themselves on the losing end of growing inequality, notably low-income individuals, gravitate toward the radical right due to its anti-elite, populist rhetoric (Norris and Inglehart, 2019)."
> 
1. **Positional deprivation**: People who experience relative economic strain compared to others find populist parties more attractive.

> "Focusing on positional deprivation, Burgoon et al. (2019) find that those who experience relative economic strain compared to others in society consider populist parties to be more attractive. Relatedly, Bolet (2022) argues that those who perceive themselves to be in social decline relative to their parents are more supportive of radical right parties."
> 
1. **Economic nationalism and welfare chauvinism**: Radical right parties capitalize on growing inequality by combining anti-globalization themes with welfare chauvinism.

> "This rise of economic nationalism has contributed to the growing appeal of welfare chauvinism, which places a national twist on issues of inequality and redistribution by arguing that those on the wrong side of inequality are suffering from a welfare system that pays out more to immigrants and ethnic others who contribute far less to the system."
> 
1. **Status threat among the affluent**: Some studies suggest high-status individuals may fear losing their status through redistributive policies designed to address inequality.

> "High status individuals may see that rising inequality—and the public scrutiny that accompanies it—will put a target on their backs. Evidence from the field of neuroscience shows that some wealthy/high status individuals fear that they will lose their status through policy efforts designed to redress economic disparities."
> 

### 2.2 Issue Ownership and the Radical Right

The competing theoretical perspective suggests that rising inequality should hurt radical right parties because they are not seen as competent on economic issues.

Key points:

1. **Issue saliency theory**: Parties gain support when issues they "own" become more salient to voters.

> "Essentially, according to issue saliency theory (Budge and Farlie, 1983), parties gain support in elections when issues they are the associated owners of become more salient. Voters will support the parties that they consider the best equipped to handle the issues that are most important to them."
> 
1. **Traditional ownership of inequality issues**: Left-wing parties, particularly social democratic parties, are seen as more competent on inequality and redistribution.

> "Importantly, inequality is an issue that is often associated with social democratic parties while the radical right is associated with immigration (Seeberg, 2017)."
> 
1. **Empirical support**: Several studies find that inequality hurts radical right electoral prospects.

> "Coffe et al. (2007) find that the radical right in Belgium suffers at the polls in municipalities where inequality is high. Importantly, they find that high levels of inequality decrease the odds that the radical right will even compete in a district as it is deemed too unfavorable of terrain given the salience of inequality."
> 

### 2.3 Inequality as a Local Issue

The authors make a novel contribution by distinguishing between national and local inequality, arguing that local inequality may have stronger effects on voting behavior.

Their key arguments:

1. **Localized political concerns**: Voters are grounded in their immediate localities, and local issues shape their voting behavior.

> "Emerging scholarship shows that voters are grounded in their immediate localities, and localized political concerns shape their voting behavior (Munis and Burke, 2023; Strebel and Shakel, 2024). In particular, people's feelings about local issues have been tied directly to their likelihood of supporting the radical right (Chou et al., 2022; Fitzgerald, 2018; Harteveld et al., 2021)."
> 
1. **Local manifestations of inequality**: The societal ills stemming from inequality have particularly pernicious implications at the local level.

> "For instance, income inequality is linked to fear of crime (Rueda and Stegmueller, 2016; Vauclair and Bratanova, 2017) as well as higher crime rates (Hsieh and Pugh, 1993). Economic inequalities generate less life satisfaction, weaker social solidarity and poorer health outcomes (Buttrick and Oishi, 2017)."
> 
1. **Urgency of local problems**: The threat of a "broken community" evokes a more urgent response than abstract national-level inequities.

> "The specter of a broken community is likely to evoke a more urgent and salient threat of inequality than the threat posed by national-level inequities which can be abstract for voters."
> 

### 2.4 Hypotheses

Based on these competing theoretical perspectives, the authors propose three hypotheses:

1. **Status Threat Hypothesis (H1)**: Respondents prompted to consider inequality will be more supportive of the radical-right Sweden Democrats compared to the control group.
2. **Issue Competency Hypothesis (H2)**: Respondents prompted to consider inequality will be less supportive of the radical-right Sweden Democrats compared to the control group.
3. **Localized Effect Hypothesis (H3)**: The effect of H1 or H2 will be stronger among respondents prompted to consider local-level inequality compared to those prompted to consider national-level inequality.

## 3. Research Design and Methodology

### 3.1 Case Selection: Sweden

The authors chose Sweden as their research setting for several reasons:

1. Sweden has relatively low but rising levels of inequality, making it "a propitious context for studying the political effects of priming individuals to consider worsening levels of inequality."
2. The Sweden Democrats (SD) are a "fairly typical populist radical right party by European standards" that advocates stringent policies on immigration, critiques the political mainstream, and draws on themes of Swedish nationalism.
3. Sweden's robust welfare state and party system allow for rigorous analysis of inequality's effect on different party families across the ideological spectrum.

### 3.2 Survey Experiment Design

The authors conducted a survey experiment with over 800 Swedish residents in August 2020. Respondents were randomly assigned to one of three groups:

1. **National Inequality Treatment** (n=269): Respondents were told about high and rising inequality in Sweden compared to other countries.
2. **Local Inequality Treatment** (n=273): Respondents were told about high and rising inequality in their specific municipality compared to neighboring areas.
3. **Control Group** (n=274): Respondents received no information about inequality.

The treatment text explicitly mentioned "the gap between the top 10% of income earners and the bottom," emphasizing economic elites to inject "a decidedly populist dimension into the inquiry."

### 3.3 Variables and Measurements

**Dependent Variables:**

- Support for Sweden Democrats (0-10 scale)
- Support for other major Swedish parties (Left Party, Social Democrats, Greens, Moderates)

**Independent Variables:**

- Pre-treatment: Voted Sweden Democrats in 2018, Inequality among friends, Inequality is a serious problem, Left-right ideology, Relative economic status (municipality and national), Competence of SD on inequality
- Post-treatment issue positions: Anti-immigrant, Pro-law and order, COVID handling is equal, Not enough good jobs in Sweden, Climate change is a serious threat

## 4. Results and Analysis

### 4.1 Main Treatment Effects

The authors find that the local inequality treatment significantly reduces support for the Sweden Democrats:

> "Reading about local level inequality is associated with approximately a 10 percent reduction in the level of support for the Sweden Democrats, which is no trivial result."
> 

The national inequality treatment shows a negative but initially non-significant effect, though it becomes significant when controlling for other variables in the full model:

> "Additionally, there is no simple, direct effect between those receiving the national level prompt and not receiving any prompt. Urging respondents to consider national level inequality, in this simple statistical comparison, has an effect that cannot be statistically distinguished from a null effect."
> 

These findings support the Issue Competency Hypothesis (H2) and the Localized Effect Hypothesis (H3):

> "Essentially, prompting respondents to consider local inequality, not national inequality, diminishes the appeal of the radical-right, supporting our expectations for H3 and lending support to the issue saliency model represented by H2."
> 

### 4.2 Heterogeneous Treatment Effects

The authors explore which voters are most affected by the inequality treatments:

1. **Ideological position**: The local inequality treatment has the strongest negative effect on SD support among those furthest to the right ideologically.

> "Those who are farthest to the right are also the ones whose views of the Sweden Democrats are most damaged by receiving the local treatment."
> 
1. **Law and order concerns**: The local inequality treatment has the strongest negative effect on SD support among those who express concerns about law and order.

> "Being prompted to think about local inequality undercuts support for the Sweden Democrats most visibly among those who want more investment in law and order."
> 

The authors interpret these findings as evidence for the issue-based explanation of inequality's effect on radical right support:

> "We interpret these interactive effects as more evidence in favor of an issue-based approach to understanding the effect of inequality on radical right support. Instead of deepening existing divides within society, the people most likely to leave the radical right are those that would otherwise be the most supportive: those furthest to the right and those most in favor of tougher law and order."
> 

Notably, they do not find heterogeneous treatment effects based on economic position, class, or education levels, contradicting the positional deprivation perspective:

> "Following a positional deprivation account of inequality's effects on radical right voting, we would expect to see these indicators of status shaping the response to our treatment. Since we instead see that our treatment impacts people across class and education level, we are more confident that our findings are driven by issue-based motivations."
> 

### 4.3 Where Do Voters Go?

The authors explore which parties benefit when the local inequality treatment reduces support for the Sweden Democrats. They find:

1. The Social Democrats and the Greens gain support from the local inequality treatment.
2. The center-right Moderates lose support, but the effect is not statistically significant.
3. The Left Party neither gains nor loses support.

When controlling for past vote choice, only the negative effect on the Sweden Democrats remains statistically significant.

The authors note that this pattern presents "mixed evidence in favor of a pure issue-based account of inequality's effect":

> "On the one hand, in the first models, we do see that voters are leaving the radical right in favor of the Social Democrats, which we would expect given extant theoretical expectations on issue competition."
> 

However, they struggle to explain why the Greens would gain support while the Left Party does not:

> "Existing theory on an issue-based model cannot adequately explain why the Greens would gain support but the Left is unaffected. Our findings are especially puzzling given that only 2% of respondents in our study view the Greens as the most competent on income inequality, which is 14% lower than the Sweden Democrats, who lose support from our treatments."
> 

## 5. Discussion and Implications

### 5.1 Main Findings and Theoretical Implications

The authors summarize their main finding:

> "When people are presented with information about inequality in their local area, they become less inclined to support the radical right. This is the central finding of our study."
> 

This challenges the status threat/positional deprivation perspective:

> "Our findings contradict the literature on the benefits radical right parties accrue as inequality rises from status threats and positional deprivation. When we prompt individuals to think about inequality in their local environment, support for the radical right declines."
> 

Their findings support the issue ownership perspective, particularly when inequality is framed locally:

> "From a birds-eye view, the evidence presented in this paper support the theoretical propositions of political economy models of electoral behavior. We show that when confronted with a problem that is particularly experienced at the local level, Swedish voters reevaluate their support for the radical right and look elsewhere."
> 

The authors emphasize that the effect is not just among moderate voters, but among the radical right's core constituencies:

> "This is not merely a transition of median voters either, but rather an abandonment from core voting blocs of radical right support: the most right-leaning and those most in favor of increased law and order spending. In other words, the exact groups that should abandon the radical right under issue competition models are the groups that abandon the radical right in response to our treatments."
> 

### 5.2 Puzzles and Further Research

The authors acknowledge that their findings raise new questions, particularly why the Green Party benefits from the inequality treatment while the Left Party does not. They offer several possible explanations:

1. **Incomplete picture of party competency**: Voters may see the Greens as the second-best party on inequality after the Social Democrats.
2. **Government coalition effects**: The Greens may benefit from being in government with the Social Democrats, gaining a "spill-over of competency."
3. **Moderation preference**: Voters moving away from the Sweden Democrats might find the Left Party too extreme, preferring the more moderate Greens.

> "More broadly, we think our results are rooted in the ways people weigh the pros and cons of who is in charge and how they may impact life at the local level, favoring those parties who they deem competent enough to handle the problem while also focusing on moderation instead of revolutionary rhetoric on both sides of the political spectrum. Perhaps the Left Party are simply too radical to ensure harmony in daily community life for those most impacted by our treatment."
> 

### 5.3 Generalizability

The authors address potential concerns about the generalizability of their findings beyond Sweden:

1. They note that Sweden represents a "least likely case" for their findings, given the country's cultural emphasis on equality.
2. They find no evidence that education levels or pre-existing concerns about inequality moderate the treatment effects, suggesting the findings may apply in other contexts.
3. They note that previous studies in other countries have found similar evidence that local inequality hurts radical right parties.

## 6. Social Psychological Mechanisms

The paper offers several social psychological mechanisms that explain how inequality affects support for radical right parties:

### 6.1 Local Community Conceptualization

The authors argue that people conceptualize their local communities differently than they do national contexts, making local inequality more politically impactful:

> "We argue that this is the case because the stakes are higher and the implications of any large-scale trends are more immediate, raising the salience of issues that are felt more at the local level such as inequality driving voters away from the radical right."
> 

### 6.2 Community Cohesion and Safety

Local inequality may heighten concerns about community safety and social cohesion:

> "If rising inequality prompts fear of crime, as Rueda and Stegmueller (2016) find, then it stands to reason that growing inequality in one's backyard should heighten support for redistributive policies."
> 

The significant interaction between the local inequality treatment and law and order concerns supports this mechanism:

> "Being prompted to think about local inequality undercuts support for the Sweden Democrats most visibly among those who want more investment in law and order."
> 

### 6.3 Issue Framing and Rhetorical Effectiveness

The authors suggest that when inequality becomes concrete and local, radical right rhetoric about distant elites becomes less effective:

> "As a result, the appeals of sensationalized rhetoric to an unnamed, distant elite may ring hollow to those currently experiencing the effects of inequality."
> 

### 6.4 Pragmatic Solution Seeking

When inequality is experienced locally, voters may prioritize practical solutions over ideological rhetoric:

> "This then pushes citizens to favor centrist parties that are expected to offer pragmatic solutions in lieu of rhetorical intensity."
> 

## 7. Strengths and Limitations

### 7.1 Strengths

1. **Novel experimental approach**: The study uses an experimental design to directly test the causal effect of inequality awareness on party support, overcoming limitations of cross-sectional or aggregate data.
2. **Local vs. national distinction**: The paper makes a valuable contribution by distinguishing between local and national inequality, offering new insights into how geographic context shapes political behavior.
3. **Theoretical implications**: The findings challenge dominant theories about inequality and radical right support, providing strong evidence for issue competency explanations.

### 7.2 Limitations

1. **Sample representativeness**: The sample skews young due to the online recruitment method, potentially limiting generalizability.
2. **Timing**: The survey was conducted during the COVID-19 pandemic in August 2020, which may have influenced responses in ways not accounted for.
3. **Post-treatment variables**: Some analyses use post-treatment variables, raising concerns about post-treatment bias.
4. **Short-term effects**: The experiment measures immediate responses to inequality information but cannot assess how durable these effects would be over time.

## 8. Conclusion

This paper makes a significant contribution to our understanding of the relationship between economic inequality and support for radical right parties. Contrary to dominant theories suggesting that inequality fuels radical right support, the authors find that making people aware of inequality—particularly at the local level—reduces support for these parties. The effect is strongest among those who would typically be most supportive of the radical right: right-wing ideologues and those concerned about law and order.

The findings support issue competency theories of voting behavior, suggesting that when inequality becomes salient, voters turn to parties they perceive as more competent on economic issues. The stronger effect of local compared to national inequality highlights the importance of considering how geographic context shapes political behavior.

The paper raises important questions for future research, particularly regarding why certain parties benefit more than others when the radical right loses support, and whether these findings would hold in different political contexts. As the authors note, understanding the relationship between inequality and radical right support requires attention to "the personal and experiential dimensions of community-level inequality and other societal factors."

Does economic inequality motivate support for the populist radical right? The authors of this study weigh in on this debate with survey experimental evidence from Sweden. When prompted to think about inequality at the national or local level, respondents on average become less inclined to support the Sweden Democrats, a radical right populist party. These effects are particularly robust and sizable when the matter at hand is local—rather than national—inequality.

The authors argue that to understand inequality's effects on voter behavior, it is essential to explore the ways people conceptualize it. This study serves as a reminder to consider the day-to-day implications of macroeconomic developments, and it provides a tool for communicating with voters in a way that undercuts the appeals of radical right parties.

When people are presented with information about inequality in their local area, they become less inclined to support the radical right. This is the central finding of our study. We also find some evidence that encouraging individuals to think about inequality at the national level weakens support for the radical right, but this is not nearly as strong as the rejection of the radical right when voters are confronted with inequality in their municipality. Faced with details of the top 10 percent in one's community pulling away economically from the rest of the local residents diminishes radical right support most significantly among right-wing ideologues and worriers about law and order.

From a birds-eye view, the evidence presented in this paper support the theoretical propositions of political economy models of electoral behavior. We show that when confronted with a problem that is particularly experienced at the local level, Swedish voters reevaluate their support for the radical right and look elsewhere. This is not merely a transition of median voters either, but rather an abandonment from core voting blocs of radical right support: the most right-leaning and those most in favor of increased law and order spending.

The appeal of the radical right's ambiguous rhetoric and symbolism diminishes when confronted with the harsh reality of a societal problem in urgent need of addressing in one's backyard. However, we find evidence that there is more going on than pure considerations of issue competency. In response to our treatment, voters turn to the Social Democrats and the Greens, but not the Left, which demonstrates that voters are not moving for pure competency concerns.

We interpret these interactive effects as more evidence in favor of an issue-based approach to understanding the effect of inequality on radical right support. Instead of deepening existing divides within society, the people most likely to leave the radical right are those that would otherwise be the most supportive: those furthest to the right and those most in favor of tougher law and order.
