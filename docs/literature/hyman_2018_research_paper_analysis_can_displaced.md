# Hyman 2018 - Research Paper Analysis: Can Displaced Labor Be Retrained? Evidence from Quasi-Random Assignment to Trade Adjustment Assistance  The study finds that TAA generates substantial short-term benefits—workers approved for TAA have approximately $50,000 higher cumulative earnings over ten years, driven by both higher wages (33% of returns) and increased labor force participation. However, these gains depreciate over time, with annual earnings differences disappearing after a decade. Critically, the research demonstrates that in economically distressed regions, TAA significantly increases worker mobility across both industries and geographic areas, suggesting the program helps overcome spatial and industrial adjustment frictions. The findings have profound implications for understanding whether active labor market policies can facilitate worker adaptation to trade shocks and technological change.

## Key Takeaways

### 1. Substantial Initial Returns with Depreciation

**"Workers inferred to take up benefits forego roughly $10,000 in income while training, yet ten years later have approximately $50,000 higher cumulative earnings relative to all-else-equal workers that do not retrain."** The program generates meaningful economic benefits, but these gains erode over time as **"annual incomes among TAA and non-TAA workers fully converge after ten years."**

### 2. Evidence of Human Capital Enhancement, Not Just Job Queue Effects

**"I estimate that 33% of these returns are driven by higher wages—a sizable share which suggests that TAA-trained workers are not only compensated through greater labor force participation or higher priority in job queues. Rather, TAA workers also appear to be paid a premium for their newly acquired human capital."**

### 3. Geographic and Industrial Mobility as Key Mechanisms

**"Workers are approximately 30 percentage points more likely to move commuting zones and 50 percentage points more likely to switch industries (at the 2-digit North American Industrial Classification System (NAICS) level), with respect to the location and industry of their pre-layoff employer."** This finding suggests TAA helps overcome adjustment frictions.

### 4. Training Quality Matters for Durability

**"Workers that are located in high-duration states prior to their layoff...do not exhibit the depreciating pattern that is common to those in lower training duration states."** This indicates that more intensive training programs produce more durable human capital gains.

### 5. Modest but Positive Social Returns

The paper estimates **"an internal rate of return (IRR) to TAA of between 0.0% and 9.1%"** while noting this represents **"a lower bound for at least two reasons"** including intent-to-treat estimation and potential substitution effects with other social programs.

## Section-by-Section Analysis

### Introduction

The introduction masterfully establishes the contemporary relevance of trade adjustment policy within broader debates about globalization's labor market impacts. Hyman contextualizes the research problem by noting that **"Between 2000 and 2016, the US shed approximately 6 million manufacturing jobs, resulting in the lowest level of manufacturing employment since the onset of World War II"** while simultaneously **"this decline contrasted with record revenue growth in both manufacturing and non-manufacturing industries over the same period."**

This juxtaposition highlights a central tension in modern political economy: aggregate economic gains from trade coexisting with concentrated labor market disruption. The author connects this to broader political concerns, noting **"The unusual speed and persistence of these structural changes has generated equal concern about how future generations will adapt to work in a rapidly automating economy."**

The paper's contribution is positioned within a gap in existing knowledge: **"Despite widespread attention, remarkably little is known about whether the US's largest and longest standing incentive program for retraining displaced workers—the Department of Labor's 1974 Trade Adjustment Assistance (TAA) program—is a necessary, effective, or efficient means to accelerate adjustment."** This establishes both the policy relevance and the empirical contribution of the research.

### Literature Review and Theoretical Framework

### Trade and Labor Market Adjustment Literature

Hyman positions his work within three interconnected literatures. The first concerns **"The impact of worker displacement and trade on inequality,"** building particularly on Autor et al. (2016) who **"study long run earnings adjustment patterns of workers exposed to Chinese import competition using administrative Social Security Administration (SSA) data."** The author notes that **"The authors find long-run negative effects from trade, and show that trade-affected workers take up disability insurance (DI) rather than trade adjustment assistance to insure themselves against these shocks."**

This literature documents a shift from earlier trade models that assumed frictionless adjustment. As Hyman explains: **"While trade economists have shown that removing import barriers can increase growth and consumer welfare through specialization, lower goods prices, and higher variety, less attention had been given to adverse consequences for labor because imports from low-wage countries were relatively inconsequential until the 1990s."** The emergence of the "China shock" literature fundamentally challenged assumptions about automatic adjustment.

### Job Training Evaluation Literature

The second literature strand focuses on job training effectiveness, which **"has been the source of several methodological advancements in labor economics and debate surrounding the econometrics of program evaluation."** Hyman traces the evolution from early studies using **"model-based econometric selection correction methods (Heckman, 1976) or difference-in-differences estimators (Ashenfelter (1978))"** that found minimal effects, through methodological critiques by LaLonde (1986), to more recent experimental approaches.

Critically, **"Card et al. (2010) follow up on prior estimates with a similarly titled handbook chapter, and conclude that 'training programmes are associated with positive medium-term impacts, although in the short term they often appear ineffective.'"** This provides important context for interpreting TAA's effects, as **"When converted to standardized effect sizes, this paper's employment results fall near the middle of those effect sizes classified as 'significantly positive' in the Card et al. (2010) study."**

### Spatial and Industrial Mobility Literature

The third strand examines **"The role of local labor market and occupational mobility frictions in earnings growth."** This literature, building on **"Pioneering work by Blanchard and Katz (1992) highlighted the empirical importance of worker mobility as an equilibrating factor across differently shocked labor markets,"** provides the theoretical foundation for understanding TAA's mobility effects.

Theoretically, Hyman draws on **"Feenstra and Lewis (1994) build on the work of Dixit and Norman (1986) and show that redistributive compensation from levying taxes on winning factors are more likely to result in a Pareto-improving allocation if combined with mobility subsidies to overcome adjustment frictions."** This theoretical insight motivates the paper's focus on geographic and industrial mobility as key mechanisms.

### Theoretical Framework: Adjustment Frictions

The paper's theoretical framework centers on various frictions that may prevent efficient labor market adjustment following trade shocks. Hyman identifies several potential barriers: **"Among several possible barriers to adjustment, these frictions may be spatial or industrial—high mobility costs across labor markets and industries stagnate worker wages in trade-afflicted labor markets; informational—search costs result in job mismatch after displacement; financial—liquidity constraints preclude the necessary investments in retraining and education required for work in the modern economy; or behavioral—workers may be 'present-biased' or make forecasting errors about the future viability of their local industries."**

The theoretical model underlying TAA approval can be conceptualized as: **"Consider a simple model of TAA selection similar to the second stage in the above 2SLS procedure: Yijktτ = βτ1{σk + Zj(i)k + X'ijkt > ξijkt} + eijktτ"** where **"the approval indicator TAAijkt is determined by whether the latent sum of three components exceeds an unobservable worker-investigator admittance threshold ξijkt."**

This framework allows for identification of causal effects through investigator leniency variation while controlling for industry-specific tradability signals. The approach recognizes that **"investigator leniency is assumed separable into an investigator-industry shift (Zj(i)k) around an industry's average tradability signal σk."**

### Methodology

### Identification Strategy

The paper employs an investigator leniency instrumental variable approach, building on the "examiner" literature. **"I define an investigator's leniency as the share of total cases approved over their entire tenure, less the current case (excluding those with less than 10 cases over their tenure)."** This "leave-one-out" or "jackknife" IV design exploits quasi-random assignment of cases to investigators with varying approval propensities.

The key institutional detail enabling identification is that **"TAA assigns two otherwise identical worker cohorts displaced from the same industry, different TAA approval probabilities based on whether their case is directed to a more lenient versus strict investigator."** The author provides evidence that **"TAA cases are assigned [to investigators] primarily based on investigator caseload, as well as previous experience with a company or industry."**

### Two-Stage Least Squares Specification

The empirical approach uses a two-stage least squares framework:

**First Stage:** TAAijkt = θLeniency^(-p)_j(i) + α₁Cjkt + δ₁Njkt + λk + X'ijktγ₁ + νijkt

**Second Stage:** Yijktτ = βτTAAijkt + α₂Cjkt + δ₂Njkt + λk + X'ijktγ₂ + eijktτ

Where key control variables include:

- **Njkt:** Raw number of cases investigator j has adjudicated in industry k by time t
- **Cjkt:** Concentration of industry k cases assigned to investigator j by time t
- **λk:** 4-digit SIC industry fixed effects
- **Xijkt:** Baseline worker characteristics and precision controls

### Variable Definitions and Measurement

**Dependent Variables:**

- Annual earnings (deflated to 2010 dollars)
- Quarters employed per year
- Formal education attainment (ordinal 1-4 scale)
- Industry mobility (probability of staying in same 2-digit NAICS)
- Geographic mobility (probability of staying in same commuting zone)

**Key Independent Variable:**

- TAA approval indicator (1 if approved, 0 if denied)

**Instrumental Variable:**

- Leave-one-out investigator leniency rate

**Sample Restrictions:**
The analysis focuses on a **"High Labor Force Attachment"** sample including **"working-age individuals (22-65) with at least 8 quarters of positive earnings in the pre-period at or above the full-time minimum wage equivalent ($7.25 * 2,082 average working hours in 2010 ≈ $15,000 annually)"** and earning **"less than $50,000/year (∼85th percentile) in all years prior to the petition filing."**

### Data Sources and Sample Construction

The research combines two primary administrative datasets: **"I have first acquired the universe of approved and denied TAA petitions (1974-2016) through Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) requests at the USDOL"** and **"I merge TAA petition data to the US Census Bureau's Longitudinal Employer-Household Dynamics (LEHD) administrative files."**

The matching process yields **"a final matched sample of 4,700 unique TAA establishments—comprising roughly 13% of plants associated with TAA claimants from 1990 to 2011."** After sample restrictions, the analysis includes **"177,000 displaced workers"** in the preferred specification.

Data limitations include geographic coverage (**"24 states and the District of Columbia approved data access"**) and potential mobility measurement error, as **"I cannot know whether a worker with a missing earnings value is in fact employed in a state outside of the set of 24 approved for this project."**

### Results: Main Effects

### Earnings and Employment Impacts

The core finding demonstrates substantial earnings returns: **"TAA takers have on average $10,256 higher annual earnings, relative to all-else-equal non-takers"** in the post-training period. However, **"large initial returns decay over time. Annual incomes among TAA and non-TAA workers in fact, fully converge after ten years."**

The employment channel accounts for a significant portion of returns: **"TAA-trained workers are employed on average 0.64 more quarters each year in the post period (τ ∈ [2, 10])."** Back-of-the-envelope calculations suggest **"roughly 67% of the prior earnings pattern can be explained by this higher employment rate."**

### Human Capital vs. Job Queue Effects

A critical finding distinguishes between human capital enhancement and mere job queue prioritization: **"I estimate that 33% of these returns are driven by higher wages—a sizable share which suggests that TAA-trained workers are not only compensated through greater labor force participation or higher priority in job queues. Rather, TAA workers also appear to be paid a premium for their newly acquired human capital."**

### Formal Education Effects

Notably, the research finds **"no statistically significant effect on the probability that a worker moves up the formal education scale"** across all specifications. This suggests TAA's human capital effects operate through vocational rather than formal educational channels.

### Heterogeneity Analysis and Mechanisms

### Training Duration and Quality

The paper provides crucial evidence on training program design: **"Workers that are located in high-duration states prior to their layoff (the series marked with an 'x') do not exhibit the depreciating pattern that is common to those in lower training duration states."** This finding suggests that **"62% of TAA training programs confer vocational degrees with shorter program lengths than typical community college or 4-year college degrees which have been shown to have durable earnings returns."**

### Geographic and Industrial Mobility

The mobility analysis reveals TAA's most striking effects occur in economically distressed areas: **"I find that workers in highly disrupted regions are more likely to switch both industries and commuting zones in response to training. Workers are approximately 30 percentage points more likely to move commuting zones and 50 percentage points more likely to switch industries."**

This finding provides strong evidence that **"TAA workers switch industries and labor markets, this is more consistent with an adjustment friction story rather than labor market crowd-out."** The mobility effects are **"large effects, when compared with baseline mobility rates of 0.26 and 0.23 for commuting zones and 2-digit industries respectively."**

### Labor Market Distress and Program Effectiveness

The heterogeneity analysis demonstrates that program effectiveness varies significantly by local economic conditions: **"workers displaced in slacker labor markets (higher unemployment counties) have returns that are almost double those of low unemployment rate counties."** This suggests TAA is most valuable precisely where alternative employment opportunities are most limited.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis

The paper conducts a comprehensive welfare analysis comparing program benefits to social costs. The approach incorporates **"two important potential sources of deadweight loss (DWL): the excess burden from taxation ('leakage' from redistribution), and moral hazard search costs associated with extended unemployment insurance transfers."**

The cost-benefit calculation yields **"an internal rate of return (IRR) on TAA of between 0.0% and 9.1% for deadweight loss values ranging between 0.25 and 0.75."** However, the author emphasizes this represents **"a lower bound for at least two reasons. First, earnings returns are calculated from an intent-to-treat (ITT) estimator which likely understates benefits due to imperfect compliance with the treatment. Second, TAA may induce worker substitution away from other costly social insurance programs such as disability insurance (DI)."**

### Connections to Research Interests

### Relevance to Far-Right Politics and Redistribution Preferences

This research provides crucial empirical evidence relevant to understanding political support for redistribution and far-right movements. The findings demonstrate that active labor market policies can meaningfully improve outcomes for trade-displaced workers, potentially reducing the economic grievances that fuel far-right support. The geographic mobility findings are particularly relevant, as they suggest such policies can help workers escape economically declining regions rather than remaining in areas susceptible to far-right mobilization.

The paper's evidence on training program effectiveness speaks to debates about whether redistributive policies should focus on "place-based" versus "people-based" approaches. The finding that TAA increases worker mobility suggests people-based policies may be more effective than attempting to revitalize declining industrial regions.

### Implications for Social Investment Policies

The research provides strong evidence for the "social investment" approach to welfare policy, demonstrating that active labor market policies generating human capital can produce positive returns. However, the finding that returns depreciate over time unless training is intensive suggests the need for substantial investment in program quality.

The paper's evidence on adjustment frictions supports theoretical arguments for government intervention in labor markets experiencing trade shocks. The mobility findings suggest that market failures in spatial and industrial adjustment may justify active policies to facilitate worker transitions.

### Limitations and Future Research

The paper acknowledges several important limitations. The geographic coverage issue means **"the LEHD sub-sample is missing some notable political swing states such as Michigan, Wisconsin, and Ohio"** which may limit generalizability to regions most affected by trade-related political mobilization.

The analysis is also limited by imperfect compliance: **"Schochet et al. (2012) estimate that 25-45% of workers offered TAA benefits actually take up the program."** This means the estimates represent intent-to-treat rather than treatment-on-treated effects.

### Conclusion and Policy Implications

The research provides nuanced evidence on active labor market policy effectiveness. While TAA generates meaningful short-term benefits and facilitates important mobility adjustments, the depreciation of returns over time raises questions about optimal program design. The findings suggest that **"more human capital investment is needed to catch up to the frontier"** and that program intensity matters crucially for durability.

For contemporary policy debates, the research suggests that well-designed retraining programs can help workers adapt to trade shocks, potentially reducing the political backlash against globalization. However, the modest social returns and need for intensive programming highlight the challenges of using active labor market policies to address large-scale economic displacement.

The paper ultimately concludes that **"TAA may serve as an important tag for redistributing the growth from trade. However, whether these results can be extrapolated to trade and automation pressures faced by future workers remains an open question."** This caveat is particularly relevant given the accelerating pace of technological change and the potential for AI and automation to create even more widespread labor market disruption.
