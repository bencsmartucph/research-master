    ## **Introduction**
    
    Current scholarship offers two strikingly different angles to look at the political repercussions of structural economic change. Some observers highlight the ever-growing share of highly educated citizens whose coveted skills guarantee aggregate prosperity and growth in modern knowledge economies. At the same time, an influential literature emphasizes that the gains of economic modernization are distributed in a highly unequal way, which produces a rising pool of dissatisfied voters. The two accounts suggest fundamentally distinct prospects for political stability in advanced capitalist democracies. While widespread upskilling in the knowledge economy promises growing prosperity and, thus, a symbiotic relationship between capitalism and democracy, the second perspective paints a much more fragile picture. It stands to reason that both narratives hold some truth: stability and contestation hinge on the relative importance of the constituencies which either perceive that they have what it takes to thrive in the knowledge economy or are worried about their future place in a dynamic and fast-changing society.
    
    This paper studies the structural foundations of party system change in postindustrial democracies by investigating how perceptions of long-term social and economic opportunity relate to electoral preferences. How does the position of voters in a changing economy affect their support for the political status quo? In the early twenty-first century electoral context, mainstream parties – and incumbents in particular – represent continuity, while radical parties fundamentally contest the existing economic and political order. Mainstream opposition parties might call for policy adaptation within the confines of the existing system, but they do not question long-term structural trajectories in the way that radical parties do (De Vries and Hobolt [Reference De Vries and Hobolt2020](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref5)). By looking at support for mainstream incumbent parties, mainstream opposition parties, and radical parties, we focus on expressions of varying degrees of willingness to upend the political status quo.
    
    Focusing on prospective evaluation of opportunities, we build on recent contributions on long-term party system change that have increasingly recognized the limited explanatory power of immediate material circumstances for individual political behaviour. A rapidly growing literature has started to look beyond objective socioeconomic status and introduced subjective, relational, and long-term understandings of societal position to identify ‘winners’ and, especially, ‘losers’ of structural change (Burgoon et al. [Reference Burgoon2019](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref2); Cramer [Reference Cramer2016](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref4); Gest [Reference Gest2016](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref10); Gidron and Hall [Reference Gidron and Hall2017](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref13); Hochschild [Reference Hochschild2016](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref17); Kurer [Reference Kurer2020](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref21); Pettigrew [Reference Pettigrew2017](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref29); Rooduijn and Burgoon [Reference Rooduijn and Burgoon2018](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref30); Smith and Pettigrew [Reference Smith and Pettigrew2015](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref32); Steenvoorden and Harteveld [Reference Steenvoorden and Harteveld2018](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref33); Teney, Lacewell, and Wilde [Reference Teney, Lacewell and Wilde2014](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref34)). What these studies have in common is a conceptualization of voter perceptions that refers to their present or retrospective experience. We argue that many of the core conflicts arising from the emerging knowledge economy are of an equally relational and long-term oriented but inherently forward-looking nature: voters' belief in the political system is based on perceptions of prospective opportunities – for themselves as well as the next generation.
    
    The expectation of a system-stabilizing effect of positive future opportunities is in line with core insights of the literature on economic voting, which concludes that voters' *perceptions* of economic conditions (versus their objective material conditions) and their short-term prospective (alongside retrospective) evaluation of the economy most consistently relate to support for incumbent government parties (Clarke et al. [Reference Clarke2004](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref3); Duch and Stevenson [Reference Duch and Stevenson2008](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref6); Lewis-Beck and Stegmaier [Reference Lewis-Beck, Stegmaier, Dalton and Klingemann2007](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref23)). However, the fundamental, long-term nature of ongoing transformations of the economy and electoral realignments implies that beyond the familiar distinction between incumbent versus opposition parties, we should further distinguish between more versus less radical forms of opposition (Hernández and Kriesi [Reference Hernández and Kriesi2016](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref16)). Moreover, the above-cited literature on changing social hierarchies in times of structural change indicates that voters' sense of (lacking) opportunity relates to deeper grievances linked to questions of dignity, identity and belonging in a changing society. Such sentiments, in particular, provide a link to support for the radical right, which has posed the primary challenge to mainstream parties in recent decades. Consequently, our study integrates insights from the economic voting literature on government support with the findings of a growing literature highlighting the implications of a more structural backlash against economic change and the political elite.
    
    More specifically, we argue and demonstrate that perceptions of economic opportunity cross-cut objective socioeconomic indicators, resulting in political coalitions that transcend vertical divisions of society. We discuss the relevance of two key constituencies emerging from our conceptual framework: aspirational and apprehensive voters. *Aspirational* voters are not immediate beneficiaries in the ongoing economic-structural context, but they are confident enough about their (and their children's) prospects to support a reformist, system-sustaining approach to policies and governance. They may play an important – perhaps even decisive – role in stabilizing democratic capitalism across advanced societies (Iversen and Soskice [Reference Iversen and Soskice2019](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref19)).
    
    However, we argue that there is also a highly relevant flipside to this line of reasoning: *Apprehensive* voters are relatively well-off in immediate material terms but are concerned about seeing society develop in a direction that threatens their and their children's sources of status and well-being. Consequently, they are wary of long-term structural change and its societal implications, perceiving ‘the system’ to steer in the wrong direction. Given that these voters perceive mainstream parties as complicit in the general thrust of economic and social change, we expect them to be generally more likely to support a radical contestation of mainstream parties' politics and policies. Specifically, a more diffuse sense of social dislocation should make these voters particularly receptive to the appeals of the radical right. In sum, we go beyond Iversen and Soskice ([Reference Iversen and Soskice2019](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref19)) by arguing that evaluations of prospective opportunities matter at lower *and* higher levels of material well-being.
    
    Drawing on original survey data fielded in eight Western European countries, we provide evidence that positive perceptions of long-term economic opportunity – intra- as well as intergenerational – are consistently related to lower support for radical parties (but not to *mainstream* opposition, as expected). Notably, ‘aspirational’ voters support radical (especially radical right) parties at below-average levels, in stark contrast to well-off but ‘apprehensive’ voters. Only voters who are both doing well and are confident about the future support incumbents at above-average levels. We discuss the political implications of these findings by pointing to the cross-national distribution of these groups: while a coalition of confident and aspirational voters makes up for 60–70 per cent of the electorate in the Nordic and Continental European countries, more than 55–60 per cent of voters in Spain and Italy evaluate economic and social long-term prospects negatively.
    
    Since this study is based on observational data, we cannot exclude that radical parties themselves generate and sustain negative views of the future among their supporters. However, while especially radical-right parties certainly mobilize such sentiment, our data indicate that the share of pessimistic voters does not relate to the size and strength of radical parties across countries. Rather, the shares of voters with optimistic versus pessimistic perceptions of future prospects seem to reflect structural context conditions: majority coalitions of optimistic voters emerge where welfare states are generous and accessible and where national knowledge economies provide labour market opportunities to match expanding skill supply.
    
    ## **Opportunity Perceptions and Electoral Choice**
    
    Empirical research on party system change in advanced democracies increasingly agrees that focusing on voters' objective and immediate socioeconomic status (for example, current income or employment status) is insufficient for explaining radical versus mainstream voting. Against many of the early hypotheses in this literature, according to which the unemployed, the poor and nonstandard ‘cheap labour’ was the recruiting ground for radical challenger parties (for example, Betz [Reference Betz1993](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref1); Esping-Andersen [Reference Esping-Andersen1999](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref8); King and Rueda [Reference King and Rueda2008](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref20); Lubbers, Gijsberts, and Scheepers [Reference Lubbers, Gijsberts and Scheepers2002](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref24); Mughan, Bean, and McAllister [Reference Mughan, Bean and McAllister2003](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref26)), we now know that the economic situation of voters needs to be theorized in their temporal and cross-sectional context. Accordingly, a burgeoning literature has adopted a more contextualized perspective on how socioeconomic circumstances might shape electoral choices.
    
    To situate our perspective, we outline three ways in which researchers have so far adapted conceptualizations of socioeconomic status as a driving factor of political behaviour by (1) highlighting the importance of status relative to others, (2) emphasizing dynamic changes in status over time, or (3) focusing on the subjective nature of status perceptions.
    
    First, research on *relative* deprivation emphasizes that voters compare their economic situation to others in society (Runciman [Reference Runciman1966](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref31)). If such comparisons lead voters to conclude that they are – unjustly in their view – dis-advantaged, this may fuel resentment and receptiveness for political appeals that play on that sentiment (Burgoon et al. [Reference Burgoon2019](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref2); Kurer [Reference Kurer2020](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref21); Pettigrew [Reference Pettigrew2017](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref29)). The importance of relative judgments of economic well-being is also evident in ethnographic studies, for instance, of members of rural or declining industrial communities who emphasize the struggles of their own working life while drawing comparisons to the (‘unfairly’) comfortable lives of professionals or public employees in thriving city centres (Cramer [Reference Cramer2016](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref4); Gest [Reference Gest2016](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref10); Hochschild [Reference Hochschild2016](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref17)).
    
    Second, some researchers have looked at how *dynamic* changes in voters' (objective or subjective) socioeconomic status might explain political preferences. Burgoon et al. ([Reference Burgoon2019](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref2)) study ‘positional deprivation’, defined as a situation where the increase in disposable income of an individual is smaller relative to the growth in income of other groups in the same country's income distribution. Kurer ([Reference Kurer2020](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref21)) explores the employment trajectories of threatened routine workers, distinguishing relative and absolute changes in status. Gest, Reny, and Mayer ([Reference Gest, Reny and Mayer2017](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref11)) explicitly ask survey respondents to compare the status of ‘people like them’ today to what they think it was 30 years ago, while a dynamic perspective is also implicit in other studies on subjective social status and nostalgia (Gidron and Hall [Reference Gidron and Hall2017](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref13); Steenvoorden and Harteveld [Reference Steenvoorden and Harteveld2018](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref33)). Again, ethnographic work has captured perceptions of relative status loss using qualitative methods. Hochschild's ([Reference Hochschild2016](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref17)) metaphor of a social escalator on which members of the ‘old’ middle classes feel that they are overtaken by members of ethnic minorities, immigrants, women, or public sector workers vividly illustrates how changes in relative social status might matter to individuals (for Germany, see also Nachtwey [Reference Nachtwey2016](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref28)).
    
    A third related strand of work has more generally emphasized the importance of *subjective* measures of immediate, relative, or dynamic deprivation (Teney, Lacewell, and Wilde [Reference Teney, Lacewell and Wilde2014](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref34)); or of subjective social status (Engler and Weisstanner [Reference Engler and Weisstanner2021](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref7); Mutz [Reference Mutz2018](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref27)). Notably, Gidron and Hall ([Reference Gidron and Hall2017](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref13)) show that lower levels of subjective social status are associated with support for the radical right. They trace how the self-perceived status of men without a college education (relative to women) has declined in the past decades due to structural social transformations. Even if the relevant temporal reference points are not always discussed upfront, these concepts inherently include a relational element. Subjective judgments of status are also ever-present in the aforementioned ethnographic studies, focusing on individuals’ interpretations of their economic circumstances.
    
    We learn from these contributions that to understand better how voters' economic situation affects their political behaviour, we need to capture how voters perceive their situation in context. And while this insight represents important progress, many status threats voters invoke when assessing their economic situation refer to the future – for example, work conditions deteriorating, risks of automation, prospects of ‘increasing’ immigration etc. Similarly, radical parties tend to operate with prospective narratives and scenarios of threat.
    
    Consequently, we explore how voters' expectations for their future relate to their electoral preferences. This prospective focus shares similarity with the economic voting literature, which emphasizes the relevance of subjective evaluations of economic circumstances for incumbent party support (Clarke et al. [Reference Clarke2004](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref3); Duch and Stevenson [Reference Duch and Stevenson2008](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref6); Lewis-Beck and Stegmaier [Reference Lewis-Beck, Stegmaier, Dalton and Klingemann2007](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref23)). Our approach is complementary in that it adds a more long-term structural perspective on evaluations of the economy. We distinguish between mainstream incumbent, mainstream opposition *and* radical parties because the contestation of established mainstream parties by radical challengers is at the core of a fundamental realignment of European party systems that cannot be conceptualized in terms of incumbent versus opposition (Hernández and Kriesi [Reference Hernández and Kriesi2016](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref16)).
    
    In theorizing the electoral implications of perceived prospects, we directly build on the argument that ‘aspirational voters’ play a vital role in stabilizing advanced capitalist democracies (Iversen and Soskice [Reference Iversen and Soskice2019](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref19)). According to Iversen and Soskice, aspirational voters are voters who are not (yet) direct beneficiaries of democratic capitalism but who believe that they or their children have good prospects of someday joining the ranks of skilled workers employed in knowledge-intensive sectors. Aspirational voters believe they will benefit from structural change in the longer run. In the authors' account, this segment of voters stands for political continuity and ‘reformism’, thereby enabling the growth that eventually fuels demand for skilled and educated labour.
    
    Conversely, Iversen and Soskice argue that anti-system appeals fall on fertile ground among those who feel excluded from the metaphorical escalator of progress. In this vein, the authors also point to the rapid spread of populism in the aftermath of the Great Recession. This observation resonates well with the findings of Hernández and Kriesi ([Reference Hernández and Kriesi2016](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref16)): With the economic crisis undermining confidence in incumbent parties, support for opposition forces is particularly likely to take the form of radical party support when voters cease to see a place for themselves and their children in an ever more knowledge-intensive, globalized society. Shorter-term, economic voting dynamics can hence accelerate the rise of radical parties.
    
    We build on the conceptual contribution of Iversen and Soskice but differentiate more explicitly between subjective prospects and current socioeconomic status. While a positive evaluation of future opportunities may function as a buffer against supporting radical parties even among lower-status voters, a negative view of future opportunities may also drive well-off voters towards the fringes of the political spectrum. There is no reason to think that economically secure individuals should be immune from losing confidence in the capacity of the political-economic order to provide prosperity for themselves and their children. Differentiating prospects and status develops our theoretical expectations beyond a median voter argument towards conceptualizing different groups, their preferences and – importantly – their relative weight in society. It also extends a classical economic voting perspective by identifying two groups (mainstream opposition voters and radical opposition voters) who might be equally prone to punish incumbent parties for short-term economic downturns but who differ in whether their rejection of the incumbent extends to a more fundamental contestation of the political-economic system as a whole.
    
    ## **Theoretical expectations**
    
    Lacking confidence in the system's capacity to provide opportunities likely results in opposition against parties representing this system. However, beyond this hypothesized general anti-system effect, we are interested in whether and how the impact of voters' opportunity perceptions on party preferences varies with their immediate economic circumstances. Can confidence in future prospects compensate for a less-than-ideal present? Even lower-status voters in today's knowledge-based economy might support the current economic framework based on a positive evaluation of social mobility prospects – for themselves or the next generation. At the same time, even someone with a relatively high economic status *today* need not necessarily believe in a bright future. This reasoning (which extends to Iversen and Soskice [Reference Iversen and Soskice2019](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#ref19)) results in a two-by-two table in which we distinguish voters with positive and negative expectations regarding their opportunities on the one hand and high versus low current income on the other (we will consider finer breakdowns later on in the empirics). [Table 1](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#tab01) summarizes our main expectations regarding distinctive electoral preference hierarchies for each quadrant.[Footnote1](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-political-science/article/aspiration-versus-apprehension-economic-opportunities-and-electoral-preferences/6B15219B0E8BABF7602AF056EA6DF3C5#fn1) Our expectations are fairly straightforward regarding the two unconflicted quadrants where economic circumstances and expectations about opportunities reinforce each other. First, high-income voters with a positive evaluation of future opportunities are beneficiaries of today's political-economic set-up, and they do not expect this to change. On average, these voters (whom we label ‘comfortable voters’) have every reason to support mainstream parties, particularly incumbent parties, because these have built and defended a system that delivers for them.

## Executive Summary and Theoretical Contribution

This groundbreaking study by Häusermann, Kurer, and Zollinger represents a significant theoretical advancement in understanding the relationship between economic conditions and political behavior. The authors move beyond traditional economic voting models to examine how **prospective evaluations** of long-term economic opportunities—rather than current material circumstances—shape support for mainstream versus radical parties across eight Western European countries.

The paper's central innovation lies in distinguishing between four distinct voter types based on the intersection of current income and future opportunity perceptions: **aspirational voters** (low income, positive prospects), **apprehensive voters** (high income, negative prospects), **comfortable voters** (high income, positive prospects), and **burdened voters** (low income, negative prospects). This framework challenges conventional wisdom by demonstrating that economic security alone does not immunize voters against radical party appeals.

## Research Motivation and Problem Statement

The authors begin by highlighting a fundamental tension in contemporary political science scholarship:

> "Current scholarship offers two strikingly different angles to look at the political repercussions of structural economic change. Some observers highlight the ever-growing share of highly educated citizens whose coveted skills guarantee aggregate prosperity and growth in modern knowledge economies. At the same time, an influential literature emphasizes that the gains of economic modernization are distributed in a highly unequal way, which produces a rising pool of dissatisfied voters."
> 

This dualistic perspective creates competing narratives about democratic stability. The first suggests that expanding education and skills in knowledge economies should create broad-based prosperity and political stability. The second warns of growing inequality producing electoral volatility and support for anti-system parties.

The research motivation emerges from recognizing that both narratives contain elements of truth, but existing scholarship lacks a framework for understanding their relative importance:

> "It stands to reason that both narratives hold some truth: stability and contestation hinge on the relative importance of the constituencies which either perceive that they have what it takes to thrive in the knowledge economy or are worried about their future place in a dynamic and fast-changing society."
> 

The authors identify a critical gap in the literature's focus on retrospective and present-oriented measures of economic conditions:

> "What these studies have in common is a conceptualization of voter perceptions that refers to their present or retrospective experience. We argue that many of the core conflicts arising from the emerging knowledge economy are of an equally relational and long-term oriented but inherently forward-looking nature: voters' belief in the political system is based on perceptions of prospective opportunities – for themselves as well as the next generation."
> 

## Literature Review and Theoretical Framework

### Evolution Beyond Objective Economic Indicators

The authors situate their contribution within the broader evolution of economic voting research. Traditional approaches focusing on objective socioeconomic status—income, employment, education—have proven insufficient for explaining contemporary political behavior, particularly the rise of radical parties:

> "Empirical research on party system change in advanced democracies increasingly agrees that focusing on voters' objective and immediate socioeconomic status (for example, current income or employment status) is insufficient for explaining radical versus mainstream voting."
> 

The literature has evolved through three key conceptual adaptations that the authors build upon:

**1. Relative Deprivation Approaches**
These emphasize social comparison processes, where voters evaluate their position relative to others. The authors note how ethnographic studies capture this dynamic:

> "The importance of relative judgments of economic well-being is also evident in ethnographic studies, for instance, of members of rural or declining industrial communities who emphasize the struggles of their own working life while drawing comparisons to the ('unfairly') comfortable lives of professionals or public employees in thriving city centres."
> 

**2. Dynamic Status Change Perspectives**
These examine how changes in economic position over time affect political preferences. The research includes studies of "positional deprivation" and employment trajectory analyses:

> "Burgoon et al. (2019) study 'positional deprivation', defined as a situation where the increase in disposable income of an individual is smaller relative to the growth in income of other groups in the same country's income distribution."
> 

**3. Subjective Status Perceptions**
This strand emphasizes how individuals' own interpretations of their circumstances matter more than objective conditions:

> "A third related strand of work has more generally emphasized the importance of subjective measures of immediate, relative, or dynamic deprivation... Notably, Gidron and Hall (2017) show that lower levels of subjective social status are associated with support for the radical right."
> 

### Integration with Economic Voting Literature

The authors connect their prospective framework to established economic voting research while extending it in important ways:

> "The expectation of a system-stabilizing effect of positive future opportunities is in line with core insights of the literature on economic voting, which concludes that voters' perceptions of economic conditions (versus their objective material conditions) and their short-term prospective (alongside retrospective) evaluation of the economy most consistently relate to support for incumbent government parties."
> 

However, they argue for going beyond the traditional incumbent/opposition distinction:

> "The fundamental, long-term nature of ongoing transformations of the economy and electoral realignments implies that beyond the familiar distinction between incumbent versus opposition parties, we should further distinguish between more versus less radical forms of opposition."
> 

### Building on Iversen and Soskice's Aspirational Voter Theory

The theoretical foundation builds directly on Iversen and Soskice's (2019) concept of aspirational voters, but with important extensions:

> "According to Iversen and Soskice, aspirational voters are voters who are not (yet) direct beneficiaries of democratic capitalism but who believe that they or their children have good prospects of someday joining the ranks of skilled workers employed in knowledge-intensive sectors."
> 

The authors' key theoretical innovation involves extending this logic across income levels:

> "We build on the conceptual contribution of Iversen and Soskice but differentiate more explicitly between subjective prospects and current socioeconomic status. While a positive evaluation of future opportunities may function as a buffer against supporting radical parties even among lower-status voters, a negative view of future opportunities may also drive well-off voters towards the fringes of the political spectrum."
> 

## Psychological Mechanisms and Social Psychology

### Status Threat and Identity Concerns Among Apprehensive Voters

The authors develop sophisticated psychological explanations for how economic vulnerability translates into political attitudes, particularly for their concept of "apprehensive voters." These are individuals who are materially well-off but perceive declining future opportunities:

> "Precisely because we define apprehensive voters as being well-off in terms of immediate material conditions (notably having relatively higher income), we would expect their concerns to take a more diffuse form, spreading to (and easily directed at) the more encompassing ways in which the economy and society are changing."
> 

The psychological mechanism operates through perceived threats to social identity and status hierarchy:

> "Rather than worrying about immediate risks that might be mitigated within the existing system of advanced democratic capitalism – for example, with social insurance and transfers – these voters experience a more fundamental sense of dislocation as occupational status hierarchies, family models, gender roles, and other aspects of social life transform."
> 

This connects to broader social psychological theories about how perceived threats to group status and identity can motivate political behavior, even among those who are objectively secure.

### Intergenerational Concerns and Future-Oriented Anxiety

A particularly important psychological mechanism involves concerns about children's prospects:

> "Such sentiments, in particular, provide a link to support for the radical right, which has posed the primary challenge to mainstream parties in recent decades. Consequently, our study integrates insights from the economic voting literature on government support with the findings of a growing literature highlighting the implications of a more structural backlash against economic change and the political elite."
> 

The authors measured both personal opportunity perceptions and assessments of children's futures, recognizing that parental anxiety about next-generation prospects can drive political behavior independently of current circumstances.

### Dignity, Identity, and Belonging

The psychological mechanisms extend beyond material concerns to deeper questions of social recognition:

> "Moreover, the above-cited literature on changing social hierarchies in times of structural change indicates that voters' sense of (lacking) opportunity relates to deeper grievances linked to questions of dignity, identity and belonging in a changing society."
> 

This insight connects to broader literatures on politics of recognition, social identity theory, and research on how economic change affects cultural and symbolic resources that individuals use to construct meaningful identities.

## Methodology and Variable Operationalization

### Data Collection and Sample

The study utilizes original survey data from eight Western European countries collected between October-December 2018, with 1,500 respondents per country (total N=12,506). Countries included Denmark, Sweden, Germany, Netherlands, Ireland, UK, Italy, and Spain, providing variation across welfare regimes and economic performance contexts.

### Key Variable Measurements

**Opportunity Perceptions (Independent Variables):**

- Personal employment opportunities: "If you think of your future, how do you rate your personal chances of being in good, stable employment until you will retire?"
- Personal life opportunities: "How do you rate your personal chances of having a safe, fulfilled life over your life course?"
- Children's employment opportunities: Similar questions about respondents' children's prospects
- Children's life opportunities: General life prospects for respondents' children

**Electoral Preferences (Dependent Variable):**
The authors create a three-category classification:

- **Mainstream incumbent parties**: Parties currently in government
- **Mainstream opposition parties**: Traditional opposition parties working within existing system
- **Radical parties**: Parties fundamentally challenging existing political-economic order (includes radical left, radical right, and anti-establishment parties like Italy's M5S)

**Income Measurement:**
Country-specific median income used to create high/low income categories, though continuous measures also employed in interaction models.

**Control Variables:**
Extensive socio-demographic controls including age, gender, education (8 categories), occupational class (7 categories using EGP scheme), plus country fixed effects.

### Analytical Approach

**Primary Models:**

- Multinomial logistic regression with mainstream incumbent support as reference category
- Separate logistic regressions for each party type to avoid reference category limitations
- Interaction models between income and opportunity perceptions

**Robustness Checks:**

- Alternative operationalizations of opportunity measures
- Different country combinations
- Separate analysis of radical left vs. radical right parties
- Models excluding Green parties from mainstream category

## Empirical Findings and Cross-References to Theory

### Direct Effects of Opportunity Perceptions

The basic relationship between opportunity perceptions and party support provides strong initial evidence for the theoretical framework:

> "Among respondents with more pessimistic views of future prospects, support for mainstream opposition parties is only marginally significantly higher than for incumbents... However, these voters are significantly more likely to vote for radical parties."
> 

This finding supports the key theoretical distinction between mainstream and radical opposition, showing that negative opportunity perceptions drive voters away from the entire mainstream political establishment, not just incumbent parties.

### The Four Voter Types: Empirical Validation

**Comfortable Voters (High Income, Positive Prospects):**
As predicted, these voters show strong support for the existing system:

> "High-income voters with a positive evaluation of future opportunities are beneficiaries of today's political-economic set-up, and they do not expect this to change. On average, these voters... have every reason to support mainstream parties, particularly incumbent parties, because these have built and defended a system that delivers for them."
> 

**Burdened Voters (Low Income, Negative Prospects):**
These voters demonstrate the expected pattern of radical party support:

> "Low-income voters with negative evaluations of their future opportunities... are not faring well in the modern knowledge economy and they perceive their long-term perspectives to be as bleak. Hence, we expect them to support radical parties over-proportionally, followed by mainstream opposition parties."
> 

**Aspirational Voters (Low Income, Positive Prospects):**
The key theoretical group shows the predicted buffering effect:

> "Lower-income voters who are nevertheless optimistic about opportunities correspond to Iversen and Soskice's aspirational voters: they have not (yet) fared particularly well, but they are confident that the existing political framework generates opportunities for them and their children."
> 

**Apprehensive Voters (High Income, Negative Prospects):**
This theoretically crucial group validates the extension beyond traditional economic voting:

> "We are left with higher-income voters who evaluate future prospects negatively (our 'apprehensive voters'): these are 'winners' of the prevailing political-economic order, but they nevertheless worry about what the future might hold for them or their children."
> 

### Radical Right Specificity

The decomposition of radical party support reveals important psychological mechanisms:

> "Indeed, we see that aspirational and apprehensive voters differ clearly regarding radical right support (with apprehensive voters displaying higher support) but not regarding radical left support, where these groups are fairly average."
> 

This finding supports the theoretical argument about diffuse status anxiety among apprehensive voters making them particularly receptive to radical right appeals focused on cultural change and identity threats.

### Interaction Effects and Reinforcement

The interaction models reveal that income and opportunity perceptions can both substitute for and reinforce each other:

> "The three panels in Fig. 6 provide evidence of a partly reinforcing impact of our two explanatory variables. Panel (a) confirms our interpretation that the combination of high-income and positive opportunity perceptions markedly increases support for parties in government."
> 

However, for radical party support, the substitution effect is limited:

> "One important implication that Figs 6 and 7 jointly provide is that higher socioeconomic status proxied by income levels cannot effectively compensate for the lack of positive opportunity perceptions when it comes to radical voting."
> 

## Policy Implications and Social Investment Connections

### Welfare State and Social Investment Mechanisms

The authors provide crucial insights into how welfare state policies might moderate radical party support through their effects on opportunity perceptions. The cross-national variation reveals systematic patterns:

> "Rather, the shares of voters with optimistic versus pessimistic perceptions of future prospects seem to reflect structural context conditions: majority coalitions of optimistic voters emerge where welfare states are generous and accessible and where national knowledge economies provide labour market opportunities to match expanding skill supply."
> 

This finding directly connects to social investment theory, suggesting that policies expanding access to education, training, and knowledge economy employment can create larger coalitions of aspirational voters who stabilize democratic capitalism.

### Policy Leverage Through Opportunity Enhancement

The authors explicitly discuss policy implications, emphasizing the forward-looking nature of their findings:

> "A key reason why we contend it is worthwhile investigating prospective evaluations relates to their policy implications: While nostalgic visions of the past may not be impossible to combat, people's prospective evaluations are arguably the more important political battleground: unlike retrospective views, parties have the chance of improving people's evaluations of opportunities not just by supplying narratives of promise and hope, but through policies that will have a tangible impact on people's lives."
> 

The policy mechanisms operate through multiple channels:

> "If open education systems, social investment, and opportunities for social mobility affect these evaluations positively, this implies certain policy leverage to limit support for radical parties. Policies could even have a double effect on political preferences via perceptions of parents on the one hand and via both immediate economic circumstances and perceptions among their children on the other hand."
> 

### Intergenerational Policy Effects

The study's focus on children's opportunities reveals important policy mechanisms:

> "The varying size of the aspirational voter group across the countries in our study indicates that some policy leverage may indeed exist for how younger voters, people with medium levels of education, or service sector workers view their future opportunities."
> 

This suggests that social investment policies targeting education, skills development, and labor market opportunities could have powerful political effects by expanding the aspirational voter coalition while reducing apprehensive and burdened voter populations.

## Cross-National Variation and Structural Context

### Knowledge Economy Development and Political Coalitions

The cross-national findings reveal striking patterns that connect economic structure to political stability:

> "Continental and Northern European countries are characterized by a large share of voters (60–70 per cent) who believe in economic opportunity for themselves and their children. However, voters in the European South show a much stronger prevalence of negative perceptions of economic opportunity."
> 

This variation occurs despite short-term economic recovery in Southern Europe:

> "This pattern across countries is particularly telling given the fact that the Southern European countries, as well as Ireland, were on a striking path of economic upswing and recovery in the years leading up to 2018, the year of our survey. Unemployment fell sharply, and growth was robust after 2015 in these countries."
> 

### Self-Reinforcing Dynamics

The authors identify potentially self-reinforcing cycles between economic structure and political coalitions:

> "The relative strength of the four groups in Southern Europe represents a political configuration with much more limited support for creating and sustaining modern knowledge economies. However, this also points towards a potentially self-reinforcing dynamic, whereby less knowledge-based economies are less likely to create and sustain the electoral coalitions that might support pro-system agendas set to further deepen the knowledge economy within the existing framework."
> 

This insight has profound implications for understanding how welfare state and social investment policies might break negative cycles by creating more optimistic opportunity perceptions and thus more stable political coalitions.

## Socio-Demographic Profiles and Targeting

### Characteristics of Key Voter Types

The detailed socio-demographic analysis provides insights for policy targeting:

**Aspirational Voters:**

> "Aspirational voters are typically relatively young (18–35)... Service workers (and small business owners, but not production workers) or people in non-routine manual work are also well-represented in the aspirational group, as are people with medium levels of education."
> 

**Apprehensive Voters:**

> "Apprehensive voters are typically middle-aged (36–55)... routine work is typical for apprehensive voters. The contrast between aspirational young service employees and apprehensive middle-aged routine employees bolsters our confidence in the operationalization of these quadrants."
> 

These profiles suggest specific policy interventions: supporting young service workers' advancement opportunities while addressing middle-aged routine workers' concerns about technological displacement and changing skill requirements.

## Theoretical Extensions and Future Research

### Beyond Economic Voting to Structural Change

The study fundamentally extends economic voting theory by incorporating structural economic transformation:

> "Our approach is complementary in that it adds a more long-term structural perspective on evaluations of the economy. We distinguish between mainstream incumbent, mainstream opposition and radical parties because the contestation of established mainstream parties by radical challengers is at the core of a fundamental realignment of European party systems that cannot be conceptualized in terms of incumbent versus opposition."
> 

### Psychological Mechanisms for Future Investigation

The authors identify several psychological mechanisms requiring further research:

1. **Status Anxiety Among the Materially Secure:** Understanding how economic security fails to translate into political security when future prospects appear dim.
2. **Intergenerational Transmission:** How parental concerns about children's opportunities affect political behavior independently of personal circumstances.
3. **Identity and Dignity Concerns:** How changing occupational hierarchies and social roles create political grievances beyond material considerations.

## Limitations and Methodological Considerations

### Causal Identification Challenges

The authors acknowledge important limitations:

> "Since this study is based on observational data, we cannot exclude that radical parties themselves generate and sustain negative views of the future among their supporters. However, while especially radical-right parties certainly mobilize such sentiment, our data indicate that the share of pessimistic voters does not relate to the size and strength of radical parties across countries."
> 

### Robustness and Sensitivity

The study includes extensive robustness checks, but several important questions remain about generalizability across different contexts and time periods, particularly regarding:

1. Whether findings hold during economic crises
2. How opportunity perceptions evolve over individual lifecycles
3. The stability of the four-group typology across electoral cycles

## Conclusion and Broader Implications

This study makes several crucial contributions to understanding the psychology of political behavior in knowledge economies:

### Theoretical Advancement

The framework moves beyond simple economic determinism to show how subjective evaluations of future opportunities create cross-cutting political coalitions that transcend traditional class divisions. The distinction between aspirational and apprehensive voters provides a nuanced understanding of how economic change affects political stability.

### Policy Implications

The findings suggest that social investment and welfare state policies can influence political outcomes not just through redistributive effects, but by shaping citizens' perceptions of future opportunities. This provides a new rationale for social investment that goes beyond traditional economic arguments.

### Social Psychological Insights

The study illuminates how economic vulnerability translates into political attitudes through mechanisms of status anxiety, identity threat, and intergenerational concern. These insights bridge individual-level psychological processes with macro-political outcomes.

The research ultimately suggests that democratic stability in knowledge economies depends not just on current prosperity, but on maintaining widespread confidence in future opportunities. This insight has profound implications for how we understand, measure, and respond to the political challenges of economic transformation in advanced democracies.

> "Hence, these findings obviously raise the question of which policies affect evaluations of future opportunities. If open education systems, social investment, and opportunities for social mobility affect these evaluations positively, this implies certain policy leverage to limit support for radical parties."
> 

The study thus provides both a theoretical framework for understanding contemporary political volatility and practical insights for policy interventions that might enhance democratic stability through expanding opportunity perceptions across different socioeconomic groups.
