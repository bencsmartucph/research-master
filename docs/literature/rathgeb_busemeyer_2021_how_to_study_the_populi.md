## Central Thesis

Studying the relationship between PRRPs and the welfare state requires bridging two research traditions that have largely operated in isolation: party politics/voting behavior research on one side, and comparative political economy/welfare state research on the other.

---

## The Particularistic-Authoritarian Welfare State

PRRPs and their voters support what the authors call a **"particularistic-authoritarian welfare state"** that:

- Caters to 'hard-working' people with long contribution records in social insurance (e.g. the elderly and labour market insiders)
- Comes at the expense of new social risk groups (e.g. women, low-skilled and the young) and people with precarious labour market attachments

PRRP supporters display:

- **Moderate support** only for 'deserving' benefit recipients (i.e. the elderly)
- **Strong support** for a workfare approach
- **Little support** for social investment

---

## Multi-Dimensional Welfare State Framework

Three key dimensions:

1. **Social consumption/transfers** — traditional redistributive policies like pensions *(PRRPs: strong support)*
2. **Social investment** — education, childcare, ALMP *(PRRPs: strong opposition)*
3. **Workfare** — conditional welfare with obligations *(PRRPs: strong support)*

PRRPs are the "main opponent to the recalibration of welfare states to the social demands of post-industrial labour markets."

## Status Anxiety and Social Decline

> "Gidron and Hall (2017) show how economic and cultural developments interact in shaping a 'status anxiety' that increases the likelihood of voting for the radical right, especially among a core group of working-class men."
> 

> "The threat of decline in social status, rather than actual outcomes of decline (e.g. unemployment, poverty), drives voters into the hands of PRRPs, as the latter offer voters the prospect of defending traditional social boundaries."
> 

### Welfare Chauvinism as Status Restoration

> "Cuts for non-citizens may help to restore the relative social status of previously dominant political groups, typically the male, core workforce in manufacturing."
> 

---

## Welfare States and PRRP Support

Vlandas and Halikiopoulou find that "the welfare state mitigates social risks, it also moderates the impact of economic insecurity on support for the radical right."

Burgoon and Schakel further demonstrate that "generous welfare states also dampen the anti-globalisation backlash among radical right parties."

## Future Research Directions

1. Whether PRRPs across countries support a common welfare state model
2. How different welfare state arrangements affect support for PRRPs post-pandemic
3. Whether welfare policies can address the status concerns driving PRRP support
4. Evolution of PRRP electorates as these parties develop clearer socioeconomic positions
5. How PRRPs' distinctive welfare model affects their potential as coalition partners
