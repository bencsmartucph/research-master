# (Rickard, 2023) Electoral Consequences of Compensation for Globalization This paper examines how targeted compensation for workers displaced by globalization affects voting behavior, specifically support for protectionist political parties. The study focuses on the European Globalisation Adjustment Fund (EGF), which provides assistance to workers in EU member states who lose their jobs due to globalization.

This paper investigates whether targeted economic assistance to workers displaced by globalization can reduce support for protectionist political parties, using the European Globalisation Adjustment Fund (EGF) as a case study. I'll provide a comprehensive analysis of the research, examining its theoretical framework, methodology, findings, and implications.

## 1. Research Context and Significance

The study addresses a critical political economy question that has gained urgency in recent years: Can governments mitigate the electoral backlash against globalization through compensation policies? This research is timely given rising anti-globalization sentiment across Europe and other developed economies.

As opposition to globalization grows, many governments seek policy responses. One response – the European Globalisation Adjustment Fund – provides support to workers in European Union member states who are made redundant as a result of globalization.

The EGF represents a direct policy response to this challenge, as Compelling evidence suggests that voters' hostility towards globalization is due, at least in part, to economic openness. Skepticism about globalization increased after a period of rising imports. In regions exposed to surging imports, voters across 15 European countries disproportionately turned to political parties that opposed globalization (Colantone and Stanig, 2018b; Milner, 2021).

The problem is particularly acute because This evidence suggests that economic liberalism itself may contribute to the backlash against globalization (Lake et al., 2021). This creates a dilemma for policymakers committed to economic openness but facing electoral opposition to those very policies.

The research makes two primary contributions:

1. The meso-level analysis, which is conducted at both the district (i.e. département) and commune levels, provides a novel assessment of how compensation affects regional voting patterns. Trade shocks impact regional voting, as a growing number of studies document (e.g. Ballard-Rosa et al., 2022; Colantone and Stanig, 2018b; Dorn et al., 2020). Yet, the influence of compensation on regional voting patterns has gone largely unexplored.
2. Using an innovative empirical strategy, this study brings new evidence to debates over compensation. While some argue that compensation can lessen protectionist parties' appeal (e.g. Foster and Frieden, 2019; Halikiopoulou and Vlandas, 2016; Roubini, 2016), others question the efficacy of compensation and cast doubt on the ability of government assistance programmes to assuage voters' discontent with globalization (e.g. Gidron and Hall, 2017; Rodrik, 2017). By employing a difference-in-differences research design, this article advances the literature beyond cross-national correlations and provides novel evidence of the causal connection between compensation and support for globalization.

## 2. Theoretical Framework: Embedded Liberalism

### 2.1 Core Concept of Embedded Liberalism

The study is grounded in the concept of "embedded liberalism," a theoretical framework with deep roots in political economy that was originally developed by John Ruggie (1982). According to this theory, governments can maintain public support for economic openness by providing compensation that minimizes the adjustment costs of international economic integration.

In the post-war period, governments anticipated that policy could be used to minimize socially disruptive adjustment costs and any national political vulnerabilities that might accrue from the multilateral system. By reducing the negative impacts of international economic integration on workers' material well-being, it was believed that government-funded 'compensation' programmes could bolster public support for economic liberalism and the political parties that support it.

The embedded liberalism thesis has been extensively developed in subsequent work by scholars such as Hays (2009) and Hays et al. (2005), who argued that government spending can help maintain public support for trade openness. Frieden (2018) further elaborated on how the post-war international economic order was built around the idea that domestic welfare states would cushion citizens from the volatility of international markets.

The EGF was explicitly designed within this framework, as evidenced by statements from its proponents. For instance, The former European Commissioner for Trade, Peter Mandelson, said that the EGF was an important policy response in light of 'a wind of opposition to openness and a certain populist drift' in 'some member states' (Tovi, 2006).

Similarly, Peter Mandelson, the European Commissioner for Trade from 2004 to 2008, said that 'the simplistic solutions of protectionism and economic nationalism will lead nowhere. The opening up of our economies is the only guarantee of our prosperity and the preservation of our social model in Europe. These changes must be accompanied [by support]. That is why I have been so vocal in favour of the principle of a European Globalisation Adjustment Fund' (Tovi, 2006).

Furthermore, When launching the Fund in 2006, Barroso said: 'We want to reconcile European public views with globalisation' (Commission, 2006). This explicitly frames the EGF as an attempt to maintain public support for economic openness.

### 2.2 Empirical Evidence on Embedded Liberalism

Prior empirical research on embedded liberalism has yielded mixed findings. Hays et al. (2005) found evidence supporting the thesis, showing that higher levels of government spending were associated with greater public support for trade openness in OECD countries. However, more recent studies like Ehrlich and Hearn (2014) suggest that the relationship may be more complex than initially thought. Using survey experiments, they found that information about trade-related compensation increased support for free trade only among lower-income individuals, not across the board.

Swank and Betz (2003) examined the relationship between welfare states and right-wing populism, finding that countries with more generous and universalistic welfare systems experienced less support for radical right parties. This suggests that comprehensive welfare provisions might help mitigate anti-globalization sentiment. However, as Gidron and Hall (2017) note, welfare state provisions alone may not address the status-based concerns that often fuel support for populist parties.

Foster and Frieden (2019) found evidence that fiscal austerity was associated with increased support for anti-EU parties, suggesting that reductions in compensation might contribute to anti-globalization sentiment. This is consistent with Fetzer's (2019) finding that austerity measures in the UK contributed to support for Brexit.

### 2.3 Theoretical Mechanisms

The theoretical framework suggests several mechanisms through which EGF assistance might reduce support for protectionist parties:

### 2.3.1 Direct Economic Impact

EGF assistance may offset some of the costs of globalization-induced job losses. Because EGF funding allowed for more significant retraining than was available to workers through national-level programmes alone, the reemployment rate of EGF beneficiaries is generally higher than that of non-beneficiaries (Weber et al., 2015). In this way, EGF assistance offsets at least some of the costs of globalization by helping displaced workers find new jobs, thereby limiting the appeal of protectionist parties who seek the support of voters in economic distress from globalization (e.g. De Bromhead et al., 2013; Swank and Betz, 2003).

This mechanism draws on the economic voting literature, which suggests that voters reward incumbent parties when economic conditions are good and punish them when conditions deteriorate (Lewis-Beck and Stegmaier, 2000). De Bromhead et al. (2013) showed that economic distress was associated with increased support for extremist parties in interwar Europe, while Swank and Betz (2003) found that economic insecurity contributed to the rise of right-wing populist parties in contemporary Europe.

### 2.3.2 Regional Economic Spillovers

EGF assistance may reduce the appeal of protectionist parties by helping to stave off the cascading regional effects of trade shocks. Trade shocks have negative effects on local labour markets (Autor et al., 2013) and because individuals are increasingly linked together by economic spillovers, individuals' economic welfare tends to rise and fall with local economic conditions (Broz et al., 2021). If EGF assistance helps globalization-displaced workers become re-employed more quickly, as evidence suggests (Claeys and Sapir, 2020), then the surrounding local area may be spared some of the negative economic outcomes that arise from trade shocks, such as lower local wages and employment levels.

Autor et al. (2013) documented how import competition from China had significant negative effects on local US labor markets, with impacts extending beyond manufacturing to affect local non-tradable sectors. Broz et al. (2021) showed how these localized economic effects translated into political outcomes, with trade-exposed regions more likely to support populist candidates.

Additionally, every re-employed worker no longer has an incentive to move away from the local community in search of a new job, which helps to avert a decrease in the local population, the subsequent shrinking of the local tax base and a decline in public services (Broz et al., 2021; Weber et al., 2015). If EGF assistance helps to improve local economic conditions, it may reduce the propensity of voters in trade-exposed regions to support protectionist parties out of economic self-interest.

### 2.3.3 Local Sociotropism

Local sociotropism refers to the idea that voters are concerned about the economic well-being of their community – not because it affects their own financial situation – but because they care about the well-being of those closest to themselves, including their neighbours and local community (e.g. Ansolabehere et al., 2014; Kiewiet and Lewis-Beck, 2011; Rickard, 2022b). Research shows that voters often consider the interests of others when formulating their attitudes about economic policies and the responsible governments (e.g. Lü et al., 2012; Mansfield and Mutz, n.d.a,n; Rickard, 2022b).

Ansolabehere et al. (2014) provided evidence that voters base their economic judgments on local economic conditions rather than national statistics. Kiewiet and Lewis-Beck (2011) argued that sociotropic economic voting reflects genuine concern for collective welfare rather than disguised self-interest. Mansfield and Mutz (2009) showed that attitudes toward trade are influenced more by sociotropic concerns about the national economy than by self-interest.

Because individuals have a social and psychological stake in their communities, voters may turn to protectionist parties out of a sense of 'place-based' threat in response to local trade shocks (Cramer, 2016). However, if EGF assistance is seen as helping members of the community, it may keep voters from turning to protectionist parties in trade-exposed areas.

Cramer's (2016) ethnographic work in rural Wisconsin documented how people developed a "politics of resentment" partly in response to perceptions that their communities were being left behind economically. This place-based identity became a powerful political force that could drive voting behavior.

### 2.3.4 Political Signaling

Voters may see the EGF as an admission by governments that greater economic openness entails costs and these costs should be addressed by government action (Cramer, 2016). Such an admission may help to 'depoliticize' globalization and lessen the appeal of protectionist parties. This may be why many governments are keen to make EGF assistance visible to voters (Claeys and Sapir, 2020).

The French government, for example, distributes information about EGF support to local stakeholders and places leaflets with information about EGF assistance on notice boards at impacted employment sites. The French government also issues press releases upon adoption of EGF support and highlights it on the relevant Ministry's website.

This mechanism relates to work on political communication and framing. By publicly acknowledging the costs of globalization and demonstrating action to address them, governments may reduce the ability of anti-globalization parties to monopolize the issue. This connects to Meguid's (2005) theory of issue ownership, which suggests that mainstream parties can reduce support for niche parties by adopting their issues.

## 3. Research Design and Methodology

### 3.1 Case Selection

The author chose to focus primarily on France, studying the impact of EGF assistance on voting for the National Front (FN, now Rassemblement National), a party that explicitly identifies globalization as the main culprit behind many of the economic difficulties faced by workers. It advocates increased barriers to trade in order to combat 'unfair competition' from foreign imports (Ivaldi, 2013; Malgouyres, 2017b; Swank and Betz, 2003). The party proposed a 'social contribution' tariff that would impose a 3% tax on the price of imported products (de Vergés, 2017).

France was selected for several compelling reasons:

1. France championed the creation of the EGF. In an article published in 26 European newspapers, French President Jacques Chirac urged EU member states to 'find the strength of a new impetus' to respond to the 'concerns' of their fellow citizens about globalization (Chirac, 2005).
2. France was the first country to use the programme.
3. Studying a single country keeps institutional factors constant and avoids the complications of comparing different anti-globalization parties across countries.

### 3.2 Empirical Strategy

The author employs a difference-in-differences research design, which compares the change in the FN's vote shares between elections in two types of trade-exposed areas: (a) those in which globalization-displaced workers received EGF assistance (i.e. treated areas); and (b) those in which globalization-displaced workers did not receive EGF support (i.e. control areas).

This methodology is appropriate because By comparing the change in choices of voters in treated and control groups over time instead of directly comparing both groups' choices in a particular election, the difference-in-differences estimator helps to rule out alternative explanations.

The key assumption for difference-in-differences is that treatment and control areas would have followed parallel trends in the absence of treatment. ### 3.3 Units of Analysis

The study primarily examines French départements, which are sub-national geographic units that approximate local economies. The original design of départements was economically motivated and départements continue to represent meaningful lines of demarcation for both economic activities and economic networks (Combes et al., 2005).

As a robustness check, the author also conducts analysis at the commune level (the smallest administrative unit in France) and examines individual-level voting data from the French Electoral Study.

### 3.4 Variables and Measurement

**Dependent Variable**: The FN's vote share in the first round of presidential elections, specifically between 2007 and 2012.

**Independent Variable (Treatment)**: The author uses three measures of EGF assistance:

1. A binary indicator (EGF) equal to 1 if workers in a département received EGF support between elections.
2. The total amount of EGF assistance as a percentage of the département's GDP.
3. The per capita amount of EGF assistance in a département.

**Control Variables**: The models include several département-level controls:

- Population (natural log)
- GDP (natural log)
- GDP per capita
- Employment rate
- Percentage of foreign-born residents

**Sample Selection**: Importantly, the sample is restricted to only those départements exposed to rising imports, as indicated by a geographic-specific measure of import exposure constructed by interacting the initial sectoral composition of each local economy with nationwide sector-specific imports originating from low-wage countries from 1995 to 2012 (Malgouyres, 2017a). This ensures a comparison between trade-exposed areas with and without EGF assistance.

### 3.5 Statistical Model

The author estimates a fixed-effects regression model:

Yit = νi + δt + αDit + Xitβ + ϵit

Where:

- Yit is the FN's vote share in département i at time t
- δt is a period dummy (Post period)
- νi is a département-level fixed effect
- α is the average treatment effect on the treated (ATT)
- Dit indicates treated departments in the post-treatment period
- Xit is a vector of time-varying covariates
- ϵit is an idiosyncratic error term

## 4. Results and Findings

### 4.1 Département-Level Results

The main results from the département-level analysis show that:

1. All estimated Average Treatment Effects on the Treated (ATTs) using the binary treatment indicator are negative but small in magnitude, ranging from -0.34 to -0.52 percentage points, and not statistically significant at conventional levels.
2. Using the intensity of treatment measures:
    - When measuring EGF as a percentage of département GDP, two ATTs are statistically significant at the 10% level. The average of these coefficients suggests that the maximum observed EGF assistance would reduce FN vote shares by about 1.03 percentage points.
    - When measuring EGF per capita, five ATTs are statistically significant, with an average coefficient suggesting that maximum observed EGF assistance would reduce FN vote shares by nearly 1.5 percentage points.

The EGF's modest impact may reflect, in part, the size of the programme. During the period under investigation, France received just €7.8m a year from the EGF on average. Because EGF assistance is relatively modest, it may be reasonable to expect its' effects to be small. Furthermore, EGF assistance is provided in addition to other national support programmes. As a result, the EGF treatment effect equals the marginal impact of additional compensation on protectionist voting.

### 4.2 Commune-Level Results

At the commune level, the estimated treatment effects are similarly negative and small in magnitude. In the restricted sample of larger communes (top 5% by population), the ATT is -1.416 and statistically significant at the 5% level. This indicates that among communes in trade-exposed départements with relatively similar sized populations, the FN's vote share was 1.416 percentage points lower in those that received EGF assistance than in those that did not.

The author concludes that the modest EGF effect is unlikely to be an artefact of the sample size or the unit of analysis. Instead, the treatment effect appears to be consistently small and likely reduces the FN's vote share by no more than 1.5 percentage points.

### 4.3 Individual-Level Results

The individual-level analysis using survey data from the French Electoral Study reveals that the treatment effect is not statistically significant. Individuals living in trade-exposed treated départements are no less likely to vote for the FN than individuals in untreated trade-exposed départements, controlling for age, education, employment status, and sex.

The author also explores potential heterogeneous treatment effects based on:

1. Attitudes toward the EU: No evidence that EU attitudes modify the treatment effect.
2. Income levels: No evidence that income levels modify the treatment effect.

### 4.4 Cross-Country Generalization: Denmark

To probe the generalizability of the French results, the author examines Denmark, where Between the 2007 and 2011 national elections, Denmark received more money from the EGF than any other EU member state, making it a potentially decisive case.

The Danish results mirror the French findings: All of the ATTs are negatively signed and estimated with uncertainty. The Danish ATTs are also similar in magnitude to the French ATTs; they range from −0.382 to −0.421.

The similarity between results from Denmark and France is notable given their different political institutions. Denmark is a parliamentary system with proportional representation, while France is a semi-presidential system with two-round plurality voting. The Danish results come from national parliamentary elections, while the French results come from presidential elections.

## 5. Social Psychological Mechanisms

The paper explores several social psychological mechanisms that might explain how compensation policies like the EGF influence voting behavior. These mechanisms help us understand the complex relationship between economic assistance and political attitudes:

### 5.1 Individual Economic Self-Interest

The most direct mechanism is that EGF assistance helps individuals who have lost jobs due to globalization, reducing their economic hardship and thereby diminishing their incentive to vote for anti-globalization parties out of economic self-interest. As discussed earlier, EGF assistance may offset some of the costs of globalization-induced job losses. Because EGF funding allowed for more significant retraining than was available to workers through national-level programmes alone, the reemployment rate of EGF beneficiaries is generally higher than that of non-beneficiaries (Weber et al., 2015). In this way, EGF assistance offsets at least some of the costs of globalization by helping displaced workers find new jobs, thereby limiting the appeal of protectionist parties who seek the support of voters in economic distress from globalization (e.g. De Bromhead et al., 2013; Swank and Betz, 2003).

De Bromhead et al. (2013) showed that economic distress in the interwar period was associated with increased support for extremist parties, particularly in regions hard-hit by unemployment. Their analysis of electoral outcomes in 171 regions across 27 countries from 1919-1939 demonstrated that economic hardship was a key factor in the rise of extremist parties.

Swank and Betz (2003) examined 16 European countries from 1981-1998 and found that extensive welfare state protections could mitigate the electoral appeal of right-wing populist parties, particularly in contexts of high unemployment and immigration. They argued that comprehensive welfare states may reduce economic insecurity and thereby limit the effectiveness of anti-globalization appeals.

However, the individual-level analysis found no robust effect of EGF assistance on the probability of voting for the FN. This suggests that direct economic self-interest may not be the primary mechanism at work, or that the EGF's modest size limits its impact at the individual level.

### 5.2 Local Economic Conditions

The paper emphasizes that trade shocks have localized effects on sub-national labor markets, creating negative spillovers that extend beyond the directly affected workers. EGF assistance may reduce the appeal of protectionist parties by helping to stave off the cascading regional effects of trade shocks. Trade shocks have negative effects on local labour markets (Autor et al., 2013) and because individuals are increasingly linked together by economic spillovers, individuals' economic welfare tends to rise and fall with local economic conditions (Broz et al., 2021).

Autor et al. (2013) provided empirical evidence from the US that rising import competition from China significantly reduced manufacturing employment in affected local labor markets. Their research demonstrated that these effects spilled over to non-tradable sectors, reducing overall employment rates and wages in affected communities.

Broz et al. (2021) extended this analysis to political outcomes, showing that regions more exposed to import competition were more likely to support populist candidates in the US, UK, and France. They documented how the localized economic effects of trade created geographic patterns of discontent that manifested in electoral outcomes.

This mechanism recognizes that voters' political preferences are shaped not just by their personal economic circumstances, but also by the broader economic environment in which they live. When local economic conditions deteriorate, even those who maintain their jobs may experience wage stagnation, declining property values, or reduced public services, potentially increasing their support for protectionist parties.

### 5.3 Local Sociotropism

Perhaps the most intriguing psychological mechanism explored is "local sociotropism," the idea that people care about their community's economic well-being independently of their own material interests. Local sociotropism refers to the idea that voters are concerned about the economic well-being of their community – not because it affects their own financial situation – but because they care about the well-being of those closest to themselves, including their neighbours and local community (e.g. Ansolabehere et al., 2014; Kiewiet and Lewis-Beck, 2011; Rickard, 2022b). Research shows that voters often consider the interests of others when formulating their attitudes about economic policies and the responsible governments (e.g. Lü et al., 2012; Mansfield and Mutz, n.d.a,n; Rickard, 2022b).

Ansolabehere et al. (2014) analyzed US survey data and found that voters' economic evaluations were strongly influenced by local economic conditions rather than national statistics. They argued that this reflected voters' use of local information to form judgments about broader economic trends rather than pure self-interest.

Kiewiet and Lewis-Beck (2011) reviewed the extensive literature on sociotropic voting and argued that it reflects genuine concern for collective welfare rather than disguised self-interest. They presented evidence that voters consider the economic well-being of their community and nation even when controlling for their personal economic situations.

Lü et al. (2012) conducted survey experiments in China and found that people's preferences on trade protection were influenced by concerns about others, particularly those perceived as vulnerable to import competition. This suggests that even in non-democratic contexts, sociotropic concerns shape attitudes toward economic policies.

Mansfield and Mutz (2009) demonstrated through survey research that Americans' attitudes toward trade were more strongly influenced by sociotropic concerns about the national economy than by their personal economic self-interest. Their work challenged the prevailing assumption that trade preferences are primarily driven by individual economic position.

Because individuals have a social and psychological stake in their communities, voters may turn to protectionist parties out of a sense of 'place-based' threat in response to local trade shocks (Cramer, 2016). However, if EGF assistance is seen as helping members of the community, it may keep voters from turning to protectionist parties in trade-exposed areas.

Her research showed how place-based identity became a powerful political force that could drive voting behavior independently of individual economic circumstances.

### 5.4 Political Signaling and Recognition

Finally, the paper proposes that the EGF might function as a signal that governments recognize and take seriously the costs of globalization. Voters may see the EGF as an admission by governments that greater economic openness entails costs and these costs should be addressed by government action (Cramer, 2016). Such an admission may help to 'depoliticize' globalization and lessen the appeal of protectionist parties. This may be why many governments are keen to make EGF assistance visible to voters (Claeys and Sapir, 2020).

Claeys and Sapir (2020) documented how EU member states implemented EGF assistance, noting that governments often took steps to increase the visibility of the program to local communities and the broader public. This suggests that policymakers recognize the political value of being seen to address the costs of globalization.

This mechanism speaks to the psychological importance of acknowledgment and validation. When governments appear to dismiss or minimize the costs of globalization, voters may turn to parties that validate their experiences and concerns. Conversely, when governments acknowledge these costs and take visible action to address them, voters may feel less alienated from mainstream politics.

The author notes that many governments are keen to make EGF assistance visible to voters. For instance, The French government, for example, distributes information about EGF support to local stakeholders and places leaflets with information about EGF assistance on notice boards at impacted employment sites. The French government also issues press releases upon adoption of EGF support and highlights it on the relevant Ministry's website.

This mechanism relates to work on political communication and framing by scholars like Meguid (2005), who developed the theory of issue ownership. Meguid argued that mainstream parties can reduce support for niche parties by adopting their issues and demonstrating action on voter concerns. By publicly acknowledging the costs of globalization and demonstrating action to address them, governments may reduce the ability of anti-globalization parties to monopolize the issue.

## 6. Methodological Strengths and Limitations

### 6.1 Strengths

1. **Causal Identification**: The difference-in-differences design allows for causal inference by comparing changes over time between treated and control groups, rather than simply correlating compensation levels with voting patterns.
2. **Multi-level Analysis**: By examining effects at the département, commune, and individual levels, the study provides a comprehensive picture of how EGF assistance influences voting behavior.
3. **Cross-Country Validation**: Testing the findings in Denmark provides evidence of external validity beyond the French context.
4. **Focus on Import-Exposed Areas**: By restricting the sample to import-exposed areas, the study ensures a meaningful comparison between regions facing similar economic challenges but differing in their receipt of assistance.

### 6.2 Limitations

1. **Limited Temporal Scope**: The analysis focuses only on the 2007-2012 period, which may limit the generalizability of findings to other time periods.
2. **Modest Treatment Effect**: The estimated effect is small and often not statistically significant, making it difficult to draw strong conclusions about the efficacy of targeted compensation.
3. **Treatment Assignment**: While the author demonstrates balance on observables, there may still be unobserved factors influencing which areas receive EGF assistance.
4. **Measurement Issues**: The study cannot directly identify which individuals received EGF assistance, limiting the precision of the individual-level analysis.
