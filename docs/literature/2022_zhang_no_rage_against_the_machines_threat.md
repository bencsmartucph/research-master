The paper examines a critical question at the intersection of technology, labor markets, and political behavior: as automation increasingly threatens jobs, does awareness of this threat shift workers' policy preferences? Recent observational studies suggested workers exposed to automation risks show greater support for redistribution and right-wing populist policies. Zhang challenges this by conducting three survey experiments where American workers were informed about workplace automation's current and future impacts. While these informational treatments successfully convinced workers that automation threatens American jobs, they failed to change respondents' policy preferences on welfare, immigration, and trade. The findings contradict assumptions that merely raising awareness about automation threats would decrease opposition to globalization or increase support for policies preparing workers for technological disruptions. This has significant implications for understanding how technological change might influence political attitudes and for designing effective policy responses to automation-driven labor market disruptions.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Informational treatments change beliefs but not policy preferences:**
"While these informational treatments convinced workers that automation threatens American jobs, they failed to change respondents' preferences on welfare, immigration, and trade policies."
2. **Sociotropic vs. egotropic concerns don't produce different effects:**
"Appeals to sociotropic concerns (i.e., the threat to all American workers) were not more effective than appeals to egotropic concerns (i.e., the threat to the respondent's own job)."
3. **Personalization of automation threat doesn't affect those at higher risk:**
"Personalized treatments that emphasized automation's threat to the respondent's own job were not more effective for those whose occupations face a higher risk of automation."
4. **Raising awareness about automation is insufficient for policy change:**
"Raising awareness about workplace automation did not decrease opposition to globalization or increase support for policies that will prepare workers for future technological disruptions."
5. **Workers may misattribute economic threats:**
"Misdirected blame towards immigrants and workers in foreign countries, rather than concerns about workplace automation, could be driving support for right-wing populism."

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Primary Theory 1: Political Economy of Automation and Labor Market Polarization

### Detailed Explanation

The paper builds on theories of labor market polarization and technological change, particularly how automation affects different segments of the workforce. This theoretical framework posits that technological advances, particularly in computing and robotics, have led to a "hollowing out" of middle-skill jobs while increasing demand for both high-skill and low-skill work. This creates a polarized labor market with fewer middle-class jobs—the foundation of economic stability for many workers.

"Recent scholarship suggests that automation has negatively impacted workers in developed economies. Experts anticipate these impacts will intensify as labor-saving technology becomes more capable and intelligent, but predictions of these impacts vary significantly between studies."

"Economists observe that workers in the U.S. and other developed economies have experienced employment and wage polarization in the past three decades due to technological advances [1, 20, 29]. Industries have used computer software and robots to automate routine tasks, thereby reducing the demand for middle-skill workers, who make up a large bulk of the American middle class [6]."

The theory is informed by seminal works from economists like Autor, Acemoglu, Dorn, and others who have documented how computerization and automation have transformed labor markets. This work includes studies on routine-biased technological change, which suggests that technology primarily replaces jobs consisting of routine, codifiable tasks while complementing jobs requiring problem-solving and interpersonal skills.

Zhang applies this theory by connecting it to predictions about future automation trends, noting that while past technological changes have already disrupted labor markets, AI and robotics may significantly expand the range of tasks that can be automated, potentially affecting a much broader range of occupations.

If you're studying support for far-right parties or redistribution preferences, this theory helps explain the underlying economic anxiety that might motivate political attitudes. You could incorporate this by examining how different types of automation threats (e.g., to routine vs. non-routine jobs) might differently affect political preferences.

### Primary Theory 2: Economic Voting and Policy Preference Formation

### Detailed Explanation

The paper engages with theories of economic voting and policy preference formation, particularly examining whether people form policy preferences based on sociotropic concerns (about the national economy) or egotropic concerns (about their personal economic situation). This theoretical framework is central to understanding how economic threats might translate into political attitudes and voting behavior.

"In Study 1, the most extensive of the three studies, I tested these secondary hypotheses: H3: Treatments that highlight sociotropic concerns (i.e., automation's threat to American jobs in general) will produce effects that are unequal to the effects produced by treatments that express egotropic concerns (i.e., automation's threat to an individual's job). H4: Among those informed of automation's threat to their own jobs, those whose jobs are more likely to be automated will be more responsive to the informational treatment."

Traditional economic voting theories suggest that voters reward incumbents during good economic times and punish them during downturns. More sophisticated versions distinguish between sociotropic and egotropic voting, with research generally finding that sociotropic concerns have a stronger effect on political attitudes. Zhang's study tests whether this applies to automation threats as well.

The associated literature includes works by Kinder and Kiewiet on sociotropic voting, as well as more recent studies on how economic insecurity affects political preferences. This theory is particularly relevant in understanding populist voting behaviors in response to economic disruption.

Zhang applies this theory by designing treatments that separately target sociotropic concerns (general information about automation threats to all American workers) and egotropic concerns (personalized information about the threat to the respondent's specific job) to test which has stronger effects on policy preferences.

**Relevance to your research**: This framework offers insights into how economic threats translate into political preferences. For your research on support for far-right parties or redistribution preferences, it suggests the importance of distinguishing between general economic concerns and personal economic vulnerability. You might incorporate this by examining whether sociotropic or egotropic framing of economic issues has different effects on support for different types of policies or political parties.

### Primary Theory 3: Attribution Theory and Policy Blame Assignment

### Detailed Explanation

Attribution theory examines how people assign responsibility for problems and how these attributions shape policy preferences. In the context of this paper, it relates to how workers attribute job losses or economic insecurity—whether to automation, immigration, trade, or other factors—and how these attributions influence their policy preferences.

"Misdirected blame towards immigrants and workers in foreign countries, rather than concerns about workplace automation, could be driving support for right-wing populism. To correct American workers' beliefs about the threats to their jobs, I conducted three survey experiments in which I informed workers about the existent and future impact of workplace automation."

This theory suggests that people often make causal attributions based on what is most salient or visible rather than the most significant actual causes. In the case of job losses, immigration and offshoring may be more visible and personifiable threats than the more abstract and gradual threat of automation.

The literature on attribution theory in political science includes work by Iyengar on how framing affects responsibility attribution and studies on how economic attributions shape policy preferences. It connects to research on scapegoating and outgroup blame in political contexts.

Zhang applies attribution theory by testing whether providing information about automation's impact on jobs can correct potential misattributions of economic threats to immigration or trade, thereby potentially changing policy preferences on these issues.

**Relevance to your research**: This framework helps explain why economic insecurity might translate into support for far-right parties that focus on immigration rather than automation. For your research, you might explore whether different ways of framing economic threats lead to different attributions of blame and consequently different policy preferences. Consider examining how competing narratives about economic challenges affect which policies voters support.

### Primary Theory 4: Information-Based Theory of Opinion Change

### Detailed Explanation

This theoretical framework assumes that providing factual information can change people's beliefs and potentially their policy preferences. It relates to theories of rational updating, which suggest that people incorporate new information into their beliefs in a somewhat Bayesian manner.

"Studies 1 and 2 show that changing workers' factual beliefs did not necessarily lead to changes in their policy preferences. My informational treatments caused respondents to believe that workplace automation is a more significant threat to their jobs and American jobs than they had previously anticipated. Nevertheless, the shift in their beliefs failed to change their support for policies to help American workers adapt to the work of the future."

"My findings are in line with social scientific research that shows that correcting false beliefs does not necessarily lead to changes in political attitudes. For years, researchers worried that presenting the public with uncongenial factual information would produce a 'backfire effect' such that the public hold even more firmly onto their misperceptions [33]. However, more recent studies suggest that this 'backfire effect' is rare and that fact-checking can correct misconceptions [24, 34, 41]. Nevertheless, correcting one's factual beliefs may not alter one's interpretation of the facts or how one uses these facts when forming political opinions [18, 26]."

This theory draws on literature about belief updating, political knowledge, and opinion formation. Key works include research on when factual corrections do and don't change political attitudes, studies on motivated reasoning, and work on the relationship between factual beliefs and policy preferences.

Zhang applies this theory by testing whether providing information about automation's threat changes both factual beliefs (e.g., perceived likelihood of automation) and policy preferences (e.g., support for redistribution or restrictions on immigration).

**Relevance to your research**: This framework has implications for how information campaigns might (or might not) affect support for far-right parties or redistribution policies. For your research, consider that merely providing information about economic threats may be insufficient to change policy preferences. You might explore what additional factors (e.g., values, identity, emotion) moderate the relationship between information, beliefs, and policy preferences.
