The paper challenges the increasingly dominant view that cultural rather than economic factors drive populism, offering a more nuanced understanding where voters' economic grievances are realized through relative group comparisons. The authors implicitly assume that Brexit support can be understood as a populist reaction, and that survey measures can accurately capture people's group-based economic perceptions.

The core argument is presented with strong empirical evidence from both cross-sectional and panel data, systematically testing hypotheses about the role of in-group economic assessments, out-group economic assessments, and particularly their relative comparison in predicting Brexit support.

## Primary Theories Used

### Group-Based Economics Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Group-Based Economics theory posits that people's political decisions are shaped not primarily by their personal economic circumstances but by how they perceive the economic fortunes of their social group relative to other groups. The theory builds on social identity theory and intergroup comparison frameworks, arguing that voters organize their economic perceptions around salient social groups rather than just considering individual self-interest or national economic performance. This is why standard economic voting models that focus on egocentric or sociotropic evaluations may miss important dynamics. The theory emphasizes three key mechanisms: (1) assessments of one's in-group's economic situation; (2) assessments of relevant out-groups' economic situation; and (3) most importantly, the relative comparison between in-group and out-group economic fortunes. The authors argue that people become politically mobilized when they perceive their in-group as experiencing economic losses while out-groups are experiencing economic gains. This sense of relative group-based economic loss creates resentments that can be politically activated, especially by populist appeals that promise to restore previous economic and social hierarchies.

**Direct Quotes**: "We argue that perceptions of economic gains for 'out-groups' (Kinder and Kam 2009) – particularly relative to one's 'in-group' – are key to understanding the basis of economic grievances. Such relative gains motivate people to vote against the status quo, in favour of causes and campaigns that advocate improving the conditions of in-groups." (p. 321)

"As Mutz and Mondak (1997, 286) put it, 'private economic experience is not, by itself, politically potent'; however, 'groups may serve as essential mediating entities, making it possible for people to politicize the economic interests of those like them without necessarily relying directly on personal economic experience'." (p. 324)

**Operationalization and Application**: The authors operationalize Group-Based Economics through novel survey questions measuring how respondents perceive the economic conditions of different groups compared to 12 months ago. For the ethnic dimension, respondents evaluate the financial situation of "white British people" (in-group for white respondents) and "ethnic minority immigrants" (out-group). For the geographic dimension, they compare perceptions of "people in your local community" (in-group) versus "people in London" (out-group). They create relative measures by subtracting out-group evaluations from in-group evaluations, with negative values indicating perceptions that the out-group is doing better than the in-group. Their analysis shows these relative evaluations strongly predict Brexit support, even controlling for standard economic evaluations, immigration attitudes, and prior referendum vote.

**Relevance to My Thesis**: This theory directly connects to my dual pathway model of how welfare institutions shape political responses to economic vulnerability. The Group-Based Economics framework bridges the status stabilization and moral framing pathways I've identified. On one hand, it shows how economic perceptions operate through status comparisons between groups, supporting my argument that welfare institutions can prevent populist backlash by maintaining subjective status across social groups. On the other hand, it demonstrates how economic evaluations are filtered through group-based moral frameworks about fairness and deservingness, which aligns with my point that welfare institutions shape interpretive frameworks citizens use to understand economic conditions.

### Status Threat Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Status Threat Theory proposes that support for populist politics stems from perceived threats to one's relative social position rather than absolute economic hardship. This theory emphasizes the psychological dimensions of economic and social change, focusing on how individuals perceive their group's standing within social hierarchies over time. When dominant groups perceive a decline in their relative status – sometimes characterized as "nostalgic deprivation" – they become more receptive to political appeals that promise to restore previous status hierarchies. Status threat occurs when previously dominant groups perceive that subordinate groups are gaining economically or socially at their expense, creating a sense of zero-sum competition. The authors draw on Hofstadter's (1967) early observation that the American radical right emerged in response to perceived drops in social status, and on contemporary work by Gidron and Hall (2017) and Gest (2016) showing that perceptions about the direction of relative economic gains and losses between groups imply potential threats to social status, evoking feelings of hostility toward out-groups.

**Direct Quotes**: "'Because social status is based on a rank ordering, it is somewhat like a positional good, in the sense that, when many others acquire more status, the value of one's own status may decline' (Gidron and Hall 2017, 66). Zero-sum thinking predicts that if people perceive that some groups have enjoyed gains, they will believe other groups have experienced losses (Bobo and Hutchings 1996; Norton and Sommers 2011)." (p. 324-325)

"Status threat and economic grievance explanations are argued to be especially relevant to lower-income groups – such as the white working class – whose prior social status had been higher and more secure (Gest 2016; Gest, Reny and Mayer 2018; Gidron and Hall 2017)." (p. 331)

**Operationalization and Application**: While the authors don't directly measure status perceptions, they use group-based economic evaluations as indicators of status threat. They test whether these perceptions have stronger effects among groups theoretically more vulnerable to status loss, specifically examining interactions with income and British identity strength. They find that group-based economic evaluations strongly predict Brexit support across income groups and identity strength, suggesting that status concerns related to relative group economic performance are widely shared rather than confined to specific subgroups.

**Relevance to My Thesis**: Status Threat Theory provides a psychological mechanism connecting economic changes to political behavior that complements my status stabilization pathway. My research argues that welfare institutions can buffer against status threats by maintaining individuals' perceived social standing during economic transitions. The findings in this paper suggest that welfare institutions might need to particularly address perceptions of relative group gains and losses, not just absolute economic protection, to prevent status-based political backlash. This extends my understanding of how welfare regimes can mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics through status perceptions.

### Social Dominance Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Social Dominance Theory posits that societies maintain hierarchical social structures through individual and institutional discrimination that favors dominant groups. Social Dominance Orientation (SDO) reflects individual preferences for group-based dominance and social hierarchy, with some people preferring group-based inequalities maintained by hierarchy-enhancing ideologies and social policies. The theory predicts that when dominant groups' status is threatened, they respond with actions that seek to reestablish the existing hierarchy. Applied to populism, Social Dominance Theory suggests that support for populist causes partly reflects attempts by dominant groups to restore advantageous social and economic hierarchies they perceive as being challenged. The authors use this theory to explain why people might respond negatively to perceptions that immigrant or minority groups are experiencing economic gains, even when their own economic situation isn't directly affected. They note that when a group's dominant status is threatened, it can respond with actions that seek to promote social change that preserves the existing hierarchy.

**Direct Quotes**: "Social dominance theory predicts that some people have preferences for group-based dominance and social hierarchy – a preference for group-based inequalities maintained by hierarchy-enhancing ideologies and social policies (Ho et al. 2012; 2015; Mutz 2018; Sidanius and Pratto 1999). Social dominance orientation would predict opposition to affirmative action policies, which would be viewed as giving unfair economic advantages against a dominant group, and this increases when people feel threatened (Morrison and Ybarra 2008)." (p. 324)

"When a group's status is threatened, it can respond with actions that seek to promote social change that preserves the existing hierarchy (Hogg and Abrams 1988)." (p. 324)

**Operationalization and Application**: The authors don't directly measure Social Dominance Orientation but use it as a theoretical framework to interpret their findings. They use the difference between perceptions of in-group and out-group economic gains/losses as an indicator of potential hierarchy threats. Their geographic comparison of London versus local communities provides additional evidence consistent with Social Dominance Theory, showing that perceptions of Londoners (a higher-status geographic out-group) gaining economically relative to one's local community also predict Brexit support.

**Relevance to My Thesis**: Social Dominance Theory helps explain why welfare institutions might face political resistance when they're perceived as benefiting out-groups more than dominant in-groups. My research on welfare institutions and political outcomes needs to consider how different welfare designs might trigger or mitigate status anxiety through their implicit hierarchies and distribution of benefits across groups. Universal welfare regimes may generate less backlash precisely because they minimize group-based distinctions that can activate social dominance concerns, while targeted programs that are perceived as disproportionately benefiting minorities might trigger stronger resistance from dominant groups concerned about hierarchy maintenance.

## Overview of Data Source and Empirical Methodology

**Detailed Description**: The authors use data from the British Election Study (BES) Internet Panel, a large nationally representative survey of British voters. They utilize Wave 11 (April-May 2017) for their primary analysis, which includes new survey questions they designed to measure group-based economic evaluations. These questions ask respondents to evaluate the financial situation of different groups ("white British people," "ethnic minority immigrants," "people in your local community," and "people in London") compared to 12 months ago, with response options ranging from "got a lot worse" to "got a lot better." The sample consists primarily of white British respondents (93% of the total), with a sample size of over 18,000 respondents.

The authors employ logistic regression models to analyze the relationship between group-based economic evaluations and support for Brexit (measured by how respondents would vote in a hypothetical second referendum). Their key independent variables are perceptions of in-group economic conditions, out-group economic conditions, and their relative difference. To address potential endogeneity, they leverage the panel structure of the BES by controlling for respondents' past referendum vote (from Wave 9, immediately after the 2016 referendum). This helps isolate the effect of economic perceptions from pre-existing Brexit preferences. They also conduct additional analyses using panel data to examine how group-based economic perceptions measured in 2017 predict immigration attitudes in 2019 (Wave 15), testing whether these measures are merely proxies for pre-existing immigration attitudes.

The authors include a comprehensive set of control variables, including standard economic evaluations (personal finances and national economy), immigration attitudes, British national sentiment, authoritarianism, and demographic variables (age, gender, education, income). To avoid post-treatment bias, they measure attitudinal controls from previous survey waves. They also employ experimental techniques, randomizing the order of their survey questions to test for question-order effects.

**Reproducibility and Adaptability**: The methodological description is thorough and transparent. The authors provide detailed information about variable construction, model specifications, and robustness checks. They make their replication data available through Harvard Dataverse, and the BES dataset is publicly accessible, making their analysis highly reproducible.

For my thesis on welfare institutions and political outcomes, their methodology offers several adaptable elements. The group-based economic evaluation measures could be extended to capture perceptions about how different welfare policies benefit various social groups. Their approach of using panel data to control for prior attitudes could help address endogeneity concerns in studying how welfare experiences shape political attitudes. Their interaction analyses examining heterogeneous effects across income groups and identity strength provide a template for exploring how welfare institutions' effects might vary across social contexts.

However, adapting this approach would require adding questions about welfare institutions to existing survey panels or fielding new surveys with both welfare and group-based measures. The BES doesn't include detailed questions about welfare experiences or attitudes, which would be needed for my research. The focus on Britain also limits direct applications to other welfare regimes, though the theoretical framework could be applied more broadly.

## Findings and Evidence

**Summary of Key Findings**:

1. People's perceptions of the economic situation of ethnic minority out-groups—both in absolute terms and relative to their in-group—strongly predict Brexit support, with those who perceive minorities as economically gaining while white British people lose ground being much more likely to support Leave.
2. When analyzed separately, perceptions of out-group economic gains have stronger predictive power than perceptions of in-group economic losses, suggesting out-group evaluations drive the effect.
3. These effects remain robust when controlling for prior referendum vote, immigration attitudes, national identity strength, and standard economic evaluations, indicating they capture a distinct phenomenon from pre-existing preferences or cultural concerns.
4. The effects extend across income groups and identity strength, rather than being confined to lower-income groups or those with stronger British identity.
5. Similar patterns appear when comparing perceptions of economic conditions in London (out-group) versus one's local community (in-group), with those who see Londoners gaining while their local community loses being more likely to support Brexit.

**Supporting Evidence**: The authors present compelling evidence for their findings. Table 1 shows that 42% of respondents viewed the economic situation of ethnic minorities and white British people differently, with minorities typically seen as doing better despite factual evidence to the contrary. Table 2 presents logistic regression results showing that perceptions of ethnic minority economic gains significantly predict Brexit support (coefficient of 1.83 in Model 4), while perceptions of white British economic conditions show a weaker relationship. The combined relative measure is also significant (coefficient of -1.31 in Model 5), with negative values indicating perceptions that minorities are doing better than white British people.

Figure 2 illustrates these effects using predicted probabilities, showing that as perceptions of ethnic minority economic gains increase from "got a lot worse" to "got a lot better," the probability of supporting Leave increases from about 40% to nearly 80%. Similar patterns emerge for geographic comparisons in Table 4 and Table 5, with those perceiving their local economy as worse but the London economy as better having a 76% probability of supporting Leave.

**Implications for My Thesis**: These findings have significant implications for my research on welfare institutions and political outcomes. They suggest that welfare institutions might prevent populist backlash not just by improving absolute economic conditions but by addressing perceptions of relative group gains and losses. The finding that out-group evaluations drive the effect more strongly than in-group evaluations suggests that welfare institutions should be particularly attentive to how their policies are perceived as benefiting different social groups. The results also highlight the importance of developing better economic measures that capture group-based perceptions rather than relying solely on standard personal or national economic evaluations. This aligns with my argument that welfare institutions shape political behavior through both material protection and perceptual frameworks about economic fairness across groups.

## Discussion

**Interpretation of Findings**: The authors interpret their findings as evidence that economic factors do matter for explaining populist support, but through the lens of group-based comparisons rather than personal economic circumstances. They argue that their results help bridge competing explanations for populism by showing how economic and cultural factors are intertwined—economic grievances are experienced through culturally-defined group comparisons. They emphasize that perceptions of relative economic gains between groups are central to understanding populist resentments, particularly when out-groups are seen as benefiting while in-groups lose ground. They note that these perceptions may not align with factual economic conditions (minorities are objectively doing worse than white Britons), suggesting that political narratives and framing significantly shape how economic changes are interpreted.

**Connections to Other Research**: The authors connect their findings to both economic and cultural explanations for populism. They note that their group-based approach helps reconcile aggregate-level evidence showing relationships between economic conditions and populist support with individual-level studies emphasizing immigration concerns. They link their work to status threat research by Gidron and Hall (2017) and Gest (2016), as well as to Cramer's (2016) work on rural resentment, showing how economic grievances become politicized through group-based frameworks. They also connect to literature on economic competition and immigration attitudes, suggesting that concerns about immigration partly reflect perceptions about unequal economic gains across groups.

**Relevance to My Research**: The authors' interpretation offers important insights for my research on welfare institutions and political outcomes. Their emphasis on group-based economic perceptions suggests welfare institutions might shape political behavior not just through objective redistribution but by influencing subjective perceptions of relative group gains and losses. This aligns with my dual pathway model, suggesting welfare institutions might prevent populist backlash by both stabilizing objective economic conditions and shaping how citizens interpret economic changes across different social groups. Their bridging of economic and cultural explanations provides a template for how my research might transcend this divide, focusing on how welfare institutions simultaneously address material needs and shape cultural frameworks for understanding economic fairness. The finding that these effects extend across income groups challenges simplistic understandings of welfare politics as driven purely by material self-interest, suggesting broader perceptual and normative processes at work.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Group-Based Economic Perceptions Matter**: Rather than personal economic circumstances or national economic evaluations, people's perceptions of how different social groups are faring economically relative to each other strongly predict support for populism. This finding suggests my research should focus more on how welfare institutions shape perceptions of relative group gains and losses rather than just absolute economic protection.
2. **Out-Group Evaluations Drive the Effect**: Perceptions of the economic gains of out-groups (ethnic minorities, Londoners) have stronger predictive power than perceptions of in-group economic conditions. This suggests welfare institutions should be particularly attentive to how their benefits to marginalized groups are perceived and framed, as these perceptions may drive political backlash more than actual in-group conditions.
3. **Bridging Economic and Cultural Explanations**: The paper demonstrates that economic and cultural explanations for populism aren't competing but interconnected—economic grievances are experienced through culturally-defined group comparisons. This supports my theoretical framework that welfare institutions operate through both material protection and cultural framing effects, rather than one or the other.
4. **Effects Extend Across Social Groups**: The effects of group-based economic perceptions aren't confined to lower-income groups or those with stronger national identity but extend broadly across society. This suggests welfare institutions need to address relative group perceptions broadly rather than focusing narrowly on protecting the most economically vulnerable.
5. **Geographic Dimensions Matter**: Group-based economic comparisons operate not just along ethnic lines but also geographic ones, with perceptions of metropolitan areas gaining at the expense of local communities predicting populist support. This suggests welfare institutions should address geographic inequities in service delivery and economic opportunity, not just class or ethnic divisions.

These takeaways offer crucial insights for my research on how welfare institutions might mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics. The paper's emphasis on relative group-based perceptions, rather than absolute economic protection, suggests welfare institutions might need to focus more on messaging, framing, and addressing perceived fairness across groups to prevent political backlash. This aligns with my dual pathway model but emphasizes the interconnection between status stabilization and moral framing effects rather than treating them as separate mechanisms.
