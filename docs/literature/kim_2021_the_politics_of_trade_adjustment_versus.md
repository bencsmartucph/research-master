## Key Takeaways

1. **Trade adjustment and trade protection function as substitutes**: "Though they have very different economic effects, we show that trade adjustment and protectionism act as substitutes. Using the first geo-coded measure of US trade protectionist demands, we show that controlling for trade shocks, counties with a history of successful TAA petitions see fewer calls for trade protection."
2. **Partisan political effects of the two policies**: "And though they are both means of addressing import exposure, the two policy options have distinct political effects: in particular, successful TAA petitions carry a significant electoral benefit for Democratic candidates."
3. **Embedded liberalism hypothesis validated at micro-level**: "We use these data to test our two first claims. First, we show that demands for trade protection and trade adjustment are concentrated in the same geographical areas, and they correlate with the same proxies for trade competition. Second, while trade adjustment assistance and protectionism are no longer considered as direct substitutes by policymakers, we show that they still effectively operate as such..."
4. **International spillover effects of domestic trade adjustment**: "Our results suggest that TAA, despite all its criticisms, appears to serve an insurance function for workers in a way that affects behavior: counties that receive more TAA benefits tend to see fewer requests for trade protection. But the politicization of TAA is likely to work against effective trade compensation."
5. **Policy sequence and time inconsistency challenge**: "This pattern is intuitive: trade protection is most useful when there is an industry to protect; whereas trade compensation usually comes after the fact, once workers have either been laid off, or their jobs face an imminent threat. AD can help firms survive, TAA cannot; it can only help individual workers after the fact."

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Primary Theory 1: Embedded Liberalism

### Detailed Explanation

Embedded liberalism is a foundational concept in international political economy that explains how democracies maintain social stability while pursuing economic openness. The theory, first articulated by John Ruggie in 1982, posits that as countries liberalize their economies and open to international trade, they must simultaneously develop domestic policies to compensate those negatively affected by increased competition. This "compromise" allows countries to pursue the economic benefits of free trade while maintaining domestic political support through redistribution and social protection.

Kim and Pelc's paper makes a significant contribution by testing this theory at the micro-level, examining whether trade adjustment assistance programs like TAA actually function as the embedded liberalism hypothesis would predict - by tempering protectionist demands. While previous research has examined this relationship at the macro/country level, this paper provides evidence at the county level, showing that areas that receive more trade adjustment assistance are indeed less likely to demand protectionist measures, even when controlling for the severity of trade shocks.

The authors highlight that this relationship is not self-evident, as it depends on workers and firms having sufficient knowledge of and trust in compensation programs to modify their behavior regarding protectionist demands. The time-inconsistency problem (compensation comes after trade liberalization) makes this especially challenging, yet the evidence suggests the mechanism still works.

"An analogous tradeoff between protection and compensation has long been understood to lie at the heart of the postwar embedded liberalism compromise: as democracies become increasingly open to foreign competition in an attempt to increase gains from trade, they must 'buy off' those opposed to liberalization through transfers of one form or another. But this tradeoff has been examined almost exclusively in structural terms: the test of the embedded liberalism hypothesis has traditionally taken the form of country-level comparisons across time that show how redistribution and liberalization tend to go hand-in-hand, without it being possible to tell which way the causal arrow runs."

### Associated Literature

The embedded liberalism theory traces back to John Ruggie's 1982 article "International Regimes, Transactions, and Change: Embedded Liberalism in the Postwar Economic Order." Subsequent significant contributions include works by Rodrik (1998) on the relationship between economic openness and government size, Hays (2009) on "Globalization and the New Politics of Embedded Liberalism," and Walter (2010) who tested the micro-foundations of the compensation hypothesis. The empirical literature on embedded liberalism has typically used cross-national comparisons of welfare spending and trade openness, whereas this paper provides more fine-grained evidence at the sub-national level.

### Application in the Paper

The authors apply embedded liberalism theory by examining the regional deployment of trade adjustment assistance and protectionist demands in the US over a 25-year period. They test whether the presence of trade adjustment programs actually reduces demands for protectionism in particular regions, thus providing a direct test of the causal mechanism assumed by the embedded liberalism theory. Importantly, they find that only successful TAA petitions reduce protectionist demands, suggesting that the credibility of compensation is essential.

"Our results suggest that TAA, despite all its criticisms, appears to serve an insurance function for workers in a way that affects behavior: counties that receive more TAA benefits tend to see fewer requests for trade protection."

The paper's finding that TAA has partisan political effects (benefiting Democrats) suggests that the design and implementation of welfare policies might have important consequences for political alignment, which could be central to your research on redistribution preferences and far-right support.

## Primary Theory 2: Political Attribution Theory and Electoral Effects

### Detailed Explanation

The paper develops an implicit theory about how voters attribute credit or blame for economic policies, focusing on how different types of trade-related policies benefit different political parties. This connects to broader theories of retrospective voting and policy attribution in political science. The authors demonstrate that even when controlling for the effects of trade shocks (which tend to increase support for right-wing or Republican candidates), successful TAA petitions have a significant electoral benefit for Democratic candidates.

This finding suggests that voters associate trade compensation programs with the Democratic party, even when Republicans are in power when benefits are delivered. This partisan attribution may explain why Republicans have less incentive to invest in trade adjustment programs, creating a self-reinforcing cycle where compensation becomes increasingly identified with Democrats, further cementing the partisan divide over these policies.

"Certified petitions have a stronger and more significant pro-Democrat effect, whereas they have a negative, though insignificant, effect on Republican vote shares. Once again, we retrieve the same pro-Republican, anti-Democrat effect for the trade shock itself. In other words, Democrats appear to consistently benefit from trade compensation, while Republicans do not, even when they are in power."

### Associated Literature

This theory connects to scholarship on retrospective voting (Fiorina 1981), partisan attribution (Rudolph 2003), and more recent work on the political consequences of trade shocks (Autor et al. 2017; Colantone & Stanig 2018). The authors explicitly build on Autor et al.'s finding that the China trade shock contributed to increased Republican vote share, adding the insight that trade compensation can counteract this effect. The theory also relates to literature on how policy design affects political attribution and support (Campbell 2012).

### Application in the Paper

The authors test this theory by analyzing county-level electoral data in presidential elections, comparing the effects of TAA petitions during Democratic versus Republican administrations. They show that successful TAA petitions increase Democratic vote share regardless of which party controls the White House, suggesting a strong partisan association between Democrats and trade compensation programs.

"Two mechanisms may explain pro-Democrat effect of trade compensation. First, a number of recent studies have suggested that trade shocks increase support for nativist or right-wing parties (Autor et al., 2017; Colantone & Stanig, 2018; Inglehart & Norris, 2016). As trade compensation alleviates the effect of such shocks, we expect compensation to have, all else equal, a pro-Democratic effect... Second, Democrats have consistently advocated for the expansion of TAA."

### Relevance to Your Research

This aspect of the paper offers valuable insights for your research on far-right support. It suggests that specific welfare policies might have distinct political attribution effects that could affect electoral outcomes. The finding that trade compensation programs counteract the pro-right effects of trade shocks may be particularly relevant, suggesting that certain types of social investment policies might be effective at reducing support for far-right parties in economically distressed regions. The partisan asymmetry in the benefits of compensation programs might also help explain variation in policy responses across different political contexts.

### Primary Theory 3: Policy Substitution Theory

### Detailed Explanation

The paper develops a theory about policy substitution in response to economic challenges. The authors argue that trade adjustment assistance and trade protection are alternative policy mechanisms that address the same underlying problem (labor dislocation from import competition) but have different economic and political consequences. The paper shows that these two policy responses were explicitly considered substitutes in the 1970s, with policymakers often choosing between trade barriers and worker compensation when responding to trade-related injury petitions.

The substitution theory helps explain how governments choose between different policy options when addressing domestic economic pain resulting from globalization. While both options aim to address the same issue, they differ in their domestic and international effects: trade protection "exports" the pain to trading partners, while trade adjustment addresses it domestically. The authors argue that understanding this substitution relationship is crucial for predicting how countries will respond to increased import competition.

"When it comes to addressing the labor dislocation resulting from import competition, democratic governments can turn to one of two options. They can protect, or they can compensate: either shield the trade-exposed firms by temporarily suspending liberalization and increasing barriers to trade, or allocate funds directly to trade-exposed firms and individuals. And while these two mechanisms are responses to the same general phenomenon, they differ in most other respects."

### Associated Literature

This theory connects to literature on policy instruments and substitution, including work by Pelc (2011) on how states "ration flexibility" by switching between policy tools. It also relates to the literature on trade remedies and escape clauses in international agreements (Rosendorff & Milner 2001; Bown & McCulloch 2005) and the political economy of trade adjustment (Margalit 2011; Rickard 2015).

### Application in the Paper

The authors test the policy substitution theory using their geo-coded dataset of trade adjustment and protection demands, showing that counties with more successful TAA petitions see fewer subsequent demands for antidumping duties. They further verify this substitution effect within a single industry (steel) to rule out the possibility that the relationship is driven by industrial composition. The paper also examines the sequence of policy demands, showing that firms typically seek trade protection first, and only later turn to trade adjustment.

"Using these data to consider over 25 years of petitions for trade protection and trade compensation side-by-side... we show that demands for trade protection and trade adjustment are concentrated in the same geographical areas, and they correlate with the same proxies for trade competition. Second, while trade adjustment assistance and protectionism are no longer considered as direct substitutes by policymakers, we show that they still effectively operate as such."

### Relevance to Your Research

The policy substitution theory offers a framework for understanding how different welfare policies might interact or substitute for one another in addressing economic insecurity. For your research on redistribution preferences and far-right support, this suggests examining not just the presence of welfare policies but also their mix and substitutability. You might consider whether certain types of social investment policies function as substitutes for protectionist policies or other forms of economic nationalism that might drive support for far-right parties.

## The Electoral Benefits of Trade Adjustment and Political Attribution

The paper provides robust evidence that trade compensation programs yield significant electoral advantages for Democratic candidates:

> "And though they are both means of addressing import exposure, the two policy options have distinct political effects: in particular, successful TAA petitions carry a significant electoral benefit for Democratic candidates." (pg. 2354)
> 

The authors' empirical analysis demonstrates this electoral effect clearly:

> "Looking at all elections from 1992 to the most recent, the trade shock appears to have a systematic pro-right effect, while trade compensation, when successfully delivered, has a pro-left effect. Controlling for the trade shock, certified TAA petitions once again have a negative effect on the Republican vote share, while rejected TAA petitions have an insignificant effect." (pg. 2373)
> 

Notably, the benefit persists regardless of which party holds power:

> "Certified petitions have a stronger and more significant pro-Democrat effect, whereas they have a negative, though insignificant, effect on Republican vote shares... Democrats appear to consistently benefit from trade compensation, while Republicans do not, even when they are in power." (pg. 2373)
> 

The authors offer two explanations for this partisan attribution:

> "Two mechanisms may explain pro-Democrat effect of trade compensation. First, a number of recent studies have suggested that trade shocks increase support for nativist or right-wing parties (Autor et al., 2017; Colantone & Stanig, 2018; Inglehart & Norris, 2016). As trade compensation alleviates the effect of such shocks, we expect compensation to have, all else equal, a pro-Democratic effect... Second, Democrats have consistently advocated for the expansion of TAA." (pg. 2374)
> 

This partisan association creates a self-reinforcing cycle:

> "Democrats appear to be sufficiently politically identified with trade compensation that they are credited with benefits flowing from the program, even when they are not in power. This attribution takes place despite considerable variation in views within the parties. Yet the electoral effects we identify may be enough to produce a self-reinforcing trend: Republicans have little reason to invest in a program they get little political credit for, which further renders trade compensation a partisan issue, thus reinforcing the political effects of trade compensation." (pg. 2358)

## Knowledge, Trust, and Credibility in Policy Effectiveness

The paper highlights how the effectiveness of trade adjustment in reducing protectionist demands depends on workers' knowledge and trust in such programs:

> "For trade adjustment to temper protectionism, unions and workers within import-competing firms must know about and trust in the existence of trade compensation programs. This trust has to be sufficiently strong to affect their behavior and decrease mobilization for trade barriers." (pg. 2356)
> 

This trust is complicated by the credibility problem inherent in ex-post compensation:

> "But the TAA faces an underlying credibility problem, since it usually delivers its benefits to workers after the fact, once jobs have been lost. This may in fact be the greatest challenge of targeted trade compensation. Tellingly, unions have been known to refer to TAA as 'burial insurance': a remedy that comes too late to do any good—especially from the standpoint of unions who fear workers will move to non-unionized sectors." (pg. 2356)
> 

Despite these challenges, TAA appears to function effectively as insurance:

> "Nonetheless, TAA may serve an insurance function, by providing workers with the knowledge that they have access to 2 years of reprieve in the event of a layoff, to invest in training and look for work in a different firm or sector. If TAA played such an insurance function effectively, it could be enough to affect workers' incentives in the face of import competition, reducing overall mobilization for trade protection." (pg. 2356)
> 

The authors' empirical findings validate this insurance function:

> "Our results suggest that TAA, despite all its criticisms, appears to serve an insurance function for workers in a way that affects behavior: counties that receive more TAA benefits tend to see fewer requests for trade protection." (pg. 2376)
> 

The paper demonstrates that only successful TAA petitions reduce protectionist demands:

> "In Table 3, we use a similar estimation strategy, but this time we distinguish between certified and rejected TAA petitions, in an effort to further pin down the effect of TAA... when we narrow our consideration to certified TAA petitions, these appear to have a stronger dampening effect on protectionist claims, while rejected petitions have no significant effect." (pg. 2370)
> 

## The Value of Targeted Compensation Programs

The authors emphasize the analytical advantage of studying targeted programs like TAA rather than general welfare policies:

> "This targeted nature of such compensation allows us to pinpoint its effect on trade policy and distinguish trade-specific responses from general welfare programs. This, in turn, makes the expectations of embedded liberalism more readily observable and testable." (pg. 2359)
> 

The US approach contrasts with European welfare states:

> "In contrast to Northern European welfare states which provide social assistance to all workers, regardless of the cause of job loss, the US relies disproportionately on the targeted compensation scheme that is only available to workers who successfully demonstrate trade-induced dislocation." (pg. 2358-2359)
> 

The paper offers evidence that even in the heavily protectionist steel industry, TAA reduces protectionist demands:

> "Next, we examine the same substitution effect focusing on a single industry: the steel sector... Table 4 shows the results: the number of TAA petitions in a given county continues to be negatively associated with the number of AD petitions in the same area. Even when we confine the analysis to the steel industry, which most frequently relies on AD, a history of successful TAA petitions appears to diminish calls for import relief through trade barriers." (pg. 2370)
> 

## Political Consequences and Policy Design

The authors connect their findings to the broader literature on policy design and political attribution:

> "The notion that a US President might point to TAA as an alternative remedy to trade barriers seems unthinkable. The current US administration, in particular, appears bent on exporting its domestic pain in the form of tariffs, rather than addressing it domestically. Meanwhile, the TAA has increasingly fallen short over the last two decades, failing to keep up with increased import exposure. What accounts for this change?" (pg. 2357)
> 

The paper provides evidence of how policy design affects political outcomes:

> "As long as voters attribute benefits from trade compensation to Democrats, adjustment programs like TAA will remain underfunded. The consequences are significant: as we show, an underprovision of trade adjustment is associated with a rise in demands for distortionary trade barriers." (pg. 2376)
> 

This connects to literature on both policy attribution and the political consequences of trade shocks:

> "Looking at the last 20 years, reliance on TAA appears to offer a statistically significant electoral benefit to Democrats, while trade protection appears to slightly favor Republicans. This result is consistent with recent findings from Autor et al. (2017), who show that the China trade shock contributed to the Republican vote share in the 2016 elections. One hypothesized reason for this one-sided political effect is that parties on the political right are better able to capitalize on trade displacement through nativist policies (Inglehart & Norris, 2016)." (pg. 2358)
> 

## Policy Sequencing and Time Inconsistency

The authors highlight how the sequence of policy responses creates fundamental challenges:

> "Trade protection is most useful when there is an industry to protect; whereas trade compensation usually comes after the fact, once workers have either been laid off, or their jobs face an imminent threat. AD can help firms survive, TAA cannot; it can only help individual workers after the fact. This sequencing is also what gives rise to the time-inconsistency that plagues not only the TAA in the US, but all compensation schemes. Once liberalization has been implemented, those reaping the gains from trade may have less incentive to deliver on the promised redistribution." (pg. 2362-2363)
> 

Despite this challenge, the mechanisms of embedded liberalism still operate:

> "This sequencing of demands for protection and compensation, and the time-inconsistency it represents, lay bare the considerable assumptions underlying the embedded liberalism hypothesis. For trade-affected individuals to lower their resistance to trade liberalization, they must trust sufficiently in the provision of compensation, which usually happens ex post, to either decrease their resistance to liberalization, or fail to mobilize for trade barriers once liberalization is achieved." (pg. 2362-2363)
> 

The evidence supports this theoretical expectation:

> "Embedded liberalism does appear to operate at the regional level; compensation for trade-affected workers can in fact preempt protectionism." (pg. 2357)
> 

## International Implications and Policy Recommendations

The authors draw important conclusions about how domestic policies have international spillover effects:

> "The substitution effect between the two mechanisms reveals the extent to which trade compensation, which is usually thought of as a purely domestic concern, actually has considerable spillover effects across borders. This is because trade partners may end up bearing the brunt of one another's failed trade adjustment." (pg. 2376)
> 

This leads to a key insight about international interdependence:

> "If China faces higher trade barriers into the US as a result of a decrease in TAA's effectiveness, as we suggest is likely, then China has an effective stake in the US trade adjustment program. In other words, the US does not capture all the benefits flowing from its trade compensation program, which means trade compensation will be under-provided from a global welfare standpoint." (pg. 2359)
> 

The authors offer an innovative policy recommendation:

> "Countries that lack broad welfare schemes covering all workers in the event of a job loss could be required by their trade partners to combine their negotiated concessions with commitments on targeted trade adjustment. This could be seen as a necessary means of insuring against 'involuntary defection' on the concessions offered." (pg. 2375)
> 

This could be implemented through existing trade mechanisms:

> "Countries lacking broad welfare schemes to assist all redundancies could then commit ex ante to combining any temporary increase in tariffs under a safeguard with a proportional amount of domestic adjustment funds for workers in the industry concerned, in the form of a reserve fund." (pg. 2376)
> 

This approach would address the international spillover effects of domestic policy:

> "Trade adjustment is not a strictly domestic matter, despite being treated as such within global governance. Compensating trade-affected workers has spillover effects beyond a country's borders, because, as we show in this article, it affects protectionist demands. As a result, governments would be well-advised to pay attention to their trade partners' adjustment schemes. The trade barriers their exporters will come to face may depend on it." (pg. 2376-2377)
>
