    One clear way in which economic or ethnic majority groups will legitimise their dominant position in the face of threat may be to turn to an alternate valued social identity such as national identity. To support this, there is evidence that inequality alters patterns of identification. Inequality prioritises national identities and concerns over and above economic ones in more unequal, economically divided, societies (Shayo, [**2009**](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/casp.2409#casp2409-bib-0047)). Shayo's ([**2009**](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/casp.2409#casp2409-bib-0047)) studies demonstrate that higher income inequality is linked to stronger national identity. In particular, and of interest to the current analysis, he argues that this has important consequences for the way that people respond to rising inequality. Shayo shows that not only is inequality linked to increased national identification, increased national identification reduces support for more economically equitable policies. And this reduced support for economic equality is made more palatable, and morally justifiable, because of the universally accepted world of nations and boundary conditions that specify with whom we are prepared to share resources (Nightingale, Quayle, & Muldoon, [**2017**](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/casp.2409#casp2409-bib-0032)).
    
    Shayo's ([**2009**](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/casp.2409#casp2409-bib-0047)) finding is important for a number of reasons. First, the finding goes against the intuitive assumption that we are more likely to want to share resources with our fellow citizens if we identify strongly with our national group. Using data sets from multiple democracies, Shayo finds no support for the contention that strong national identity and strong preferences for wealth redistribution go hand in hand. Rather, Shayo's studies suggest that inequality enhances support for right-wing policies and curtails the appetite for wealth redistribution in a recursive way. Thus, as inequality increases, people are less willing to fund educational, security, health, and welfare projects. Similarly and most likely reflecting the same underlying lack of social trust and cohesion, higher income inequality leads to higher levels of tax evasion (Bloomquist, [**2011**](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/casp.2409#casp2409-bib-0003)). Lack of redistribution further increases inequality, which makes national identification more attractive. Second, and more generally, Shayo's ([**2009**](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/casp.2409#casp2409-bib-0047)) work is unique in that it highlights *how* inequality and tolerance for inequality are linked to national identity. His analysis goes some way to explaining how national identification is linked to FR populist appeal as well as the maintenance of, and support for, inequality via right-wing politics.
    
    Ironically then, those whose concerns are a direct result of rising inequality are distracted away from the systematic explanations for such inequality. Because of reduced social cohesion and increased perceptions of threat within their increasingly unequal nation, they focus on the national agendas and in particular on emotive issues such as patriotism, the boundaries of the national community, and immigration. And so national allegiances drive national protectionism, associated anti-immigrant feelings, racism, and intolerance of cultural diversity. These feelings are used by FR populist to feed support for the right-wing agenda, and so support for FR populism is increased, which serves to sustain inequality and the status quo (see Figure [**1**](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/casp.2409#casp2409-fig-0001)).
    

Inequality therefore, as well as reducing social cohesion, is also linked to altered patterns of identification whereby individuals turn to social identities such as national identities that provide security in the fragmented society that inequality creates. Consistent with this, evidence suggests that in situations of rising inequality, both the rich and the poor alike prioritise national identities. This threatening and mistrusting social climate where rising inequality pertains also results in increased cultural intolerance and reduced support for resource redistribution. It promotes a right-wing agenda where distrust undermines support for economic redistribution of economic resources. This creates further electoral opportunities for FR populists in an iterative process.

At the same time, people's understanding of inequality is obscured. FR populist leaders enlist the problems created by inequality and whip up people's fears about national decline, cultural threats, and status anxiety. Social divisions and insecurities associated with inequality are used strategically to vilify mainstream politicians and elites, foreigners, and minorities. In doing so, any systematic explanations for, or policies to help alleviate, inequality are sidestepped. Unfortunately then, FR populists do not address issues of inequality, mask systematic explanations for it, and can further contribute to it by limiting wealth redistribution and failing to offer any political policies that might address the problems. In this way, the status quo is protected and a spiral of inequality maintained. A social psychology that works to reveal these processes is an important first step in tackling both inequality and FR populism.

## Paper Summary

This 2019 paper by Jay et al. presents a social psychological analysis of how economic inequality fuels far-right (FR) populism in Western democracies. The authors develop a recursive model integrating three key social psychological processes: (1) how inequality erodes social trust and cohesion while heightening perceptions of threat among both low and high-income groups; (2) how these threats lead to increased national identification and decreased tolerance for cultural diversity; and (3) how FR populist leaders exploit these dynamics by positioning themselves as defenders of national identity against perceived threats from immigrants, foreign powers, and mainstream politicians. The authors argue that FR populist rhetoric effectively redirects anxieties stemming from inequality toward cultural and national identity concerns rather than addressing the underlying economic issues. This creates a recursive cycle where support for FR populism leads to policies that maintain or worsen inequality, further fueling the conditions that enable populist appeal. This theoretical contribution is particularly valuable for understanding the psychological mechanisms connecting economic conditions to political outcomes.

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Primary Theory 1: Social Identity Theory and Self-Categorization Theory

The paper utilizes social identity theory and self-categorization theory (from the social identity tradition) as foundational frameworks to explain how inequality affects group dynamics and identity processes.

**Detailed explanation**: Social identity theory, developed by Henri Tajfel and John Turner in the 1970s and 1980s, proposes that individuals derive a significant portion of their self-concept from their membership in social groups. In contexts of threat, individuals often strengthen their identification with in-groups and increase differentiation from out-groups. Self-categorization theory extends this by explaining how contexts influence which social identities become salient and how this salience affects perceptions and behavior.

The authors apply these theories to explain how rising inequality makes wealth-based categorizations more salient, which increases stereotyping between economic groups and heightens intergroup tensions:

> "According to self-categorisation theory, increased inequality will heighten the salience of wealth as a fitting category upon which intergroup comparisons are made (Jetten, Wang, et al., 2017). In turn, such comparisons trigger a search for normative fit, an increased attention on income, and wealth-based self and other stereotyping." (p. 420)
> 

The authors operationalize these theories by examining how inequality:

1. Makes wealth differences more salient as a basis for social categorization
2. Increases stereotyping between wealth groups
3. Creates conditions where people seek more secure identities (like national identity)

This theoretical framework helps explain why inequality has psychological effects beyond material deprivation, affecting social relations at both individual and group levels.

### Primary Theory 2: Identity Threat and Ontological Security

The paper employs concepts of identity threat and ontological security to explain responses to inequality.

**Detailed explanation**: Identity threat occurs when individuals perceive their group membership or identity as being devalued or undermined. The concept of ontological security, developed by Anthony Giddens, refers to the sense of order and continuity regarding one's social and material environments. The paper argues that inequality creates identity threats and ontological insecurity for both wealthy and poor groups, albeit for different reasons.

> "Kinnvall (2004) proposes that the ontological insecurity, which can accompany rising inequality, drives people towards identities that provide security." (p. 421)
> 

> "Jetten, Mols, Healy, and Spears (2017) demonstrated in a series of studies that inequality evokes anxiety and fear among both the poor and the wealthy, albeit for different reasons. Although inequality amplifies perceptions of relative deprivation among the poor, inequality has an equally pernicious effect for those who are relatively wealthy." (p. 421)
> 

The authors operationalize these theories by showing how:

1. Economic uncertainty creates anxiety about status and position
2. This anxiety drives people toward more secure and clearly defined identities (national identity)
3. FR populists exploit this process by offering narratives of threatened national identity

This theoretical approach helps explain why economic inequality manifests as cultural anxiety and national identity concerns rather than direct class consciousness.

### Primary Theory 3: National Identity Consolidation and FR Populism

The paper develops a theoretical model linking national identity consolidation to reduced support for economic redistribution and increased support for FR populism.

**Detailed explanation**: Drawing on work by Shayo (2009) and others, the authors present a novel theoretical connection between inequality, national identification, and political preferences. They argue that increased national identification—as a response to the threats associated with inequality—reduces support for redistributive policies and increases susceptibility to FR populist appeals focusing on national protection and anti-immigration sentiment.

> "Shayo's (2009) studies suggest that inequality enhances support for right-wing policies and curtails the appetite for wealth redistribution in a recursive way. Thus, as inequality increases, people are less willing to fund educational, security, health, and welfare projects." (p. 422)
> 

> "FR populist rhetoric aligned with nationalism can blame immigrants, 'foreign' powers, and mainstream politics for both the lack of social cohesion and reduced economic circumstances of many." (p. 418)
> 

The authors operationalize this framework by:

1. Analyzing how FR populist rhetoric combines nationalist appeals with anti-elite messaging
2. Explaining the "heartland" narrative that romanticizes a past of national unity and prosperity
3. Demonstrating how national identity concerns distract from addressing economic inequality

This theoretical innovation helps explain the paradox of why many economically disadvantaged people support political movements whose economic policies may not benefit them.

### Theoretical Innovation: The Recursive Model of Inequality and FR Populism

The paper's most significant theoretical contribution is its recursive model connecting inequality to FR populism through interconnected social psychological processes.

> "We suggest that these processes are recursive although we do not specify their temporal order." (p. 418)
> 

The authors visualize this model in Figure 1 (p. 423), showing how:

1. Inequality leads to reduced social cohesion
2. Reduced cohesion increases perceptions of threat
3. Threat leads to increased national identification, decreased tolerance, and decreased support for redistribution
4. Leaders exploit these conditions using populist rhetoric
5. This leads to increased support for FR populism and non-egalitarian policies
6. These policies maintain or worsen inequality, completing the cycle

This theoretical innovation integrates multiple psychological processes into a coherent explanatory framework that accounts for both the psychological and political dimensions of inequality and populism. It demonstrates how economic conditions create psychological states that are then politically mobilized in ways that can perpetuate economic problems.

## Findings & Discussion

### Key Finding 1: Inequality Erodes Social Trust and Cohesion

The authors present evidence that inequality damages social relations at both interpersonal and group levels.

> "A social identity analysis suggests that growing inequality makes wealth and people's place on the social ladder more salient (Jetten, Wang, et al., 2017), which increases people's stress and anxiety about status-related concerns." (p. 421)
> 

> "Using time-lagged correlation data from 34 European Union (EU) and Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development members, a strong negative association between income inequality and social cohesion is evident (Dragolov et al., 2016)." (p. 420)
> 

This finding is particularly relevant to my research on welfare institutions, as it suggests welfare systems might play a crucial role in maintaining social cohesion during periods of economic change. The paper indicates that the erosion of social trust affects political attitudes and could potentially be mitigated by welfare state institutions that reduce inequality and provide security.

### Key Finding 2: Inequality Increases Threat Perceptions Across Income Groups

Contrary to assumptions that only the poor are threatened by inequality, the paper demonstrates that both low and high-income groups experience threat from inequality, albeit for different reasons.

> "Among the latter, inequality fuels an awareness that one can fall low when wealth evaporates." (p. 421)
> 

> "In unequal countries, people are more likely to report anxiety over status in society and fear that others are looking down on their job situation or income." (p. 421)
> 

This finding connects directly to my research on status anxiety as a mediator between economic conditions and populist support. It suggests welfare institutions could reduce status anxiety not only by providing material support to lower-income groups but also by creating more generalized security that benefits all income groups.

### Key Finding 3: National Identity Becomes More Salient Under Conditions of Inequality

The authors demonstrate that inequality is associated with increased national identification, which has important political consequences.

> "Inequality prioritises national identities and concerns over and above economic ones in more unequal, economically divided, societies (Shayo, 2009)." (p. 422)
> 

> "Shayo's (2009) studies demonstrate that higher income inequality is linked to stronger national identity." (p. 422)
> 

This finding is significant for my research as it suggests welfare institutions might mitigate populist appeal not only through economic mechanisms but also by affecting how people identify and which identities become politically salient. Universal welfare programs might reduce the salience of national identity as a response to economic threat.

### Key Finding 4: FR Populist Leaders Strategically Exploit Threat and National Identity

The paper analyzes how FR populist rhetoric harnesses the psychological conditions created by inequality for political gain.

> "FR populists use the injuries of inequality strategically as 'antisystem' politics to evidence that their political rivals have failed and cannot be trusted." (p. 424)
> 

> "By presenting the past as glorious and the present as in decline, they create a potent sense of urgency and, more importantly, the need for immediate, harsh, and drastic measures to ensure the continuity of national identity into the future." (p. 424)
> 

This finding is highly relevant to my research on populist mobilization strategies. It suggests that countering populist appeals requires not only economic policies but also alternative narratives that address the status and identity concerns inequality creates.

### Relevance to My Research on Welfare Institutions, Status Anxiety, and Populist Mobilization

This paper provides several valuable insights for my research:

1. **Welfare Institutions**: The paper suggests welfare institutions could interrupt the recursive cycle they identify by:
    - Reducing inequality directly through redistribution
    - Maintaining social cohesion during economic change
    - Providing security that mitigates feelings of threat
2. **Status Anxiety**: The paper offers a sophisticated understanding of how inequality creates status anxiety across income groups, supporting my hypothesis that status anxiety mediates economic vulnerability and political attitudes.
3. **Populist Mobilization**: The paper's analysis of FR populist rhetoric illustrates how populists exploit economic anxieties by redirecting them toward cultural and nationalist concerns, which is central to my research on mobilization strategies.

I could incorporate these insights by examining how different welfare regime types (universal vs. targeted) affect the salience of national identity and the effectiveness of populist appeals. The paper suggests universal programs might better maintain social cohesion and reduce the effectiveness of divisive rhetoric.
