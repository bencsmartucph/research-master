### Core Argument

The authors argue that contemporary welfare reforms (means-tested benefits, demanding activation policies, and basic income schemes) each appeal to different principles of distributive justice (need, equity, and equality respectively), but citizen support for these reforms is only weakly rooted in these normative principles. While traditional welfare institutions effectively socialize citizens into accepting their underlying normative frameworks, newer and less institutionalized reforms fail to create strong normative foundations for public support. The authors test whether distributive justice preferences actually shape attitudes toward these contemporary reforms and find limited evidence that they do, suggesting these reforms have weak normative foundations in public opinion.

This core argument contains several implicit assumptions:

1. Welfare institutions can socialize citizens into accepting certain normative frameworks
2. More established welfare institutions create stronger normative foundations than newer reforms
3. Specific welfare reforms are anchored in specific distributive justice principles
4. Public opinion should align with the moral principles embedded in policy designs

The strength of this argument is its theoretical grounding in moral economy and policy feedback literatures, but it reveals a key limitation: the presumed connection between policy design and public opinion isn't as straightforward as these theories suggest.

## Primary Theories Used

### 1. Moral Economy Theory

**Detailed Explanation:** Moral economy theory posits that welfare institutions don't merely provide material benefits but also serve as normative frameworks that shape attitudes and beliefs about fairness and social obligations. The theory suggests that welfare institutions embody specific moral principles about how resources should be distributed, and citizens internalize these principles through their interactions with these institutions. As Van Hootegem et al. explain: "According to the moral economy and policy feedback theories, mass attitudes towards welfare do not merely reflect material interests and cost-benefit calculations. Instead, public opinions are grafted on the moral principles and social norms that are embodied by welfare institutions and policies" (p. 1034). This theory views welfare institutions as having norm-shaping functions that go beyond their redistributive purposes, creating shared conceptions of justice that serve as reference points for individual preferences.

**Direct Quotes:** "The moral economy and policy feedback literatures argue that institutions socialize citizens within normative frameworks and shared conceptions of social justice, which serve as a reference point in the formation of individual preferences" (p. 1036).

**Operationalization and Application:** The authors operationalize moral economy theory by examining whether preferences for specific distributive justice principles (equality, equity, and need) predict support for welfare reforms that embody these principles. They use single-item measures for each distributive justice principle and test their associations with support for means-tested benefits, demanding activation policies, and basic income schemes. The application shows that while these associations exist in some cases (e.g., equity and activation policies), they are weak, suggesting limitations to the moral economy framework's explanatory power for contemporary welfare reforms.

**Relevance to My Thesis:** This theory is directly relevant to my dual pathway model, which posits that welfare institutions shape political responses to economic vulnerability through both status stabilization and moral framing. The moral economy theory helps explain the second pathway in my framework - how welfare institutions create moral frameworks that mediate between economic conditions and political attitudes. The finding that newer welfare reforms have weak normative foundations may help explain why some welfare regimes are less effective at preventing populist responses to economic shocks than others.

### 2. Policy Feedback Theory

**Detailed Explanation:** Policy feedback theory examines how existing policies shape subsequent politics by altering resources, incentives, and preferences of political actors. In the welfare context, this theory suggests that welfare policies, once implemented, reshape public attitudes by creating constituencies who benefit from these policies and by normalizing the principles embedded in their design. The theory establishes a reciprocal relationship between policies and politics - policies aren't just outcomes of political processes, but actively reshape those processes over time. As applied in this paper, policy feedback suggests that well-established welfare arrangements should create stronger alignments between their embedded principles and public attitudes compared to newer reforms that haven't had time to "feed back" into opinion formation.

**Direct Quotes:** "If welfare policies function as norm-shaping institutions, individual attitudes are structured by existing welfare arrangements and tend to align with the (re)distributive logics inherent to policy designs. In the case of traditional welfare arrangements, this claim has been corroborated repeatedly" (p. 1036).

**Operationalization and Application:** The authors operationalize this theory by comparing their findings to prior research showing stronger policy feedback effects for traditional welfare arrangements. They note that "Raven and colleagues (2011) demonstrate that while policy feedback effects do occur for well-established welfare arrangements, relatively recent welfare proposals have not yet been sufficiently institutionalized to impact citizens' opinions" (p. 1037). Their findings support this view - the weak relationship between distributive justice principles and support for contemporary welfare reforms suggests limited policy feedback effects for these less institutionalized policies.

**Relevance to My Thesis:** Policy feedback theory provides a crucial explanatory mechanism for why different welfare regimes produce different political responses to similar economic pressures. My thesis argues that universal welfare regimes (Nordic model) show smaller increases in populist voting following economic shocks compared to liberal market economies partly because their institutions have created stronger normative foundations that stabilize politics during crises. The Van Hootegem study suggests this requires long-term institutionalization, which helps explain the variation in populist responses across different welfare regimes.

### 3. Distributive Justice Principles Framework

**Detailed Explanation:** The distributive justice framework identifies three distinct principles that guide resource allocation in welfare states: equality, equity, and need. Equality grants resources to all citizens without additional requirements, representing universalism in welfare provision. Equity makes distribution dependent on contributions to the common good, emphasizing reciprocity and desert. Need allocates resources selectively to those demonstrating insufficient resources, focusing on poverty relief. This framework provides a conceptual tool for understanding the normative foundations of different welfare arrangements. The authors argue that each contemporary welfare reform is anchored in one of these principles: means-tested benefits rely on need, demanding activation policies on equity, and basic income schemes on equality.

**Direct Quotes:** "The design of the three above-mentioned reforms is based on three distinct principles of distributive justice that guides the allocation of goods and services in the welfare state – namely, equality, equity and need (Deutsch, 1975)" (p. 1036).

**Operationalization and Application:** The authors operationalize the three distributive justice principles using single items from the Basic Social Justice Orientations scale. For equality: "For a society to be fair, differences in people's standard of living should be small." For equity: "A society is fair when hard-working people earn more than others." For need: "A society is fair when people who look after their children or their relatives in need of care receive special support and financial benefits" (p. 1041). They then examine how preferences for these principles relate to support for each welfare reform, finding weak relationships that suggest limited normative anchoring.

**Relevance to My Thesis:** This framework directly informs my conceptualization of how welfare institutions shape moral frameworks that mediate between economic vulnerability and political responses. My thesis posits that universal welfare regimes promote universalistic moral frameworks (equality-based), while liberal welfare regimes promote individualistic moral frameworks (equity-based). The findings from Van Hootegem et al. suggest that simply designing policies based on specific principles doesn't automatically create public support anchored in those principles - institutionalization matters, which helps explain the resilience of established welfare regimes against populist challenges.

## Overview of Data Source and Empirical Methodology

**Detailed Description:** The study uses data from the Cross-National Online Survey (CRONOS) panel linked to the European Social Survey (ESS) round 8 (2016/2017), covering Estonia, United Kingdom, and Slovenia. The sample consists of probability-based respondents who first completed face-to-face ESS interviews and were then invited to participate in online surveys. From 5,285 ESS respondents, 2,437 participated in CRONOS. The authors conducted a pooled analysis across countries (with country fixed effects) rather than a comparative analysis.

The dependent variables measuring support for welfare reforms came from the ESS survey: (1) means-tested benefits (single item on limiting social benefits to those with lowest incomes), (2) demanding activation policies (three items on benefit sanctions for refusing job offers or community service), and (3) basic income schemes (single item on implementing an unconditional basic income). The independent variables measuring distributive justice preferences came from the CRONOS survey: preferences for equality, equity, and need principles (single items for each). Additional variables included sociodemographic indicators and left-right political placement.

Due to substantial dropout between ESS and CRONOS (53.9% missing data for distributive justice items), the authors employed multiple imputation techniques to handle missing data, creating multiple datasets with imputed values. They then applied structural equation modeling (SEM) to estimate the effects of distributive justice preferences on support for welfare reforms while controlling for sociodemographic and ideological factors.

**Reproducibility and Adaptability:** The authors provide sufficient methodological details to understand their approach, but full reproducibility would require access to the ESS and CRONOS datasets. The approach of linking survey data with panel data is sound, though the substantial missing data is a limitation. The structural equation modeling approach is appropriate for analyzing relationships between latent constructs and observed variables.

## Findings and Evidence

**Summary of Key Findings:**

1. Support for means-tested benefits is not significantly associated with any distributive justice principles, despite the policy's theoretical anchoring in the need principle
2. Support for demanding activation policies is positively associated with preference for equity (β = 0.102) and negatively associated with preferences for equality (β = -0.054) and need (β = -0.065)
3. Support for basic income schemes is positively associated with preferences for equality (β = 0.091) and need (β = 0.079), and negatively associated with preference for equity (β = -0.067)
4. All relationships between distributive justice principles and welfare reform attitudes are weak, suggesting limited normative foundations
5. Sociodemographic factors and political ideology have significant but moderate effects on welfare attitudes

**Supporting Evidence:** The authors present their evidence in a structural equation model (Table 2, p. 1043) showing standardized regression coefficients. The statistical significance is indicated (*p≤0.05; **p≤0.01; ***p≤0.001), and all relationships between justice principles and welfare attitudes have small effect sizes (β < 0.11). The R-squared values are low for all models (means-tested benefits: 0.043; demanding activation: 0.057; basic income: 0.069), indicating that the variables explain only a small portion of the variation in attitudes.

Demographic patterns show that support for means-tested benefits is higher among older respondents and those with financial difficulties, but lower among the tertiary educated and white-collar workers. Support for activation policies is higher among older respondents but lower among tertiary educated, financially struggling respondents, service workers, and the unemployed. Basic income support is higher among younger respondents and those with financial difficulties.

Political ideology shows expected patterns: right-wing individuals express more support for means-tested benefits (β = 0.042) and demanding activation policies (β = 0.084), while left-wing individuals favor basic income schemes (β = -0.061).

**Implications for My Thesis:** These findings suggest that the moral pathway in my dual pathway model may be weaker than anticipated for newer welfare reforms. This helps explain why economic vulnerability might more readily translate into populist politics in contexts of welfare reform or retrenchment - the moral frameworks that could buffer this relationship are weakly institutionalized. The stronger relationship between ideology and welfare attitudes suggests that political framing may be more important than inherent normative logic in shaping responses to welfare policies. This could mean that populist actors have more space to frame welfare issues when the policies lack strong normative foundations.

## Discussion

**Interpretation of Findings:** The authors interpret their findings as evidence that attitudes toward contemporary welfare reforms are not strongly crystallized according to distributive justice principles, challenging assumptions in the moral economy and policy feedback literatures. They suggest several explanations: (1) these reforms are not yet fully institutionalized, limiting socialization effects; (2) the reforms are implemented in diverse ways that may obscure their underlying principles; (3) public debates around these reforms often invoke multiple, competing moral frameworks; and (4) unintended consequences of implementation may undermine their normative foundations.

The authors note that their findings "indicate that only basic income schemes and demanding activation policies are to some extent connected to each of the justice principles" (p. 1045), while support for means-tested benefits appears unrelated to any justice principle despite its theoretical anchoring in need-based distribution. They conclude that "the normative foundation of their public support remains unclear" (p. 1046).

**Connections to Other Research:** The authors situate their findings within existing literature on welfare attitudes, noting that while traditional welfare arrangements show clear connections to justice principles (Andreß and Heien, 2001; Jaime-Castillo, 2013; Lewin-Epstein et al., 2003), newer reforms do not. They reference Raven et al. (2011) to explain that recent welfare proposals may not be sufficiently institutionalized to impact citizens' opinions. The authors also connect their work to research showing that welfare debates invoke multiple heuristics beyond pure distributive principles (Arni et al., 2013; Perkiö et al., 2019; Rossetti et al., 2020a; Zimmermann et al., 2020).

The authors acknowledge limitations including measurement issues (single-item measures for justice principles), the pooled analysis across countries that may mask regime-specific patterns, and the need to investigate alternative mechanisms driving welfare reform preferences.

**Relevance to My Research:** This discussion provides crucial context for my thesis on welfare institutions as mediators between economic vulnerability and populist politics. The finding that newer reforms have weak normative foundations helps explain why welfare retrenchment or reform might increase vulnerability to populist appeals - it disrupts established moral frameworks without creating new ones. This supports my hypothesis that welfare reforms introducing more means-testing or conditionality will increase the relationship between economic vulnerability and populist voting.

The authors' observation that "debates on these new types of policy reforms do not only seem to partly transcend traditional class coalitions and partisan alliances (Häusermann, 2006), but also to not be fully based on the classical organizing principles of the welfare state" (p. 1046) suggests that welfare reforms may create political realignments that open space for populist mobilization, which is directly relevant to my research question.
