## 11. Social Psychological Mechanisms

The paper theorizes several social psychological mechanisms that explain the relationship between status perceptions and radical right support, particularly in welfare state contexts.

The authors draw several important theoretical implications from their findings, challenging and extending existing understandings of the relationship between status decline and radical right support.

### 7.1 Revision to Dominant Narratives

Their central finding revises the dominant narrative in the literature:

> "In short, expectations of status decline, not experiences of status decline, drive support for the radical right in Finland." (p. 132)
> 

They explain this pattern in psychological terms:

> "Expectations of status decline can strongly influence radical right support because voters react strongly to fears of losing status." (p. 132)
> 

### 7.2 Differing Mechanisms for Experience vs. Expectation

The authors suggest that experienced and expected status decline might operate through different mechanisms:

For voters who have experienced status decline:

> "With 'sticky floors' that prevent upward social mobility, radical right parties' appeal may do little to restore status that has already been lost, especially without radical and profound political change." (p. 133)
> 

For voters who expect status decline:

> "In this regard, radical right parties' appeals are perhaps more successful at reasserting symbolic boundaries to bolster the status of social groups whose position in the national pecking order is threatened but is not yet lost." (p. 133)
> 

### 7.3 The Nostalgic Appeal of Radical Right Parties

The authors emphasize the effectiveness of nostalgic appeals for voters fearing status decline:

> "As Lamont and colleagues (2017) described, radical right parties destigmatize these social groups, attribute their fraying social status to structural transformations, and marginalize social groups that were previously status-inferior. In doing so, radical right parties buttress the moral worth of voters who fear status decline and thus consolidate their fraying social position." (p. 132)
> 

### 7.4 Welfare State Implications

Although Finland has a comprehensive Nordic welfare state, the authors suggest this does not insulate against radical right support when voters fear status decline:

> "If there are voters with middling income and middling education who worry about their status, even in a country with a comprehensive Nordic welfare state such as Finland, there will likely be more of such voters in countries with less comprehensive welfare states." (p. 133)
> 

They theorize that the relationship might be even stronger in less comprehensive welfare states:

> "In other words, the relationship between the expectation of status decline and radical right support could be even more pronounced in countries with more residual welfare states." (p. 133)
> 

### 11.1 Status Anxiety and Risk Aversion

The authors theorize that voters who expect status decline may be more risk-averse when considering political options:

> "If these voters fear falling down the social ladder and seek to prevent status decline, they may be less inclined towards solutions that are framed as radical as it conveys uncertainty. Instead, they may prefer solutions that they feel familiar with such as solutions which rely on nostalgic frames, even if these solutions are radical in practice." (p. 119)
> 

This risk aversion makes radical right parties' nostalgic framing particularly appealing:

> "Nostalgia enables radical right parties to frame their solution in terms of the 'pleasure of familiarity, the comfort of tradition and feelings of reassurance' (Kenny, 2017, p. 263) which evokes familiarity rather than uncertainty." (p. 120)
> 

### 11.2 Relativity of Social Status

The authors emphasize the relational nature of social status:

> "Leveraging psychology and sociological literature, subjective social status refers to voters' perceptions of their status vis-à-vis others living in the same society (Jackman and Jackman, 1973). It refers to how people 'rank their level of social esteem or respect that society is according to them' (Bolet, 2022, p. 2). Subjective social status is thus relational because voters form views about themselves based on a referent group within society." (p. 117)
> 

This relational aspect makes status particularly vulnerable to various social transformations:

> "Specifically, recent economic and social transformations have dislodged voters' perceptions about their position in society. While some voters benefit from these transformations, others do not." (p. 117)
> 

### 11.3 Welfare State as Partial Buffer

The findings suggest that even comprehensive welfare states like Finland's don't fully mitigate status anxiety:

> "However, the strength and persistence of radical right parties in Denmark, Norway, Sweden, and Finland suggest that universal welfare may not insulate against radical right support." (p. 121)
> 

This implies that welfare state protections may address material insecurity more effectively than they address subjective status concerns.

### 11.4 Status Restoration vs. Status Prevention

The authors propose that voters who have experienced versus expect status decline have different psychological motivations:

> "Voters who have experienced status decline may seek to restore their lost status. Voters who expect a status decline may instead seek to prevent a loss of status." (p. 119)
> 

This distinction helps explain why radical right parties' nostalgic appeals might be particularly effective for those expecting status decline:

> "When voters expect status decline, their electoral responses may be triggered by a fear of falling down the social ladder." (p. 119)
> 

### 11.5 Symbolic Status Protection

The authors suggest radical right parties offer symbolic status protection that's particularly appealing to those fearing status decline:

> "In Western Europe today, radical right parties latch onto these voters' status grievances by offering nostalgic and populist appeals that seek to roll back these social and economic transformations." (p. 117)
> 

More specifically:

> "Radical right parties attract these social groups by appealing to nostalgia, offering to roll back the transformations that have brought about their status decline or assuage their diminished status by marginalising social groups that have seen their social position improve like immigrants." (p. 118)
> 

### 12.3 Policy Implications

While the authors don't explicitly outline policy recommendations, several implications can be drawn from their analysis:

1. **Addressing status anxiety, not just material insecurity**: Policies focused solely on material welfare may not address the status concerns driving radical right support among middle-income voters.
2. **Targeting (lower) middle-class anxieties**: Interventions should consider the specific concerns of (lower) middle-income groups who fear status decline, not just the already-disadvantaged.
3. **Broadening social safety nets**: The persistence of status anxiety even in comprehensive welfare states suggests the need for broader approaches beyond traditional economic security measures.
4. **Symbolic inclusion**: Addressing feelings of cultural displacement and status threat may require symbolic policies that affirm the value and belonging of groups fearing marginalization.
5. **Cross-national implications**: The findings suggest these dynamics may be even more pronounced in less comprehensive welfare states:

> "If these expectations trigger radical right support in Nordic welfare states, they may be even more pertinent in less comprehensive welfare states." (abstract)
> 

## 1. Theoretical Framework and Research Gap

The authors position their work within a growing literature examining how changes in subjective social status influence electoral behavior, particularly support for radical right parties. They note that while numerous studies link radical right support to experienced status decline, fewer examine how anticipated status decline shapes voting behavior.

The key theoretical contribution is their distinction between two aspects of status decline:

> "Although this burgeoning scholarship demonstrates the political implications of voters' subjective social status, there is still ambiguity as to whether the relationship between status decline and support for radical right and radical left parties is led by the experience of status decline, or expectation of status decline or both." (p. 118)
> 

The authors identify a significant research gap:

> "Yet, few studies compare the experience of status decline against the expectation of status decline within this burgeoning scholarship... One of the main reasons for the lack of this comparison is the paucity of direct measures of both types of status decline which, in turn, increases a reliance on imperfect proxies." (p. 118)
> 

They argue that researchers typically use indirect measures of status decline, such as:

- Exposure to industrial robots
- Differences in growth rates across income deciles
- Occupational trajectories among routine workers
- Differences between actual and predicted status based on parental background

Their study addresses this gap by employing direct subjective measures of both experienced and expected status decline, allowing for a more precise comparison of their relative impact on electoral behavior.

The authors suggest these two aspects of status decline may operate through different psychological mechanisms:

> "When voters experienced status decline, their electoral responses may be triggered by resentment and grievance over their diminished social status. When voters expect a status decline, their electoral responses may be triggered by a fear of falling down the social ladder." (p. 119)
> 

This distinction leads them to propose that these voter groups might prefer different political solutions:

> "Correspondingly, these two groups of voters may have somewhat different demands. Voters who have experienced status decline may seek to restore their lost status. Voters who expect a status decline may instead seek to prevent a loss of status." (p. 119)
> 

## 2. Hypotheses Development

The paper develops four specific hypotheses about how experienced and expected status decline affect support for radical right and center-right parties:

**H1:** Voters who have experienced status decline support radical right parties more than voters who have experienced status improvement.

**H2:** Voters who have experienced status decline support center right parties less than voters who have experienced status improvement.

**H3:** Voters who expect status decline support radical right parties more than voters who expect status improvement.

**H4:** Voters who expect status decline support center right parties less than voters who expect status improvement.

The authors draw on risk aversion literature to theorize why voters who expect status decline might favor radical right over radical left parties, despite both offering "radical" solutions:

> "Related literature on risk aversion shows that individuals who are exposed to both economic and noneconomic risks are unwilling to make risky decisions... Furthermore, Fernandez and Rodrik (1991) demonstrated that individuals favour the status quo over reforms when the distributive outcomes from these reforms are uncertain, even if these individuals are risk-neutral, forward-looking, and rational." (p. 119-120)
> 

This framework suggests that voters under status threat may prefer solutions that feel familiar (even if radical) rather than those that convey uncertainty. The authors note that while both radical right and radical left parties propose transformative solutions, they differ in how they frame these solutions:

> "When compared with the frames of the radical left..., the frames of radical right parties rely much more on nostalgia... Nostalgia enables radical right parties to frame their solution in terms of the 'pleasure of familiarity, the comfort of tradition and feelings of reassurance'... which evokes familiarity rather than uncertainty." (p. 120)
> 

## 3. Research Context: Finland

The authors justify their selection of Finland as a research context by highlighting several key features:

1. Finland has undergone similar economic and social transformations to other Western European countries
2. The Finnish political landscape has shifted from class-based parties toward smaller parties, including the radical right Finns Party:

> "For the latter half of the 20th century, large class-based parties like the centre-left workers' party (the Social Democratic Party), the centrist agrarian party (the Centre Party), and the centre-right bourgeois party (the National Coalition Party) dominated the Finnish political landscape. Recently, however, the landscape has fragmented, and electoral outcomes have become less predictable." (p. 121)
> 
1. The Finns Party has become a persistent feature of the Finnish electoral landscape:

> "The rise and persistence of the Finns Party thus bring Finland in line with other Western European countries, where radical right parties are a mainstay in their electoral landscapes." (p. 121)
> 

The authors note that Finland lacks an "electorally successful radical left party," which limits their ability to test relationships regarding radical left support.

They argue that findings from Finland are likely applicable to other Western European contexts:

> "Overall, we consider the results from Finland relevant for other Western European countries because it has undergone similar societal and economic transformations and has a radical right party that is regarded as a credible and popular party within the electoral landscape." (p. 121)
> 

## 4. Methodology

### 4.1 Data Collection

The study utilizes two nationally representative cross-sectional surveys from Finland:

**2018 Survey (n=4,076):**

- Collected in three stages to improve representativeness
- First stage: online survey of 20,000 randomly sampled individuals (response rate: 12.1%)
- Second stage: targeted 13,197 underrepresented groups for in-person/phone interviews (response rate: 7.6%)
- Third stage: focused on lower-educated, working-age people through an internet panel (response rate: 16.2%)
- Applied weights to adjust for gender, age, and education
- Final analytical sample: 2,680 respondents (after accounting for missing values)

**2020 Survey (n=2,106):**

- Collected during the COVID-19 pandemic (August-September 2020)
- Administered via an online panel by Taloustutkimus
- Applied weights to adjust for gender, age, and place of residence
- Final analytical sample: 1,190 respondents (after accounting for missing values)

### 4.2 Variables

**Dependent Variable:**

- Party choice if parliamentary elections were held now
- Focused on the six largest parties: Left Alliance, Green League, Social Democratic Party, Centre Party, National Coalition Party, and Finns Party

**Key Explanatory Variables:**

- Experience of status decline: measured by asking respondents to rate how their status had changed during the past five years
- Expectation of status decline: measured by asking respondents to rate how their status would change during the upcoming five years
- Both variables were originally measured on a five-point Likert scale but recoded to three categories due to small case numbers at the extremes:
    1. Status improved/will improve
    2. Status stayed/will stay the same
    3. Status declined/will decline

**Control Variables:**

- Sociodemographic: age, education, gender, marital status, labor market status, household income, native language, childhood economic conditions
- Political ideology: self-reported left-right and liberal-conservative positioning
- Issue positions: views on redistribution and immigration (2018 survey only)

### 4.3 Analytical Strategy

The authors employed multinomial logit regression models with:

- Region-clustered standard errors
- Region fixed effects
- Applied weights for age, gender, and education (2018) or age, gender, and place of residence (2020)

For each dataset, they estimated multiple models with increasing control variable sets:

**2018 Data:**

- Model 1: Status variables + sociodemographic controls
- Model 2: Model 1 + left-right and liberal-conservative ideologies
- Model 3: Model 2 + issue positions on redistribution and immigration

**2020 Data:**

- Model 4: Status variables + sociodemographic controls (excluding childhood economic conditions and mother tongue)
- Model 5: Model 4 + left-right and liberal-conservative ideologies

Results are presented as average marginal effects, showing differences in group means relative to the reference categories "status has improved" and "status will improve."

## 5. Key Findings: Descriptive Results

The authors first present descriptive findings on the sociodemographic profiles of respondents who experienced versus expected status decline.

### 5.1 Sociodemographic Profiles

**Similarities between groups:**

- Both groups tend to be older
- Both groups are typically not higher educated
- Both groups often live in less economically prosperous regions (northern and eastern Finland)

**Key differences:**

> "Voters who have experienced status decline have low income, whereas voters who expect status decline have (lower)middle income." (p. 126)
> 

This pattern persisted across both surveys:

> "Crucially, this sociodemographic difference was observed both during normal times in 2018 as well as during exceptional times of the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020." (p. 132)
> 

The authors interpret these patterns as suggesting different social positions of the two groups:

> "Echoing recent research (Engler and Weisstanner, 2020; Mayer, 2015), voters who expect and are threatened by status decline are not at the bottom of the social ladder but are instead located somewhat higher up and fear falling." (p. 132)
> 

More specifically:

> "Put differently, it seems that voters who experienced status decline are often located among the lower classes, whereas voters who expect status decline are often located among the 'sandwiched' (lower)middle class." (p. 132-133)
> 

### 5.2 Specific Sociodemographic Patterns (2018 data)

**Experience of status decline:**

- Most prevalent among those with comprehensive school and vocational education
- No substantial gender differences
- Most prevalent among older age groups (particularly 60-65 and 65+ years)
- Most prevalent in northern and eastern Finland
- Most prevalent in the lowest income quartile

**Expectation of status decline:**

- Most prevalent among those with vocational education (medium education levels)
- Little variation by gender
- Most prevalent among those near retirement age (60-65 years)
- Most prevalent in northern and eastern Finland
- Most prevalent in the third income quartile (3,000-4,999 euros)

## 6. Key Findings: Multivariate Results

The authors' multivariate analyses reveal several crucial findings regarding the relationship between status decline and electoral preferences.

### 6.1 Experience of Status Decline and Voting Preferences

The experience of status decline showed an inconsistent relationship with radical right support:

> "There is no consistent evidence that voters who had experienced status decline support the Finns Party more than voters who had experienced status improvement in 2018 and 2020." (p. 132)
> 

This led to the rejection of Hypothesis 1:

> "Based on data from the 2018 and 2020 surveys, the overall results do not show that respondents who experienced a status decline favour the radical right Finns Party more than respondents who experienced status improvement as stated in Hypothesis 1. Hypothesis 1 is thus rejected." (p. 129)
> 

However, the authors did find that voters who experienced status decline were less likely to support the center-right National Coalition Party in 2018:

> "Respondents who reported that their status has stayed the same or has declined support the National Coalition Party less than those who reported that their status had improved." (p. 128)
> 

This relationship was less consistent in 2020:

> "Respondents who experienced a status decline support the bourgeois centre-right National Coalition Party less than those who experienced status improvement in 2018, as stated in Hypothesis 2. However, it is not significant when respondents' political ideologies were controlled for in the 2020 data set." (p. 129-130)
> 

In their most comprehensive model from 2018 (Model 3), voters who experienced status decline were 8.4 percentage points less likely to support the National Coalition Party compared to those who experienced status improvement (p < 0.01). This difference declined to 6.3 percentage points in 2020 (Model 5) and became statistically non-significant (p = 0.063).

### 6.2 Expectation of Status Decline and Voting Preferences

The study's most consistent and robust finding was the relationship between expected status decline and support for the radical right Finns Party:

> "Respondents who expect their status to decline are consistently more likely to support the radical right Finns Party than respondents who expect their status to improve." (p. 132)
> 

This relationship held across both survey waves and all model specifications:

> "In 2018 and 2020, voters who expect their status to decline are consistently more likely to support the radical right Finns Party than voters who expect their status to improve." (p. 132)
> 

The magnitude of this effect was substantial:

> "On a probability scale from 0 to 1, respondents whose status will decline support the Finns Party 0.063 points more than respondents who expect their status to improve. When the predicted intercept for support for the Finns Party starts from a low baseline of 0.124 points, this difference of 0.063 points is substantial." (p. 128)
> 

In the 2020 dataset, this effect was even stronger:

> "On a probability scale from 0 to 1, respondents who expect their status to decline support the Finns Party by 0.113 points more than respondents who expect their status to improve. When the predicted intercept for support for the Finns Party starts from a low baseline of 0.193 points, this difference of 0.113 points is substantial." (p. 129)
> 

This led to the acceptance of Hypothesis 3:

> "Therefore, we can accept Hypothesis 3, which states that respondents who expect status decline support radical right parties more than their counterparts who expect status improvement." (p. 130)
> 

However, the authors found limited support for Hypothesis 4:

> "We do not find that respondents who expect a status decline support centre-right parties less than those who expect a status improvement, as stated in Hypothesis 4... Hence, Hypothesis 4 can be rejected." (p. 130)
> 

### 6.3 Summary of Hypothesis Testing

- **H1** (Experienced decline → Radical right): **Rejected** - No consistent relationship
- **H2** (Experienced decline → Less center-right): **Partially supported** - Supported in 2018, not in 2020
- **H3** (Expected decline → Radical right): **Supported** - Consistent effect across all models
- **H4** (Expected decline → Less center-right): **Rejected** - No consistent relationship

## 8. Methodological Strengths and Limitations

### 8.1 Strengths

1. **Direct measurement of status perceptions**: Unlike prior studies that relied on proxies, this research used direct subjective measures of both experienced and expected status decline.
2. **Cross-time comparison**: Using data from both 2018 and 2020 allowed the authors to test the stability of their findings across different contexts, including during the COVID-19 pandemic.
3. **Comprehensiveness of controls**: The models included extensive sociodemographic controls, political ideology measures, and issue positions.
4. **Detailed sociodemographic profiles**: The study provided detailed descriptive analyses of the sociodemographic characteristics of voters experiencing and expecting status decline.

### 8.2 Limitations

1. **Lack of radical left party**: As the authors note, Finland lacks an electorally successful radical left party, limiting their ability to test hypotheses about radical left support:

> "Notably, Finland does not have an electorally successful radical left party. The previously hard-left, the Left Alliance is now, by most accords, a new-left party (Dunphy, 2007). Therefore, we cannot test the relationship between experience and expectations of status decline and support for radical left parties." (p. 120)
> 
1. **Cross-sectional data**: The study uses cross-sectional rather than panel data, limiting causal inference about the relationships between status perceptions and voting preferences.
2. **Limited generalizability**: While the authors make a case for Finland's similarity to other Western European contexts, they acknowledge the uniqueness of Finland's Nordic welfare state model.
3. **Vote abstention not measured**: The dependent variable didn't include vote abstention as an option:

> "Unfortunately, the survey did not include an option asking if respondents would not vote if elections were held now. As such, vote abstention could not be included in the dependent variable." (p. 122)
>

## 12. Conclusions and Policy Implications

The study's findings have several important implications for understanding political behavior and policy responses in contemporary democracies.

### 12.1 Key Empirical Conclusion

The paper's central empirical finding challenges conventional wisdom:

> "Using multivariate analyses, we find that voters who expect status decline consistently prefer radical right parties more than voters who expect status improvement. However, there is no robust evidence of radical right support among voters who have experienced status decline. These findings suggest that the expectation, not experience, of status decline drives radical right support." (abstract)
> 

This finding reorients our understanding of radical right support:

> "These differences indicate that it would be useful for researchers to conceptually distinguish between experience and expectation of status decline." (p. 132)
> 

### 12.2 Contribution to Broader Academic Debates

The authors situate their findings within broader political science debates:

> "Political science research has historically paid much attention to economic voting, that is, voting based on voters' economic interests. Recently, it has begun focusing on the electoral consequences of subjective social status to understand the growth and persistence of radical right support in Western Europe." (p. 117)
> 

Their findings suggest that understanding status anxieties among middle-income voters may be as important as examining the grievances of economic "losers":

> "Further, we suggest that to understand political flux, it is just as important to identify those who feel that they are losers of past socioeconomic transformations, as it is essential to pay attention to those who fear worsening social status." (p. 117)
> 

### 12.3 Policy Implications

While the authors don't explicitly outline policy recommendations, several implications can be drawn from their analysis:

1. **Addressing status anxiety, not just material insecurity**: Policies focused solely on material welfare may not address the status concerns driving radical right support among middle-income voters.
2. **Targeting (lower) middle-class anxieties**: Interventions should consider the specific concerns of (lower) middle-income groups who fear status decline, not just the already-disadvantaged.
3. **Broadening social safety nets**: The persistence of status anxiety even in comprehensive welfare states suggests the need for broader approaches beyond traditional economic security measures.
4. **Symbolic inclusion**: Addressing feelings of cultural displacement and status threat may require symbolic policies that affirm the value and belonging of groups fearing marginalization.
5. **Cross-national implications**: The findings suggest these dynamics may be even more pronounced in less comprehensive welfare states:

> "If these expectations trigger radical right support in Nordic welfare states, they may be even more pertinent in less comprehensive welfare states." (abstract)
> 

### 12.4 Final Reflections

The paper concludes by emphasizing the importance of distinguishing between past experiences and future expectations:

> "In short, it seems that expectations of status decline, not experiences of status decline, explain persistent support for the radical right in Finland." (p. 132)
> 

This insight reframes our understanding of the social psychology driving radical right support:

> "Overall, this persistent link between expectation of status decline and radical right support suggests that other parties will struggle to dislodge this segment of the Finns Party's electorate." (p. 132-133)
> 

The authors suggest this psychological dynamic may explain patterns observed elsewhere:

> "This may also explain why support for Donald Trump is driven not only by white working-class Americans but also by middle-class Americans." (p. 133)
> 

These findings invite us to reconsider conventional political narratives about the drivers of populism, suggesting that "fear of falling" among the (lower) middle class may be as politically consequential as resentment among those who have already fallen.
