## Comprehensive Summary

This research investigates a critical paradox in contemporary political economy: how labour market policies intended to mitigate occupational risks may actually intensify support for populist radical right parties (PRRP). Bergman's core theoretical argument centers on the concept of "nostalgic producerism" – the idea that PRRP economic ideology privileges "makers" (traditional workers) over "takers" (bureaucrats, immigrants, welfare recipients). The paper demonstrates that different labour market policies produce heterogeneous effects across occupational groups, with some policies creating "self-undermining" feedback effects that catalyze rather than cushion PRRP support. Using European Social Survey data from 14 West European nations (2014-2018), the study reveals that active labour market policies increase PRRP voting across most occupational groups, while employment protection legislation produces divergent effects depending on workers' skill specificity and occupational risk levels. This research significantly advances our understanding of how welfare state policies interact with political behavior, challenging conventional assumptions about the mitigating effects of social protection on populist voting.

## Key Takeaways

### 1. Nostalgic Producerism Framework

The paper establishes "nostalgic producerism" as a central organizing principle of PRRP economic ideology, combining elements of welfare nostalgia and producerist frames. As Bergman explains: "Focussing on 'hardworking' typically male breadwinners is not simply rhetoric, as emerging studies have also identified what policies result from PRRP in government" (p. 523-524). This framework distinguishes between deserving "makers" and undeserving "takers," fundamentally shaping policy preferences.

### 2. Heterogeneous Policy Effects

Labour market policies do not uniformly reduce PRRP support but create differentiated impacts across occupational groups. The research demonstrates that "workers most at risk (those with highly specialized skills or low-skilled workers in routine occupations) might actually increase their support for PRRP with strong government interventions in the economy" (p. 535).

### 3. Self-Undermining Feedback Effects

Active labour market policies paradoxically increase PRRP support through visibility and salience mechanisms. Bergman finds that "active labour market policy spending appears to have a catalyzing effect on support for PRRP, with greater spending leading to greater support for PRRP" (p. 530), challenging conventional cushioning hypotheses.

### 4. Employment Protection Complexity

Employment protection legislation produces both cushioning and catalyzing effects depending on occupational characteristics. The analysis reveals that "employment protection legislation is found to both catalyze and cushion support for PRRP depending on the level of occupational risk" (p. 530).

### 5. Modernization Winners vs. Losers Distinction

The research emphasizes the importance of distinguishing between different occupational groups affected by technological change. "Too often overlooked are the political behaviours of those experiencing socio-economic status increases in the contemporary economy" (p. 521), highlighting how professionals and managers respond differently to labour market policies.

### 6. Policy Visibility and Salience

Large-scale programmes increase the political salience of labour market interventions. "More 'visible' welfare states and labour market interventions shape the overall salience of social programmes" (Gingrich, 2014, p. 527), making them more likely to influence voting behavior.

## Introduction and Motivation Analysis

### Research Problem and Significance

Bergman's research addresses a fundamental empirical puzzle in contemporary political economy: why labour market policies designed to protect workers from economic insecurity may paradoxically increase support for parties that oppose such interventions. The author positions this investigation within broader debates about the relationship between welfare states and populist voting, noting that "only recently there have been attempts to bridge the literatures between the welfare state and party politics focussed on the populist radical right" (p. 520).

The research significance stems from challenging conventional wisdom about policy feedback effects. Traditional theories suggest that welfare state policies create constituencies that support their continuation, but Bergman identifies circumstances where policies may "catalyze" opposition. This puzzle becomes particularly acute given the post-Great Recession clarity in PRRP ideological positioning, as the author observes: "after the Great Recession, there is now a general agreement of the ideologies of Populist Radical Right Parties (PRRP) as combining nativism, authoritarianism, and populism" (Mudde, 2015, p. 520).

The broader questions addressed relate to fundamental issues in political economy regarding how economic policies shape political coalitions. As Bergman frames it: "how might the welfare state [and economic policies] shape the fortunes of RPPR" (Rathgeb and Busemeyer, 2022: 2, p. 520)? This inquiry extends beyond immediate policy effects to examine how institutional arrangements create political dynamics that may undermine their own sustainability.

The author situates this work within evolving understanding of populist constituency formation, arguing that "one of the growing constituencies of populist movements has been those facing labour market risks" (p. 520). This framing connects micro-level occupational experiences to macro-level political transformations, addressing gaps in existing literature that often treat economic and cultural explanations of populism as separate phenomena.

### Theoretical Positioning and Innovation

Bergman positions his theoretical contribution at the intersection of three distinct research traditions, creating novel synthesis opportunities. First, he builds upon demand-side explanations of populism focused on occupational risk and status anxiety, referencing scholars like Gidron and Hall (2017) and Im et al. (2019). Second, he incorporates supply-side analysis of PRRP policy preferences, drawing on recent work by Fenger (2018), Ivaldi and Mazzoleni (2019), and Rathgeb and Busemeyer (2022). Third, he integrates policy feedback theory from welfare state literature, particularly Gingrich's work on visibility and salience effects.

The theoretical innovation lies in connecting these previously separate literatures through the "nostalgic producerism" framework. As Bergman explains: "This paper blends these three lines of research: occupational labour market risks, preferences over labour market policies, and heterogeneous feedback effects of such policies" (p. 521). This synthesis allows for more nuanced predictions about when and how policies will affect political behavior.

The author challenges existing research by arguing that previous studies have been too broad in their categorizations. He notes that "subsuming all workers in occupations subject to automation into a single measure produces some inexplicable findings" (p. 521), advocating for more fine-grained analysis of different occupational groups. This methodological innovation enables the identification of heterogeneous effects that aggregate analyses miss.

Bergman also advances theoretical understanding by incorporating PRRP policy preferences into explanations of voter behavior. Unlike previous research that focused primarily on voter characteristics, this approach recognizes that "the supply side of economic policy of PRRP is congruent with their voters" (p. 524), creating feedback loops between party positions and voter preferences.

## Literature Review and Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Major Theoretical Traditions Engaged

### Demand-Side Populism and Occupational Risk Theory

The paper extensively engages with literature on demand-side explanations of populist support, particularly focusing on how economic transformations create constituencies receptive to populist appeals. Bergman draws heavily on the "modernization winners and losers" framework developed by scholars like Halikiopoulou and Vlandas (2016), Oesch (2013), and Autor et al. (2015).

**Theoretical Foundation:** This approach assumes that technological change and globalization create systematic advantages for some occupational groups while disadvantaging others. The core mechanism involves "skill-biased technological change" that complements high-skilled workers while substituting for routine, middle-skill occupations. As Bergman explains: "The diffusion of computers complemented skilled workers, increased their salaries, and led to job growth in high-tech high-skill fields... however, this same process substituted for labour in routine tasks performed in industrial, sales, or clerical middle-income occupations" (p. 522).

**Key Scholars and Works:** Central contributors include David Autor's work on job polarization, Daniel Oesch's analysis of occupational change in Europe, and Torben Iversen and David Soskice's varieties of capitalism approach to skill formation. Bergman particularly builds on Im et al. (2019) regarding automation risks and Gidron and Hall's work on status anxiety.

**Empirical Evidence:** Research consistently demonstrates that routine occupations have declined relative to both high-skill analytical jobs and low-skill service positions. "In the 1990s and 2000s, routine occupations decreased by a relative third while nonroutine analytical and interactive occupations have increased by a third" (Oesch, 2013, p. 522). This polarization correlates with increased support for populist parties among affected groups.

**Limitations and Debates:** The literature faces challenges in explaining why some affected workers turn to populist parties while others do not, and why support varies significantly across countries with similar economic structures. Additionally, the focus on "losers" has meant less attention to how "winners" of modernization might also support populist parties under certain conditions.

**Authors' Engagement:** Bergman builds upon this foundation while addressing its limitations through more nuanced occupational categorizations and attention to heterogeneous effects. He argues that "too often overlooked are the political behaviours of those experiencing socio-economic status increases in the contemporary economy" (p. 521), extending the framework to include modernization winners.

### Supply-Side Analysis of PRRP Economic Ideology

The second major theoretical tradition involves analyzing the policy preferences and ideological positioning of populist radical right parties themselves. This literature has evolved significantly since the Great Recession, moving from characterizations of PRRP as economically ambiguous to recognition of coherent policy agendas.

**Theoretical Foundation:** This approach assumes that party positions matter for voter choice and that PRRP have developed distinctive economic ideologies that differentiate them from mainstream parties. The core theoretical innovation is the identification of "welfare chauvinism," "nostalgic producerism," and "particularistic-authoritarianism" as organizing principles.

**Key Scholars and Works:** Major contributors include Cas Mudde's definitional work on populist radical right ideology, Menno Fenger's analysis of welfare nostalgia, and Gilles Ivaldi and Oscar Mazzoleni's research on producerism. Philip Rathgeb and Marius Busemeyer have provided important recent syntheses of PRRP economic positions.

**Empirical Evidence:** Content analysis of party manifestos and speeches reveals consistent patterns across countries. As Bergman notes: "An analysis of manifestos, from mainly 2017 and later, have identified that PRRP take the strongest stances against social investment measures – childcare, higher education, rehabilitation, retraining, job-search assistance – and the strongest stances favouring old age pensions" (Enggist and Pinggera, 2022, p. 523).

**Limitations and Debates:** The literature still grapples with variation across parties and countries, and questions remain about how these ideological positions translate into actual policy when parties enter government. There's also debate about whether these positions represent genuine policy preferences or strategic positioning.

**Authors' Engagement:** Bergman synthesizes this literature through the "nostalgic producerism" concept, which combines welfare nostalgia with producerist frames. He uses this synthesis to generate specific predictions about how different labour market policies will affect PRRP support.

### Policy Feedback Theory and Welfare State Politics

The third major tradition involves policy feedback theory, which examines how existing policies shape political preferences and coalition formation. This literature, pioneered by Paul Pierson and developed by scholars like Jane Gingrich, provides tools for understanding how policies create or undermine their own political support.

**Theoretical Foundation:** Policy feedback theory assumes that policies create incentives, resources, and cognitive frameworks that influence subsequent political behavior. The core mechanisms include material effects (who benefits), interpretive effects (how policies are understood), and visibility effects (how salient policies are to voters).

**Key Scholars and Works:** Paul Pierson's work on path dependence and policy feedback provides the foundational framework. Jane Gingrich has made important contributions regarding visibility and salience effects, while Jacob Hacker and Kathleen Thelen have developed complementary approaches to institutional change.

**Empirical Evidence:** Research demonstrates that welfare state policies generally create constituencies that support their continuation, but also identifies conditions under which policies may undermine their own support. The literature on "self-undermining" effects is more limited but growing.

**Limitations and Debates:** The literature has been stronger at explaining policy stability than policy change, and has paid less attention to how policies might create opposition. There's also ongoing debate about the relative importance of different feedback mechanisms.

**Authors' Engagement:** Bergman extends policy feedback theory by identifying conditions under which labour market policies produce "self-undermining" rather than "self-reinforcing" effects. He argues that "the implementation of such policies that intend to 'cushion' radical right support, might have an adverse feedback effect of 'catalyzing' greater support for radical right parties" (p. 521).

### Theoretical Synthesis and Framework

Bergman's theoretical synthesis creates a novel framework that integrates insights from all three traditions while addressing their individual limitations. The synthesis centers on the concept of "nostalgic producerism," which provides a lens for understanding how occupational risks, policy preferences, and feedback effects interact.

**Integration of Theoretical Perspectives:** The framework begins with demand-side explanations of occupational risk, but incorporates supply-side analysis of party positions to explain why some policies cushion PRRP support while others catalyze it. Policy feedback theory provides the mechanisms through which these effects operate. As Bergman explains: "Taking inspiration from similar studies on the topic, this article explores to what extent employment risk can predict voting for populist parties and if public policy can mitigate or exacerbate these risks" (p. 521).

**Causal Mechanisms:** The framework proposes several interconnected causal mechanisms. First, occupational characteristics create differential risk exposure, with some workers facing greater threats from automation, globalization, or domestic competition. Second, these risks translate into policy preferences, but these preferences are mediated by ideological frameworks that distinguish between "deserving" and "undeserving" recipients. Third, existing policies create feedback effects through visibility and salience mechanisms, but the direction of these effects depends on whether policies align with or contradict producerist principles.

**Theoretical Grounding:** The synthesis is grounded in recognition that "the risks a family-wage earner faces in the labour market determine one's preferences for social and labour policy" (Iversen and Cusack, 2000, p. 524), but this relationship is mediated by ideological factors. The framework addresses tensions between different theoretical approaches by specifying scope conditions under which each mechanism dominates.

**Scope Conditions:** Bergman identifies several boundary conditions for his theoretical framework. The effects are expected to be strongest among workers who identify with producerist values, in contexts where labour market policies are sufficiently visible to influence political behavior, and in political systems where PRRP represent viable alternatives to mainstream parties. The framework also specifies that effects may vary depending on the institutional context and the specific design of labour market policies.

### Hypotheses and Theoretical Predictions

The theoretical framework generates several specific hypotheses that differentiate this research from previous studies. These hypotheses flow logically from the nostalgic producerism framework and make testable predictions about differential policy effects.

**Core Hypotheses:** The paper's central prediction is that labour market policies will have heterogeneous effects depending on their alignment with producerist principles. "Employment protection legislation would serve as a preferred form of labour market intervention as it captures the producerist aspect of PRRP economic policy" (p. 526), while "active labour market spending... would serve as the least preferred form of labour market intervention" (p. 526).

**Differential Predictions:** Unlike previous research that predicted uniform cushioning effects, Bergman hypothesizes that some policies will catalyze PRRP support. Specifically: "Spending on active labour market policies catalyzes support for PRRP, with the exception of lower-skilled non-tertiary workers, whose occupations tend to have greater employment as a result of these programs" (p. 527).

**Alternative Explanations:** The framework explicitly considers alternative explanations while differentiating its predictions. For employment protection, the hypothesis recognizes both cushioning and catalyzing possibilities: "The probability of PRRP voting is cushioned for most workers through increasing levels of EPL, though protected highly-specialized and low-skill-routine workers may be catalyzed by strong EPL, promoting a PRRP vote" (p. 527).

## Methodology Overview

### Data Sources and Coverage

The empirical analysis employs data from the European Social Survey (ESS) rounds 7-9, covering the period 2014-2018 across 14 West European nations. This temporal coverage is strategically chosen to capture the post-Great Recession period when PRRP economic positions became more coherent and consistent. As Bergman explains: "This timeframe updates by one round the surveys used that identified routine workers as more likely to support PRRP (Im et al., 2019) and corresponds to the dates of the content analyses cited that indicated a shift to a 'nostalgic producerism' frame of PRRP economic policy stances" (p. 528).

The country selection includes Austria, Belgium, Czech Republic, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Ireland, Netherlands, Norway, Poland, Sweden, Switzerland, and the United Kingdom. Post-communist nations are included despite some theoretical differences, while countries without viable PRRP are excluded from the analysis. The author justifies this selection by noting that "post-communist nations are excluded given alternative theoretical mechanisms connecting voter preferences to PRRP support" for some analyses, while including them for broader comparative leverage.

**Data Strengths:** The ESS provides high-quality, harmonized survey data with detailed occupational coding using the International Standard Classification of Occupation (ISCO), allowing for precise measurement of occupational characteristics. The survey includes comprehensive information on political preferences, economic attitudes, and demographic characteristics necessary for the analysis.

**Data Limitations:** The cross-sectional nature of the data limits causal inference, though the author addresses this through careful model specification and robustness checks. The focus on employed workers excludes potentially important groups like the unemployed, though this restriction is theoretically justified given the focus on occupational risk among active labour market participants.

### Key Variables and Operationalization

The research employs sophisticated measurement strategies that advance methodological approaches in this research area. The dependent variable captures voting intentions for PRRP using established party classifications from the PopuList database, updated for the latest ESS rounds.

**Dependent Variables:** PRRP vote choice is measured as a binary variable indicating intention to vote for populist radical right parties. The author follows established coding schemes from previous research (Foster and Frieden, 2019; Gingrich, 2019; Vlandas and Halikiopoulou, 2022) while updating party classifications for recent rounds.

**Independent Variables - Occupational Risk:** The research employs three sophisticated measures of occupational risk that represent theoretical advances over previous studies. Specialization is measured using Cusack et al.'s (2006) adaptation of Iversen and Soskice's skill specificity measure, which "measures the 's1' 'relative skill specificity' measure" by examining the number of subcategories within occupational classifications. As Bergman explains: "If more of the workforce is contained within an occupational category or there is a greater level of skill specialization, then there will be more codes at the ISCO - 4 digit level" (p. 528).

Skill Level comes directly from ISCO classifications, grouping occupations on an ordinal scale from 1 to 4 based on educational requirements. The Routine Task Intensity (RTI) index follows Goos et al. (2014) and has been widely used in automation research, measuring "routine cognitive, routine manual, nonroutine physically manual, nonroutine personal manual, nonroutine cognitive analytical, and nonroutine cognitive personal task inputs" (p. 529).

**Policy Variables:** Three key labour market policy measures are derived from OECD data. Employment Protection Legislation (EPL) measures "the strictness of job dismissal regulations along four dimensions: i) procedural requirements before notice is given; ii) notice period and severance pay; iii) the regulatory framework for unfair dismissals; and iv) enforcement of unfair dismissal regulation" (p. 529). Active Labour Market Policy (ALMP) spending captures government expenditure on training, job placement, and employment subsidies as a percentage of GDP. Unemployment spending measures traditional passive compensation.

**Measurement Innovations:** The research introduces important methodological innovations, particularly in harmonizing spending measures. "These %GDP-based measures are divided by unemployment rate to harmonize these measures per unemployed worker" (Kweon, 2018, p. 529), addressing comparability issues across countries with different unemployment levels.

### Analytical Model and Justification

The analytical approach employs multi-level random intercept models that account for the hierarchical structure of the data, with individuals nested within country-year observations. This specification addresses both theoretical requirements and methodological challenges inherent in comparative research.

**Model Specification:** Following established practices in the literature (Gingrich and Ansell, 2012; Vlandas and Halikiopoulou, 2022), the analysis uses random intercept logistic regression models. As Bergman explains: "Following the approaches of others who also have individual-level variables, national-level variables, and cross-level interactions, multi-level random intercept models will be used" (p. 528).

The basic model specification can be represented as:

$$\text{logit}(P(\text{PRRP Vote}*{ij})) = \alpha_j + \beta_1\text{OccupationalRisk}*{ij} + \beta_2\text{Policy}*{j} + \beta_3(\text{OccupationalRisk}*{ij} \times \text{Policy}*{j}) + \mathbf{X}*{ij}\boldsymbol{\gamma} + \mathbf{Z}*j\boldsymbol{\delta} + \epsilon*{ij}$$

where $i$ indexes individuals, $j$ indexes country-year observations, $\alpha_j$ represents random intercepts, and the interaction terms capture the core theoretical predictions about heterogeneous policy effects.

**Identification Strategy:** The research addresses causal inference challenges through several strategies. The multi-level structure accounts for unobserved country-specific factors that might confound the relationship between policies and voting behavior. The inclusion of comprehensive individual-level controls addresses potential selection effects, while country-level economic controls (GDP growth, unemployment, inequality) account for macroeconomic conditions that might drive both policy choices and political behavior.

**Endogeneity Concerns:** While the cross-sectional design limits causal claims, the author addresses endogeneity concerns through careful theoretical reasoning and model specification. The focus on individual-level variation within countries helps isolate the effects of occupational characteristics, while the policy measures reflect institutional features that change slowly relative to individual voting decisions.

**Robustness and Validation:** The research includes several validation exercises, including analysis of subjective risk perceptions to confirm that occupational risk measures correspond to individual experiences. "First to test the assumption that those with the predicted occupational risks discussed above do indeed feel subjective risk, a fixed effects ordinal logistic model with country clustered standard errors is run using a question for a rotating welfare attitudes module in ESS Round 8 on the subjective likelihood of having enough money to pay bills in the next year" (p. 529).

## Empirical Findings and Analysis

### Validation of Theoretical Assumptions

The empirical analysis begins with validation exercises that confirm key theoretical assumptions underlying the nostalgic producerism framework. These tests demonstrate that occupational risk measures correspond to subjective experiences and that PRRP voters hold distinctive policy preferences.

**Subjective Risk Validation:** Analysis of ESS Round 8 welfare attitudes data confirms that workers in objectively risky occupations report higher levels of subjective economic insecurity. "Key to the argument presented, all of the hypothesized variables that assume to capture labour market risk indeed are correlated with individuals indicating they would be more likely to not have enough money in the coming year: those whose work entails high levels of routine tasks, those in professions that have highly specific skills each are more likely to expect subjective income risk" (p. 530).

**Policy Preference Differences:** The research demonstrates that PRRP voters hold distinctive preferences regarding labour market policies. Analysis reveals that "twice the percentage of PRRP voters being 'strongly against' such a shift in spending and also being less likely to be 'in favor' and 'strongly in-favor' of spending more on education for the unemployed" compared to other voters (p. 530). This finding validates the theoretical assumption that PRRP supporters oppose active labour market policies that align with social investment approaches.

### Core Empirical Results

The main empirical findings support the paper's central theoretical predictions while revealing important nuances in how different policies affect various occupational groups.

**Active Labour Market Policy Effects:** The analysis reveals strong evidence for the catalyzing effects of ALMP spending across most occupational groups. "Active labour market policy spending appears to have a catalyzing effect on support for PRRP, with greater spending leading to greater support for PRRP" (p. 530). This finding challenges conventional cushioning hypotheses and supports the self-undermining feedback theory.

**Employment Protection Legislation:** The results for EPL demonstrate the predicted heterogeneous effects, with both cushioning and catalyzing mechanisms operating simultaneously. "Employment protection legislation is found to both catalyze and cushion support for PRRP depending on the level of occupational risk" (p. 530). The analysis reveals that workers with median skill specificity experience cushioning effects, while those with very high skill specificity show catalyzing effects.

**Heterogeneous Effects by Skill Level:** The research uncovers important differential effects across skill levels that previous aggregate analyses missed. For elementary occupations, "the positive marginal effect of EPL on voting for PRRP for those in the most routine (risky) low-skilled educations" (p. 532) suggests that even protective policies may increase PRRP support among the most vulnerable workers.

### Complex Interaction Effects

The empirical analysis reveals sophisticated interaction patterns that demonstrate the importance of considering occupational heterogeneity in policy feedback research.

**Skill Specificity and Employment Protection:** Figure 1 in the paper illustrates how the effects of employment protection vary dramatically across levels of skill specificity. "In countries with high levels of EPL, those with greater skill specificity are more likely to support PRRP" (p. 531-532), providing evidence for self-reinforcing feedback effects among workers who benefit most from these protections.

**Routine Tasks and Policy Interactions:** The analysis of routine task intensity across different skill levels reveals that policy effects depend critically on occupational context. "Of particular note is how those holding professional occupations are nearly all cushioned by the effects of EPL. Comparing an all-else equal worker, with greater employment protection, the worker is less likely to support PRRP" (p. 532), while elementary occupations show the opposite pattern.

**Active Labour Market Policy Universality:** Perhaps most striking is the near-universal catalyzing effect of ALMP spending. "The impact of active labour market policies is almost universal in their catalyzing effect on PRRP voting" (p. 532), with the notable exception of craft, assembly, clerk, and service occupations where "evidence would suggest that the catalyzing effects and cushioning effects counter on another with no overall effect" (p. 532).

These findings demonstrate that simple aggregate analyses of policy effects can mask important heterogeneity that has significant implications for both theoretical understanding and policy design. The research shows that the same policies can simultaneously cushion and catalyze PRRP support depending on occupational characteristics, suggesting that policy makers face complex trade-offs in designing labour market interventions.

## Theoretical Contributions and Implications

### Advancing Policy Feedback Theory

Bergman's research makes several important contributions to policy feedback theory that extend beyond the immediate study of populist voting. The identification of systematic "self-undermining" feedback effects challenges the field's traditional focus on self-reinforcing mechanisms and policy stability.

**Self-Undermining Mechanisms:** The paper provides evidence that policies can systematically create opposition to themselves under identifiable conditions. This occurs not through failed implementation or unintended consequences, but through the normal operation of visibility and salience mechanisms when policies conflict with voters' ideological frameworks. As the author demonstrates, "larger government interventions may produce a positive feedback on populist voting" (p. 521) when these interventions are perceived as benefiting undeserving recipients.

**Conditional Policy Effects:** The research advances theoretical understanding by specifying conditions under which policies produce different types of feedback. Rather than assuming uniform effects, the framework predicts that policy impacts depend on the interaction between institutional design, target populations, and existing ideological frameworks. This conditional approach provides a more sophisticated foundation for understanding policy sustainability and reform dynamics.

**Visibility and Salience Refinements:** Building on Gingrich's work on policy visibility, the paper refines understanding of how salience mechanisms operate. The research suggests that visibility effects depend not just on programme size but on alignment with dominant ideological frameworks, providing more nuanced predictions about when visibility will enhance or undermine policy support.

### Implications for Welfare State Research

The findings have broad implications for understanding welfare state politics and development in post-industrial societies. The research suggests that traditional approaches to welfare state analysis may need fundamental reconceptualization to account for populist mobilization.

**Coalition Formation:** The paper's findings challenge conventional wisdom about welfare state coalitions by demonstrating how policies intended to protect workers can drive them toward anti-welfare state parties. This suggests that coalition formation in post-industrial societies may be more complex and unstable than traditional theories predict.

**Policy Design Considerations:** The research implies that policy design must consider not only direct effects on target populations but also broader political consequences through visibility and feedback mechanisms. Policies that appear technically sound may be politically counterproductive if they conflict with dominant ideological frameworks or create opportunities for populist mobilization.

**Institutional Sustainability:** The findings raise questions about the long-term sustainability of welfare state institutions in contexts where they may systematically generate opposition to themselves. This suggests need for new approaches to institutional design that account for populist mobilization dynamics.

### Democratic Representation and Responsiveness

The research has important implications for broader questions about democratic representation and responsiveness. If policies designed to help citizens actually increase their support for anti-system parties, this raises fundamental questions about democratic governance.

**Representation Paradoxes:** The paper identifies a potential paradox of democratic representation where responsive policies may undermine the political support necessary for their continuation. This suggests that democratic responsiveness may be more complex than simple policy congruence models assume.

**Elite-Mass Disconnection:** The findings help explain growing disconnection between political elites and mass publics in post-industrial democracies. If mainstream parties' policy responses to economic insecurity actually increase support for their populist competitors, this creates structural challenges for democratic governance.

**Reform Implications:** The research suggests that traditional approaches to labour market reform may need reconsideration. Rather than simply expanding programmes that appear to address worker needs, reformers may need to consider how different policy designs interact with existing ideological frameworks and political dynamics.

## Limitations and Future Research Directions

### Methodological Considerations

While the research makes important theoretical and empirical contributions, several methodological limitations should be acknowledged and addressed in future research.

**Causal Inference:** The cross-sectional nature of the data limits strong causal claims, though the author addresses this through careful model specification and theoretical reasoning. Future research employing panel data or natural experiments could strengthen causal inference about policy feedback effects.

**Measurement Precision:** While the occupational risk measures represent advances over previous research, they may still miss important sources of variation in risk perception and policy preferences. Future research might benefit from more detailed survey instruments that capture subjective risk assessments and policy preferences directly.

**Country Selection:** The focus on Western European cases provides analytical leverage but may limit generalizability to other contexts. Future research should examine whether similar patterns hold in other democratic systems with different institutional arrangements and political party systems.

### Theoretical Extensions

The nostalgic producerism framework opens several promising avenues for future research that could further advance theoretical understanding.

**Populist Left Comparison:** As Bergman notes, "Additional research might also integrate the labour policies and preferences of populist left voters, as one might imagine this group to have different opinions on some of the labour policies discussed in this paper" (p. 535). Comparative analysis of different types of populist parties could refine understanding of when and how economic policies affect anti-establishment voting.

**Policy Domain Extension:** The theoretical framework could be extended to other policy domains where similar dynamics might operate. Areas like immigration policy, trade policy, or environmental regulation might exhibit comparable patterns of self-undermining feedback effects under certain conditions.

**Longitudinal Analysis:** Future research should examine how these relationships evolve over time, particularly as parties enter government and implement their preferred policies. This could provide insight into whether populist policy preferences remain stable or evolve in response to governing experience
