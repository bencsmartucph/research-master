## Core Research Questions

1. To what extent are subjective perceptions of technological risk related to preferred policy responses to automation and digitalization?
2. How does the institutional context of the welfare state affect these individual preferences?

---

## Key Hypotheses

**H1**: Individuals who perceive technological change as an imminent employment risk will generally prefer **compensatory policies over social investment policies**.

> "This may reinforce the general human tendency to prefer less valuable but certain options — that is, limited, but reliable passive compensation — over potentially more valuable but uncertain ones — that is, active labour market policies."
> 

**H2a**: Individuals in residual welfare states prioritise compensation; those in mature welfare states are more likely to prioritise social investment as a 'luxury good' since basic insurance needs are already met.

**H2b**: As perceived technological risk increases, social policy preferences among high-risk individuals **converge** across different welfare state contexts.

---

## Key Findings

### Individual-Level

- Higher perceived risk → less relative support for education/training, more support for public benefits, subsidies, and UBI
- A one SD increase in perceived risk = ~5% increase from mean value allocated to public benefits
- Evidence of a cleavage between technology-embracing "winners" and fearful "losers"

### Cross-Level Interactions

- At low risk: pronounced difference between residual and mature welfare states
- At high risk: **preferences converge** — risk perceptions trump institutional context

> "Yet as the perceived technological risk grows, the gap between more and less generous welfare state contexts increasingly disappears. This suggests that perceptions of individual risk trump the contextual effect of welfare state institutions."
> 

---

## Political Implications

> "The strong preference for short-term compensation among individuals perceiving themselves to be vulnerable to technology-related labor market risks may significantly impact the range of viable policy options in response to technological change."
> 

> "Experts commonly recommend policy responses to automation and digitalization that focus on increasing investments in human capital formation... While these measures may pay off in the longer term, our analysis shows that those most affected by technological change are more likely to seek direct forms of compensation."
> 

> "Recent empirical work on this topic has already shown that those on the losing end of this divide may be more susceptible to the siren calls of right-wing populist parties."
> 

---

## Theoretical Contributions

1. **RBTC Theory**: Current automation threatens middle-class routine cognitive jobs, not just manual labor
2. **Policy Feedback Theory**: Existing welfare institutions shape what people demand — self-undermining feedback in residual states
3. **Social Investment Theory**: Tension between expert recommendations (invest in human capital) and public preferences (compensate immediately)
