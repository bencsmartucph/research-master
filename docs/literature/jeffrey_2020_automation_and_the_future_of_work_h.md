## Core Argument

Simply increasing perceived vulnerability to automation doesn't automatically increase support for redistributive policies. Political rhetoric plays a decisive role in shaping whether vulnerable workers support or resist redistribution.

> "One explanation for this 'paradox of redistribution' is that some individuals hold beliefs about inequality, fairness, or the trustworthiness of government that cause them to oppose redistributive policies that they would otherwise benefit from."
> 

> "When individuals perceive themselves to be vulnerable to a labor market shock, their beliefs are particularly responsive to rhetoric that speaks to their concerns about economic hardship — a phenomenon that has been exploited by populist commentators to misdirect voters' angst about economic hardship towards immigrants or the 'undeserving poor.'"
> 

---

## Research Design

Experimental study testing how different rhetorical framings interact with automation threats to shape policy preferences. Hypotheses:

1. Rhetoric stigmatizing welfare claimants or suggesting taxing 'wealth creators' threatens jobs will suppress support for redistribution
2. Alternative rhetoric designed to challenge these narratives can increase support for redistribution

---

## Key Findings

### Job Protection vs. Income Support

> "The 'Automation Shock' treatment increases support for the policy of reducing working time with no reduction in pay... This may suggest that job-protecting policies would be viewed more favorably by vulnerable workers than policies intended to offer protection from economic hardship via a social safety net."
> 

### The Role of Fairness Perceptions

One mechanism via which rhetoric acts on preferences is by appealing to individuals' sense that automation-induced inequality would be **unfair**. This aligns with findings from Almås, Cappelen, and Tungodden (2020).

### Heterogeneous Effects by Education

- Bachelor's degree holders show the **most sensitivity** to rhetoric
- As more-educated workers are increasingly exposed to automation, a key political outcome could be **greater resistance to redistribution**
- But carefully designed rhetoric may counteract such resistance

### General vs. Specific Preferences

People concerned about automation are more supportive of redistribution **in general** but not significantly more likely to support **specific** redistributive policies (welfare, minimum wage, unemployment benefits). Concrete policy proposals make costs more salient.

---

## Implications

> "In an era of 'post-truth politics,' understanding how exposure to an automation shock interacts with exposure to politicized rhetoric is an important part of the puzzle."
> 

Economic threats don't operate in a rhetorical vacuum — their political consequences depend heavily on how they're framed.
