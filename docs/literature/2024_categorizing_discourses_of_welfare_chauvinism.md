# 2024 "Categorizing Discourses of Welfare Chauvinism" This paper by Leruth, Taylor-Gooby, and Györy introduces a categorization framework for welfare chauvinism - defined as "the exclusion of non-citizens who live permanently within a state from social benefits and services" - examining how it manifests in both populist radical right party platforms and public discourse across different welfare state contexts.

## Key Conceptual Framework: Five Categories of Welfare Chauvinism

The authors identify five distinct categories or frames of welfare chauvinism:

### 1. Temporary Welfare Chauvinism

- **Definition**: "Refers to the limitation in the access of non-natives to benefits and services in time, for instance after they have worked and paid taxes for 1 year" (p.4)
- **Example**: "Alternative for Germany: four years of employment before gaining access to social benefits" (p.11)
- **Justification**: "The argument is that the restriction of access to benefits and services is justified by a period of integration into the domestic system" (p.4)

### 2. Selective Welfare Chauvinism

- **Definition**: "Relates to the 'space' dimension as reflected in the legal status of people in a specific country. It limits the access of some but not all non-natives to benefits and service" (p.4)
- **Example**: "Danish People's Party: no access to early retirement schemes for refugees" (p.11)
- **Basis**: "Such categories are mostly driven by nationality criteria, but also by residence status which can be directly related to the citizenship of the subject" (p.5)

### 3. Functional Welfare Chauvinism

- **Definition**: "Restricts access to specific benefits and services, such as healthcare or unemployment benefits" (p.5)
- **Example**: "UK Independence Party: no access to non-urgent public healthcare (National Health Service; combined with a temporal dimension, i.e. paying taxes for five consecutive years)" (p.11)
- **Targeting**: "The benefits and services that are targeted by welfare chauvinist policies can be driven by the levels of domestic politicization" (p.5)

### 4. Cultural Welfare Chauvinism

- **Definition**: "Restricts access to benefits and services to non-natives until their integration can be demonstrated, either through language skills or cultural knowledge" (p.5)
- **Example**: "German mini-public participants: conditioning access to cash benefits to participation in a language course" (p.11)
- **Historical context**: "Cultural chauvinism can be traced back to the roots of welfare chauvinism in the 1970s, when debates took place in the Danish Parliament regarding the introduction of language requirements to gain access to employment benefits" (p.5)

### 5. Unconditional Welfare Chauvinism

- **Definition**: "The total exclusion of all non-natives from any benefits and services, no matter their work, family or health status, for an indefinite period" (p.5)
- **Implementation note**: "In practice, unconditional chauvinism is unlikely to become policy within the European Union, as it would effectively exclude all immigrants from the system and would break EU legislation" (p.5)
- **Electoral strategy**: "Unconditional chauvinism as a category of welfare chauvinism is perhaps best understood as an electoral strategy rather than as an inherent part of a party's ideology" (p.5)

## Research Method and Countries

The authors examined welfare chauvinism in three countries representing different welfare state types:

- **Denmark**: Social democratic/universal citizenship system
- **Germany**: Corporatist/social insurance-based system
- **UK**: Liberal/targeted system

The study analyzed:

1. Political discourse through party manifestos (2014-2017) of:
    - Danish People's Party (DF)
    - Alternative for Germany (AfD)
    - UK Independence Party (UKIP)
2. Public discourse through mini-publics (focus groups of 34-37 participants over two days) in:
    - Copenhagen
    - Berlin
    - Birmingham

## Key Findings

### Temporary Welfare Chauvinism

- **Denmark**: Both Danish People's Party and mini-public participants favored extending a wait period to 6 years before accessing benefits
    - *"No entitlement to social benefits until you've contributed for say six years. I should say I'm not talking about medical assistance here."* (Danish mini-public participant, p.7)
- **Germany**: AfD advocated for minimum 4 years of employment before benefit access
- **UK**: UKIP proposed 5-year waiting period for housing and NHS access

### Selective Welfare Chauvinism

- **Denmark**: DF specifically targeted refugees and asylum seekers
    - *"If you come here as a refugee then we need to get you out onto the labour market quickly so that you can make money. You don't have the right to come here and receive social security benefits."* (Danish mini-public participant, p.8)
- **Germany**: AfD distinguished between refugees, EU migrants, and skilled non-EU migrants
    - *"Each single asylum seeker immigrates into the social system, as he [sic] is entitled to benefits as soon as he crosses the border."* (AfD, p.8)
- **UK**: UKIP focused on EU citizens, while mini-public participants targeted non-EU nationals

### Functional Welfare Chauvinism

- **Denmark**: Focused primarily on cash benefits rather than in-kind services
    - *"Asylum seekers should be allowed to contribute from the outset but they mustn't get welfare benefits such as child benefit from day one."* (Danish mini-public participant, p.9)
- **Germany**: Little evidence of functional welfare chauvinism
- **UK**: Strong focus on health services and child benefits
    - UKIP proposed restricting migrants' access to non-urgent healthcare services

### Cultural Welfare Chauvinism

- **Germany**: Mini-public participants proposed language requirements for benefit access
    - *"Those who don't take part in a language course [should not] get any benefits. Clearly."* (German mini-public participant, p.10)
- **Denmark & UK**: Little evidence of cultural welfare chauvinism in either party platforms or public discourse

### Unconditional Welfare Chauvinism

- Not advocated by any of the three populist radical right parties
- Not proposed in any mini-public discussions

## Cross-Country Comparisons and Context

The authors note significant differences in how welfare chauvinism manifests across countries:

> "The most striking feature in view of the literature discussed earlier is that, while welfare chauvinism emerges differently in different national political economic and party-political contexts, there is no observable overall pattern and no linkage between the policies that ordinary citizens favour and welfare institutions. In terms of public discourse, welfare chauvinism is powerful, nationally distinctive, complex and contextual." (p.10)
> 

### Germany

- Focus on **cultural integration** of refugees
- Concerns about system pressure balanced by concern for the neediest
- Mini-public participants supported language requirements for benefits

### Denmark

- Strong emphasis on **economic concerns**
- Both DF and citizens favored temporary, selective and functional welfare chauvinism
- Strong focus on refugees and asylum seekers

### UK

- Combination of economic and normative considerations
- Strong focus on **healthcare access** (NHS)
- Pre-Brexit context emphasized distinction between EU and non-EU nationals

## Theoretical and Policy Implications

### National vs. Transnational Character

> "Welfare chauvinism is a form of welfare policy opposition that operates across national politics and is not limited to particular parties and their supporters. At the same time, national differences in welfare state traditions remain important in influencing how it operates." (p.11)
> 

This has two key implications:

1. "Chauvinism will find it hard to become a transnational force or to figure prominently in direct cross-party links" (p.11)
2. "Chauvinism has much stronger implications for the modification of national welfare states in more universalist citizenship and in liberal selective systems than in corporatist social insurance ones, because it implies a categorization of recipients on a basis other than need or contribution." (p.11)

### Research Contributions

The authors argue their work:

> "Extends the empirical work started by Ennser-Jedenastik (2018) on welfare chauvinist discourse by identifying different categories of restrictions. We also contribute to the evolving debates surrounding welfare chauvinism (and, more broadly, welfare state opposition) by identifying which welfare chauvinist frames also appeal to the electorate beyond populist radical right voters." (p.11)
> 

### Discursive Complexity

The authors note that these categories are not mutually exclusive:

> "While the dimensions identified in our categorization (time, space, function, culture) are not mutually exclusive, it could be argued that these are part of a more complex discursive puzzle that yield different policy outcomes." (p.11)
> 

## Conclusion

The authors conclude that welfare chauvinism manifests differently across national contexts, influenced by both institutional welfare state structures and domestic political dynamics. They argue for greater attention to these contextual factors:

> "Because chauvinism is not an international movement but one which stresses national differences it is more fruitful to focus on specific forms of welfare restrictions rather than seeking to examine welfare chauvinism holistically." (p.12)
> 

The paper demonstrates the value of categorizing welfare chauvinism to understand how different forms of welfare restrictions are advocated and justified in different contexts, and how these different frames yield different policy implications.
