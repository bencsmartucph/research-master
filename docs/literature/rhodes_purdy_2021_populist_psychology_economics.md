## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### 1. Affective Political Economy Theory

### Detailed Explanation

The authors develop "affective political economy" as a theoretical framework that connects economics and culture through emotions. This theory addresses a fundamental inconsistency in populism research: while historical evidence shows populism emerging after economic crises, survey research finds cultural variables better predict populist attitudes. The theory posits that economic threats generate emotional responses (particularly anger and fear/anxiety), which then influence how individuals perceive their cultural environment and ultimately affect their political attitudes.

The framework reconceptualizes how economic factors influence political attitudes by moving beyond purely materialistic, self-interested pathways. Instead, it focuses on how economic threats create emotional states that color perceptions of one's social and cultural environment. This connection allows the theory to bridge the typically disparate domains of economic and cultural analysis without requiring either domain to adopt incompatible assumptions about human motivation.

A key insight is that the emotions triggered by economic hardship are not contained to economic concerns—they "spill over" to affect how individuals interpret their entire social environment, particularly regarding cultural values and identity. The theory suggests that economic crises make people more sensitive to perceived threats to their values and identity, leading to cultural discontent and subsequently populist attitudes.

### Direct Quotes

> "Our approach, which emphasizes economic antecedents, starts with economic crises or severe economic threat, which generate a great deal of anxiety and anger. The historical proximity of populism to economic calamity suggests that the most hospitable environments for antisystem politics are those in which economic factors drive cultural resentments and populist antagonisms." (p. 1560)
> 

> "By using emotions as a bridge between the two fields, we can gain greater insight into other political tendencies (e.g., prejudice, ideological extremism, political violence) that may be influenced by negative emotions." (p. 1560)
> 

> "This theory predicts effects similar to the spreading activation theory of memory. In other words, economic hardships can lead to a mental state that predisposes people to embrace certain values, develop or express certain attitudes, and increase the salience of specific elements of their identity." (p. 1563)
> 

### Relevance to Your Research

This framework offers a valuable lens for understanding how economic policies (including welfare and labor policies) might affect support for far-right parties not just through material self-interest, but through emotional pathways. You might incorporate this by:

- Examining how social investment policies mitigate negative emotions resulting from economic insecurity
- Investigating whether welfare policies reduce cultural discontent by addressing the emotional impact of economic threats
- Analyzing how far-right parties exploit emotional responses to economic policies
- Considering how labor market interventions might affect redistribution preferences through emotional mediators rather than purely rational calculations

### 2. Cultural Discontent Theory

### Detailed Explanation

The authors develop a novel concept of "cultural discontent" as a key mediating variable between emotional responses to economic threats and populist attitudes. They define cultural discontent as "a sense that one's values or identity is not respected in one's social environment" (p. 1560) and operationalize it through two subdimensions: value/belief discontent and identity discontent.

This concept is distinct from but related to other cultural variables studied in populism research, such as authoritarianism, racial resentment, or concerns about cultural decline. Cultural discontent represents a more general sense of alienation from one's cultural environment that can manifest across the political spectrum, not just among the far right. It captures the subjective perception that one's values and identity are marginalized, disrespected, or threatened by dominant cultural forces.

The theory posits that when individuals experience fear or anger (particularly anger) in response to economic threats, they become more sensitive to signs that their values or identity are not respected, leading to increased cultural discontent. This discontent then becomes the proximate cause of populist attitudes, as individuals blame political elites for embracing values contrary to their own or for favoring outgroups.

### Direct Quotes

> "We expect both anger and fear to contribute to cultural discontent, or the sense that one's values or identity are ignored, disrespected, and generally not given the weight they deserve." (p. 1564)
> 

> "There is growing evidence that the people on the extreme right are not the only ones who feel disconnected from their culture; leftists and marginalized groups can experience a sense of discontent, especially when the far right is in power." (p. 1566)
> 

> "Cultural discontent also had the predicted effect on populism, but only value discontent was positive and significant: the effect of identity marginalization was negative for both dimensions of populism, although only significantly so for people centrism." (p. 1568)
> 

### Associated Literature

The concept of cultural discontent connects to literature on cultural backlash (Inglehart & Norris), social identity theory, and research on authoritarian values (MacWilliams). It also relates to Elchardus and Spruyt's work on declinism and Spruyt et al.'s research on social identity in populism. Unlike measures that focus specifically on prejudice toward minorities or immigrants, cultural discontent is designed to capture a more general sense of alienation that can occur across the political spectrum.

### Application by Authors

The authors measure cultural discontent using 10 original items assessing value/belief discontent and identity discontent. They experimentally manipulate economic threat and show that it increases cultural discontent, mediated through emotional responses. They also demonstrate that cultural discontent significantly influences populist attitudes, particularly antielitism and people centrism.

### Relevance to Your Research

Cultural discontent offers a useful concept for understanding how welfare and labor policies might affect support for far-right parties and redistribution preferences:

- You could examine whether social investment policies reduce cultural discontent by providing security and reducing threat perceptions
- Investigate how specific policy designs might affect different dimensions of cultural discontent
- Analyze whether far-right messaging connects welfare policies to threats to cultural values
- Consider how universal versus targeted welfare approaches might differentially affect identity discontent and value discontent

### 3. Emotional Models of Political Behavior

### Detailed Explanation

The paper employs a sophisticated understanding of discrete emotions and their distinct effects on political attitudes and behavior. The authors differentiate between anger and fear/anxiety as distinct emotional responses to economic threats, each with different cognitive and behavioral consequences. This approach builds on established research in political psychology that shows emotions are not simply valenced reactions but have specific action tendencies and information-processing effects.

Anger is conceptualized as an "approach" emotion that increases reliance on existing beliefs, encourages punishment of perceived wrongdoers, and promotes attribution of negative events to intentional wrongdoing. The authors argue that anger is particularly important for populism because it matches populism's hostile orientation toward elites and its tendency to assign blame for societal problems.

Fear/anxiety is described as promoting information-seeking, increasing attention to threatening stimuli, and breaking existing habits of mind. The authors note that fear's relationship with populism is theoretically ambiguous—it could promote questioning of the political status quo, but it also tends to demotivate action and encourage flight rather than fight, which contrasts with populism's aggressive nature.

### Direct Quotes

> "Unlike fear, anger is generally seen as an 'approach' emotion, causing citizens to participate in politics. It also leads individuals to rely more heavily on their preexisting beliefs, rather than seek out new information." (p. 1563)
> 

> "Anger encourages individuals to punish those they deem responsible for wrongdoing, as anger is commonly rooted in perceptions of intentional wrongdoing or unfairness." (p. 1563)
> 

> "Additionally, angry individuals tend to interpret all undesirable events in their environment as resulting from intentional wrongdoing and have an increased desire to punish anyone whose conduct they disapprove of." (p. 1563)
> 

### Associated Literature

This emotional approach builds on Marcus, Neuman, and MacKuen's Affective Intelligence Theory, as well as work by Brader, Albertson and Gadarian on emotional reactions to threat. It also connects to research on specific emotions and politics by Valentino, Banks, Petersen, and others. The authors cite Rico et al.'s findings on anger and populism in Spain and Vasilopoulos et al.'s work on fear, anger, and far-right voting in France.

### Application by Authors

The authors use a modified PANAS scale to measure discrete emotions and experimentally manipulate economic threat to generate different emotional responses. Their findings consistently show that anger mediates the relationship between economic threat and cultural discontent, and directly influences populist attitudes. Fear/anxiety has more ambiguous effects across their studies.

### Relevance to Your Research

This emotional approach offers important insights for your research:

- You might examine how different welfare policies generate distinct emotional responses
- Investigate whether social investment policies specifically reduce anger about economic conditions
- Consider how far-right messaging might exploit anger about economic policies to direct blame toward outgroups
- Analyze whether universal versus targeted policies generate different emotional responses that affect redistribution preferences
