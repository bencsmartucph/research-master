### Intergroup Threat Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Intergroup threat theory posits that anti-immigrant attitudes emerge from perceived threats that immigrants pose to the majority ethnic group. Drawing from Blumer's (1958) group position theory and later developed by scholars like Stephan et al. (2016), this framework distinguishes between symbolic threats (to values, culture) and realistic threats (to material resources). The theory suggests members of dominant groups feel they have proprietary claims to privileges and fear subordinate groups may threaten their position. In welfare contexts, this manifests as natives perceiving immigrants as competitors for limited resources like jobs, benefits, and housing. The theory holds that economic competition is especially salient for individuals with lower socioeconomic status who occupy similar economic positions as immigrants, leading them to view welfare distribution as a zero-sum game where immigrant gains mean native losses.

**Direct Quotes**:
"According to Blumer (1958), members of the dominant group feel that they have a proprietary claim to certain areas of privilege, and they fear that the subordinate group will threaten their position."

"We are primarily interested in the realistic threat perceptions, as they are related to concerns of the loss of material resources (Stephan et al., 2016). Here, it is posited that natives may perceive immigrants as competitors for jobs, benefits, affordable housing and so on."

**Operationalization and Application**: The authors operationalize intergroup threat theory through socioeconomic variables (education, income satisfaction, and unemployment status) as predictors of welfare chauvinism. They hypothesize that individuals with lower socioeconomic status would perceive greater economic threat from immigrants and thus display stronger welfare chauvinistic attitudes. This is tested in a linear regression model (Model 1) that examines associations between these socioeconomic factors and attitudes about when immigrants should receive equal welfare rights.

**Relevance to My Research**: This theory directly relates to my research on how welfare institutions mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics. It highlights how economic insecurity can shape status perceptions and create competition-based frameworks that may fuel populist responses. The paper's findings that socioeconomic factors have limited explanatory power in Nordic welfare states suggests that welfare institutions may be effectively buffering against direct economic threat perceptions, supporting my hypothesis about welfare institutions' role in status stabilization.

### Scapegoat Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Scapegoat theory, originally developed by Allport (1954), explains how prejudice and hostility toward outgroups can arise when individuals attribute societal problems to these groups rather than acknowledging systemic causes. This displacement of blame allows the ingroup to externalize responsibility for societal difficulties. In contemporary contexts, immigrants often become convenient scapegoats for economic downturns, austerity measures, or declining public services. The theory suggests that when people experience deteriorating social conditions, they seek simple explanations and targets for their frustration. The association between immigration and welfare decline in public discourse creates a cognitive framework where immigrants are blamed for straining welfare systems, regardless of empirical reality. This process has been exacerbated in recent decades by neoliberal policies coinciding with increased immigration, creating what Bello (2017) calls a perfect storm for scapegoating immigrants.

**Direct Quotes**:
"The implication is that some outgroups innocently attract the aggression generated by the frustrations suffered by members of the ingroup (Allport, 1954). It is not 'we' who are responsible for the misfortunes; it must be someone else's fault."

"Bello (2017) argues that decreased public expenditure in several OECD countries in the 1990s and 2000s combined with an increase in immigration made immigrants an easy scapegoat. She asserts that vulnerable groups saw deteriorating living standards combined with a rising immigrant population in their neighbourhoods and concluded it had to be the fault of the immigrants that living standards deteriorated."

**Operationalization and Application**: The authors test scapegoat theory by examining whether dissatisfaction with society predicts welfare chauvinism. They include two variables in Model 2 of their regression analysis: satisfaction with the economy and satisfaction with health services. The hypothesis is that individuals less satisfied with these aspects of society would be more likely to support welfare chauvinistic policies, blaming immigrants for perceived deterioration of services.

**Relevance to My Research**: Scapegoat theory provides valuable insight into my research question about how welfare institutions shape moral frameworks around economic vulnerability. When welfare institutions fail to maintain status stability during economic transitions, scapegoating of outgroups may increase, facilitating populist narratives. The paper's finding that satisfaction with society has limited explanatory power in Nordic contexts suggests strong welfare institutions may be preventing scapegoating dynamics, supporting my hypothesis about welfare institutions' role in shaping attributional processes and moral frameworks.

### Deservingness Theory

**Detailed Explanation**: Deservingness theory examines how people make moral judgments about who deserves welfare benefits based on perceived characteristics and behaviors of recipient groups. This theoretical framework, developed in welfare research by scholars like Van Oorschot and Roosma (2017), suggests that public support for welfare distribution depends on assessments of recipients' control, need, identity, attitude, and reciprocity. In welfare chauvinism contexts, deservingness judgments often operate along ethnic and cultural lines, with majority populations perceiving certain immigrant groups as less deserving than others. The theory posits that cultural distance plays a crucial role in these assessments, with culturally dissimilar immigrants facing stricter deservingness criteria than culturally similar ones. These judgments create hierarchies of deservingness that impact policy preferences and may undermine universalist welfare principles by introducing conditional, identity-based criteria for benefit access.

**Direct Quotes**:
"Reeskens and Van der Meer (2017) find in their Dutch sample that individuals feel that certain nationalities 'deserve' welfare benefits more than other nationalities; for example, a Surinamese immigrant is considered more deserving of welfare benefits than an Afghan or a Moroccan immigrant."

"More culturally dissimilar immigrants are also often the target of welfare chauvinistic rhetoric (Edwards et al., 2021)."

**Operationalization and Application**: The authors operationalize deservingness theory by examining attitudes toward different immigrant groups as predictors of welfare chauvinism. They include two variables that distinguish between attitudes toward culturally similar immigrants and culturally dissimilar immigrants (measured by willingness to allow immigrants of same race/ethnicity versus immigrants from poor countries outside Europe). Model 4 of their regression analysis tests whether more negative attitudes toward either group predict stronger welfare chauvinistic attitudes.

**Relevance to My Research**: Deservingness theory directly connects to my research on how welfare institutions shape moral frameworks that mediate between economic vulnerability and populist politics. It helps explain how welfare regimes can either reinforce or counteract particularistic moral frameworks that distinguish between deserving and undeserving recipients based on group identity. The strong association found between attitudes toward culturally dissimilar immigrants and welfare chauvinism in all three Nordic countries suggests that even universal welfare institutions may not fully eliminate identity-based moral frameworks, a crucial insight for my thesis about the dual status-moral pathways.
