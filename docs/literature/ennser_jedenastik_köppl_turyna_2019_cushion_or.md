## 1. Introduction and Theoretical Framework

The paper presents two competing hypotheses about how welfare state generosity might influence support for PRRPs:

1. **The Inoculation Hypothesis**: Generous social policy regimes may mitigate the adverse economic effects of globalization and thus make workers less vulnerable to the appeal of populist radical right parties.
2. **The Welfare Chauvinism Hypothesis**: Generous welfare regimes may make voters more concerned about increased numbers of low-skilled immigrants entering a country and potentially claiming benefits paid for largely by the taxes and contributions of the native population.

---

## 2. Data and Methods

### Econometric Model

The authors employ a sophisticated methodology combining a spatial voting model with individual and country-specific characteristics.

### Data Sources

- The 2014 Chapel-Hill expert survey for data on party positions
- The 2014 and 2016 European Social Survey waves for voter ideological stances and demographic characteristics
- The Quality of Government dataset and Bertelsmann Foundation's social justice indicators

### Key Independent Variables

**Economic Vulnerability** measured through four alternatives:

1. Income in first three deciles (Tercil1)
2. Subjective difficulty making ends meet (IncSub3)
3. Manufacturing/elementary occupations (Isco89)
4. Recent unemployment experience (Unemp)

**Welfare State Generosity** measured through:

1. Social Policies index (Bertelsmann Foundation)
2. Poverty Prevention subindex
3. Relative Redistribution (Gini reduction)

## 3. Results and Findings

### 3.1 Direct Effects

Economic vulnerability is generally associated with increased PRRP support. Welfare state generosity appears to have a direct positive correlation with PRRP support.

### 3.2 Testing the Inoculation Hypothesis

Strong support: consistently negative interaction terms between welfare state measures and economic vulnerability indicators. Welfare states function as a cushion against economic insecurity, reducing the appeal of PRRPs among those who might otherwise be most attracted.

### 3.3 Testing the Welfare Chauvinism Hypothesis

When examining how welfare state generosity interacts with ideological distances on immigration, evidence consistent with welfare chauvinism emerges. A stronger welfare state contributes to increasing the salience of the immigration platform for voting decisions.

### 3.4 Key Conditional Finding

For voters ideologically close to populists on immigration, having low income combined with a strong welfare state has a **positive** effect on PRRP support. For low-income voters further from populist platforms, a strong welfare state **reduces** PRRP support.

---

## 4. Social Psychological Mechanisms

### 4.1 Economic Security and Risk Perception

Welfare states reduce support for PRRPs among the economically vulnerable by increasing economic security and reducing risk perception.

### 4.2 Intergroup Relations and Welfare Deservingness

Immigrants are consistently viewed as less deserving of welfare than native populations. These perceptions may be activated more strongly in contexts where welfare benefits are more substantial.

### 4.3 Value Conflicts and Identity Protection

Generous welfare states often embody values of solidarity and universalism. When these values are perceived to be threatened by immigration, PRRPs can exploit the tension.

### 4.4 Individual versus Aggregate Effects

While at the country level, more generous welfare states tend to correlate with more positive immigration attitudes, the individual-level mechanisms show a more complex pattern.

---

## 5. Conclusion

Welfare states have a dual effect on PRRP support:

- They **reduce** the impact of economic vulnerability on PRRP support (inoculation hypothesis)
- They simultaneously **increase** the salience of immigration attitudes in determining PRRP support (welfare chauvinism hypothesis)

Voters in high-redistribution countries are more likely to vote for the PRRP if they have a more moderate view on migration, compared to countries with lower levels of redistribution, where voters for the populist right are predominantly those who very strongly oppose migration.
