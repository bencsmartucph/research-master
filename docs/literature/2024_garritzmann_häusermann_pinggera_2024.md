## Comprehensive Summary

This groundbreaking study by Garritzmann, Häusermann, and Pinggera addresses a fundamental puzzle in contemporary welfare state politics: why do citizens often resist reforms that are necessary to adapt welfare systems to post-industrial challenges, even when these reforms promise future benefits? The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by demonstrating that public support for welfare reforms is not primarily driven by narrow self-interest or technical policy design features, but rather by citizens' positions on a second ideological dimension that pits universalistic values against particularistic orientations. Using novel survey experiments across nine European countries, the authors reveal that universalistic citizens strongly favor social investment policies (particularly education), while particularistic citizens prefer traditional compensatory policies (particularly pensions). This finding challenges conventional wisdom that reduces welfare politics to a simple left-right economic divide and establishes the crucial importance of "second dimension politics" for understanding contemporary welfare reform dynamics.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Second dimension ideology trumps self-interest**: Citizens' positions on the universalism-particularism dimension are more predictive of welfare reform support than traditional beneficiary status or economic calculations.
2. **Policy design matters less than expected**: Supply-side factors like reform costs, time horizons, and distributive profiles have surprisingly weak effects on public support compared to ideological orientations.
3. **Differential policy preferences by value orientation**: "Universalistic respondents show very high levels of pension support, but remarkable low inclination to support education expansion and improvement. Universalistic respondents, on the other hand, even display much higher support for education reform than for pension expansion" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 23-24).
4. **Pensions vs. education reveal deeper divides**: The contrast between these two popular policy areas illuminates how different welfare approaches resonate with distinct value systems and visions of social order.
5. **Cross-national implications for reform capacity**: Countries with larger shares of universalistic voters have greater political leeway for social investment reforms, helping explain divergent reform trajectories across Europe.
6. **Limited scope for strategic policy design**: The authors conclude that "the leeway for governments to meticulously design 'winning' reforms by tailoring them to specific interests and rational calculations seems rather constrained" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 23).

## Introduction and Motivation Analysis

### Research Problem and Significance

This study addresses a critical empirical puzzle that has vexed policymakers and scholars alike: the persistent difficulty of enacting welfare reforms that adapt social protection systems to post-industrial realities. The authors situate their investigation within the broader challenge of "time-inconsistency problems" where "citizens and policy-makers face" immediate costs for uncertain, long-term benefits (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 3). This temporal mismatch creates a fundamental governance dilemma in democratic societies.

The empirical puzzle emerges from repeated policy failures across European contexts. As the authors note, "policymakers have struggled to enact such reforms, especially because public opinion often is a latent veto point for future-oriented reforms" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 3). They provide concrete examples: "a recent major pension reform proposal destined to secure both fiscal stability and a more flexible retirement age has failed in Switzerland despite a long and inclusive political negotiation process" and "in Germany numerous education policy reforms supposed to adapt the system to changing labor market demands have been vetoed, reversed, or watered down because of unfavorable public opinion" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 3).

The theoretical gap the study addresses concerns the limited understanding of what drives public support for welfare reforms beyond simple economic self-interest. While extensive literature exists on welfare state preferences, the authors argue that existing research has primarily focused on the first ideological dimension (state-market) while neglecting how the second dimension (universalism-particularism) shapes policy preferences. This oversight is significant because, as they demonstrate, "we cannot understand the politics of contemporary welfare reform without paying close attention to second dimension politics" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 4).

The broader questions this research addresses span multiple domains of political economy and welfare state theory. At its core, the study engages with fundamental questions about democratic responsiveness, policy feedback effects, and the changing nature of political conflict in post-industrial societies. The authors connect their work to longstanding debates about "the capacity of governments to adapt welfare states to present and foreseeable challenges" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 3), referencing key works by Pierson and Palier that have shaped our understanding of welfare state transformation dynamics.

### Theoretical Positioning and Innovation

The authors position their theoretical contribution at the intersection of several established research traditions while offering novel insights about the role of second dimension politics in welfare reform. They build directly on the foundational work of comparative welfare state scholars like Esping-Andersen, Bonoli, and Hemerijck who have identified the mismatch between traditional welfare systems and post-industrial challenges. However, they extend this literature by focusing on the microfoundations of reform support rather than institutional or political factors.

The paper's key theoretical innovation lies in systematically connecting second dimension politics to welfare policy preferences beyond the traditional domains where this connection has been established. As the authors explain, "Most of the evidence demonstrates clear links to fields of social policy that have a direct connection to questions of authority, community, and freedom, such as welfare chauvinism, welfare misuse, gender equality, and childcare, or commodification and sanctioning" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 4). Their contribution extends this logic to "pension and education reform, two cornerstones of the mature European welfare state" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 4).

The authors challenge existing scholarship by questioning the sufficiency of first dimension (economic left-right) explanations for welfare preferences. They argue that "we argue that we cannot understand the politics of contemporary welfare reform without paying close attention to second dimension politics" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 4). This represents a significant departure from research that treats welfare politics primarily through the lens of redistribution and state intervention.

The theoretical synthesis they propose connects authoritarian-libertarian value differences to concrete policy preferences through multiple mechanisms: value-based preferences for different types of social change, compositional effects related to education and occupation, and normative orientations toward traditional versus progressive social arrangements.

## Literature Review and Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Major Theoretical Traditions Engaged

**Self-Interest and Rational Choice Theory**

The paper engages extensively with the rational choice tradition that emphasizes material self-interest as the primary driver of policy preferences. This theoretical foundation assumes that "beneficiaries of social policies are usually much more supportive of said policies than individuals who are unlikely to benefit" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 6-7). The core mechanism posits direct utility maximization where "unemployed persons prefer higher unemployment benefits, students prefer more education spending, pensioners support strong old-age pension benefits" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 7).

Key scholars in this tradition include Alesina and Giuliano (2011), whose work on preferences for redistribution provides foundational insights about how economic position shapes policy attitudes. The empirical evidence supporting self-interest explanations is substantial, particularly in trade-off scenarios where costs are made explicit to respondents.

However, the authors identify important limitations in this literature. The theory struggles to explain why citizens sometimes support policies that don't directly benefit them, or why policy design features that should matter for rational calculations (like costs and time horizons) often have minimal effects on preferences. The authors note that existing research has shown preferences "hardly change when respondents are simply reminded that increasing public expenditure 'might require' tax increases" unless presented with explicit trade-offs (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 6).

The authors' engagement with this tradition is respectful but critical. They acknowledge that "self-interest matters" while arguing that "individuals' ideological positions and values matter, too" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 7). Their empirical findings provide a nuanced view where self-interest operates alongside, but is often dominated by, ideological factors.

**First Dimension Politics: Economic Left-Right Theory**

The paper thoroughly reviews the extensive literature on how economic left-right ideology shapes welfare preferences. This tradition, rooted in class-based theories of political conflict, predicts that "Left-leaning individuals tend to favor a larger welfare state, whereas right-leaning individuals tend to favor a smaller welfare state, being more in favor or market solutions" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 7).

The theoretical foundation rests on fundamental differences in beliefs about the appropriate role of government versus markets in addressing social risks. Scholars like Blekesaune and Quadagno (2003), Feldman and Zaller (1992), and Svallfors (1997) have provided extensive empirical support for these relationships across different welfare regime types.

The authors acknowledge the robust empirical support for first dimension effects while identifying a crucial limitation: this framework cannot explain why support varies across different types of welfare policies among individuals with similar economic ideological positions. They argue that "the link between state-market ideology and education expansion in particular is rather weak, even though the size and effects of education policies in Western European welfare states are similar in size to the relevance of pension systems" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 23).

The authors' contribution builds on this tradition by demonstrating that first dimension politics alone provides an incomplete picture of welfare preferences, particularly for understanding differential support across policy types.

**Second Dimension Politics: Authoritarian-Libertarian Values**

The paper's most significant theoretical engagement concerns the growing literature on second dimension politics, rooted in early work by Lipset (1959) on "working-class authoritarianism" and later developed by scholars like Rokeach (1973), Flanagan and Lee (2003), and Achterberg and Houtman (2009). This theoretical tradition emphasizes that "ideological conflict in European societies revolves around at least two dimensions" where the second dimension "structures conflict around issues of freedom, autonomy, and community" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 4).

The core theoretical insight concerns how authoritarian versus libertarian values create different orientations toward social and political questions. In contemporary European politics, this divide has been mobilized as "universalistic vs. particularistic programmatic positions, by parties of the new left and the far right" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 4), referencing seminal work by Inglehart (1984), Kitschelt (1994), and Kriesi et al. (2008).

Existing empirical evidence has demonstrated clear connections between second dimension positions and welfare attitudes in specific domains. Research by De Koster et al. (2013), Achterberg et al. (2014), and others has shown how authoritarian orientations relate to "welfare chauvinism, welfare misuse, gender equality, and childcare, or commodification and sanctioning" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 4).

The authors' theoretical innovation extends this literature by arguing that second dimension politics should influence preferences over core welfare state policies, not just peripheral or culturally-charged issues. They propose that "citizens with strongly universalistic ideological positions are likely to be particularly supportive of any welfare reforms that they perceive to enhance inclusion, social mobility, and progressive social change, while citizens with strong particularistic positions are more likely to support social transfers to traditional core constituencies" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 9).

**Policy Design and Supply-Side Factors**

The paper also engages with the growing literature on policy design effects, building on work by scholars like van Oorschot (2006) on deservingness perceptions, Korpi and Palme (1998) on distributive profiles, and recent research on time horizons by Jacobs and Matthews (2012, 2017). This literature suggests that specific features of policy proposals—their scope, beneficiary groups, costs, and implementation timelines—should systematically affect public support.

The theoretical mechanisms underlying supply-side effects include deservingness perceptions where "some groups (in particular the elderly) are perceived as more deserving than others, especially on the basis of merit or reciprocity" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 5), scope effects where "policies catering to wider groups of beneficiaries receive more support than those catering to a concentrated range of risks" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 5), and cognitive biases like risk aversion and temporal discounting.

The authors' critical engagement with this literature reveals important limitations. Their findings suggest that "supply-side factors only explain a minor part of the variation in reform support/opposition" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 16), leading them to conclude that "the leeway for governments to 'design' and cater support for reforms by highlighting certain policy characteristics is rather limited" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 17).

### Theoretical Synthesis and Framework

The authors develop an innovative theoretical framework that synthesizes insights from multiple traditions while prioritizing second dimension politics as a key explanatory factor. Their synthesis argues that welfare reform support results from the interaction of supply-side factors (policy characteristics) and demand-side factors (individual characteristics), with ideological positions on both dimensions playing crucial but distinct roles.

The theoretical framework proposes that "positions on the economic state-market dimension relate to stronger support for expansive social policies in both education and pensions (i.e. to increase support for the welfare state in general)" while "positions on the universalism-particularism dimension" create "a differential effect on the type of welfare reform" where "universalism is related to support for social investment, while particularism is related to social compensation" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 10).

The authors identify several causal mechanisms linking second dimension politics to welfare preferences. First, they propose a value-based mechanism where "the expected benefits of social investments are more widely spread, hard to predict and may enhance inclusion and social mobility, properties that relate them directly to universalistic positions, whereas social transfers to clearly identifiable groups of traditional, well-established groups of beneficiaries resonate with reaffirming boundaries of solidarity" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 9).

Second, they suggest an orientation toward social change mechanism: "given that the second ideological dimension expresses a general orientation towards progressive social change vs. traditional social order, it might be the case that people leaning towards universalism find it both more desirable to and more possible to alter society with political means such as education and other social investments" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 9).

Third, they identify normative underpinnings where "different social policy approaches have different normative underpinnings. Proponents of social investments support more progressive normative ideas, such as dual-earner families, early childhood education and care, and a generalized effort towards social upskilling" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 9).

The framework also acknowledges compositional effects that may partially explain observed relationships, including occupational differences where "universalistic positions are closely related to occupation, with people in highly skilled, public or semi-public professions exhibiting clearly the strongest support for such positions" and educational differences where "people with universalistic positions are also on average more educated and skilled than people with particularistic positions" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 9-10).

### Hypotheses and Theoretical Predictions

The theoretical framework generates seven specific hypotheses that test different aspects of supply-side and demand-side factors. The supply-side hypotheses predict that policy characteristics should systematically affect support levels, while the demand-side hypotheses focus on individual-level determinants.

**Supply-Side Hypotheses:**

Hypothesis 1 predicts policy field effects: "Both pensions and education are popular policy areas, receiving high public support levels, but given its larger scope and deservingness of recipients, support is likely to be even higher for pension expansion than for education expansion" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 5).

Hypothesis 2 addresses distributive profiles: "Inclusive reforms are more popular than targeted reforms" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 5), based on the logic that universal policies reach larger potential beneficiary groups.

Hypothesis 3 concerns time horizons: "Public support for reforms decreases the more distant in the future the reforms' returns are expected to appear" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 6), reflecting cognitive biases toward short-term benefits and uncertainty about long-term outcomes.

**Demand-Side Hypotheses:**

Hypothesis 5 captures self-interest effects: "Support for a policy (and its expansion) is higher among policy beneficiaries" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 7).

Hypothesis 6 addresses first dimension politics: "Individuals leaning towards the economic left (pro-state) are more in favor of social policy expansion than individuals leaning towards the economic right (pro-market)" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 7).

The crucial theoretical prediction comes in Hypothesis 7: "Individuals leaning towards universalistic positions are more in favor of social investments than those leaning towards particularistic positions (the latter prefer stronger social compensation)" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 9).

## Broader Theoretical Implications

### Connections to Other Research Programs

The paper's theoretical contributions create bridges across multiple research programs in political science, sociology, and economics. The findings connect directly to the extensive literature on political cleavages and voting behavior, particularly work on realignment in post-industrial societies. The authors note that their results help explain "why countries differ so tremendously in their commitment to social investment" by identifying "another puzzle-piece to the explanation" that "it is simply easier and there is more political leeway for social investment reforms in countries with more universalistic values" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 25).

The research also contributes to the growing literature on policy feedback effects and welfare state development. By demonstrating that citizens evaluate welfare policies through ideological lenses that extend beyond economic interests, the study suggests that policy feedback effects may operate through value-based as well as material channels. This has implications for understanding how welfare state configurations become self-reinforcing or create momentum for reform.

The findings bridge political economy and political psychology by showing how deep-seated value orientations translate into concrete policy preferences. This connection suggests fruitful avenues for interdisciplinary research on the psychological foundations of political attitudes and the role of values in democratic representation.

## Methodology Overview

### Data Sources and Coverage

The study employs a sophisticated methodological approach using "two representative public opinion surveys: an online survey that was conducted in eight European countries among 12,000 respondents (ANONYMISED), as well as the Austrian National Election Study (AUTNES) a representative survey with 3099 participants" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 4). This dual-survey strategy provides both cross-national breadth and single-country depth for testing theoretical claims.

The WP survey covers "eight Western European countries (Denmark, Germany, Ireland, Italy, Netherlands, Spain, UK, Sweden)" that "represent the variation of welfare regimes in Western Europe" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 10). This strategic sampling enables the authors to test their theoretical claims across different institutional contexts while maintaining sufficient statistical power within each country.

The data collection strategy addresses several important methodological challenges. The authors acknowledge that their "respondents tend to be younger (as often is the case with online surveys) and more educated than in the ESS data" but argue that "the data is representative in terms of the population's demographics and ideological distribution" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 10-11). This representativeness along key theoretical dimensions is crucial for their claims about ideological effects.

The temporal coverage from 2018-2019 provides a snapshot of public opinion during a period of relative stability before the COVID-19 pandemic, making the findings particularly relevant for understanding baseline attitudes toward welfare reform under normal circumstances.

### Key Variables and Operationalization

The study's measurement strategy reflects careful attention to construct validity and cross-national comparability. The dependent variable, reform support, is measured using responses to experimental vignettes where "Respondents could (dis)agree on a range from 1-10" in the WP survey and 1-7 in AUTNES, with the authors rescaling "all variables for which the answer scales differed between the two surveys to yield from 0 to 1" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 13).

The operationalization of ideological dimensions represents a particular methodological strength. In the WP survey, the authors derive dimensions "from an explorative factor analysis based on seven statements" covering economic redistributive preferences and cultural-social issues (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 14). The first dimension includes items like "For a society to be fair, income differences should be small" and "Social benefits and services in [COUNTRY] place too great a strain on the economy" while the second dimension captures attitudes toward "Immigration is a threat to our national culture," "Gay and lesbian couples should have the same rights to adopt children," and "European integration has gone too far" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 14).

The AUTNES survey uses a similar factor analytic approach with eight indicators, providing validation across different measurement contexts. This dual operationalization strategy strengthens confidence in the construct validity of the ideological measures.

Control variables include standard demographic and political measures: "AGE, GENDER, INCOME, EDUCATION, HAVING CHILDREN, POLITICAL TRUST, a dummy variable for PENSIONERS, and another dummy variable that indicates whether the respondent VOTED FOR THE INCUMBENT party" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 14-15). This comprehensive set of controls addresses potential confounding factors and alternative explanations.

### Analytical Model and Justification

The analytical approach employs split-sample survey experiments with random assignment, allowing causal identification of supply-side effects while using observational analysis for demand-side factors. As the authors explain, "we use split-sample survey experiments with random assignment in order to experimentally manipulate specific policy elements while holding other factors constant" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 10).

For supply-side analysis, the experimental design manipulates four key factors across different treatment conditions:

- **Policy type**: Education reform vs. pension reform
- **Time horizon**: 2 years vs. 15 years implementation
- **Distributive effect**: Inclusive vs. targeted (AUTNES only)
- **Costs**: Low vs. high cost levels (AUTNES only)

The statistical models employ OLS regression with country fixed effects when pooling data across surveys. The authors justify this approach by noting that "whenever multiple countries are included in the estimation, we add country fixed-effects, which simultaneously function as survey-fixed effects as the AUTNES survey was conducted in a single country (Austria)" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 13).

The identification strategy for demand-side effects relies on the assumption that ideological positions are predetermined relative to reform preferences, though the authors acknowledge potential endogeneity concerns. They address this through robustness checks and by demonstrating that effects hold across different subgroups, including those least likely to benefit from reforms.

A key methodological strength lies in the realistic but hypothetical reform scenarios. The vignettes describe plausible policy proposals with specific details about implementation and costs, enhancing external validity while maintaining experimental control. For example, the WP survey asks: "Imagine the [COUNTRY] government were planning a large [TYPE OF POLICY]. The reform would take [TIME HORIZON] to be implemented and would cost 5 billions. Would you support such a reform?" (Garritzmann et al., 2024, p. 11).

The regression specifications for testing demand-side effects take the general form:

$\text{Reform Support}{i} = \alpha + \beta_1 \text{First Dimension}{i} + \beta_2 \text{Second Dimension}{i} + \gamma \mathbf{X}{i} + \delta_c + \epsilon_{i}$

where the key coefficients of interest are $\beta_1$ and $\beta_2$, representing the effects of first and second dimensional ideology, $\mathbf{X}_{i}$ represents individual-level controls, and $\delta_c$ captures country fixed effects.

## Conclusion

This comprehensive analysis reveals how Garritzmann, Häusermann, and Pinggera's research fundamentally advances our theoretical understanding of welfare reform politics. By demonstrating the primacy of second dimension politics over traditional economic explanations, the study challenges scholars to reconceptualize the microfoundations of welfare state transformation. The paper's theoretical innovations, methodological rigor, and substantive findings establish it as a landmark contribution to comparative political economy and welfare state research, with implications extending far beyond the immediate question of reform support to encompass broader questions about democratic representation, political cleavages, and policy change in post-industrial societies.
