The paper employs a mix of survey experiments and panel data to demonstrate that "anxious meritocrats" are particularly receptive to rhetoric blaming trade for economic problems, explaining the rising antiglobalization sentiment in the United States.

> "We argue that this politicization reflects an interaction between elite rhetoric on unfair trade practices and American meritocratic values. As the twenty-first century progressed, elites increasingly argued that economic malaise was a result of predatory practices by US trading partners. This 'trade is unfair' rhetoric resonated most strongly with meritocratic Americans: individuals who believed in principles of market fairness but also worried about future economic prospects."
> 

## 3. Literature Review and Theoretical Framework

### 3.1 Material Interests and Trade Preferences

The paper reviews prior literature on trade preferences, beginning with approaches that emphasized material interests:

> "The initial wave of studies on trade preferences focused on an assumed confluence between material interests and policy position (Rogowski 1987; Scheve and Slaughter 2001). To determine economic interest, pollsters drew from either a Hecksher-Ohlin or a Ricardo-Viner model to classify respondents."
> 

However, the authors note that this material interests approach proved insufficient:

> "This focus on material endowments alone, however, proved to be an uneven predictor of attitudes. Exogenous market events and government interventions were also found to cause a shift in public perceptions."
> 

### 3.2 Sociotropic Effects

The authors then discuss how scholars moved toward considering broader sociotropic effects:

> "Findings such as these, questioning intrinsic economic interests as a predictor of beliefs, spurred scholars to look more broadly at sociotropic effects—that is, individuals caring about social and not simply personal utility when choosing a policy position."
> 

> "The view that sociotropic concerns dominate individual economic circumstances fueled a number of influential studies on rising antiglobalization attitudes in the United States (Davidson, Matusz, and Nelson 2012; Fordham 2008; Mansfield and Mutz 2009)."
> 

### 3.3 Identity and Ethnocentrism

The paper acknowledges literature highlighting identity factors:

> "A group of scholars argued that ethnocentrism itself could fuel antitrade attitudes (Guisinger 2017; Mansfield, Mutz, and Brackbill 2019; Margalit 2012; O'Rourke et al. 2001). Specifically, nativism—or fear of 'outsiders'—was shown to be a driver of attitudes on globalization."
> 

### 3.4 Theoretical Gap: Elite Framing and Individual Values

The authors identify an important theoretical gap - understanding how elite messaging interacts with individual core values:

> "If perceptions are key to attitude formation, and not material interests, what is their source? Scholars have offered a possible answer to this question: public perceptions reflect elite messaging."
> 

> "Given these findings, predicting and understanding shifts in public attitudes on trade may require a deeper understanding of elite trade policy 'cues' and how and when these frames change."
> 

### 3.5 Meritocratic Beliefs and Response to Messaging

The authors propose a novel theoretical framework focusing on meritocratic beliefs:

> "We suggest that individuals holding particular underlying values are more responsive to elite rhetoric on unfair trade policy and adopt negative attitudes toward globalization as a consequence. Meritocrats are people who ascribe to a core principle of fairness; as long as markets are 'fair,' meritocrats believe individual hard work and effort will be justly rewarded with social mobility."
> 

> "For meritocrats, the elite frame of trade as allowing 'unfair market access,' often to less deserving 'outsiders' (e.g., workers in China, India, Japan), effectively places foreign commerce into the same category as welfare and affirmative action."
> 

### 3.6 Economic Anxiety and Prospects for Mobility

The paper further refines its theoretical framework by introducing economic anxiety:

> "We anticipate that 'economically anxious' meritocrats, that is, those individuals who hold meritocratic norms but are worried about their own household's economic prospects, are the most open to this pro-protection trade prompt. The absence of social mobility is exceptionally disquieting to these individuals."
> 

This theoretical innovation - identifying "anxious meritocrats" as particularly susceptible to anti-trade rhetoric - is a core contribution of the paper.

## 6. Key Findings

### 6.1 Effect of Frame on Trade Attitudes

The authors demonstrate that framing trade as threatening the American Dream reduces support for trade:

> "When we perform a bivariate ordinary least squares regression of our outcome variables on a measure of assignment to the AD frame, we find that exposure to the AD frame reduced support for trade relative to the level of support among respondents exposed to the inequality frame."
> 

> "In terms of substantive magnitude, this reduction of 0.12 points on a five-point Likert scale is approximately the same size as the effect of higher income on the same outcome (0.15)."
> 

### 6.2 Meritocrats and Trade Frames

The authors find strong support for their first hypothesis:

> "We find that there is a large and statistically significant reduction in preferences over free trade among meritocrats exposed to the treatment. Among nonmeritocrats there is no appreciable treatment effect, lending support to our primary hypothesis that this set of beliefs makes Americans particularly sensitive to elite messaging on the effects of trade on the US economy."
> 

### 6.3 Anxious Meritocrats

The findings also support the second hypothesis about anxious meritocrats:

> "More tellingly, we see that anxious meritocrats are the most open to the frame... As displayed in figure 2, respondents who expressed that they were worried about the financial mobility of their children are the ones who are most influenced by the prompt. As predicted, opposition to trade is noticeably more pronounced among these economically anxious meritocrats."
> 

### 6.4 Panel Data Confirmation

The authors find consistent results in their panel data:

> "We now find that it was anxious meritocrats who demonstrate a statistically significant decline in their support for trade in 2016. This again supports our account that our anxious meritocrats are the group most susceptible to a narrative linking trade policy with threats to hardworking Americans."
> 

### 6.5 Regional Income Mobility

The authors further validate their findings using objective data on regional economic mobility:

> "We find significant evidence of declines in trade views among meritocratic respondents living in regions with low income mobility. In contrast, among respondents at the highest levels of income mobility, there is no evidence that meritocratic respondents had any systematic change in their own views over trade."
> 

### 6.6 Market Fairness Perceptions

A key underlying mechanism is revealed in additional experiments:

> "Results suggest that individuals who believe that hard work leads to success do believe that international trade makes the US markets less fair (table 4 col. 1). As expected by prior work, such individuals also are more likely to report that affirmative action policy reduces the fairness of the market (col. 2)."
> 

> "However, there is no evidence that more impersonal technological forces like automation—despite actually causing significant difficulties for workers—are believed to make the domestic market less fair (col. 3)."
> 

## 7. Alternative Explanations and Robustness Checks

### 7.1 Partisanship

The authors conduct multiple robustness checks to ensure their findings aren't simply capturing partisan effects:

> "We continue to find strong evidence that our AD treatment significantly reduced preferences for trade among meritocrats even after controlling for partisanship."
> 

> "Were our meritocracy results simply proxying for the effects of political party, we should expect to also find here that our AD treatment significantly reduced trade preferences (only) among Republicans. In contrast, we find no evidence of significant treatment differences across Republicans and non-Republicans."
> 

### 7.2 Alternative Economic Shocks

The authors test whether any economic shock would produce the same effect:

> "While linking foreign economic policies to threats to the AD significantly increased opposition to trade, would any economic shock lead to antitrade attitudes among those with meritocratic beliefs?"
> 

Through additional experiments, they find:

> "Priming respondents to (ostensibly) blame automation for declining social mobility had no significant effect on trade preferences, suggesting that individuals do not prefer trade protectionism in response to a different—albeit related—labor market threat: automation."
> 

### 7.3 Nationalism

The authors address whether their findings might be simply capturing nationalist sentiment:

> "While concerns that views on US meritocracy and nationalism may be coterminous are plausible theoretically, there is only a weakly positive association between the two in our data; our meritocracy belief measure correlates at about 0.24 with the US national superiority score."
> 

> "After controlling for nationalist sentiment directly we continue to find a robust negative effect of our AD frame on the trade views of meritocrats, suggesting that our primary findings are not simply suffering from the omission of a control for nationalist sentiment."
> 

### 7.4 White Identity Politics

The authors also address whether their findings might be driven by white identity politics:

> "Given these responses, we find that meritocracy beliefs are distinct from notions of white identity: our measure of meritocrats correlates at about 0.17 with Jardina's white identifier measure."
> 

## 8. Theoretical Implications

### 8.1 Elite Messaging and Public Opinion

The authors draw broader conclusions about how elite messaging shapes public opinion:

> "What is important to emphasize, however, is that some frames are more adaptable and appealing than others, reflecting variation in prior individual beliefs."
> 

> "This one-sided message on trade's negative effects combined with unmet economic expectations resulted in a spike in antitrade sentiment."
> 

### 8.2 Values and Policy Preferences

The paper highlights how core values shape policy preferences:

> "A commitment to core American beliefs may be spurring current disenchantment with trade policy. For generations, Americans were taught a rags-to-riches myth: as with a Horatio Alger character, anyone willing to work hard could achieve economic success. But the promise of upward mobility is increasingly less universal, and not all Americans are equally able to compete in the global economy."
> 

### 8.3 Fairness Perceptions and Policy Attitudes

The authors emphasize the importance of fairness perceptions:

> "For meritocrats, the elite frame of trade as allowing 'unfair market access,' often to less deserving 'outsiders' (e.g., workers in China, India, Japan), effectively places foreign commerce into the same category as welfare and affirmative action."
> 

> "As elites increasingly agreed that trade was undermining a fair market, and hampering upward mobility, meritocrats came to see trade as inherently problematic."
> 

## 9. Connections to Research on Welfare Politics and Far-Right Support

### 9.1 Meritocratic Beliefs and Social Policy Preferences

The paper provides important insights on how meritocratic beliefs shape policy attitudes beyond trade:

> "Those with meritocratic beliefs have been found to be critical of social policies that tip the scale in favor of one societal group over others. For instance, affirmative action and welfare policies go against their ideal that individuals get rewarded only if they put in hard work. Redistribution, in general, is inconsistent with their priors that one's own actions should be the determinant of economic reward: government should not take from hard workers in order to reward the 'lazy poor.'"
> 

This has direct implications for understanding attitudes toward welfare policies and social investment - individuals with strong meritocratic beliefs may view redistributive policies as "unfair" in the same way they view trade as unfair.

### 9.2 Economic Anxiety and Political Attitudes

The authors' concept of "anxious meritocrats" could help explain support for far-right parties:

> "The message that trade has taken their jobs because of unfair practices resonates less, in part, because they are already predisposed toward regarding the economic system as unfair and the AD as pure myth."
> 

> "Our expectation that anxious meritocrats will be particularly receptive to negative elite framing of trade's distributional effects is consistent with earlier research in political science and behavioral economics that has found that the prospect for social mobility can have a strong effect on political attitudes."
> 

This suggests that when individuals with meritocratic beliefs feel economically threatened, they become more receptive to policy messages that promise to restore "fairness" - which could extend to far-right messaging about economic nationalism and anti-immigration policies.

### 9.3 Elite Framing and Populist Rhetoric

The paper highlights how elite framing of economic issues intersects with populist rhetoric:

> "The antitrade position resonates, not only because of underlying changes in the interests of some Americans but also because it offers a simple explanation for the causes of economic hard times and then an accompanying simple remedy of closing off the US market."
> 

This has direct implications for understanding how welfare and labor policies might affect support for far-right parties. If welfare policies are framed in ways that align with meritocratic values (emphasizing fairness, reward for work), they might be more acceptable to "anxious meritocrats" who might otherwise be drawn to far-right populism.

### 9.4 Fairness Perceptions and Policy Legitimacy

The findings about fairness perceptions are particularly relevant to welfare policy:

> "Results suggest that individuals who believe that hard work leads to success do believe that international trade makes the US markets less fair."
> 

This suggests that the legitimacy of welfare policies among meritocrats may depend on whether these policies are perceived as rewarding effort rather than violating the fairness principle.

### 9.5 Geographic Variation and Economic Policy

The authors' findings on regional variation in trade attitudes have implications for understanding geographic patterns of far-right support:

> "After matching respondents to these data on regional intergenerational income mobility, we reestimate our within-respondent change in trade preferences regression described above, replacing our subjective measure of IME with objective data on average upward income mobility by county."
> 

This suggests that social investment policies might be more effective at reducing far-right support in regions with lower economic mobility.

## 11. Conclusion and Significance

The paper makes several significant contributions:

1. It identifies a key psychological mechanism (meritocratic beliefs + economic anxiety) that helps explain receptivity to anti-trade rhetoric
2. It demonstrates how elite framing interacts with individual values to shape policy preferences
3. It provides evidence that perceptions of "fairness" are central to understanding attitude formation on economic policies
4. It suggests that anti-globalization sentiment is not simply about material self-interest but reflects deeper values about how markets should operate

> "Our study sheds light on both the empirical literature on trade policy attitudes and the theoretical debate on how policy attitudes should be studied."
> 

This research has significant implications for understanding the psychological foundations of economic policy preferences, particularly how fairness perceptions shape attitudes toward both trade and welfare policies. The concept of "anxious meritocrats" provides a valuable theoretical framework for understanding how economic anxiety can interact with deeply-held values to produce political attitude shifts across multiple policy domains.

The paper's findings suggest that the effectiveness of welfare and social investment policies in reducing support for far-right parties might depend crucially on how these policies are framed in terms of fairness and meritocratic values.
