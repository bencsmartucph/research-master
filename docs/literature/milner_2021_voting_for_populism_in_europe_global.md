## Primary Theories Used:

### 1. Globalization Cleavage Theory

**Detailed Explanation:** This theory posits that globalization has created a new political cleavage between the "winners" and "losers" of economic integration. Kriesi and colleagues (2006, 2010, 2014) argue that this cleavage has transformed European party systems, with extreme right parties positioning themselves as defenders of the "losers" from globalization. According to this framework, groups negatively affected by globalization tend to favor protectionist policies and emphasize national boundaries, while beneficiaries of globalization support further international integration. This cleavage has allowed extreme right parties to formulate an "attractive ideological package" for those experiencing economic losses due to globalization.

**Direct Quotes:** "The 'new right-populist parties which, for more than 20 years now have spearheaded the nationalist reaction to economic (neoliberal reform of the economy including delocalisation, liberalisation of financial markets, and privatisation), cultural (immigration), and political (European integration, internationalisation of politics) processes of denationalisation... articulate a new structural conflict that opposes globalisation 'losers' to globalisation 'winners'." (Kriesi, 2014, p. 369, quoted on p. 2288-2289)

**Operationalization and Application:** Milner operationalizes this theory by examining regional exposure to trade (particularly imports from China and low-wage countries), foreign direct investment, and immigration, creating measures that capture the differential impact of these globalization forces across regions. She matches these measures with electoral outcomes for different party families (extreme right, center right, center left, and extreme left) to test whether regions more exposed to globalization show increased support for extreme right parties.

**Relevance to Your Thesis:** This theory directly connects to your research on how welfare institutions mediate between economic vulnerability and political outcomes. Milner's finding that regions experiencing stronger globalization pressures show increased support for extreme right parties aligns with your interest in how institutional arrangements process economic pressures. Her focus on the "globalization cleavage" complements your focus on the dual pathway model of status stabilization and moral framing.

### 2. Embedded Liberalism Theory

**Detailed Explanation:** This theory, associated with Ruggie (1982), argues that the post-WWII international economic order was built on a compromise where states would maintain open markets while simultaneously providing social protection to their citizens. This "embedded liberalism" bargain suggests that voters facing economic vulnerability due to global market exposure would demand compensation through welfare state protections. Political support for economic openness was thus dependent on government capacity to compensate the losers from globalization through social insurance, education, and other welfare programs.

**Direct Quotes:** "For a long time, scholars assumed that voters faced by the vicissitudes of openness to the global economy would demand compensation in exchange for such openness, the so called 'embedded liberalism' bargain (Ruggie, 1982). Government compensation is defined as public programs that transfer resources to those facing income shocks or economic vulnerability. Compensation via redistribution was the program of the left and seemed to attract working class voters and damp preferences for extremism." (p. 2295-2296)

**Operationalization and Application:** Milner tests this theory by examining whether individuals receiving unemployment benefits are less likely to support extreme right parties when exposed to globalization shocks. At the individual level, she interacts a measure of unemployment benefit receipt with regional exposure to import competition to determine if social welfare spending moderates the relationship between globalization exposure and support for the extreme right.

**Relevance to Your Thesis:** This is highly relevant to your interest in how welfare institutions buffer the political effects of economic vulnerability. Milner's finding that unemployment benefits do not reduce support for the extreme right when individuals are exposed to globalization shocks challenges a core assumption of your research about the institutional buffering capacity of welfare states. This suggests that certain types of welfare programs may be insufficient to prevent populist backlash in the face of significant economic restructuring.

## 3. Economic Insecurity Theory

**Detailed Explanation:** This theory suggests that support for populist or nationalist politicians stems directly from material hardship or economic vulnerability. Voters who experience or perceive threats to their economic well-being due to globalization or technological change are theorized to rationally turn to anti-establishment candidates who promise economic protection. Economic insecurity can arise from various sources, including job displacement, wage stagnation, or increased economic risk. Both objective economic conditions and subjective perceptions of economic vulnerability are considered important in shaping political preferences.

**Direct Quotes:** While not explicitly labeled as "economic insecurity theory" in the paper, Milner notes: "As for the causal mechanism, the main sources identified in the literature have been cultural and economic. The focus here is on the economic side and how growing globalization has had distributive consequences within countries that have generated losses for parts of the population." (p. 2292)

**Operationalization and Application:** The theory is operationalized through various measures of economic exposure to globalization and technological change at the regional level. These include exposure to import competition (especially from China and low-wage countries), automation via robots, and routine task intensity of jobs. At the individual level, the author also examines individuals' occupational vulnerability to offshoring and automation.

**Relevance to Your Thesis:** This connects to your focus on economic vulnerability as a driver of political change. Your thesis examines how welfare institutions mediate this relationship, and Milner's findings suggest that economic insecurity—particularly from trade and technological change—is associated with populist right support even in the presence of welfare compensation, which raises questions about the effectiveness of traditional welfare institutions in buffering these pressures.

### 4. Technological Change Theory

**Detailed Explanation:** This theory focuses on how technological innovation, particularly automation, affects labor markets and political preferences. As articulated by Boix (2019) and others cited in the paper, recent technological change has increasingly substituted for labor rather than complementing it, creating pressures similar to those from globalization. Certain occupations, particularly those with routine tasks that can be easily automated, are more vulnerable to displacement. The theory suggests that workers in such occupations will experience economic anxiety and potentially support parties that promise protection from these forces.

**Direct Quotes:** "Boix (2019) argues that capitalism and democracy seemed compatible in the period from 1940 to 1990 because technological change was complementary to labor. But in the current period technological change is substituting for labor and creating the same tensions between capitalism and democracy as in the late 19th century. Skill-biased technological change and rising inequality due to it are generating intense pressures on democracies." (p. 2295)

**Operationalization and Application:** Milner operationalizes this theory by creating regional measures of exposure to robots in manufacturing and routine task intensity (RTI) of jobs. At the individual level, she also measures the routine task intensity of an individual's occupation and their vulnerability to offshoring. These variables are used to test whether regions and individuals more exposed to technological change show increased support for extreme right parties.

**Relevance to Your Thesis:** This theory connects to your interest in how economic transformations affect political outcomes. Technological change represents another source of economic vulnerability beyond globalization, and understanding how welfare institutions might mediate its political effects is important for your research. Milner's finding that automation is associated with increased support for extreme right parties suggests another economic pressure that welfare institutions may need to address.

## Overview of Data Source and Empirical Methodology:

**Detailed Description:** Milner employs a multi-level analysis strategy, examining both regional-level and individual-level data across 15 Western European democracies from 1990 to 2018. For regional analysis, she constructs measures of exposure to globalization (trade, FDI, migration) and technological change (robots, routine task intensity) at the NUTS-1/2 regional level. These measures are weighted by 1992 regional and national labor shares to create "shock" variables that capture the differential impact of these forces across regions. The dependent variable is vote share by party family (extreme right, center right, center left, extreme left) at the regional level.

For individual-level analysis, Milner uses the European Social Survey from 2002-2016, matching respondents to their NUTS regions and the corresponding regional globalization measures. She also includes individual-level variables such as education, gender, age, union membership, and religiosity as controls.

To address endogeneity concerns with the import shock variables, Milner follows Autor et al. (2013) in using an instrumental variable approach, using US imports from China and low-wage countries as an instrument for European imports. This helps isolate exogenous supply-side shocks from demand-side factors or political elements that might be correlated with import changes.

The data has significant missing values (approximately 24%), which Milner addresses through multiple imputation to create 30 datasets. Statistical models include OLS and 2SLS with country-year (election) fixed effects and robust standard errors clustered at the regional level, as well as multinomial logistic regression for the individual-level analysis.

## Findings and Evidence:

**Summary of Key Findings:**

1. Trade shocks, particularly imports from China and low-wage countries, are associated with increased support for extreme right parties. At the regional level, a one standard deviation increase in import exposure (approximately 245 Euros per worker) is associated with a 1.11% increase in extreme right voting.
2. After addressing endogeneity concerns with instrumental variables, the effect of trade shocks becomes even stronger: a one standard deviation increase in the China Shock variable leads to a 2.18 percentage point increase in right populist voting.
3. The 2008 financial crisis substantially increased support for extreme right parties and magnified the negative effect of trade shocks on center-left parties.
4. Technological change, measured through robot exposure and routine task intensity, is also associated with increased support for extreme right parties. A one standard deviation increase in robot exposure is associated with a 0.7 percentage point increase in extreme right voting, while a similar increase in routine task intensity is associated with a 0.85 percentage point increase.
5. Foreign direct investment and migration show no consistent relationship with voting patterns, contradicting expectations about immigration driving extreme right support.
6. 
7. Social welfare compensation, measured through unemployment benefits, does not appear to moderate the relationship between trade exposure and support for extreme right parties. In fact, individuals receiving unemployment benefits in regions more exposed to import competition show a higher likelihood of voting for extreme right parties.

**Supporting Evidence:**

For the trade shock findings, the paper presents robust statistical evidence in Tables 1 and 2, showing positive and statistically significant coefficients for the China shock variable on extreme right voting. The instrumental variable approach strengthens these findings, with the 2SLS estimates showing even larger effects.

For the finding about the financial crisis, Figure 4 visualizes how the post-crisis period affected vote shares differently across party families, with right populist parties gaining substantially after the crisis, especially in regions with higher exposure to Chinese imports.

For technological change, the paper shows positive and statistically significant coefficients for both the robot shock and RTI variables on extreme right voting in Table 1.

For the welfare compensation finding, Table 3 shows a positive and statistically significant interaction between unemployment benefits and the China shock variable for right populist voting, visualized in Figure 5, indicating that receiving unemployment benefits does not dampen but actually enhances the relationship between trade exposure and extreme right support.

## Discussion:

**Interpretation of Findings:** Milner interprets her findings as evidence that globalization, particularly trade, is reshaping party systems in Western Europe by increasing support for extreme right parties and undermining support for traditional centrist ones. She argues that the distributive effects of trade have fallen heavily on certain regions and demographic groups, generating economic grievances that extreme right parties have successfully mobilized. She further suggests that technological change is amplifying these effects, creating additional economic pressures that feed into support for populist politics.

Regarding welfare compensation, Milner interprets the findings as challenging the "embedded liberalism" thesis. She suggests that unemployment benefits may no longer be sufficient to compensate voters for the economic disruptions caused by globalization, or that voters experiencing economic hardship may be seeking recognition rather than redistribution, as suggested by Gidron and Hall (2017).

**Connections to Other Research:** Milner connects her findings to Rogowski's (1989) classic work on how trade creates winners and losers domestically and shapes political coalitions. She notes that while Rogowski predicted the ultimate political victory of the economic winners from trade, what we see today is "the growing influence of the economic losers from trade who are forming opposition to globalization in all its forms via support for extreme right populist parties" (p. 2313-2314).

The paper also connects to research by Autor et al. (2013, 2020) on the China shock and political polarization, Colantone and Stanig (2018a, 2018b) on trade and right-wing voting in Europe, and Anelli et al. (2019) on automation and extreme right support. Milner's findings on welfare compensation relate to debates raised by Gidron and Hall (2017), who suggest that people whose status has fallen prefer recognition over redistribution, and Gingrich (2019), who questions the ability of welfare programs to compensate voters effectively.

**Relevance to Your Research:** Milner's findings on the limited effectiveness of welfare compensation in moderating the political effects of globalization directly connect to your research on how welfare institutions mediate between economic vulnerability and political outcomes. The paper suggests that traditional welfare mechanisms may be insufficient to prevent populist backlash, raising questions about what types of institutional arrangements might be more effective.

The paper's multi-level approach, examining both regional exposure to economic pressures and individual voting behavior, aligns with your interest in how institutional effects operate at different levels. The finding that both trade and technological change contribute to extreme right support suggests that welfare institutions may need to address multiple sources of economic vulnerability simultaneously.

The interaction between the financial crisis and trade shocks, with their combined effect particularly undermining support for center-left parties, raises important questions about how welfare institutions might need to adapt during periods of acute economic crisis, a topic that could be relevant to your research on institutional buffering.

## Key Takeaways:

1. **Welfare compensation appears insufficient to buffer political effects of globalization**: Contrary to the "embedded liberalism" thesis, Milner finds that receiving unemployment benefits does not reduce support for extreme right parties among individuals exposed to import competition and may even increase it. This challenges fundamental assumptions about how welfare states can mediate between economic pressures and political outcomes, suggesting either that current welfare arrangements are inadequate or that economic grievances are operating through non-material pathways (such as status concerns) that welfare transfers cannot address.
2. 
3. **Trade has stronger political effects than other forms of globalization**: Among the various globalization forces examined (trade, FDI, migration), trade consistently shows the strongest relationship with political change, particularly driving support for extreme right parties. This suggests that the distributive effects of trade may be particularly salient in shaping political preferences, which is relevant for your research on which economic pressures welfare institutions need to prioritize in buffering.
4. **Technological change reinforces the political effects of globalization**: The finding that automation (measured through robot exposure and routine task intensity) is associated with increased support for extreme right parties suggests that technological change represents another important source of economic vulnerability beyond globalization. This highlights the complex challenge facing welfare institutions in addressing multiple, overlapping sources of economic disruption simultaneously.
5. **Economic crises can amplify the political effects of globalization**: The paper shows that the 2008 financial crisis not only directly increased support for extreme right parties but also strengthened the negative relationship between trade shocks and support for center-left parties. This suggests that welfare institutions may face particularly severe challenges during periods of acute economic crisis, when their buffering capacity is most needed but potentially most strained.
6. **The traditional "left behind" narrative is supported but complicated by empirical evidence**: While the paper generally supports the notion that economic "losers" from globalization and technological change are turning to extreme right parties, it complicates this narrative by showing unexpected patterns, such as the finding that offshoring vulnerability is negatively associated with extreme right support. This suggests that the relationship between economic vulnerability and political behavior may be more complex than commonly assumed, with implications for how welfare institutions might more effectively address these dynamics.
