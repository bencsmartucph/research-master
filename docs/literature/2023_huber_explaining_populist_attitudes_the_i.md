## Key Takeaways

1. **Populist attitudes arise from perceived representation deficits on salient issues**: "Following the conception of populism as a thin-centred ideology, we argue that high levels of populist attitudes are not connected with certain issues per se. Rather, our argument is that people are more populist when they feel poorly represented on policy issues that they care strongly about." (p.133-134)
2. **Populist attitudes exist across party lines, not just among populist party supporters**: "This argument provides an explanation for the observation that even voters of non-populist parties sometimes show high levels of populist attitudes." (p.133)
3. **Policy discontent is a significant predictor of populist attitudes**: "The results are in line with our expectations: Individuals with higher policy discontent are more populist." (p.134) The effect size is substantial, comparable to education and satisfaction with democracy.
4. **Populist attitudes can be dynamic rather than static**: "The latter suggests that levels of populist attitudes are dynamic and may change if grievances are addressed (or remain unaddressed)." (p.135)
5. **Issue salience moderates the relationship between policy positions and populist attitudes**: "In this regard, our argument follows the idea of, for example, Bélanger and Meguid (2008), that issue salience is an important moderator of how policy positions affect other political attitudes and political behaviour." (p.135)

## Theoretical Framework Analysis

### Primary Theory 2: The Activation Model of Populist Attitudes

**Detailed explanation:**
The paper engages with what the authors describe as the "activation" model of populist attitudes, primarily developed by Hawkins and colleagues (Hawkins et al. 2012, 2020). According to this perspective, populist attitudes are latent or "dormant" predispositions within individuals that become activated under specific contextual conditions, particularly when there are perceived failures of democratic governance.

"The first argument states that populist attitudes are widespread among citizens and comparable to an inherent personality trait that lies 'dormant until activated by weak democratic governance and policy failure' (Hawkins et al. 2019, iii). According to this view, populist attitudes are not inherently tied to a particular ideology or certain issues. Instead, they are conceptualised as a response of individuals that is activated once they feel badly governed and not sufficiently represented by political elites (Hawkins et al. 2012, 2020)." (p.134-135)

This theory suggests that populist attitudes are relatively stable characteristics of individuals that vary in strength but are triggered by specific contextual factors rather than being created anew in response to grievances. The activation model portrays populist attitudes as more of a predisposition that manifests when triggered by perceptions of elite failure or democratic deficits.

The authors engage with this theory but ultimately offer a more dynamic perspective. While they acknowledge the idea that populist attitudes respond to representation deficits, they challenge the notion that these attitudes are merely static traits being activated. Instead, they suggest populist attitudes can actually change over time in response to how well grievances are addressed.

"Populist attitudes are thus understood as a static characteristic of each individual that may be more or less pronounced and activated by contextual deficits. In other words, the level of populist attitudes remains rather constant and is activated by a (perceived) lack of representation." (p.134-135)

The literature associated with this perspective includes works by Busby et al. (2019) and other scholars who have examined how populist rhetoric and framing can activate latent populist attitudes.

**Connection to your research:** This theoretical perspective provides insight into how contextual factors might trigger support for far-right parties. For your research, this suggests examining how specific events, policy changes, or governance failures might activate latent populist predispositions and potentially channel them toward far-right support. You might consider investigating whether economic downturns, immigration surges, or cultural conflicts serve as activating contexts for populist attitudes, and under what conditions these activated attitudes specifically manifest as support for far-right parties rather than other populist alternatives.

### Primary Theory 3: Grievance-Based Populism Model

**Detailed explanation:**
The paper contrasts the activation model with what might be called a "grievance-based" model of populist attitudes, which suggests that populist attitudes emerge directly from specific grievances or forms of discontent and are therefore more dynamic and changeable. This perspective is associated with scholars like Rico and Anduiza (2019) and Giebler et al. (2020).

"The second argument holds that populist attitudes arise as a manifestation of specific grievances (Rico and Anduiza 2019) or discontent (Giebler et al. 2020; Rooduijn et al. 2016)." (p.135)

According to this view, populist attitudes are not dormant predispositions but rather direct responses to specific grievances that can change if those grievances are addressed. This perspective emphasizes the dynamic nature of populist attitudes and their connection to contextual factors.

"In this view, populist attitudes are dynamic and may change if grievances are addressed (or remain unaddressed)." (p.135)

The grievance-based model often emphasizes the role of economic and cultural grievances stemming from globalization, modernization, and other macro-level changes. Several important theoretical contributions are associated with this perspective:

1. The "losers of globalization" thesis by Kriesi et al. (2006, 2008, 2012), which suggests that globalization creates winners and losers, with the latter being more receptive to populist appeals.
2. The "four Ds" framework by Eatwell and Goodwin (2018): distrust, destruction, dealignment, and deprivation.
3. The "cultural backlash" thesis by Norris and Inglehart (2018), which argues that populism is partly a reaction against progressive cultural changes.

"Norris and Inglehart (2018) do not focus on "national populism" but rather explain why there is a cultural backlash against globalisation (also see Schäfer 2021). According to their argument, as societies turn more liberal, citizens with strongly conservative and traditional–in essence, illiberal–stances perceive a lack of substantive representation in certain (mostly cultural) policy areas." (p.137-138)

The authors integrate elements of this grievance-based model into their theoretical framework, acknowledging the role of specific grievances in generating populist attitudes while emphasizing the importance of issue salience as a moderating factor.

**Connection to your research:** This theoretical perspective directly relates to your interest in the social and psychological mechanisms that drive support for far-right parties. The grievance-based model suggests examining specific economic, cultural, and political grievances that might make far-right messaging appealing. For your research, this highlights the importance of investigating how specific policy domains (welfare, immigration, cultural policies) generate grievances among different population segments, and how these grievances might be channeled into support for far-right parties. The dynamic nature of this model also suggests that addressing specific grievances through policy interventions might reduce populist attitudes and potentially diminish support for far-right parties.

### Primary Theory 4: Integrated Policy Discontent × Issue Salience Model

**Detailed explanation:**
The authors' key theoretical contribution is their integrated model, which combines elements of both the activation and grievance-based approaches while emphasizing the critical role of issue salience. This integrated approach suggests that populist attitudes emerge from the combination of policy discontent and issue salience—people become more populist when they care deeply about an issue and feel their position on it is not well represented.

"In light of this argument, it is essential to emphasise that populist attitudes are not a function of specific issue positions per se. Although, empirically, individuals who oppose immigration may often be fairly populist, this merely reflects–according to our argument–that these citizens care strongly about this issue and, at the same time, perceive the government to be poorly representing their preferences on immigration." (p.139)

In which P Aj are an individual j 's populist attitudes, Dij captures the discontent of individual j on issue i and Sij represents the salience of issue i for individual j." (p.138-139)

This theory builds on the work of scholars like Bélanger and Meguid (2008), who have examined how issue salience moderates the relationship between policy positions and political attitudes/behavior. It also connects to research on issue voting and issue ownership in political behavior.

The authors operationalize this theory by creating a composite measure that weights issue-specific discontent by the relative salience of each issue for the individual respondent. This approach allows them to test whether populist attitudes are indeed driven by discontent on issues that people care about, rather than by specific ideological positions.

"To create the final score, we multiply each issue-specific discontent di;j with the respective relative importance weight and create an overall score, Di, of policy discontent..." (p.143)

The empirical analysis supports this theoretical model, showing that policy discontent weighted by issue salience is indeed a significant predictor of populist attitudes, even when controlling for other factors like satisfaction with democracy, education, and left-right ideology.

**Connection to your research:** This integrated theoretical model offers a sophisticated framework for understanding the conditions under which policy dissatisfaction might translate into support for far-right parties. For your research on redistribution preferences and far-right support, this suggests examining not just whether people support certain welfare or labor policies, but how salient these issues are to them and whether they feel their preferences are represented. The model suggests that even traditionally left-leaning voters might support far-right parties if they feel unrepresented on highly salient economic issues, despite the far-right's often less redistributive economic platforms. Similarly, those with redistributive preferences might be attracted to far-right parties if cultural issues are more salient to them than economic ones, and they feel unrepresented on these cultural dimensions.
