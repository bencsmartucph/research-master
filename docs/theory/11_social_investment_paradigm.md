# Social Investment Paradigm

> **Context Abstract:** This module examines the social investment approach as a comprehensive framework addressing material needs, status concerns, and political legitimacy simultaneously. The core argument is that human capital development, capabilities enhancement, proactive policy design, and dignity-affirming implementation can create "upward spirals" of solidarity while resolving the trilemma between material adequacy, status recognition, and democratic sustainability.

---

## Conceptual Foundations: Capabilities and Human Development

The Capabilities Approach (Sen)

Nicaise (2021), drawing on Sen's capabilities approach, explains: "One of the novelties of Sen's theory of capabilities is the explicit link between freedom and welfare. Persons with the same level of functioning(s), but with unequal freedom, have a different degree of welfare, because the 'capability' of the less free persons (i.e. the set of functionings that are feasible for them) is more restricted than the capability of the more free persons."

Hemerijck et al. (2023) connect to social investment: "From the perspective of political theory, there is a striking normative connection between social investment and the Nobel laureate Amartya Sen's 'capability approach' with its strong emphasis on 'human flourishing', development and autonomy, signalling a shift from the Beveridgean notion of 'freedom from want' towards 'freedom to act'."

### Long-Term Investment Orientation

Nicaise (2021) defines social investment as: "'investing in people' (as opposed to financial or material investments): the common feature to all forms of investment is that costs are incurred in the short and medium term, with a view to yielding returns in the longer term."

## Proactive vs. Reactive Policy Orientation

### Anticipatory Policy Design

Malo (2020) defines proactive policies as: "not necessarily designed to react to a specific problem at a given moment in the short term, but to potentially pre-empt a wide range of problems or imbalances caused by economic shocks or other sources of unexpected changes."

### Skill Investment Preferences

Busemeyer and Garritzmann (2017) find: "Rather than demanding compensation for globalization-related income losses ex post, individuals in open economies are more likely to demand educational investments in order to avoid unemployment ex ante by demanding increased skill-investments."

Rationale: "Hence, skill-investment might be preferred as an effective insurance policy against labor market risk compared to ex-post compensation through transfer payments."

## Predistribution vs. Redistribution Framework

### Educational Cleavage in Policy Preferences

Kuziemko, Longuet-Marx, & Naidu (2023) find: "Less-educated Americans differentially demand 'predistribution' policies (e.g., a federal jobs guarantee, higher minimum wages, protectionism, and stronger unions), while more-educated Americans differentially favor redistribution (taxes and transfers)."

They identify democratic misalignment: "We thus uncover a new and durable educational cleavage over pre- versus post-fiscal dimensions of egalitarian policies. Second, we focus on the parties' supply of economic policies and show that since the mid-1970s, the Democrats have moved away from the preferences of less-educated voters."

### Work Preferences vs. Income Preferences

Kuziemko et al. (2023) challenge standard assumptions: "Standard models of distributive politics assume that voters only care about consumption (in static models, equivalent to after-tax income) and leisure. If voters maximize utility based exclusively on consumption and leisure, then demand for predistribution should be low. Under such assumptions, a coal-miner, say, choosing between industrial policy that would allow her to keep working or a monthly check that would cover her lost salary but not provide future work would always choose the latter."

But in reality, workers often prefer continued work. Pelc (2025) finds: "Overall, a sizeable portion of workers are resistant to being freed from work. Strikingly, individuals are more willing to reduce their work hours by half than to cease work entirely, even when offered full compensation."

## Multidimensional Evaluation of Welfare Outcomes

### Subjective Well-Being as Outcome

Hemerijck et al. (2023) argue: "21st century social investment research should take the subjective capabilities dimension on board as a litmus test of the 'goodness of fit' of social investment for well-being progress."

### Quality of Employment Beyond Job Creation

Heins (2023) emphasizes: "The quality of employment, not just quantity, is crucial for wellbeing... 'Getting people off benefits and into low-paid, insecure and health-damaging work is not a desirable option' (Marmot, 2010)."

Gidron and Hall (2018) note: "having a decent job is central to being in the mainstream."

### Social Inclusion and Participation

Mettler (2002) identifies how certain policies lead to "inclusion effects," while Wagner (2023) shows social investment policies can promote social inclusion beyond material transfers.

Nicaise (2021) frames it as: "employability was defined as one dimension of human capabilities; and social investment as a strategy to extend human capabilities with the aim of implementing fundamental rights."

## Reconciling Material Support with Status Preservation

### Combining Economic Security with Status Recognition

Nicaise (2021) shows that social investment programs emphasizing autonomy and dignity can produce positive human capital and subjective well-being outcomes, while avoiding the defensive "othering" responses that conditionality triggers.

Wagner (2023) contrasts approaches: "Conditionality/sanctions → fear/exclusion; Social investment → inclusion."

Pelc (2025) adds: "Source/framing of compensation matters for dignity." The source of compensation affects acceptance: "Program design matters: respondents are significantly more likely to accept compensation from their former employer than from the government."

### Procedural Justice and Implementation Quality

De Blok and Kumlin (2022) explain: "Procedural fairness theory... predicts that people care about the decision-making and implementation process..."

Malo (2020) emphasizes: "Implementation quality essential for ALMP efficacy."

### Capabilities Enhancement vs. Conditionality

Heins (2023) finds: "'Enabling' types of ALMP focusing on human capital development... seem to have mainly wellbeing-enhancing effects," while punitive conditionality has negative effects.

## Voter Preferences: Predistribution and Program Design

### Skill Investment and Ex Ante Prevention

Evidence suggests voters in open economies prefer skill investments for preventative protection. Busemeyer and Garritzmann (2017) identify: "Skill-investment might be preferred as an effective insurance policy against labor market risk compared to ex-post compensation through transfer payments."

### Program Design Preferences

Pelc (2025) notes: "Program design matters significantly: respondents prefer compensation from employers over government and partial hour reductions over complete work cessation."

Heins (2023) finds: "Wage subsidies/subsidised self-employment enhance well-being more than some training."

Wagner (2023) observes: "Social investment can foster solidaristic attitudes."

## Social Investment as Trilemma Solution

The social investment paradigm offers a potential framework for addressing the fundamental trilemma by simultaneously targeting:

1. **Material needs** through human capital development that enhances long-term employability while preserving dignity
2. **Status concerns** through capabilities enhancement that expands freedom and agency
3. **Political legitimacy** through rights-based implementation emphasizing equality of citizenship and human dignity, plus participatory governance involving recipients in design

### Policy Recommendations and Considerations

Pitts & Winter (2024) call for: "positive and proactive message that centres on empowerment through equality and social protection... Also, address broader perceptions of downward mobility by challenging national myths, bolstering the dignity of those who see themselves as marginalized."

Im & Komp-Leukkunen (2021) suggest: "policies that help retain dignity and status previously conferred by routine jobs may better avert the political fallout from automation."

Ciccolini (2024) on left-wing dilemma: "progressively redistributive policies may not represent an adequate solution. Status politics is not about material hardship, and voters facing status loss demand the very opposite of a redistribution of wealth."

Kim (2021) on Trade Adjustment Assistance (TAA): "TAA can build support for mainstream/left parties, suggesting positive attribution for compensation."

### Freedom and Autonomy in Program Design

Nicaise (2021) provides example: "The degree of autonomy, choice and participation is maximised... participation is completely voluntary... Participants negotiate the rules..."

## Upward Spirals of Solidarity

Wagner (2023) proposes that social investment creates positive feedback:

"On the other hand, social investment policies (Morel et al. 2012) provide active resources enabling recipients to improve their human capital, labor market position, and generally their capacity to socially participate, what can lead to experiences of social inclusion (Mettler 2002). In reaction, I hypothesize that there is a tendency to perceive that 'others should be offered the same opportunity', showcasing the formation of more inclusive welfare attitudes even towards outgroups in an 'upward spiral' of solidarity bonds."
