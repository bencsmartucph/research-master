# Status, Recognition & Relative Position

> **Context Abstract:** This module examines status anxiety and recognition needs as distinct mechanisms driving political behavior separate from material hardship. The core argument is that economic status operates as a zero-sum game where relative position matters more than absolute wealth, and that voters facing status loss demand recognition and dignity rather than additional material compensation. This framework helps explain the limited effectiveness of traditional welfare expansion.

---

## Status Versus Material Hardship: A Fundamental Distinction

Economic insecurity and status anxiety represent distinct mechanisms that can drive political behavior, though they are often interrelated. This distinction is crucial for understanding how different forms of vulnerability translate into political preferences:

As Kurer (2020) explains: "Voters who experience material hardship become dependent on state support and should thus have a strong interest in a generous welfare state to cushion the economic downfall and support their re-entry in the labor market. Voters who primarily suffer from status anxiety are more likely to look for propositions that promise to maintain or reinstall their place in the social hierarchy."

Several scholars emphasize that economic hardship alone may be insufficient to explain support for populist radical right parties (PRRPs). Kurer (2020) argues: "When relative societal decline rather than material hardship are at the heart of socially conservative resentment, traditional welfare policy may be an insufficient response to satisfy exposed workers and hence an ineffective remedy to counter the ascent of right-wing populist movements."

## Status Loss vs. Material Deterioration

Ciccolini (2025) provides important evidence for this distinction by demonstrating that economic status loss affects political attitudes differently than absolute economic decline: "Hence, we can conclude that, as expected, economic status loss, not a deterioration of material circumstances, explains class alignment to PRR parties."

Economic status is understood as relational rather than absolute: "Because economic resources are at the basis of economic status, but the two concepts do not coincide. This implies that economic status is structured as a zero-sum game: Someone's gain in status coincides with someone else's loss. It is thus clear that an improvement in material circumstances does not translate straightforwardly into a gain in economic status."

## Economic Status as Zero-Sum

Ciccolini (2022) provides a crucial insight by distinguishing between economic resources and economic status: "I define economic status as the relative position within a social hierarchy ordering subjects on an inferiority-superiority scale with respect to the comparative degree to which they possess economic resources."

A key feature of economic status is its zero-sum nature: "Because a subject's economic status depends on others' resources, the scale of economic status in a society is virtually inexpansible... In laypeople's terms, it is not possible for everyone to get richer and simultaneously acquire more economic status."

This zero-sum quality makes status concerns politically potent, as changes to the status hierarchy are perceived as deviations from a supposedly legitimate order, generating feelings of resentment.

Ciccolini provides strong empirical evidence that economic status loss drives support for populist radical right parties: "The coefficient for positional income change in the logistic regression predicting PRR voting is -0.14 (p<0.01), indicating that a one-unit increase in positional income decreases the probability of voting for a PRR party by 14 percentage points."

Importantly, this relationship holds even when controlling for absolute income levels and changes, indicating that relative economic positioning matters more than objective financial circumstances.

## Recognition Over Redistribution

Drawing on Gidron and Hall (2017a,b), the importance of social recognition becomes clear in understanding political reactions to economic changes:

"In their BJS article they show that the social status of white, working class men- defined by their levels of education, income, and occupation- has declined for various reasons in the past twenty years and that this group is the most supportive of populism in the West."

Importantly, Milner notes that these status concerns may transcend purely economic interests, making traditional compensation mechanisms less effective:

"Gidron and Hall (2017b) maintain that people whose social status has fallen and who feel left behind by globalization because they are its unacknowledged 'losers' prefer recognition over redistribution. 'Our analysis suggests that 'compensation' is not what these people are looking for. Voters for right populist parties are not especially strong supporters of redistribution. What distinguishes them from other voters is a feeling that they have not been treated with respect a sense that they are not fully valued members of society. They care as much, or even more, about recognition as about redistribution'" (Gidron and Hall 2017b, p. 26).

This preference for recognition over redistribution helps explain why social welfare spending may be losing its mitigating power and why right-wing populism has become increasingly attractive to working-class voters.

## Status Discordance Theory

Status discordance theory examines how unmet status expectations drive political behavior.

Kurer and van Staalduinen (2022) explain: "The analysis reveals that political dissatisfaction is widespread among voters who fall short of intergenerational status expectations. We show that such dissatisfaction is associated with higher abstention rates, less mainstream party support, and more radical voting."

Their research finds that "A one-standard-deviation increase in negative status discordance associated with: 1-2 percentage point increase in abstention, 1-2 percentage point increase in support for radical parties, 3 percentage point decrease in support for mainstream parties."

The magnitude of this effect is significant: "In terms of magnitude, the coefficient of status discordance indicates a larger effect than that of log income and age. This favorable comparison to important and well-known predictors of radical-right voting underscores the importance of expectations in shaping voter behavior."

## Gender Differences in Status Effects

Research shows significant gender differences in how status concerns translate into political behavior. Kurer and van Staalduinen (2022) find: "The marginal effect of status discordance is indeed much weaker among women. It is no longer statistically different from zero with respect to the radical right and is virtually zero with respect to the radical left."

They suggest this might be because: "The empowering and emancipatory experience of increasingly equal access to paid employment may moderate political reactions among the individual women who do experience status loss as conceptualized in this article."

Gidron and Hall (2017) note how changing gender roles interact with status perceptions: "Moreover, in a 2008 poll, while a quarter of American men earning more than $60,000 a year agreed with the statement that 'with the rise of women in society and the workplace, men no longer know their role', a third of men earning less than that did so (Kimmel 2009, 2013; Cassino 2016; Jones and Cox 2016). In sum, increases in the social status of women may have induced some decline in the subjective social status of men, especially among those who lack other sources of status."

Their historical analysis shows that "While men without college education had higher status than women without college education in most countries in 1987, by 2014 this gender gap had narrowed or reversed."

## Status-Based Political Reactions

Status anxiety and perceived relative decline can produce specific political reactions distinct from those generated by material hardship. Kurer (2020) associates "threat and anxiety about status with 'the development of socially conservative or even authoritarian character traits', and threats to the 'perceived value' of some forms of work and those that perform them with increased hostility to groups 'seen as competitors in the social hierarchy', as a means of achieving a relative stabilisation of their own status and value."

Status concerns may even generate opposition to redistribution, contrary to what pure economic self-interest would predict. Ciccolini (2025) finds: "A drop in positional income is associated with further cultural conservatism. More precisely, it fuels hostile attitudes toward migrants, as well as criticism of the European Union [...] I found no association with right-leaning economic orientation. Yet it should be noted that economic status loss boosts opposition to redistribution."

This counterintuitive finding challenges conventional wisdom: "When economic resources are portrayed as a zero-sum game, those facing relative loss would be further harmed by governments bringing relief to the have-nots: This would shift the balance in favor of the latter, further exacerbating voters' fear of ending up in last place."
