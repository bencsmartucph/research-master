# Mechanisms Catalog

> **Context Abstract:** This module provides a comprehensive catalog of causal mechanisms linking economic vulnerability, welfare institutions, and political behavior. It serves as a reference guide organizing mechanisms by category—economic, institutional, psychological, and political—with precise theoretical definitions and empirical operationalizations from the source literature. This facilitates systematic analysis of how different mechanisms operate and interact.

---

## I. ECONOMIC VULNERABILITY MECHANISMS

### A. Economic Activation

**Economic Grievance Activation**

**Definition:** Economic hardship, insecurity, or decline acts as a trigger for political responses, including support for populist movements.

**Operationalization:** Trade shocks, automation exposure, income losses, unemployment, precarity measures.

**Note:** The causal link remains debated, often juxtaposed with cultural explanations. Specific policy choices, such as austerity measures leading to welfare cuts, can be seen as concrete mechanisms activating these grievances.

### B. Economic Vulnerability Pathways

**Globalization Impact Mechanism**

**Definition:** Trade exposure creates winners and losers through unequal distribution of gains.

**Core Mechanism:** Trade liberalization → Unequal gains distribution → Geographic concentration of losses → Regional decline

**Evidence:** Colantone and Stanig (2018) on China shock: areas with Chinese competition saw persistent employment decline across industries.

**Spillover Effects:** Individual economic welfare linked to local economic conditions through economic spillovers.

**Automation Vulnerability Mechanism**

**Definition:** Routineness and codifiability of job tasks determine automation susceptibility.

**Core Mechanism:** Task codifiability → Automation vulnerability → Gradual displacement → Status decline

**Distinction:** Anticipated automation risk (fear) drives stronger political response than actual job loss.

**Key Finding:** Routine workers still employed but threatened show strongest radical-right support.

**Skill-Specific Vulnerability**

**Definition:** Skill specificity and transferability determine occupational unemployment risk.

**Mechanism:** Specific skills → Limited transferability → Higher occupational risk → Greater labor market vulnerability

**Example:** Petroleum engineer skills useless in other professions.

**Age Factor:** Vulnerability varies by cohort—older workers face different risks than younger workers entering labor force.

**Precarity Mechanisms**

**Definition:** Multidimensional insecurity across work conditions, tenure, and financial stability.

**Components:**
- Work-related precarity (working conditions)
- Precarity of tenure (job stability)
- Financial precarity (income insecurity)

**Finding:** Work-related precarity shows stronger populist association than tenure precarity.

**Unbundling Effect**

**Definition:** Globalization through global value chains reduces predictability and control of economic impacts.

**Mechanism:** Global value chains → Unpredictable impact → Individualized risk → Sudden/uncontrollable change

**Result:** More uncertainty and insecurity at individual level.

## II. POLICY FEEDBACK MECHANISMS

### A. Policy Feedback Effects Theory

**Core Mechanism:** Policies → Experiences → Political attitudes → Future policy preferences

**Self-Reinforcing Effects:** Policies create constituencies that support their continuation.

**Self-Undermining Effects:** Policies generate opposition to themselves.

**Resource Effects:** Policies distribute resources affecting citizens' capacity for political action.

**Interpretive Effects:** Policies convey messages about target groups' social worth and government's role.

**Institutional Effects:** Policies create administrative arrangements shaping political possibilities.

### B. Specific Feedback Pathways

**Inclusion Effects**

**Example:** US GI-Bill led to increased social and public participation among recipients (Mettler, 2002).

**Downward Spiral Mechanism**

**Definition:** Welfare cuts and conditionality → Fear of exclusion → Harsh judgments of "undeserving" → Downward spiral of solidarity erosion

**Evidence:** Wagner (2022): "welfare cuts and conditionality may spurred fears of entitlement loss and social exclusion among recipients. In reaction, many tend to harshen their views on entitlements for those seen as less deserving."

**Upward Spiral Mechanism**

**Definition:** Social investment → Inclusion experiences → Inclusive attitudes toward others → Upward spiral of solidarity

**Mechanism:** Social investment → Capacity improvement → Inclusion experience → Perception that "others should have same opportunity"

**Self-Undermining Policy Feedback**

**Definition:** Well-intended policies → Unintended political backlash → Increased support for opposition

**Condition:** If policies favor out-groups displeasing potential radical-right voters, voters may turn to radical right to reorient policies.

**Trust-Performance Loop**

**Definition:** Performance → Trust → Future evaluations (recursive relationship)

**Mechanism:** Poor welfare state performance → Decreased political trust → More negative future performance evaluations → Downbound spiral

**Evidence:** Haugsgjerd and Kumlin (2020): malperformance sets in motion broader, longer-lived process affecting trust.

## III. INSTITUTIONAL DESIGN MECHANISMS

### A. Deservingness and Moral Economy Mechanisms

**CARIN Criteria Framework**

Five dimensions shaping deservingness perceptions:
- **Control:** Personal responsibility for situation
- **Attitude:** Norm-conforming behavior
- **Reciprocity:** Contribution to society
- **Identity:** Similarity and cultural closeness
- **Need:** Financial/health severity

**Producerism Mechanism**

**Definition:** Economic moral framework dividing society into productive and parasitic strata.

**Structure:** Virtuous producers squeezed by parasites above (elites) and below (welfare recipients, immigrants)

**Effect on Welfare Attitudes:** Creates narrow deservingness conceptions excluding unemployed.

**Welfare Chauvinism Mechanism**

**Definition:** In-group preference combined with welfare support.

**Mechanism:** Reciprocity criterion + Identity criterion → Native-citizen preference for welfare access

**Status Mechanism:** Status-anxious workers use welfare boundaries to distinguish themselves from lower-status groups.

**Othering as Status Maintenance**

**Definition:** Individuals distance from lower-status groups to maintain position.

**Paradox:** Welfare recipients engage in "othering" of even less deserving groups, reinforcing negative narratives against themselves.

**Effect:** Re-circulates and embeds "scrounger" narratives.

### B. Implementation Quality Mechanisms

**Procedural Fairness Mechanism**

**Definition:** Fair processes matter more for "losers" than "winners."

**Core Finding:** Unfair procedures exacerbate dissatisfaction more than fair procedures alleviate it.

**Implication:** Losers highly sensitive to how programs are implemented.

**Visibility and Salience Mechanism**

**Definition:** Visible welfare policies prime negative attitudes among populist-inclined voters.

**Effect:** More visible welfare states and interventions increase salience, potentially triggering opposition.

**Respectful Treatment and Dignity**

**Definition:** Implementation emphasizing choice, autonomy, and respect affects psychological outcomes.

**Evidence:** Nicaise (2021): "Restoring decent rights... requires investment in awareness raising, accessibility for all citizens, free choice and respectful treatment of claimants."

## IV. STATUS AND IDENTITY MECHANISMS

### A. Status Mechanisms

**Status Loss as Zero-Sum Game**

**Definition:** Economic status depends on relative position; gains for some mean losses for others.

**Mechanism:** Someone's gain in status = Someone else's loss → Status hierarchy competition → Resentment at change

**Political Effect:** Status loss drives support for status-affirming policies, sometimes even opposing material interest.

**Recognition Over Redistribution**

**Definition:** Status-anxious voters demand recognition and respect rather than material compensation.

**Evidence:** Gidron and Hall (2017): "people whose social status has fallen prefer recognition over redistribution."

**Status Discordance Mechanism**

**Definition:** Gap between expected and actual status drives political dissatisfaction.

**Effect:** Unmet intergenerational status expectations → Higher abstention, radical voting, mainstream party rejection

**Magnitude:** One std-dev in status discordance = 1-2pp increase in radical party support.

**Status Decline vs. Material Hardship**

**Definition:** Relative position loss drives different political demands than absolute income loss.

**Evidence:** Ciccolini (2025): Status loss predicts PRR support; material circumstances do not.

**Gender Differences in Status Effects**

**Finding:** Status discordance effects much weaker for women, possibly due to emancipatory experiences.

### B. Identity Switching Mechanism

**Definition:** Voters switch identification between economic class and cultural group based on context.

**Triggers:** Economic shocks + cultural contestation → Switch to cultural identity

**Consequences:**
- Belief slanting toward group stereotype
- Reduced demand for universalistic redistribution
- Increased demand for cultural protection
- Political realignment (class → cultural-based voting)

**Effect:** Explains why economic vulnerability sometimes produces cultural/nationalist rather than leftist responses.

## V. PSYCHOLOGICAL MECHANISMS

### A. Ontological Security Mechanisms

**Ontological Insecurity as Threat**

**Definition:** Rapid change threatens ability to maintain coherent sense of self.

**Dimensions:**
- **Exogenous:** Maintaining consistent self-notion in relation to others
- **Endogenous:** State as provider of security for citizens

**Trigger:** Globalization challenges simple definitions of "who we are and where we come from"

**Anxiety as Political Mobilizer**

**Definition:** Existential anxiety independent of fixed attitudes can mobilize political support.

**Finding:** Anxious individuals vulnerable to populist mobilization even without attitude change.

**Fantasy and Fulfillment**

**Definition:** Populist fantasies promise restoration and control without being realistic.

**Function:** Fantasies "connect subjects to social orders by arousing desire and channelling it."

**Risk:** When fantasies fail, blame redirected to "saboteurs," deepening alienation.

### B. Emotional Mechanisms

**Anger as Political Emotion**

**Definition:** Anger → Approach behavior + Reliance on pre-existing beliefs + Punishment desire

**Effect:** Different from fear; promotes action and hostility toward blamed groups.

**Responsibility Attribution Effect:** Anger's political consequence depends on whether domestic political system blamed.

**Life Dissatisfaction Mechanism**

**Definition:** General life dissatisfaction predicts populist attitudes independent of specific economic concerns.

**Effect:** Stronger than societal economic discontent.

**Pathways:** Utility mechanism (status quo undesirable), emotional mechanism (openness to change), social identity mechanism (in-group/out-group thinking)

**Loneliness and Political Vulnerability**

**Definition:** Loneliness → Desire for shared identity + Social anxiousness + Lower trust

**Effect:** Makes individuals vulnerable to populist mobilization offering community.

## VI. REPRESENTATION AND ATTRIBUTION MECHANISMS

### A. Policy Attribution Mechanisms

**Government Signaling Through Policy Choices**

**Definition:** Policy responses signal government priorities to voters.

**Mechanism:** Fiscal cutbacks → Inference of policy incompatibility with voter needs → Accountability withdrawal

**Effect:** Vulnerable voters punish governments perceived as incompatible with their interests.

**Instrumental Pull-In**

**Definition:** Voters perceive populist agendas as responding to their insecurity.

**Mechanism:** Precarity → Perception of PRR responsiveness → Electoral support

**Representation Gaps**

**Definition:** Perceived distance between voter preferences and politician priorities.

**Class Gradient:** Upper classes perceive better representation; lower classes perceive worse representation on salient policies (pensions, unemployment).

### B. Information Processing Mechanisms

**Misattribution of Economic Problems**

**Definition:** Economic problems attributed to more psychologically gratifying scapegoats.

**Mechanism:** Automation → Blamed on immigration/trade (clear out-groups, straightforward policies) → Inadequate policy responses

**Three Causes:**
1. Psychological gratification of human scapegoats vs. machines
2. Political entrepreneur supply of populist discourse
3. Cognitive availability (frequent encounters with goods/immigrants, not automation)

**Identity-Based Belief Formation**

**Definition:** Identity determines interpretations of economic facts.

**Effect:** Same facts interpreted differently based on cultural/class identity adopted.

**Selective Information Processing**

**Definition:** Prior beliefs filter information exposure and acceptance.

**Finding:** Those with inaccurate views most resistant to corrective information; partisan filtering prevents fact-based opinion change.

## VII. POLICY REMEDY MECHANISMS

### A. Active Labor Market Policy (ALMP) Mechanisms

**Implementation Quality Effect**

**Definition:** Policy effectiveness depends on implementation quality, not just design.

**Factors:** Funding stability, administrative capacity, tripartite coordination

**Evidence:** Funding stability during business cycles increases aggregate effectiveness.

**Enabling vs. Punitive ALMP**

**Definition:** ALMP type determines wellbeing and political effects.

**Enabling ALMPs** (human capital, regular employment emulation):
- Wellbeing-enhancing
- Positive employment outcomes
- Inclusive attitudes

**Punitive ALMPs** (conditionality, sanctions):
- Negative wellbeing effects
- Stigma increase
- Culture of self-blame
- Defensive othering responses

**Upskilling vs. Activation**

**Definition:** Social investment orientation (upskilling) vs. activation orientation (making work incentives).

**Historical Pattern:** Post-war: high social investment; 1970s-90s: activation; 1990s+: weak investment focus

**Evidence:** Nordic countries maintained upskilling focus longer.

### B. Social Investment Mechanisms

**Capabilities Enhancement Mechanism**

**Definition:** Programs expanding freedom and agency address status concerns alongside material needs.

**Theory:** Freedom matters independently of consumption; agency/capability affects dignity.

**Proactive vs. Reactive Policy**

**Definition:** Proactive policies prevent problems; reactive policies address after occurrence.

**Preference:** Voters prefer skill investment (ex ante prevention) over compensation (ex post).

**Rationale:** Insurance value of skill investment vs. post-hoc compensation.

**Dignity and Source of Support**

**Definition:** Source of compensation affects acceptance.

**Finding:** Employer compensation more acceptable than government transfers.

**Effect:** Signals different messages about deservingness and reciprocity.
