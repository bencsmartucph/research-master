# Moral Economy, Deservingness & Welfare Chauvinism

> **Context Abstract:** This module examines how moral frameworks—particularly producerism and the CARIN deservingness criteria—shape welfare attitudes and political responses. The core argument is that welfare attitudes reflect not just material self-interest but moral judgments about who deserves help, that these moral frames vary systematically across welfare regimes, and that status anxiety often leads to defensive "othering" where vulnerable populations distance themselves from even lower-status groups.

---

## The CARIN Deservingness Framework

Van Oorschot and Laenen (2023) identify five key deservingness criteria that shape welfare attitudes:

"In general, target groups are considered more deserving if they (a) are not considered personally responsible for their situation ('Control'), (b) adhere to prevailing societal norms and values, such as showing gratitude when help is offered ('Attitude'), (c) make a contribution to our society, in the past, present or future ('Reciprocity'), (d) belong to the dominant social or cultural group with which most people can easily identify ('Identity'), and (e) have severe financial and/or health needs ('Need')."

Operationally:

"The first criterion, control, is about the perceived degree of personal responsibility welfare claimants have for being in the situation they are in... The second criterion, attitude, refers to a broader collection of norm-conforming attitudes and behaviours, such as people's gratitude, docility, compliance, honesty and pleasantness. The third criterion, reciprocity, relates to perceived contributions to society claimants make (either in the past, present or future), such as paid work, tax payments, caring responsibilities and job-seeking efforts. The fourth criterion, identity, has to do with feelings of similarity, proximity and cultural closeness between those judging the deservingness and those being judged... The fifth criterion, need, refers to the perceived financial destitution and/or poor health status of those seeking help." (Van Oorschot and Laenen, 2023: 6)

## Producerism and Moral Economy

Research on "producerism" offers important insights into welfare attitudes and populist politics. Guery et al. (2023) explain:

"Producerism suggests that workers are virtuous, being squeezed by non-productive 'parasites' above – bureaucrats, politicians, elites, bankers, and international capital – and below – immigrants, undeserving poor using benefits – who live off their labour." (Guery et al., 2023: 523)

The producerist framework divides society into:

"On the one hand, the 'productive members of society', portrayed as 'virtuous' producers and potential victims and, on the other hand, 'parasitical strata both above and below them in the social hierarchy', which include both 'economic and political elites' and 'the poor, unemployed, and those who receive social welfare'" (2024 study on Producerist Populist Attitudes: 3).

Producerism connects economic concerns with moral judgments:

"In producerism, wealth and social justice are not solely material entities but also depend on how individuals perceive and experience practices, objects, political causes and their interactions with others. This links producerism with the concept of 'moral economy', which has been extensively discussed in social sciences and, more recently, in political science" (2024 study on Producerist Populist Attitudes: 21).

The concept helps explain the complexity of welfare attitudes:

"Fairness, the authors explain, stipulates that the rewards from cooperation should be principally divided according to individual contribution... When someone violates the fairness rule for their own benefit, they are considered a 'cheater', often with commensurately severe consequences" (2024 study on Producerist Populist Attitudes: 21).

## Fairness and Reciprocity Principles

A key dimension of moral economy involves fairness in exchange:

"Fairness, the authors explain, stipulates that the rewards from cooperation should be principally divided according to individual contribution... When someone violates the fairness rule for their own benefit, they are considered a 'cheater', often with commensurately severe consequences." (Guery et al., 2023: 21)

## Authoritarian Values and Particularistic Deservingness

Busemeyer et al. (2023) find authoritarian values shape welfare attitudes:

"We argue that authoritarian values translate into particularistic deservingness conceptions, which implies a preference for the punishment of out-groups that are perceived to break with the social norm to be 'hard-working' and seek paid employment. In this way, the unemployed get often associated with 'lazy free-riders' that are unwilling to work as opposed to otherwise 'hard-working citizens' that are unable to work due to old age, disability or sickness." (Busemeyer et al., 2023: 82-83)

They define a specific welfare preference bundle: "We define the policy mix described above – strong support for workfare, moderate support only for 'deserving' recipients of social transfers, and low support for social investment – as a particularistic-authoritarian type of welfare state. First, building on the welfare state literature (e.g. Beramendi et al. 2015), the particularistic dimension of this preference structure stems from narrow conceptions of welfare deservingness, which exclude the unemployed and poor from entitlements to social transfers, as they are considered to break with the social norm to be 'hard-working'." (Busemeyer & Rathgeb, 2022: 85)

## Welfare Chauvinism

Welfare chauvinism represents an important mechanism linking welfare institutions to populist politics. The welfare state has become a site of tension with immigration: "The welfare state has been pointed out as a site where there is tension between welfare and immigration, as immigrants are deemed newcomers and 'lack' contribution to the system based on reciprocity. It often develops into welfare chauvinism that supports social protection but contends that access to welfare services should be limited to native-born citizens."

Status-anxious workers may support stricter welfare requirements as a way of distinguishing themselves: "Status-anxious individuals thus seek to distinguish themselves from these groups to validate themselves. They may hence draw boundaries based on characteristics that allow 'members of other groups to be [seen as] deficient in respect to the criteria they value most'." (Im and Komp-Leukkunen, 2021)

Unemployed workers may become targets of this boundary-setting: "We think that unemployed workers may be one social group which status-anxious workers may seek to distinguish themselves from. Unemployed workers are generally viewed unfavourably because employment is frequently considered as a marker of individuals' place in society."

This helps explain welfare chauvinism among automation-exposed workers: "For automation, most routine workers may find economic concerns less salient than status ones because they manage to avoid unemployment (Kurer, 2020). They may hence support workfare because they prioritise their status concerns." (Im and Komp-Leukkunen, 2021)

## Othering as Response to Welfare Stigma

An essential theoretical mechanism connecting economic change to political attitudes involves social distancing or "othering" – the process by which individuals seek to maintain status by differentiating themselves from lower-status groups.

Van Hootegem (2023) describes this psychological process: "'Othering', sometimes referred to as self-group distancing, encompasses a process whereby individuals increasingly differentiate and demarcate between groups, and distance themselves ('the deserving') from those with a similar social status who are considered to be immoral and undeserving of acquiring public resources."

Patrick's (2016) research demonstrates how welfare recipients often engage in "othering" as a response to stigma:

"While there were some signs of resistance to the dominant 'scrounger' narrative, what was most notable in this research was the 'othering' in which so many of the participants engaged, where they shored up their own deservingness to benefits via a critique of some 'other' deemed less deserving and whose behaviour was characterised as more problematic."

This othering reinforces rather than challenges the dominant negative narrative: "Through their engagement in 'othering', itself a response to the 'othering' they experience as members of a stigmatised and presumed deficit population, out-of-work benefit claimants are themselves re-circulating and extending the reach of the 'scrounger' narrative. As the 'othered' also 'other', the framing consensus on 'welfare' is further embedded and strengthened, in ways that reduce scope for an alternative narrative or solidaristic challenge to the status quo" (Patrick, 2016).

Patrick interprets this behavior as a defensive form of citizenship engagement: "Arguably, this 'othering' is best understood as an admittedly defensive form of citizenship engagement (Ellison, 2000) and claim-making, which sees claimants make use of the dominant narrative as a discursive resource (Fohrbeck et al, 2014) that can be deployed to shore up their own deservingness and entitlement to social welfare."

## Last-Place Aversion and "Kicking Down"

A related theoretical perspective examines how people avoid identifying with lower-status groups:

"In contrast to what simple 'winner/loser' dichotomies suggest, this literature often discusses conservative-authoritarian voters as being 'a few rungs up' from the bottom of the social ladder, with last-place-aversion in fact making them prone to emphasizing and legitimizing hierarchies of various types towards the lowest-placed (income, ethnic, etc.) groups in society." (Häusermann et al., 2023: 15)

This can lead to what scholars call "kicking down" on those perceived as below oneself:

"The study finds strong evidence for the 'kicking down' mechanism, where welfare recipients who experience exclusion or misrecognition often justify their own deservingness by blaming groups perceived as less deserving ('exclude them – rather than me'). These recipients apply the same deservingness criteria to others that they feel the state has unfairly applied to them." (Wagner, 2022: 3)

The motivation appears to be maintaining positive self-image: "While 'kicking down' is the most common reaction among the manual workers in the data set, some interviewees react inversely by challenging the norms enacted by the state and proposing categorically different norms, including solidaristic and universalistic visions of welfare access." (Wagner, 2022: 3)

## Universalism vs. Particularism: Enke's Moral Geography

Benjamin Enke's research identifies universalism-particularism as a fundamental dimension of moral psychology with profound implications for welfare attitudes and electoral geography.

**Core Definition:** "Universalism is formalized as the slope of altruism as a function of social distance — a full universalist exhibits altruism that is invariant to social distance, whereas a particularist's altruism decreases with distance." (Enke et al., 2023: 2). More concretely: "A particularist or communal morality emphasizes relationship-specific obligations (loyalty and treating in-group members well), whereas a universalist morality emphasizes equal treatment and impartiality." (Enke et al., 2023: 1)

**Electoral Geography:** Enke et al. (2023) provide evidence that spatial variation in universalism predicts political outcomes better than income or education: "Our results thus suggest that a considerable fraction of the geographic political divide may result from disagreement over universalist versus particularist moral ideals." The correlation is striking: "The correlation between vote shares and universalism that we identify is substantially stronger than those relating vote shares to log median household income (r = 0.05) or share of population with a college degree (r = 0.14)." (Enke et al., 2023: 11)

**Interaction with Status Anxiety:** Enke's (2020) research demonstrates that individuals with more universalistic moral values show greater resilience against status threats, placing less emphasis on relative group position. In contrast, those with particularistic moral frameworks show heightened sensitivity to status threats, as group position constitutes a core moral concern. This helps explain why status anxiety so reliably triggers welfare chauvinism — the shift from universalistic to particularistic moral framing is psychologically proximate to perceived relative decline.

**Political Polarization Implications:** Häusermann et al. (2023) propose universalism-particularism as a key frame for understanding contemporary political divides: "The emergence of sociocultural dimensions of inequality on the political agenda has not necessarily crowded out economic inequality, but has extended the 'egalitarian' programme of the 'new left' to novel constituencies and beneficiaries." This suggests the universalism-particularism divide restructures, rather than replaces, class-based coalitions.
