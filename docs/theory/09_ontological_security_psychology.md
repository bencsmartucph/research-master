# Ontological Security, Existential Anxiety & Psychology

> **Context Abstract:** This module examines ontological security—the fundamental security of being and stable identity—as a dimension of economic vulnerability distinct from material hardship or status concerns. The core argument is that rapid economic and social change threatens citizens' ability to maintain a coherent sense of self, creating existential anxiety that populist narratives exploit by offering fantasies of restoration and control. This framework explains why traditional economic policies struggle to address populist appeal.

---

## Ontological Security: Definition and Dimensions

Multiple sources highlight the importance of deeper psychological dimensions of economic vulnerability beyond material concerns and relative status positioning. The concept of "ontological security" - the security of being and having a stable sense of identity - emerges as a critical factor in explaining political responses to economic change.

Browning (2018) defines ontological security as "fundamentally concerned with people's ability to 'go on' with their everyday lives without slipping into melancholic or psychotic states."

Metten and Bayerlein expand this concept to identify two key dimensions of "existential anxiety" (their adaptation of ontological insecurity):

"Taking into account the early and later receptions of ontological security, two analytical dimensions emerge that constitute this interaction. First, an intersubjective or exogenous dimension, where individuals 'are concerned with maintaining a consistent notion of self to enhance their ontological security with other[s]'... Second, an intra-subjective or endogenous dimension, in which the state serves 'as a provider of ontological security for its citizens'" (Metten and Bayerlein, p. 7)

## Globalization as Ontological Threat

Several researchers identify globalization, automation, and rapid social change as sources of ontological insecurity. According to Metten and Bayerlein:

"The perceived disruption of the traditional order, the impression of chaos, and the threat of the 'other' links the concept of ontological insecurity to globalisation processes, as globalisation 'challenges simple definitions of who we are and where we come from'" (Metten and Bayerlein, p. 6)

Browning (2018) similarly argues that Brexit was a response to ontological insecurities generated by rapid social and economic changes:

"For McKenzie (2017: 277) the post-referendum popularisation of the term the 'left behind' utterly 'misunderstands and underestimates the depth and intensity of the devastation that has been experienced by working-class people, their communities and their identities for over 30 years'." (Browning, 2018, p. 11)

Metten and Bayerlein identify how anxiety can be independent of fixed attitudes: "People with high levels of anxiety are not per se ideologically close to the anti-globalisation policy position of RWPPs but can be mobilised to vote for these parties if RWPPs are able to fuel their existential anxieties without necessarily changing their globalisation attitudes. In that regard the vote choice becomes not a question of attitude but of anxiety."

## Anxiety as Political Mobilizer

Importantly, anxiety can mobilize individuals politically independent of their fixed attitudes: "People with high levels of anxiety are not per se ideologically close to the anti-globalisation policy position of RWPPs but can be mobilised... In that regard the vote choice becomes not a question of attitude but of anxiety." (Metten & Bayerlein, 2023: 12)

Globalization and rapid social change can trigger anxiety that is then exploited by populists. According to Metten and Bayerlein: "By occupying globalisation issues and defining globalisation in and by itself as existential threats to society, culture, religion, and tradition, populists securitise globalisation processes and stress the need for quick action to halt allegedly dangerous societal changes. A need for action that is—for selfish reasons—not addressed by the 'ruling elite' of the political system, but only by the RWPP."

## Fantasy and Fulfillment in Populist Politics

Populist politics operates through fantasies that promise fulfillment and the restoration of control. Browning (2018) argues: "The paper argues that while the promise of closure central to populist fantasies that have underpinned much of the politics of Brexit has been emotionally seductive and politically mobilising it is also fundamentally problematic, offering impossible promises of fulfilment that may therefore only end up further enhancing feelings of disillusionment, alienation and ontological anxiety amongst those to whom they appeal."

These fantasies are not simply delusions but serve important psychological functions: "Such fantasies are not 'unreal' – as in popular understandings of the word – but rather help 'connect subjects to social orders by arousing desire and channelling it'." (Browning, 2018: 13)

Brexit illustrates how populist fantasies operate in practice: "The Leave campaign was built around a series of (populist) fantasy narratives promising freedom and liberty, sovereignty and control, subjectivity and agency that tapped into signifiers embedded within national narratives of self-identity." (Browning, 2018: 16)

When these fantasies fail to deliver, blame is redirected: "If Brexit 'fails', if we cannot 'have our cake and eat it', it will not be because Brexit was ultimately invested with impossible dreams, but because it was thwarted by saboteurs." (Browning, 2018: 25)

## Loneliness and Social Isolation

A growing body of research links social isolation and loneliness to political vulnerability. Langenkamp & Bienstman (2022) note:

"Being unresolved, loneliness is associated with numerous emotional and psychological outcomes. Among others, lonely individuals are more likely to desire shared identity, community, and reaffiliation (Qualter et al., 2015), while they also tend to suffer from increased social anxiousness, more negative expectations of future events, increased fear of being negatively perceived by others, and lower social trust (Cacioppo et al., 2006; Rotenberg et al., 2010)."

## Emotions as Political Mediators

Research increasingly recognizes emotions as crucial mediators between economic conditions and political attitudes, with particular attention to anger, anxiety, and fear.

Rhodes-Purdy et al. (2021) emphasize the role of anger as particularly relevant for populist attitudes: "Unlike fear, anger is generally seen as an 'approach' emotion, causing citizens to participate in politics; it also leads individuals to rely more heavily on their pre-existing beliefs, rather than seek out new information. Anger encourages individuals to punish those they deem responsible for wrongdoing as anger is commonly rooted in perceptions of intentional wrongdoing or unfairness."

They further note that "Additionally, angry individuals tend to interpret all undesirable events in their environment as resulting from intentional wrongdoing and have an increased desire to punish anyone whose conduct they disapprove of."

Auer and Freitag (2023) find that responsibility attribution determines when anger undermines political support: "As a specifically moral emotion, anger is linked to the condemnation of the alleged perpetrator(s) of a threat-induced norm violation and to punitive attitudes and behaviors toward them."

They argue: "Perceived mismanagement of threatening situations is also associated with a malfunctioning of the principal–agent relationship. As a consequence, threat-induced anger promotes populist attitudes and is associated with a decrease in democratic support." (Auer & Freitag, 2023: 1103)

## Life Dissatisfaction and Populist Attitudes

A broader understanding connects general life dissatisfaction to political preferences. Lindholm and Rapeli (2023) explore how general life satisfaction relates to populist attitudes: "The broader notion of self-centered discontent, that is, life dissatisfaction, has received very little attention. This is a remarkable gap in the literature since people may not be able to identify the exact reason why they are dissatisfied or not feeling well."

Their findings show that lower subjective well-being is associated with populist attitudes even after controlling for demographic factors and socioeconomic status: "The (standardized) relationship between life dissatisfaction and populist attitudes is stronger than the influence of societal economic discontent." (Lindholm and Rapeli, 2023, p. 474)

Lindholm and Rapeli propose several mechanisms connecting low subjective well-being to populist attitudes:

1. **Utility mechanism**: Low subjective well-being serves as a signal that the status quo is undesirable, making citizens more receptive to populist calls for change.
2. **Emotional mechanism**: Negative states make people more open to changing their political predispositions.
3. **Social identity mechanism**: Low subjective well-being promotes in-group/out-group thinking and blame attribution.

## Institutional Trust and Ontological Security

Metten and Bayerlein identify trust in institutions as crucial: "Social programs that reduce such anxieties by maintaining economic security and institutional trust may diminish the appeal of populist narratives that frame economic changes as existential threats."
