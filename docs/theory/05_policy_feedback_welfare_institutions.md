# Policy Feedback Effects & Welfare Institutions

> **Context Abstract:** This module examines how welfare policies shape subsequent political dynamics through policy feedback effects—self-reinforcing or self-undermining mechanisms. The core argument is that policies create experiences and convey messages about deservingness, social worth, and government responsibility that fundamentally shape citizens' political attitudes and preferences. This framework connects material and interpretive effects of welfare design to political sustainability.

---

## Policy Feedback Theory: Core Mechanisms

Policy feedback effects operate through several mechanisms. A central theoretical perspective explains how welfare institutions shape subsequent politics through feedback effects. This approach, associated with scholars like Pierson (1994, 2001) and Mettler (2002), suggests that "policies create experiences that influence citizens' political attitudes and capacity for action."

Pitts and Winter (2024) elaborate: "Policy feedback effects theory proposes that existing policies shape subsequent political dynamics and preferences through various mechanisms. The paper distinguishes between 'self-reinforcing' and 'self-undermining' feedback effects. Self-reinforcing effects occur when policies create constituencies that support their continuation, while self-undermining effects arise when policies generate opposition to themselves."

Policy feedback effects operate through multiple channels:

1. **Resource effects**: Policies distribute resources that enhance or diminish citizens' capacity for political action
2. **Interpretive effects**: Policies convey messages about target groups' social worth and government's role
3. **Institutional effects**: Policies create administrative arrangements that shape political possibilities

## Positive Feedback Effects

Positive feedback can build broader constituencies. Mettler (2002) describes: "Mettler (2002) describes how the US-American GI-Bill has led to 'inclusion effects' among its recipients, namely to increased levels of social and public participation."

Gingrich and Ansell (2023) explain: "welfare states not only are the product of political struggles but also are crucial determinants of welfare politics in their own right" (p. 1628). This suggests that "welfare states not only are the product of political struggles but also are crucial determinants of welfare politics in their own right." They build on this tradition by focusing on how policies modify the experience of risk rather than simply creating beneficiary groups. They argue that policies can modify the distribution of risk in society, creating a more uniform "social risk" that reduces the importance of individual-level risk factors in preference formation.

## Negative and Self-Undermining Feedback

But feedback can also be negative or self-undermining. Bergman (2023) notes: "Finally, the implementation of such policies that intend to 'cushion' radical right support, might have an adverse feedback effect of 'catalyzing' greater support for radical right parties. Should policies favour socio-demographic groups that potential populist radical right voters displease of, such as those not affiliated with nostalgic views of the welfare state like immigrants or labour market outsiders or those not involved in 'producerist' activities, like the unemployed, voters might turn to the populist radical right to reorient policies in favour of those currently employed."

Bergman further explains that "More formally, this suggests that some policies might have a 'self-undermining' feedback effect, whereby high levels of intervention might lead to public opinion (and voting behaviour) to decrease interventionism and vice versa."

## Downward and Upward Spirals of Solidarity

Wagner (2022, 2023) describes these contrasting mechanisms:

"I propose that on the one hand, welfare cuts (Pierson 1994) and increases in welfare conditionality as they typically comes with 'activation' strategies (Bonoli 2010; Dwyer et al. 2022) may have spurred fears of entitlement loss and of social exclusion among recipients. In reaction, many tend to harshen their views on entitlements for those who are seen as even less deserving than themselves in what we could call a 'downward spiral' of solidarity erosion."

In contrast: "On the other hand, social investment policies (Morel et al. 2012) provide active resources enabling recipients to improve their human capital, labor market position, and generally their capacity to socially participate, what can lead to experiences of social inclusion (Mettler 2002). In reaction, I hypothesize that there is a tendency to perceive that 'others should be offered the same opportunity', showcasing the formation of more inclusive welfare attitudes even towards outgroups in an 'upward spiral' of solidarity bonds."

This distinction is fundamental: "My basic theory is that welfare recipients learn social norms that govern welfare access during their own welfare experiences and subsequently mirror these norms in their attitudes towards other recipient groups." (Wagner, 2023: 11)

## Welfare Recipients' Paradoxical Attitudes

Van Hootegem, Abts, and Meuleman (2021) identify a paradox where welfare criticism comes from those who benefit most:

"Multiple studies report that while individuals in socio-economically vulnerable positions are more supportive of social redistribution (Achterberg and Houtman, 2009; Achterberg et al., 2011; Jaeger, 2006), they appear at the same time less convinced that the welfare state is capable of achieving just economic, moral and social outcomes."

They explain the duality: "The duality between direct effects of self-interest and indirect effects of relative deprivation is crucial in understanding welfare state criticism of lower social groups. When only total effects are investigated, misleading conclusions could be drawn, as they do not provide insight into these patterns."

However, "Individuals in a vulnerable position are indirectly more critical of the welfare state, they are directly often more positive, as they benefit from extensive redistribution and a strong welfare state."

## Trust-Performance Recursive Loops

Research reveals potential downward spirals in the relationship between welfare performance and political trust. Haugsgjerd and Kumlin (2020) demonstrate: "The results indeed suggest that political trust is both a consequence and a cause of welfare state evaluations. First, we find that initial levels (t1) of welfare state evaluations affect changes (t1-t2) in political trust. That is, people respond to low levels of welfare state evaluations by becoming less politically trustful."

"The implication is a 'downbound spiral' in which malperformance sets in motion a broader and more long-lived process than implied by past research: performance hurts trust, which in turn makes future performance evaluations more negative." (Haugsgjerd and Kumlin, 2020)

## Welfare State Moderation of Economic Insecurity

Vlandas and Halikiopoulou (2022) argue for institutional buffering:

"The effect of social risk exposure on radical right voting is contingent on the generosity of social policies targeting these particular social risks." This means "One important implication of our argument is that societal anxieties in themselves are not enough to explain under what circumstances insecure people are more or less likely to vote for far right parties. If we are right, the answer also lies in institutions. Governments can play a significant role in shaping political outcomes based on the policies that they implement."

They suggest broad coalition building: "Welfare state policies may serve as an effective remedy for far right party support precisely because they deter not only the most vulnerable, but a broad range of distinct social groups thus preventing the far right from forging voter alliances and extending support beyond its secure voting base."

## Benefit Retrenchment, Austerity, and Populist Backlash

Traditional compensatory welfare responses — early retirement, public service expansion, labor market regulation — have been weakened across OECD countries, with measurable populist consequences.

Foster and Frieden document the scale of retrenchment: "In most European jurisdictions, policymakers have weakened unemployment protection by reducing income replacement rates and shortening the length of benefits. In 1975, the average replacement rate of unemployment insurance in the OECD was 65%; by 1995, it had fallen to 55%. Since the beginning of the 21st century, replacement levels have fallen even further."

The electoral consequences are substantial: "We find that cuts to unemployment benefits are associated with greater support for populist parties in general and leftwing parties in particular. A 20% cut in labor market spending per unit of unemployment from 1995-levels is associated with a six-percentage point higher likelihood of supporting a populist party and a three-percentage point higher likelihood of supporting a radical leftwing party." This effect is strongest among the most economically vulnerable: "This effect is more pronounced for individuals who have experienced three or more months of unemployment and among those who report their household financial situation as 'very difficult'." (Foster & Frieden)

**Fetzer (2019) — Austerity and Brexit:** Fetzer provides perhaps the most powerful causal identification of welfare retrenchment effects: "This paper provides evidence suggesting that austerity policies from late 2010 onward are key to understanding Brexit. The welfare reforms are a strong driving factor behind the growing support for the populist UKIP party in the wake of the EU referendum, contributed to the development of broader anti-establishment preferences, and are strongly associated with higher levels of support for Leave."

**Haugsgjerd & Kumlin (2020) — The Downbound Trust-Performance Spiral:** Welfare cuts initiate a recursive spiral: poor institutional performance → eroding trust → reduced political engagement → further policy deterioration. This "downbound spiral" is the self-undermining feedback dynamic operating at its most damaging.

### Limits of Compensation: Why Material Transfers May Be Insufficient

Kurer (2020) argues that "When relative societal decline rather than material hardship are at the heart of socially conservative resentment, traditional welfare policy may be an insufficient response to satisfy exposed workers and hence an ineffective remedy to counter the ascent of right-wing populist movements."

Gingrich (2019) finds that workers highly exposed to automation "are not less likely to vote for populist parties in countries with more generous early retirement policies and in-kind spending, nor in countries with more protective labor market regulation. This is a concerning finding, as it suggests that compensating workers is not effective at preventing their turn to protest voting." (cited in Gallego & Kurer, 2022)

Vlandas and Halikiopoulou (2022) introduce a productive distinction: welfare policies can be *compensatory* (cushioning after risk materializes, e.g. unemployment benefits) or *protective* (preventing risk, e.g. EPL and minimum wages). The difference matters politically because compensatory transfers trigger deservingness scrutiny while protective regulation reinforces felt belonging in the labor market.
