# Automation & Technological Change

> **Context Abstract:** This module examines automation as a distinctive form of economic vulnerability, where "routineness" and "codifiability" of job tasks determine susceptibility rather than skill levels. The core argument distinguishes between anticipated hardship (fear of future decline) and experienced hardship (actual job loss), finding that anticipated automation risk—not unemployment—drives support for right-wing populism. This connects to broader debates about status anxiety versus material hardship as drivers of political realignment.

---

## Defining Automation Vulnerability

Automation represents a distinctive form of economic vulnerability. Pitts and Winter (2024) explain that "For the likes of Autor et al (2003) and Cortes (2016), routineness defines a job's susceptibility to automation. Rather than a synonym for simplicity, routineness describes compliance with a set of tasks that, whilst initially often the result of considerable skill and training, can thereafter be performed by following a series of 'rules and procedures'. This 'codifiability', suggest Acemoglu and Autor (2011), is the aspect that renders routine work susceptible to automation, whether it occurs in factories, in offices or elsewhere."

The task-based approach distinguishes between routine and non-routine tasks as the key factor in determining automation risk: "For the likes of Autor et al (2003) and Cortes (2016), routineness defines a job's susceptibility to automation. Rather than a synonym for simplicity, routineness describes compliance with a set of tasks that, whilst initially often the result of considerable skill and training, can thereafter be performed by following a series of 'rules and procedures'." (Pitts & Winter, 2024: 6)

## Technological Displacement: Gradual Not Sudden

Importantly, automation affects employment gradually rather than causing immediate mass layoffs. As Kurer (2020) observes: "Routine workers' situation in an increasingly automated world of work thus provides an ideal case to disentangle the political consequences of fearing as opposed to experiencing economic adversity."

Pitts and Winter (2024) further note: "routine workers do not experience the process of technological unemployment through direct, immediate exits and lay-offs, so much as a gradual decline in new entrants to replace natural attrition in the workforce."

## Empirical Evidence: Automation and Right-Wing Populist Support

A growing body of empirical literature finds that losers of technological change are disproportionately represented among those turning against the political status quo. Gallego & Kurer (2022) synthesize this evidence:

"A rapidly growing empirical literature finds that losers of technological change are disproportionately represented among those who are turning against the political status quo. Gingrich (2019) uses International Social Survey data and finds that workers in occupations with high RTI [Routine Task Intensity] are more likely to vote for the populist right and for the mainstream left. Im et al. (2019) show in a cross-sectional analysis covering Western Europe that a measure of occupational automation risk is associated with voting for right-wing populist parties among citizens who are 'just about managing' financially. This effect is not observed for those who already find it difficult or very difficult to live on their current income. Similarly, a panel-data analysis by Kurer (2020) covering Germany, the United Kingdom, and Switzerland shows that routine workers who are strongly exposed to automation but still manage to cling to their threatened jobs are particularly likely to vote for right-wing populist parties."

A connection between RTI and voting for populist parties is also supported by Milner (2021), who finds in both regional- and individual-level analyses that RTI is positively associated with populist voting; by Dal Bó et al. (2021) for the case of Sweden; and by cross-national studies that do not focus on technology but use RTI as a control variable (Gidron & Hall 2017, Guiso et al. 2018, Oesch & Rennwald 2018, Inglehart & Norris 2019).

This basic finding is confirmed by analyses about the impact of robotization on voting behavior using International Federation of Robotics data. Based on a regional-level analysis, Frey et al. (2018) document that support for Donald Trump in the 2016 US election was significantly higher in local labor markets more exposed to robotization. Caselli et al. (2021) document an increased vote share for the far right in Italian municipalities more exposed to robotization.

## The Distinction: Anticipated vs. Experienced Hardship

The critical distinction between anticipated and experienced automation risk reveals important political dynamics. Kurer (2020) distinguishes: "The materialisation of anticipated hardship through impoverishment or unemployment, meanwhile, does not appear as a driver of support for socially conservative or right-wing populist parties, but rather 'pro-welfare' parties."

This means that the strongest political responses occur among those who are threatened but not yet displaced. Kurer elaborates: "As a consequence, we might not observe the strongest political reaction among the hardest-hit but rather among those who are most concerned about their economic well-being and future prospects in the labor market."

## Automation and Status Decline

Beyond immediate job loss, automation affects workers through status anxiety. Kurer (2020) argues that "routine workers who are threatened by automation have to cope with parallel challenges to both their material and psychological well-being." These psychological challenges include status anxiety and fears about social positioning.

Im and Komp-Leukkunen (2021) elaborate on how automation affects status: "Kurer (2020) argues that routine workers face the threat of social decline even if they cling onto their routine jobs, because societal recognition and status go hand in hand with the level of demand for jobs. When demand for non-routine jobs outstrips demand for routine ones, the status of routine work declines vis-à-vis the status of non-routine work. Automation thus 'reshapes the employment structure and hence the relative importance and value attached to different kinds of work'."

This creates patterns of winners and losers:

"Political economic studies on the 'great U-turn' in the development of inequalities (i.e. the reversal of the declining trend to a growth of inequality after the 1970s) have launched a massive research agenda on the re-emergence of income, wealth, occupational, educational and risk inequalities in Western economies since the 1990s." (Häusermann et al., 2023: 4)

## Relative Economic Decline and Political Responses

One key insight is that it's not just actual economic hardship that drives political attitudes:

"I argue that individual employment trajectories and relative shifts in the social hierarchy are key to better understand recent political disruptions. A perception of relative economic decline among politically powerful groups—not their impoverishment—drives support for conservative and, especially, right-wing populist parties." (Kurer, 2020: 1798)

"Automation has contributed to a decline in the overall demand for mid-skilled work." (Gingrich, 2019: 3)

"Existing mid-skilled workers thus face the risk of occupational downgrading (as upgrading through the career cycle is limited) while for new cohorts of workers mid-level skills no longer provide a secure route to a middle-class status." (Gingrich, 2019: 3)

## RBTC (Routine-Biased Technological Change) Theory

"RBTC theory posits that technology substitutes for labor performing repetitive and codifiable (routine) tasks that are generally concentrated in medium-skilled jobs. According to Cirillo (2018: 40), the empirical literature seems to find more evidence of polarisation in both Europe and the US, and this view is affirmed by Biagi and Sebastian's (2020) meta-review of the literature." (Im and Komp-Leukkunen, 2021: 457)
