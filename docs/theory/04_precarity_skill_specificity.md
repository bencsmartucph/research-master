# Precarity, Skill Specificity & Occupational Risk

> **Context Abstract:** This module examines how skill transferability, occupational specificity, and multi-dimensional precarity shape economic vulnerability. The core argument is that vulnerability varies along a continuum determined by skill level, task specificity, and employment stability, with precarity having distinct dimensions (work-related precarity, tenure precarity, financial precarity) that show differential relationships to populist attitudes. These factors create age-differentiated risk profiles requiring varied policy responses.

---

## The Continuum of Occupational Unemployment Risk

Economic vulnerability varies significantly based on skill specificity and transferability. Bergman (2022) notes that "The underlying risk of occupational unemployment can thus be thought of as a continuum (Marx and Picot, 2020) that varies by skill level (Iversen and Soskice, 2001), skill specificity (Cusack et al., 2006), and the tasks performed at work."

Workers with highly specific skills face particular challenges: "Workers in professions requiring specific skills are likely to face greater labour market risks. A petroleum engineer in a petrochemical surveying firm might have skills that would be useless in other professions."

The vulnerability also varies by age cohort, as Gingrich (2019) observes: "For older workers, who are unlikely to retrain and join the public sector workforce, early retirement benefits and high EPL are particularly important." Meanwhile, "For younger workers, entering the labor force in an era of automation support for greater job opportunities (rather than compensation) is more crucial."

## Skill Level and Education Effects

Education level and skill level significantly influence vulnerability to automation and political responses. Kurer (2020) notes transitions such as "Moving from routine to nonroutine cognitive jobs," "Moving from routine to nonroutine manual jobs," or becoming "unemployed after holding routine jobs." Furthermore, "College education strongly decreases likelihood of supporting radical-right parties among those experiencing status discordance." (Kurer & van Staalduinen, 2022)

## Precarity as Multidimensional Insecurity

Precarity represents a multidimensional form of economic vulnerability that extends beyond employment status. Zhrnov (2024) explains: "Expanding on this, we have operationalized the sociological notion of precarity that was popularized by Standing (2011) and is now widely used in sociological research (Parfitt and Barnes, 2020) to examine the potential effect of precarity on populist voting, where precarity refers to 'a generalized set of social conditions and an associated sense of insecurity'."

"While this concept originates in the field of the sociology of work, its relevance and implications are far broader given the intersections between work and financial insecurity that individuals face in current times."

Research distinguishes between different forms of precarity, each with distinct effects on political attitudes. Zhrnov (2024) identifies that "Work-related precarity, particularly precarity at work (working conditions), is significantly associated with populist outlook, while precarity of tenure shows a weaker relationship when both are considered together."

Additionally, "There is a positive significant association between financial insecurity and all dimensions of populist outlook across all countries studied..."

## The "Unbundling" and Individual Uncertainty

The "unbundling" of production and increased individualized uncertainty creates new risk patterns. As Baldwin (2016, cited by Milner's notes) points out, globalization's impact is increasingly individual and less predictable:

"globalization's impact on national economies is less predictable and more individual. The fact that it is driven by information and communication technology means the impact is also more sudden and less controllable. All of which means that there is more uncertainty and insecurity at individual level."

## Anxious Meritocrats and Economic Anxiety

The concept of "anxious meritocrats" captures how economic vulnerability affects political attitudes:

"We anticipate that 'economically anxious' meritocrats, that is, those individuals who hold meritocratic norms but are worried about their own household's economic prospects, are the most open to this pro-protection trade prompt. The absence of social mobility is exceptionally disquieting to these individuals." (Ballard-Rosa, Goldstein, & Rudra, 2024)

## Middle-Class Vulnerability and Downward Mobility

Research on middle-class vulnerability highlights the importance of economic stability:

"In addition to income, economic stability and a secure lifestyle can be seen as one of the main characteristics of the middle class. As Banerjee & Duflo (2008, p. 26) put it, 'nothing seems more middle class than the fact of having a steady well-paying job.'" (Derndorfer, 2025: 4)

The risk of downward mobility particularly affects political behavior:

"As people are generally loss averse (Kahneman & Tversky, 1984), losing one's position and moving down the social ladder has more far-reaching consequences than maintaining or improving one's standing in society." (Derndorfer, 2025: 2)
