# Populism, Welfare Chauvinism & Right-Wing Mobilization

> **Context Abstract:** This module examines populist parties' appeals to economically vulnerable and status-anxious voters, focusing on how populists offer remedies for status anxiety rather than material hardship, and how welfare chauvinism (restricting benefits to native citizens) reflects both status concerns and moral boundary-drawing. The core argument is that populist appeal rests on offering fantasies of restoration and scapegoating rather than effective economic solutions.

---

## Status Remedies vs. Economic Relief

Right-wing populist parties offer specific responses to status anxiety rather than material hardship. Kurer (2020) explains: "What they [right-wing populist parties] offer is not primarily relief from economic stress but remedies against status anxiety by promoting the values of an idealized past where routine workers were a valued and crucial pillar of society."

He contrasts with other approaches: "What differentiates them from left-authoritarian parties that might purport a similarly nostalgic worldview is their derogatory rhetoric toward migrants as well as 'undeserving' or 'lazy' parts of the native population, both of which offer an obvious lower status target at which to direct hostility in an attempt of comparative status enhancement."

## Nostalgia and Idealized Pasts

This nostalgia connects to Fenger's 'welfare nostalgia' perspective (2018), which "holds that social benefits should be reserved for traditional workers ('modernization losers') based on traditional economic and family patterns at the expense of migrants, women, the self-employed, and temporary workers."

## Securitization of Globalization

Metten & Bayerlein (2023) describe how populists exploit ontological insecurity: "By occupying globalisation issues and defining globalisation in and by itself as existential threats to society, culture, religion, and tradition, populists securitise globalisation processes and stress the need for quick action to halt allegedly dangerous societal changes. A need for action that is—for selfish reasons—not addressed by the 'ruling elite' of the political system, but only by the RWPP."

## Fantasy Narratives and Emotional Mobilization

Populist politics operates through fantasies that promise fulfillment. Browning (2018) analyzes Brexit rhetoric: "The Leave campaign was built around a series of (populist) fantasy narratives promising freedom and liberty, sovereignty and control, subjectivity and agency that tapped into signifiers embedded within national narratives of self-identity."

The psychological function: "Such fantasies are not 'unreal' – as in popular understandings of the word – but rather help 'connect subjects to social orders by arousing desire and channelling it'." (Browning, 2018: 13)

When fantasies fail to deliver, blame is redirected: "If Brexit 'fails', if we cannot 'have our cake and eat it', it will not be because Brexit was ultimately invested with impossible dreams, but because it was thwarted by saboteurs." (Browning, 2018: 25)

## In-Group/Out-Group Construction

Hameleers, Reinemann, Schmuck, and Fawzi (2019) identify the crucial mechanisms: "Integrating the theoretical frameworks of social identity, blame attribution, and negative stereotyping, the crucial mechanisms behind the effects of populist communication can be understood as the construction of a positive self-image of the blameless people opposed to a culprit out-group of the elites, or threatening others. Populist communication constructs a deprived in-group of ordinary people who are not deemed responsible for the collective crisis they are facing."

Message selection based on attitudes: "The various mechanisms discussed in this chapter can be reduced to one crucial factor: attitudinal congruence. Regarding selection, it can be expected that populist messages are only selected by people with prior attitudes congruent with populist communication." (Hameleers et al., 2019)

## Group-Based Economic Grievances

Ciccolini (2025) emphasizes the amplification of group losses: "Research in social and political psychology demonstrates that loss matters more politically when it is lived as a group experience (for a review, see Smith and Ortiz, 2001). This is because individuals tend to blame themselves for misfortunes that are confined to their own lives but criticize the system and react politically when facing group deprivation."

## Sociotropic Status Decline

Im and Komp-Leukkunen (2021) describe how individual status fears amplify with community decline: "Ballard-Rosa et al. (2020) demonstrate that Americans living in communities suffering from worsening economic hardship over the long term develop sociotropic feelings of status decline, even if they themselves do not suffer direct economic costs. Bromley-Davenport et al. (2018) also show that British citizens who live in regions that have suffered economic decline experience feelings of marginalisation and exclusion."

## Relative Group Gains and Outgroup Resentment

Green et al. (2017) argue: "We argue that perceptions of economic gains for 'out-groups' (Kinder and Kam 2009) – particularly relative to one's 'in-group' – are key to understanding the basis of economic grievances. Such relative gains motivate people to vote against the status quo, in favour of causes and campaigns that advocate improving the conditions of in-groups."

They add: "As Mutz and Mondak (1997, 286) put it, 'private economic experience is not, by itself, politically potent'; however, 'groups may serve as essential mediating entities, making it possible for people to politicize the economic interests of those like them without necessarily relying directly on personal economic experience'."

Social dominance orientation: "Social dominance theory predicts that some people have preferences for group-based dominance and social hierarchy – a preference for group-based inequalities maintained by hierarchy-enhancing ideologies and social policies (Ho et al. 2012; 2015; Mutz 2018; Sidanius and Pratto 1999). Social dominance orientation would predict opposition to affirmative action policies, which would be viewed as giving unfair economic advantages against a dominant group, and this increases when people feel threatened (Morrison and Ybarra 2008)." (Green et al., 2017: 324)

## Welfare Chauvinism as Status Boundary-Drawing

Welfare chauvinism represents an important mechanism linking status concerns to welfare politics. The welfare state provides both material support and a site of moral boundary-drawing. Welfare chauvinism—supporting redistribution only for native-born citizens—reflects both material and status concerns.

Im and Komp-Leukkunen (2021) explain how routine workers support stricter welfare boundaries: "For automation, most routine workers may find economic concerns less salient than status ones because they manage to avoid unemployment (Kurer, 2020). They may hence support workfare because they prioritise their status concerns."

The mechanism: "Status-anxious individuals thus seek to distinguish themselves from these groups to validate themselves. They may hence draw boundaries based on characteristics that allow 'members of other groups to be [seen as] deficient in respect to the criteria they value most'." (Im and Komp-Leukkunen, 2021)

## Welfare Attitudes and Outgroup Exclusion

Beyond material self-interest, welfare support becomes conditional on in-group membership: "Moreover, people may even resist spending that they could also benefit from, if 'out-groups' also gain from those shared resources." (Yeandle et al., 2024: 1069)

"Many poorer voters might not perceive themselves as beneficiaries of government spending, current or proposed, and will hence have less material reason to support redistribution." (Yeandle et al., 2024: 1067)

"Empirically, aggregate support for redistribution declines when individuals believe that minority groups are its primary beneficiaries and are disproportionately taking scarce resources." (Yeandle et al., 2024: 1069)

## Identity Switching and Cultural Politics

Bonomi et al. (2021) provide a sophisticated framework: voters can identify with either economic class or cultural group, with identity switching occurring when the relative salience of different conflicts changes. Their theory predicts that economic shocks disproportionately harming culturally conservative voters can trigger a switch to cultural identity, causing them to demand less redistribution despite their economic interests.

Key mechanisms:

1. **Belief Slanting:** Identification causes voters to slant their beliefs towards their chosen group's stereotype, exaggerating differences with outgroups.

2. **Shifting Policy Demands:** As voters switch from class to cultural identity, their demand for universalistic redistribution often decreases, partly because cultural identity can make voters more optimistic about their own future economic prospects and partly because it fosters a more communitarian outlook focused on the cultural in-group.

3. **Political Realignment:** This identity switching can drive political realignment, with lower-class conservative voters switching from left to right parties that emphasize cultural conservatism, even if those parties advocate for less redistribution.

## Misattribution and Blame Displacement

Gallego and Kurer (2022) identify why voters misattribute economic problems: "First, it may be more psychologically gratifying to attribute economic decline to globalization. Immigration and trade offer clear out-groups to mobilize against (migrants, China), and there are relatively straightforward policies to counteract them (borders, tariffs). Technological change is different. Seeing one's tasks performed by machines can be particularly hard on self-esteem. It may be more difficult psychologically (and practically) to mobilize against nonhumans than against human out-groups."

"Second, and most likely as a consequence of the first reason, political entrepreneurs (especially populist parties) supply discourses against immigration and trade that connect economic grievances to policy solutions. Parties contribute to voters' misattributions by making some explanations more cognitively available than others."

"Third, most likely as a consequence of the first two reasons, citizens are much more likely to encounter everyday examples of globalization (foreign goods, immigrants) than automation, making the former more cognitively available."
