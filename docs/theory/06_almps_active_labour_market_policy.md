# Active Labor Market Policies (ALMPs)

> **Context Abstract:** This module examines different types of active labor market policies—upskilling, employment assistance, occupation (skill maintenance), and incentive reinforcement—and how implementation quality, design features, and orientations shape their effectiveness and political effects. The core argument is that enabling, skill-enhancing ALMPs with respectful implementation produce wellbeing gains and inclusive solidarity, while punitive approaches generate stigma and defensive othering.

---

## ALMP Typology and Classification

Malo (2020) defines proactive labor market policies as those "not necessarily designed to react to a specific problem at a given moment in the short term, but to potentially pre-empt a wide range of problems or imbalances caused by economic shocks or other sources of unexpected changes."

Bonoli (2013) classifies ALMPs along two dimensions: their pro-market employment orientation and their human capital investment intensity. This framework produces four distinct types:

1. **Upskilling**: High human capital investment with strong pro-market orientation, providing vocational training to help people gain new skills or update obsolete ones
2. **Employment assistance**: Low human capital investment with strong pro-market orientation, including placement services and job search programs
3. **Occupation**: Human capital maintenance without strong pro-market emphasis, keeping jobless people active to prevent skill depletion
4. **Incentive reinforcement**: No human capital investment with strong pro-market emphasis, focusing on strengthening work incentives through benefit reductions

## Implementation Quality and Efficacy

Malo (2020) emphasizes implementation as crucial: "Policy implementation is found to be a key issue that has an impact on the efficacy of specific ALMPs and their aggregate impacts, and on the effects of the integrated approach between ALMPs and income support policies."

Several implementation factors are critical:

1. **Sustained funding**: "Funding stability during the business cycle increases the aggregate effectiveness of ALMPs and is a key proactive feature of ALMPs from an aggregate perspective."
2. **Administrative capacity and coordination**: Effective implementation requires sufficient administrative resources and coordination between different agencies.
3. **Tripartite social dialogue**: "Tripartite social dialogue seems an ideal instrument to find avenues to enhance the positive effects of these implementation variables and proactive features."

Malo's analysis emphasizes implementation as a crucial factor: "Implementation seems to be a key channel for enhancing proactive characteristics in labour market policies in developing and emerging countries as well as developed economies." (Malo, p. 17)

And more broadly: "Policy implementation is found to be a key issue that has an impact on the efficacy of specific ALMPs and their aggregate impacts, and on the effects of the integrated approach between ALMPs and income support policies." (Malo, p. 1)

## Three Competing Paradigms of ALMPs

Nicaise (2021) contrasts three competing paradigms that reflect different normative approaches to welfare:

1. **Neoliberal "making work pay" approach**:
"Neoliberal 'making work pay' policies are largely based on the assumption that individual behaviour can be 'steered' by financial incentives." (Nicaise, 2021, p. 223)

2. **Conservative "workfare" approach**:
Emphasizes that "a stronger work ethos needs to be inculcated through reduced rights and more duties." (Nicaise, 2021, p. 221)

3. **Social investment approach**:
"[E]mployability was defined as one dimension of human capabilities; and social investment as a strategy to extend human capabilities with the aim of implementing fundamental rights." (Nicaise, 2021, p. 227)

Nicaise's research challenges orthodox economic assumptions about the relationship between benefits and employment:

"At country level, a slight positive correlation was found between the coverage rate of job seekers by unemployment benefits and the rate of re-entry into work – even after controlling for other determinants. Likewise, a positive correlation was found between the replacement rate (in unemployment benefits) and the probability of re-entry into work." (Nicaise, 2021, p. 223)

## Dignity, Choice, and Respectful Treatment

This focus on implementation quality connects to Nicaise's findings about the importance of choice, voice, and respectful treatment in successful programs:

"Restoring decent rights in social protection and labour market services requires investment in awareness raising, accessibility for all citizens, free choice and respectful treatment of claimants." (Nicaise, 2021, p. 231)

## Differential Wellbeing Impacts of ALMP Types

Research shows different ALMP types have varied impacts on well-being and political attitudes. Heins (2023) finds: "While 'enabling' types of ALMP focusing on human capital development and programmes that emulate regular employment seem to have mainly wellbeing-enhancing effects, 'employment assistance' types of ALMP or those that do not take into account personal circumstances such as lone parenthood have no or even negative wellbeing effects."

Participant experiences differ significantly: "Recipients of wage subsidies and those in subsidised self-employment – who are thus gaining actual work experience – show significantly enhanced wellbeing while those in training programmes – which in theory should increase future job prospects but in practice might not – show a smaller increase."

These different experiences may have important political implications: "The findings from research on the wellbeing effects of ALMP indicate that enabling and skill-enhancing programmes leading to sustainable good quality employment, rather than serving as a condition for benefit receipt without any prospect of proper labour market integration are the way forward for a progressive labour market policy that takes wellbeing into account."

## The Punitive Trap: Conditionality and Sanctions

The negative consequences of punitive approaches are well-documented:

"The evidence on benefit sanctions and welfare conditionality is more unanimous, showing clear negative effects on wellbeing... The increased stigmatisation of the unemployed in a punitive activation context might be creating a 'culture of self-blame' that could lead to even more stress in the life of ALMP participants." (Heins, 2023: 516)

Quality of employment emerges as crucial: "The quality of employment, not just quantity, is crucial for wellbeing... 'Getting people off benefits and into low-paid, insecure and health-damaging work is not a desirable option' (Marmot, 2010)." (Heins, 2023: 518)

## Historical Evolution of ALMPs

Bonoli's (2013) historical analysis reveals counter-intuitive findings. Contrary to common assumptions about progressive development of social investment, "the social investment orientation of policy is not a recent phenomenon. On the contrary the notion that labour market policy should increase the re-employment chances of jobless people by investing in their human capital was probably stronger in the 1950s and 1960s than today, at least in those countries that intervened in this field (most notably Sweden)."

ALMPs evolved through three historical phases:

- **Post-war years (1950s-1960s)**: During economic growth and labor shortages, ALMPs focused on upskilling (strongest social investment orientation)
- **Employment crisis (mid-1970s to mid-1990s)**: During sluggish growth and industrial restructuring, ALMPs functioned primarily as alternatives to market employment
- **Activation turn (mid-1990s onward)**: With improved economic conditions, ALMPs emphasized employment assistance and reinforcement of work incentives, with declining emphasis on training
