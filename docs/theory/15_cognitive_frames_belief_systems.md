# Cognitive Frames, Belief Systems & Political Realignment

> **Context Abstract:** This module examines how individuals cognitively process and interpret economic vulnerability through misattribution, identity switching, and deeply embedded moral belief systems. It argues that political responses to economic change are mediated not only by material conditions and emotions but by prior belief frameworks—meritocracy, universalism versus particularism—and by how inequality's spatial segregation limits the visibility of its own scale. Understanding these cognitive-cultural mediators is essential for explaining why economically vulnerable voters sometimes support parties that appear to act against their material interests, and for designing policies that can break that cycle.

---

## I. Misattribution Theory: Why Automation Gets Blamed on Immigration

Economic anxieties that originate in automation are frequently redirected toward immigration and trade. Gallego and Kurer (2022) identify three psychological mechanisms driving this misattribution:

1. **Psychological Gratification**: "It may be more psychologically gratifying to attribute economic decline to globalization. Immigration and trade offer clear out-groups to mobilize against… Seeing one's tasks performed by machines can be particularly hard on self-esteem."

2. **Political Entrepreneurship**: "…political entrepreneurs (especially populist parties) supply discourses against immigration and trade that connect economic grievances to policy solutions. Parties contribute to voters' misattributions by making some explanations more cognitively available…"

3. **Cognitive Availability**: "…citizens are much more likely to encounter everyday examples of globalization… than automation, making the former more cognitively available."

The policy consequences are severe. Wu (2022) warns: "If confirmed, the misattribution or diversion hypothesis… is likely to lead to harmful policy responses. If people misperceive the source of an economic problem, they are likely to support inadequate remedies…" He elaborates that "grievances against globalization have economic origins, but economic losers often misattribute blame… and consequently, demand policies that poorly advance their interests."

Workers at higher automation risk disproportionately oppose free trade and immigration rather than technological investment (Wu, 2021a,b; Kaihovaara & Im, 2020; Rodrik & di Tella, 2020). Gallego & Kurer (2022) observe: "When individuals perceive themselves to be vulnerable to a labor market shock, their beliefs are particularly responsive to rhetoric that speaks to their concerns about economic hardship – a phenomenon that has been exploited by populist commentators to misdirect voters' angst about economic hardship towards immigrants or the 'undeserving poor'."

### Cultural Reading of Economic Phenomena

Ciccolini (2024) integrates this further: "I join other social scientists in considering that the dispute… around whether far-right voters are motivated more by cultural or economic reasons is a flawed one. This is because voters give a cultural reading to economic phenomena, namely those that they experience firsthand." Economic events are not processed in a material vacuum—they are interpreted through cultural identity, reinforcing political narratives that may be disconnected from the structural cause.

## II. Identity Switching Theory

Bonomi et al. (2021) model how voters switch their primary social identity—from economic class to cultural/national group—in response to economic shocks or shifts in political conflict salience:

**The Core Model**: "At any given point in time, voters perceive their social and political reality from the vantage point of the most salient group among the many latent ones to which they belong… As political cleavages change, voters switch identity from their income class to their cultural, regional, or racial group."

**Belief Slanting Effects**: "When voters abandon their class identity and redefine themselves in terms of their moral or religious values, the latter become more important to explain their beliefs in several domains." This means that the same individual—facing the same objective economic circumstances—will form different policy preferences depending on which identity is currently activated.

**Consequences for Redistribution**: "When identity switches to culture, conflict over cultural policies intensifies because cultural beliefs polarize. Conservative voters increase their opposition to immigration and civil rights and become less favorable to redistribution. Progressive voters move in the opposite direction."

This mechanism explains a central puzzle: why economic vulnerability sometimes produces cultural-nationalist rather than redistributive left-wing responses. If the workplace no longer provides reliable social status, "voters who suffer from relative status decline may seek redress by adopting other identities… such as national identities" (Gallego & Kurer, 2022, citing Shayo, 2009).

**Empirical Pattern**: Ciccolini (2025) finds that status loss "fuels hostile attitudes toward migrants, as well as criticism of the European Union… economic status loss boosts opposition to redistribution." The same economic grievance produces cultural-political rather than redistributive demands—consistent with identity switching.

## III. The Paradox of Inequality: Social Insulation and Legitimation

Mijs (2021) articulates a counterintuitive mechanism: rising inequality can paradoxically produce *greater* legitimation of inequality because it fosters spatial and social segregation that limits direct experience of the full spectrum of disadvantage.

**The Core Mechanism**: "People underestimate the structural forces that make for inequality because they are increasingly unable to see it from their (isolated) position at the bottom or top of their (segregated) society. Without direct experiences, news reporting and statistics about (growing) inequality are not likely to change people's perspectives, nor lead them to develop empathy for the plight of unseen others living across the income divide."

**Social Insulation Process**: "Growing levels of income inequality mean that experiences and interactions with people across income, wealth and racial fault lines are becoming more seldom. Research suggests that income inequality creates greater spatial and social distance between the wealthy and the poor, as they live their lives in different institutions." As the income gap grows, "other people's lives fade out of view."

**Self-Perpetuating Legitimation**: "I hypothesize that citizens' consent to inequality can be explained by their belief that inequalities reflect the legitimate accomplishments of hard work rather than the operation of ('unfair') structural forces beyond their control." This produces a feedback loop: inequality → segregation → limited exposure → belief in meritocracy → reduced pressure for redistribution → more inequality.

**Policy Implication** (Mijs, 2021): "Breaking that loop requires taking seriously people's beliefs and lived experiences, and designing social policy to bring the two in closer alignment."

## IV. Meritocratic Belief Systems

Meritocracy—the belief that success reflects individual effort and talent—legitimizes inequality by displacing structural explanations. Its prevalence is striking: "Throughout the West, we find a striking convergence in citizens' views of success: in the most recent surveys, at least two-thirds of citizens in all countries—and as much as 95% of Americans—attribute success to meritocratic factors." (Mijs, 2021)

The "Belief in a Just World" perspective (Mijs, 2021) holds "a universal tendency to believe that inequalities reflect a meritocratic process, this allows people to maintain their belief that they live in a fair world rather than having to address inequities."

**The 'Anxious Meritocrat' Variant**: Ballard-Rosa, Goldstein, and Rudra (2024) identify individuals who hold meritocratic norms but fear for their own economic prospects as particularly susceptible to protectionist appeals: "We anticipate that 'economically anxious' meritocrats… are the most open to this pro-protection trade prompt. The absence of social mobility is exceptionally disquieting to these individuals."

Their mechanism: "For meritocrats, the elite frame of trade as allowing 'unfair market access,' often to less deserving 'outsiders' (e.g., workers in China, India, Japan), effectively places foreign commerce into the same category as welfare and affirmative action." Trade competition is perceived as rule-breaking in a meritocratic game, not structural economic change.

**Attributional Patterns and Welfare Regimes**: Alesina and Angeletos (2005) show that societies with more redistribution tend to believe luck and structural factors determine outcomes, while limited redistribution fosters belief in individual effort—a reciprocal relationship between policy design and meritocratic belief. The "submerged state" (Mettler, 2011), where benefits are delivered invisibly via tax expenditures, reinforces attribution of economic security to individual merit rather than government support.

## V. Universalism vs. Particularism as Moral Orientations

Enke et al. (2023) and Häusermann et al. (2023) identify a fundamental moral-political cleavage between universalism and particularism that organizes a wide range of political attitudes:

**The Core Definition** (Enke et al., 2023): "A particularist or communal morality emphasizes relationship-specific obligations (loyalty and treating in-group members well), whereas a universalist morality emphasizes equal treatment and impartiality." Formally: "Universalism is formalized as the slope of altruism as a function of social distance — a full universalist exhibits altruism that is invariant to social distance, whereas a particularist's altruism decreases with distance."

**Inequality Attitudes Alignment** (Häusermann et al., 2023): "Our findings mostly confirm the expectations of the universalist-particularist framework suggesting the alignment of both socioeconomic and sociocultural inequality concerns: progressive, left voters are more concerned about all types of inequality than conservative and especially radical-right constituencies." This produces a single organizing dimension: "from new left 'universalists' being inequality-averse to right-wing 'particularists' being more inequality-tolerant."

**Political-Geographic Predictive Power** (Enke et al., 2023): "Our results thus suggest that a considerable fraction of the geographic political divide may result from disagreement over universalist versus particularist moral ideals." Importantly, "the correlation between vote shares and universalism that we identify is substantially stronger than those relating vote shares to log median household income… or share of population with a college degree."

**Implications for Welfare Design**: Authoritarian values (strongly particularist) translate into narrow deservingness conceptions, "which implies a preference for the punishment of out-groups that are perceived to break with the social norm to be 'hard-working'… the unemployed get often associated with 'lazy free-riders'" (Busemeyer et al., 2023). Busemeyer & Rathgeb (2022) define a "particularistic-authoritarian" welfare type with narrow deservingness that excludes the unemployed and poor from entitlements.

## VI. Affective Polarization

Affective polarization involves partisans holding increasingly negative feelings toward out-partisans, often based on perceived fundamental differences in identity and morality rather than policy disagreement alone.

Campos & Federico (2025) identify three dimensions (citing Finkel et al., 2020):
1. **Othering**: Believing partisan identity marks fundamental differences between groups.
2. **Aversion**: Disliking and avoiding out-party members.
3. **Moralization**: Perceiving one's own partisan identity as reflecting fundamental values.

**Democratic Implications**: Campos & Federico (2025) find that "only certain forms of affective polarization are universally associated with attitudes and behaviors that are bad for democratic functioning. Looking only at the relationship between the composite APS and anti-democratic attitudes, one might incorrectly conclude that affective polarization is always bad for democracy." The moralization dimension is most strongly linked to democratic backsliding.

**Connection to Economic Vulnerability**: Affective polarization is not simply a cultural phenomenon—the identity-switching mechanism (Bonomi et al., 2021) shows how economic shocks can shift voters to cultural identities, intensifying cultural conflict and affective polarization between progressive and conservative camps. Cena (2022), citing Jetten et al. (2017), notes that "inequality evokes anxiety and fear among both the poor and the wealthy, albeit for different reasons"—creating conditions for mutual status anxiety and cross-cutting polarization.

## VII. Political Representation Gaps

A crucial link between economic vulnerability and populist support runs through perceived failures of democratic representation, particularly on issues salient to lower-income voters.

**Policy Discontent and Issue Salience** (Huber, 2023): "The paper proposes that populist attitudes among citizens are driven by a combination of policy discontent and issue salience. Specifically, the authors argue that individuals become more populist when they feel poorly represented on policy issues they care strongly about." Populism is not an ideological fixed trait but a responsive attitude triggered by representation failure on salient issues.

**Class Gradient in Perceived Representation** (Ares and Häusermann, 2023): "The upper classes are indeed more likely to perceive politicians' (social policy) priorities as congruent with their own." The class gap is largest for the most salient lower-class policies: "The strongest class gradient appears for two social consumption policies that are largely salient across countries and strongly demanded by lower-class voters: pensions and unemployment benefits."

**Policy Choices as Signals** (Baccini, 2024): "The policy response to these outcomes still signals the government's economic priorities to voters." Fiscal cutbacks communicate values, not just economic adjustment: "Vulnerable voters infer from fiscal cutbacks that the government's policy position is incompatible with their needs and interests, and hold it accountable accordingly."

**Governance Capacity and Left-Behind Perceptions** (De Ruyter et al., 2022): Whether voters feel "economically left behind" is partly shaped by whether they feel "politically left behind"—devolved, responsive governance structures reduce left-behind perceptions even in economically declining regions, as illustrated by contrasting Scotland's accountable governance with England's fragmented sub-national structure.

**Routine Workers and Representation** (Gallego & Kurer, 2022): Routine workers "perceive mainstream parties haven't represented their views descriptively or substantively" and "turn against establishment as protest." When economic anxiety is combined with perceived non-representation, support for anti-establishment parties follows as an instrumental rational response, not merely an emotional reaction.
