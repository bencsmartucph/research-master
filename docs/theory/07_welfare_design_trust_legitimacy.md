# Welfare Design, Trust & Legitimacy

> **Context Abstract:** This module examines how welfare policy design—through visibility, procedural fairness, deservingness criteria, and implementation processes—shapes institutional trust and political legitimacy. The core argument is that procedural fairness matters more for "losers," that visible and legible welfare states create broader constituencies, and that welfare design communicates moral messages about deservingness that shape both direct recipients and broader publics.

---

## Visibility, Salience, and Political Priming

The visibility and salience of welfare policies significantly affect their political impact. Bergman (2023) observes: "More 'visible' welfare states and labour market interventions shape the overall salience of social programmes (Gingrich, 2014). Voters tending towards PRRP might then be strongly primed by observations of a policy they tend to disfavour."

Crescenzi's research on EU funds demonstrates that "EU funds only build support for the EU when they generate tangible benefits that citizens can observe in their daily lives. They suggest that voters respond to concrete improvements in their local economic environment rather than to the abstract idea of receiving EU support."

A study on Italian structural funds found: "These results suggest that economic aids alleviate somehow the potential populist voter so that he/she refrains from express his/her protest at polls."

## Procedural Fairness and Political Trust

Research shows that procedural fairness significantly influences how welfare experiences translate into political attitudes. De Blok and Kumlin (2022) find: "Procedural fairness theory was introduced as a counterargument to output-oriented theories. It predicts that people care about the decision-making and implementation process preceding the output. This approach can help explain why citizens accept decisions, even though they do not benefit directly from them or otherwise assess outcomes as dissatisfactory."

They identify a critical interaction: "One common assumption is that citizens are first and foremost result-oriented but that fair procedures, as it were, soften the blow of not getting one's desired outcome. Conversely, procedures perceived as unfair exacerbate the harmful influence of output dissatisfaction. Under this view, then, procedural fairness makes a larger difference for output 'losers' than for 'winners', that is, those who are satisfied with the outcome."

This insight is crucial for understanding welfare legitimacy: "Procedural fairness matters more for 'losers' (their primary hypothesis): 'One common assumption is that citizens are first and foremost result-oriented but that fair procedures, as it were, soften the blow of not getting one's desired outcome. Conversely, procedures perceived as unfair exacerbate the harmful influence of output dissatisfaction. Under this view, then, procedural fairness makes a larger difference for output 'losers' than for 'winners', that is, those who are satisfied with the outcome.'" (de Blok and Kumlin, 2022: 872)

## Deservingness Criteria and the CARIN Framework

The perceived legitimacy of welfare institutions depends significantly on their alignment with widely held values about deservingness and reciprocity. Van Oorschot and Laenen (2023) identify five key deservingness criteria:

"In general, target groups are considered more deserving if they (a) are not considered personally responsible for their situation ('Control'), (b) adhere to prevailing societal norms and values, such as showing gratitude when help is offered ('Attitude'), (c) make a contribution to our society, in the past, present or future ('Reciprocity'), (d) belong to the dominant social or cultural group with which most people can easily identify ('Identity'), and (e) have severe financial and/or health needs ('Need')."

These criteria shape welfare attitudes across contexts: "The core components: the CARIN criteria. The first criterion, control, is about the perceived degree of personal responsibility welfare claimants have for being in the situation they are in... The second criterion, attitude, refers to a broader collection of norm-conforming attitudes and behaviours, such as people's gratitude, docility, compliance, honesty and pleasantness. The third criterion, reciprocity, relates to perceived contributions to society claimants make (either in the past, present or future), such as paid work, tax payments, caring responsibilities and job-seeking efforts. The fourth criterion, identity, has to do with feelings of similarity, proximity and cultural closeness between those judging the deservingness and those being judged... The fifth criterion, need, refers to the perceived financial destitution and/or poor health status of those seeking help." (Van Oorschot and Laenen, 2023: 6)

This creates tensions for welfare state legitimacy: "The welfare state has been pointed out as a site where there is tension between welfare and immigration, as immigrants are deemed newcomers and 'lack' contribution to the system based on reciprocity. It often develops into welfare chauvinism that supports social protection but contends that access to welfare services should be limited to native-born citizens."

## Producerism and Moral Economy

Research on "producerism" offers important insights into welfare attitudes and populist politics:

"On the one hand, the 'productive members of society', portrayed as 'virtuous' producers and potential victims and, on the other hand, 'parasitical strata both above and below them in the social hierarchy', which include both 'economic and political elites' and 'the poor, unemployed, and those who receive social welfare'" (2024 study on Producerist Populist Attitudes: 3).

Producerism connects economic concerns with moral judgments:

"In producerism, wealth and social justice are not solely material entities but also depend on how individuals perceive and experience practices, objects, political causes and their interactions with others. This links producerism with the concept of 'moral economy', which has been extensively discussed in social sciences and, more recently, in political science" (2024 study on Producerist Populist Attitudes: 21).

The concept helps explain the complexity of welfare attitudes:

"Fairness, the authors explain, stipulates that the rewards from cooperation should be principally divided according to individual contribution... When someone violates the fairness rule for their own benefit, they are considered a 'cheater', often with commensurately severe consequences" (2024 study on Producerist Populist Attitudes: 21).

## Authoritarian Values and Particularistic Deservingness

Busemeyer et al. (2023) find authoritarian values shape welfare attitudes:

"We argue that authoritarian values translate into particularistic deservingness conceptions, which implies a preference for the punishment of out-groups that are perceived to break with the social norm to be 'hard-working' and seek paid employment. In this way, the unemployed get often associated with 'lazy free-riders' that are unwilling to work as opposed to otherwise 'hard-working citizens' that are unable to work due to old age, disability or sickness." (Busemeyer et al., 2023: 82-83)

Guery et al. (2023) explain how producerism provides a moral framework:

"Producerism suggests that workers are virtuous, being squeezed by non-productive 'parasites' above – bureaucrats, politicians, elites, bankers, and international capital – and below – immigrants, undeserving poor using benefits – who live off their labour." (Guery et al., 2023: 523)

"We define the policy mix described above – strong support for workfare, moderate support only for 'deserving' recipients of social transfers, and low support for social investment – as a particularistic-authoritarian type of welfare state. First, building on the welfare state literature (e.g. Beramendi et al. 2015), the particularistic dimension of this preference structure stems from narrow conceptions of welfare deservingness, which exclude the unemployed and poor from entitlements to social transfers, as they are considered to break with the social norm to be 'hard-working'." (Busemeyer & Rathgeb, 2022: 85)

## Institutional Communication of Moral Worth

The communicative power of welfare institutions operates through both policy designs and implementation practices. Welfare institutions communicate powerfully about moral frameworks through their policy designs and implementation. They demonstrate how welfare policies targeting "deserving" groups (e.g., veterans, elderly) employ respectful, enabling administrative processes, while those targeting "undeserving" groups (e.g., welfare recipients) use punitive, restrictive processes. These administrative distinctions communicate moral judgments about beneficiaries, shaping both their self-perceptions and public attitudes toward them.

The moral communication of welfare institutions operates through the criteria they use to determine eligibility and support levels. Welfare systems activate different moral foundations through their institutional designs, typically emphasizing care and fairness foundations in universal systems, loyalty and authority foundations in conservative systems, and fairness-as-proportionality in liberal systems.
