# Council Ideation — "Extend the asymmetric-welfare mechanism to within-country variation using Danish registry data"

**Date:** 2026-05-08
**Input:** Topic string: *"extend the asymmetric-welfare mechanism to within-country variation using Danish registry data"*
**Framing:** Ben Smart's asymmetric-welfare paper documents that decommodifying welfare design dampens the conversion of routine-task vulnerability into anti-immigration sentiment, but does not symmetrically increase support for redistribution — across 15 Western European countries cross-sectionally. The proposal is to extend this within-country using Danish administrative registry data, exploiting individual-level longitudinal variation in welfare receipt, occupation, family, residence, and (via linkage to DNES, ESS Denmark, or Folketing electoral files) political behaviour. Danish registers offer full-population coverage, monthly resolution on labour-market and benefit transitions, and multi-generational linkage that ESS cannot match.
**Personas:** Obvious Extension, Adjacent Outsider, Constraint Inverter (synthesis by main session)

---

## Synthesis

### The convergent thread

Across all three personas, the same idea recurs in different vocabularies: **welfare encounters are identity-forming events, not just economic transfers, and the registers can see the encounter where ESS can only see the residue.** The Obvious Extension wants displacement-DiD around the 2010 dagpengereform; the Adjacent Outsider wants stigma sociology and mental-accounting frames on programme form and contribution history; the Constraint Inverter wants within-individual displacement-to-vote panels and intergenerational transmission. The methodological vocabularies differ — DiD, mediation, sibling-FE, triple-difference — but the substantive thread is the same: the seminar paper estimates the *reduced form* of welfare-as-moderator; Denmark's registers measure the *encounter* itself, person by person, month by month. Three personas independently land on the same underlying question: what does the welfare state *do* to people, and is the political signature in attitudes a function of the design or of the lived experience?

### The boldest single move

**Within-individual welfare exposure and political behaviour after job displacement** (Constraint Inverter Angle 1, also implicit in Obvious Extension Angle 1 and Adjacent Outsider Angle 1). The reason this is the boldest move is not the difficulty — it is the highest-information move because it directly disposes of the deepest objections that the council-critique on the same paper raised: cross-sectional confounding from institutional bundle, selection into routine occupation, and the N=15 ceiling on identification. Within-person event studies around mass layoffs in Denmark, with ISCO-RTI heterogeneity and validated voting outcomes via the Folketing electoral register, give a clean test of the asymmetric-welfare mechanism that the cross-national design cannot. The Dal Bó-Finan-Folke-Persson-Rickne (2023) Swedish template demonstrates the method works for political outcomes; the Dustmann-Vasiljeva-Damm (2019) Danish dispersal paper demonstrates the institutional pipeline (Statistics Denmark + electoral registers) is publishable at top journals. The boldness is not the design — it is doing the design with an *RTI*-coded vulnerability instrument, which (per the personas' literature scans) appears not to have been done in the Danish register tradition. Year-2 paper of the PhD; depends on Forskerservice clearance landing by month 6.

### A five-year research programme combining these

**Year 1 (current).** *Seminar paper / job-market paper-1.* Cross-national EU-15 evidence on asymmetric welfare moderation. ESS rounds 6-9, CWED, BLUPs vs. cross-level interaction. Already done; tightening into journal version through 2026.

**Year 2.** *Mechanism replication paper.* Danish registers, mass-layoff event studies, RTI-heterogeneous response in voting (DF, Nye Borgerlige, Danmarksdemokraterne). Identifies the within-person analogue of the cross-sectional moderation, with the 2010 dagpengereform as quasi-experimental tightening of decommodification. The reduced-form result for Denmark — does within-Denmark institutional variation reproduce the cross-national pattern? Target: AJPS, AEJ:Applied. Co-author needed for Statistics Denmark project; CEBI is the natural host.

**Year 3.** *Mechanism interrogation paper.* Stigma vs. contribution-account vs. economic exposure as competing mediators. Combine survey-experimental module (Stantcheva-style elicitation, van Oorschot deservingness battery) with register linkage on lifetime tax history (SKAT) and benefit-form history (DREAM). Direct test of whether the asymmetry is about decommodification *as such* or about the felt experience of conditionality. Target: APSR for the survey-experimental angle, JESP for the comparative welfare angle.

**Year 4.** *Generational transmission paper.* Multi-generational register linkage. Parental welfare experience during the child's formative years (ages 6-18) → child's political behaviour at adult labour-market entry. Sibling FE controls for household selection; the 1994 ALMP reform provides cohort-specific exposure variation. Categorically impossible in ESS; uniquely possible in Danish registers. The deepest theoretical move: welfare regimes shape political coalitions over generational time. Target: APSR, AJPS.

**Year 5 / book or first major post-PhD paper.** *Cross-national synthesis with mechanism evidence.* Return to the comparative frame — but now armed with three within-Denmark mechanism papers — and test whether the patterns generalise to Sweden, Norway, Finland (where parallel register infrastructure exists). Coordinate via the Nordic register-research network. The narrative arc closes: started cross-national reduced form, drilled to mechanism in one case, brought the mechanism evidence back to the comparative frame. The book is *Dignity Is a Baseline* expanded with the register-mechanism chapters.

The thread that binds the programme: each paper goes deeper into the mechanism while narrowing the case; year 5 returns to the comparative frame with mechanism evidence the original paper could only assume. Coherent five-year arc that ends with a cross-national claim *defended by* within-country mechanism evidence — exactly the structural move that makes a CPE thesis publishable in top political-economy outlets.

### Three things to check before committing

These are the falsifiers / scoop checks / feasibility blockers — Mode 2 calibration, top three only.

1. **Forskerservice timeline and CEBI affiliation must be locked in by month 6 of the PhD.** All three Danish-register angles depend on Statistics Denmark clearance. Without secured access by ~September 2026, the year-2 paper slips, and the entire programme cascades. Concrete step before committing: confirm with CEBI (Sandberg, Druedahl, Kreiner) whether an incoming PhD can join an existing Forskerservice project rather than applying *de novo*. The difference is 6+ months of timeline slack.

2. **Scoop risk on RTI × Danish-register × political-outcomes.** Three personas independently flagged the Dustmann-Vasiljeva-Damm (2019) refugee-dispersal paper, Dal Bó et al. (2023) on Swedish populist voting, Mitrut-Wolff working papers on Danish welfare-transmission, and the Andersen-Svarer-Kreiner Danish UI-reform tradition. Concrete step: targeted literature scan in IZA / NBER / SSRN for any paper combining ISCO-task-coded RTI exposure with Danish register political outcomes. If it exists with a 2024-2026 vintage, the year-2 paper needs reframing or a different displacement margin.

3. **Does Denmark have enough decommodification variation to test the asymmetry, or does the within-country project test a different mechanism?** Denmark sits at the high-decommodification end of every cross-national index. Within-Denmark variation is mostly in *conditionality* (means-tested vs. contributory) and in *temporal retrenchment* (the 2010 reform), not in steady-state generosity. Decide whether this is a feature (sharper mechanism test of conditionality vs. generosity) or a bug (the within-Denmark project tests a related-but-different claim than the cross-national paper). If it's a bug, the year-2 paper needs to position itself as a *complement* to the cross-national reduced form, not a *replication* of it. The Obvious Extension persona flagged this as the central objection to the entire research programme; surface it in the proposal rather than letting a referee surface it.

## Three concrete angles

**1. Does losing a routine-task job push workers toward populist parties, and does Danish UI generosity blunt that conversion?** The 2010 dagpengereform halved UI duration from 4 to 2 years and tightened eligibility from 52 to 26 weeks of work. DiD: workers displaced before vs. after the cutoff, with high-RTI occupations as the high-exposure group. If decommodification dampens the routine-vulnerability-to-xenophobia channel, then a sharp reduction in decommodification should *re-activate* it.

**2. Does municipal variation in active labour market policy (ALMP) intensity moderate the occupation-to-radical-right pipeline?** Danish municipal-level variation in ALMP spending per displaced worker, 2007-2023. Outcome: Danish People's Party / Nye Borgerlige vote share at polling-station level. Baccini-Pinto-Weymouth's regional design ported to a single country with cleaner administrative data.

**3. Regression discontinuity at the integrationsydelse eligibility threshold: does benefit cuts on natives shift attitudes toward immigrants?** The 2015 introduction of integrationsydelse cut benefits for households failing residency requirements (7/8 years in Denmark). Sample: Danish citizens who lived abroad and returned. Sharp eligibility threshold, identified causal effect on the cut's beneficiaries' political behaviour.

## What you'd need to do this

- Forskerservice authorization (~6-9 months from application), IDA, DREAM, DNES with CPR linkage
- Method: Callaway-Sant'Anna or de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfoeuille for staggered treatment
- Partnership: CEBI / ROCKWOOL Foundation
- Time horizon: 9-15 months from application to first results

## Closest existing literature

- Marx & Naumann (2018), Anelli, Colantone & Stanig (2019), Gingrich (2019), Baccini, Pinto & Weymouth (2024)
- Andersen & Svarer (Danish UI reform labour-market outcomes); Stockemer & Lentz (2018), Patana (2020) on Danish municipal radical right; Engler & Weisstanner (2021); Margalit (2019); Cavaillé & Trump (2015); Hangartner et al. (2019)

## Why someone smart would dismiss this

The Danish UI reform of 2010 has been studied by labour economists at Copenhagen, Aarhus, ROCKWOOL — Andersen, Svarer, Kreiner. Linking to political outcomes is the obvious extension. Doing all three within Denmark gives depth but sacrifices the comparative leverage. Does Denmark have enough variation in welfare-design *generosity* (rather than just labour-market policy) to test the asymmetry, given that Denmark sits near the high-decommodification end of every cross-national index?

## Three concrete angles

**1. Stigma as the missing mediator: does means-tested receipt produce different attitudinal trajectories than universal receipt, holding economic exposure constant?** Among Danes facing identical RTI exposure, do those who transit through means-tested benefits develop more anti-immigration and anti-redistribution attitudes than those who transit through universal/contributory benefits? The form of receipt as itself an identity-altering event (Goffman, Soss, Wacquant). 2014 kontanthjælpsreform as sharpening shock.

**2. Mental accounting of "what I paid in": does contribution history moderate the conversion of exposure into welfare-chauvinist attitudes?** Tax registers (cumulative lifetime tax paid) + ATP contribution history + deservingness module. Akerlof-Kranton identity economics + Thaler mental accounts. People do not treat welfare as a fungible transfer but as a withdrawal from a personal account they have been depositing into.

**3. Neighbourhood as the unit of recognition: does residential exposure to visible welfare receipt drive the asymmetry?** DST address registers + ghetto-list designations 2010-2024 + benefit-receipt density at sogn level. The 2018 ghettopakke and forced relocations in designated areas provide quasi-experimental shock. The state's speech-act of designation may be the operative variable, not the personal balance sheet.

## What you'd need to do this

- VIVE (welfare-attitude expertise), ROCKWOOL (registry pipeline), KU Sociology (Larsen, Gundelach lineage)
- For Angle 2: CEBI for behavioural-economics module; possibly Stantcheva for survey-experimental complement
- For Angle 3: AAU geography, Lund Centre for Economic Demography, possibly Ryan Enos
- Time horizon: 12-24 months across the three

## Closest existing literature

- Soss (1999, 2000), Bruch-Ferree-Soss (2010), Larsen (2008, 2013), Wacquant (2009)
- van Oorschot (2006), Akerlof-Kranton (2000, 2010), Stantcheva (2021), Cavaillé-Trump (2015)
- Enos (2014, 2017), Hopkins (2010), Damm (2009, 2014), Andersen & Larsen (2022)

## Why someone smart would dismiss this

A comparative political economist would say: you have replaced a tractable cross-national institutional comparison with three within-Denmark micro-mechanism papers. Angle 1 is Soss-style policy-feedback; Angle 2 is a behavioural-economics curiosity; Angle 3 is a neighbourhood-effects paper. None answers the original cross-national puzzle. Registry-only designs cannot benchmark Denmark against Sweden, Germany, or the UK, which is where the institutional-moderator argument has to live to publish in CPS or APSR.

<summary>Constraint Inverter (general-purpose)</summary>

## Three concrete angles

**1. Within-individual welfare exposure and political behaviour after job displacement.** Statistics Denmark register linkage of IDA, DREAM, BEF, UDD, KOTRE, and the Folketing electoral register. Mass layoffs identified via firm-level FIDA shocks. The same person before and after a displacement shock, with within-treatment heterogeneity in benefit replacement rate. Turnout is an actually-observed behaviour rather than a stated intention.

**2. Intergenerational transmission of populist preferences via parental welfare-state experience.** Generation linkage in BEF (parent-child identifiers back to 1968) tied to parent's DREAM transfer history during child's formative years (ages 6-18). Categorically impossible in ESS — no parent-child linkage, no measurement of parental welfare experience, no panel. If unconditional benefit receipt during a parent's unemployment correlates with one set of adult political dispositions in the child while activation-track receipt correlates with another, that is a direct test of the dignity-vs-deservingness mechanism.

**3. Residential sorting and political geography as a welfare-state response.** When a Danish municipality tightens or loosens local social-assistance conditionality, do exposed individuals (a) move, (b) sort into politically distinct neighbourhoods, and (c) shift voting behaviour? The 2016 kontanthjælpsloft reform: large families with high housing costs were mechanically cut. Triple-difference design.

## What you'd need to do this

- CEBI (cleanest route to Forskerservice); alternatives ROCKWOOL, AKF/VIVE, UCL via IFS Centre for Microeconomic Analysis of Public Policy
- 4-6 months Statistics Denmark approval; 6 months data construction; 6-9 months analysis. Realistic first paper in 18 months
- For Angle 2: 2-3 year project; data construction alone is 9-12 months
- For Angle 3: 12-15 months; 2016 reform follow-up windows now adequate

## Closest existing literature

- Margalit (2019, ARPS), Dal Bó et al. (2023, QJE) on Sweden Democrats — the methodological closest existing template
- Mitrut & Wolff (2024 IZA) on Danish welfare transmission; Dahl-Kostøl-Mogstad (2014, QJE) "Family Welfare Cultures"; Cesarini et al. (Swedish lottery work)
- Andersson, Jofre-Monseny & Solé-Ollé; Cantoni et al. (2019 AEJ:Applied); Dustmann-Vasiljeva-Damm (2019, RES) — the most directly relevant prior demonstration that DK registers + electoral outcomes can produce a top-5 paper

## Why someone smart would dismiss this

External validity weaponised twice over. Denmark is the most generous, most universalist, most administratively coherent welfare state in the comparison set — exactly where the asymmetric-welfare hypothesis predicts the *smallest* populist response. Finding null effects in DK tells you almost nothing about Italy, Hungary, the UK; finding large effects raises the question of why the most buffered population would still respond. You are abandoning the comparative-institutional variation that *is* the theoretical contribution. CEBI, Rockwool, and the Aarhus-Copenhagen political-economy group are working this seam with five-year head starts.
