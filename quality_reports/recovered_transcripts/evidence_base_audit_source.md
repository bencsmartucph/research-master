## §I — Introduction {toggle="true"}
	<details>
	<summary>Direct quotes</summary>
		- "When relative societal decline rather than material hardship are at the heart of socially conservative resentment, traditional welfare policy may be an insufficient response… and hence an ineffective remedy to counter the ascent of right-wing populist movements" — Kurer 2020, p. 1801. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/3217844a449d801bae06e755787d5766"/> → licenses the paper's pivot from quantity to quality.
		- "We believe that this appeal to personal dignity is key to winning routine workers' support. Perhaps even more than social protection, they demand economic and cultural protection." — Kurer & Palier 2019, p. 5. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/2057844a449d800fa0e0c3e41d0b194b"/> → names the recognition demand directly.
		- "However, the reverse mechanism (more spending, less populism) is not borne out by the data – despite demonstrably positive effects on individual welfare." — Kurer & Palier 2019, p. 5. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/2057844a449d800fa0e0c3e41d0b194b"/> → the empirical anchor for asymmetry.
		- "Right-populist voters care as much, or even more, about recognition as about redistribution… compensation is not what these people are looking for." — Gidron & Hall 2017, p. S26. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e37844a449d80e3ae49e2eea1f3cfcf"/> → recognition ≠ redistribution; central to the asymmetric framing.
		- "Recipients internalize deservingness criteria and mirror them onto others… typically ends with blaming of immigrants (welfare chauvinism)." — Wagner 2022, p. 2. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e27844a449d80e2b4cdce2a5e7c7b83"/> → mechanism by which welfare design produces exclusion.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Statistics &amp; empirical findings</summary>
		- ESS rounds 6–9, 34 countries, N = 188,764; RTI match rate 87.8% (N = 165,667). Anti-immigration index Cronbach's α = 0.864. (paper §V.A)
		- ALMP spending vs RTI→exclusion slope: r = 0.01, p = 0.97, N = 15 (matched Western European sample). (paper §V.D)
		- CWED decommodification vs same slope: r = −0.848, p &lt; 0.001, N = 15 — accounts for 72% of cross-country variance. (paper §V.D)
		- Cross-level interaction RTI × CWED: β = −0.059, p = 0.015 (N = 81,885); strengthens to β = −0.066 with GDP-growth and Gini macro controls. (paper §V.D)
		- Slope ordering: Liberal β = 0.512 &gt; Southern 0.462 &gt; Continental 0.443 &gt; Nordic 0.413 &gt; Eastern 0.263. (paper §V.C)
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Objections to pre-empt</summary>
		- *N = 15 is too thin for headline correlation.* → Robustness covered in §V.D and Appendix A: jackknife, single-country exclusions, BLUPs vs OLS bivariate. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/0e05b220489d4f299cafa63d0c776b44"/>
		- *CWED is just a Nordic-regime proxy.* → Pre-empt with within-regime variation (Denmark's relatively steep slope despite high CWED) and macro-control robustness.
		- *"Compensation works" is well-established (Burgoon & Schakel 2022).* → Unit-of-analysis distinction: party-platform vs individual-attitude (paper §II). <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1ec7844a449d80f8851bca960afb19f8"/>
		- *Cross-sectional design cannot identify causality.* → Conceded openly in §V.G; registry-based panel work flagged as next step.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Open questions</summary>
		- Does the CWED–slope relation hold beyond Western Europe? CEE countries excluded for CWED-coverage reasons.
		- Is the pivot from quantity to quality reversible — could a high-CWED country with new conditionality re-create a damage signature within a decade? (Danish 2003/06/13 reforms hint yes; not yet tested.)
		- Are decommodification scores measuring what the theory needs (lived encounter quality), or just statutory benefit replacement rates?

## §II — The Limits of the Buffering Model {toggle="true"}
	<details>
	<summary>Direct quotes</summary>
		- "On the one hand, social protection has an 'embedded liberalism' effect… On the other hand, social protection has an 'embedded nationalism' effect, awakening worries that globalisation may undercut it." — Burgoon & Schakel 2022, p. 51. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1ec7844a449d80f8851bca960afb19f8"/> → states the dual-channel puzzle the paper resolves at the individual level.
		- "Coal closures have not led to an objective decline in community-wide economic well-being… \\[yet\\] perceived economic deprivation and widespread disappointment with the introduced phase-out policy \\[led\\] to higher abstention rates and lower vote shares for the Social Democrats." — Stutzmann 2025, p. 18. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/3217844a449d805889cccdc0d76d32ac"/> → compensation-that-backfires evidence.
		- "A sizeable portion of workers are resistant to being freed from work… Strikingly, individuals are more willing to reduce their work hours by half than to cease work entirely, even when offered full compensation." — Pelc 2025, p. 3. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1f67844a449d8058ae02d5b851bdd084"/> → form/source/meaning of compensation matters independent of material content.
		- "Workers highly exposed to automation are not less likely to vote for populist parties in countries with more generous early retirement, more in-kind spending, or more protective labour market regulation." — Gallego & Kurer 2022 summarising Gingrich 2019. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1f17844a449d80eaa719f6ef126a0ee1"/> → core empirical anchor for the buffering model's failure.
		- "Welfare cuts predicted Leave support not through material deprivation but through erosion of political trust and efficacy." — paraphrase of Fetzer 2019. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e37844a449d805ebd92fe5c7df9488c"/> → institutional/recognition channel does the work, not material channel.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Statistics &amp; empirical findings</summary>
		- Gingrich (2019) finds null moderation of automation→populism by ALMP, in-kind spending, or employment protection. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1f17844a449d80eaa719f6ef126a0ee1"/>
		- Pelc 2025: three survey experiments, &gt;7,500 US respondents; respondents prefer employer compensation over identical government compensation, partial-hours over full cessation. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1f67844a449d802ca127e051eab16eaf"/>
		- Albanese et al. (2021): EU structural-fund disbursements reduce populist voting in recipient regions — shows visible/local investment can buffer where individualised transfers cannot.
		- Foster & Frieden 2025 (17 W. European countries): austerity cuts associated with populist voting; spending levels alone insufficient. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e27844a449d80aeafb7c03d79928795"/>
		- Stutzmann 2025: "least-likely case" — German coal regions had compensation AND interest-group representation; nonetheless higher abstention + lower SPD vote share. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1f27844a449d80ebb961ed535a20c649"/>
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Objections to pre-empt</summary>
		- *Burgoon & Schakel show welfare generosity dampens anti-globalization platforms — your null contradicts.* → Different unit (parties vs individuals), different mechanism (supply vs demand). Both can be true.
		- *Albanese et al. show compensation works — your buffering critique overreaches.* → Their finding is consistent: visible investment communicates recognition, individualised transfers don't. Spending effort alone is too coarse.
		- *Halikiopoulou & Vlandas (2016) and Vlandas & Halikiopoulou (2022) find welfare generosity moderates radical-right success.* → Their measure conflates generosity with quality; this paper isolates which dimension does the work.
		- *Covid-era spending didn't produce a populist backlash (Funke et al.) — buffering can succeed.* → Visible, broadly-shared, time-bounded, non-stigmatising; consistent with the asymmetric reading. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/18f7844a449d8115bd17f3ff4a1f5f1c"/>
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Open questions</summary>
		- Does Burgoon & Schakel's platform-level finding survive disaggregation by welfare-design type (decommodification vs spending)?
		- Pelc's resistance varies by political ideology and Protestant work ethic — does that interact with welfare regime?
		- Why does Albanese's local-investment finding work in Italy but Stutzmann's compensation fail in Germany? Is it the visibility, the issue-ownership, or the activation requirements?
	</details>

## §III.A — What the Evidence Demands {toggle="true"}
	<details>
	<summary>Direct quotes</summary>
		- "Right-populist voters care as much, or even more, about recognition as about redistribution." — Gidron & Hall 2017, p. S26. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e37844a449d80e3ae49e2eea1f3cfcf"/> → recognition is not fungible with redistribution; ground floor for asymmetric framing.
		- "They demand economic *and* cultural protection." — Kurer & Palier 2019, p. 5. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/2057844a449d800fa0e0c3e41d0b194b"/> → second non-fungibility, on the supply side.
		- "The redistribution null and the supplementary ISSP null are both substantive findings, not measurement failures." — Two-DV asymmetry note. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/31218071f61f41cbaeda96013fe7fa22"/> → reframes the empirical asymmetry as theoretically informative.
		- "A theory that predicts the null and observes the null is doing exactly what theories are supposed to do." — Defence Catalogue. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/0e05b220489d4f299cafa63d0c776b44"/> → epistemic license for treating the solidarity null as confirmation.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Statistics &amp; empirical findings</summary>
		- ESS Model 5 (redistribution): RTI main effect β = +0.044 (p &lt; 0.001) but RTI × Liberal interaction β = +0.011 (p = 0.285) — wrong direction for symmetric account. (paper §V.F)
		- ISSP 2006 supplementary test: RTI × CWED on unemployment-spending support β = +0.010, SE = 0.016, p = 0.55 (N = 10,216, 12 countries). (paper §V.F, Appendix C)
		- Damage side: RTI × Liberal on anti-immigration β = +0.127, p = 0.003 (paper §V.E) — clean, large, robust.
		- Two independent samples (ESS 2012–2018, ISSP 2006), two outcome variables, both produce solidarity nulls.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Objections to pre-empt</summary>
		- *The solidarity null is just measurement failure.* → Two independent failures with different instruments + theory pre-specifies the null on three grounds. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/0e05b220489d4f299cafa63d0c776b44"/>
		- *Redistribution preferences and exclusionary attitudes are correlated; you should test both jointly.* → Häusermann et al. 2023 universalism-particularism axis already shows the joint structure; the asymmetry survives. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1f47844a449d8006a0f5e44c0decedb1"/>
		- *Underpowered to detect a real protective effect at N = 15.* → ISSP supplement and ESS Model 5 are both individual-level; statistical power is not the binding constraint.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Open questions</summary>
		- Does an outcome more sensitive to active solidarity (e.g., welfare-chauvinism reversal, support for inclusive deservingness) yield a non-null protective effect?
		- Time-period heterogeneity: would 2020–2024 ESS rounds (post-Covid) show a different asymmetric signature?
	</details>

## §III.B — Why Welfare, and Not Something Else {toggle="true"}
	<details>
	<summary>Direct quotes</summary>
		- "Programmes with respectful implementation foster civic engagement; programmes characterised by surveillance and conditionality generate alienation." — paraphrase of Soss 1999 (paper §III.B). → mechanism is implementation, not benefit level.
		- "Procedural fairness preserves institutional trust most strongly for those whose outcomes are unfavourable." — De Blok & Kumlin 2022. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/3217844a449d80089c6bf3bc83e0131b"/> → fairness matters most for the most vulnerable.
		- Tax expenditures and indirect benefits remain "invisible as government assistance"; recipients of submerged welfare attribute their security to personal merit. — Mettler 2011, *The Submerged State* (paper §III.B). → the dual welfare state fractures political identity.
		- "Universal welfare regimes create different moral frameworks than selective ones." — Larsen 2008, p. 162. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e37844a449d80eabb70d723fd4e7aba"/> → only welfare both allocates and judges in the same act.
		- "Welfare institutions create distinctive 'deservingness discussions'… that mediate between welfare regime types and public support." — Larsen 2008. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e37844a449d80eabb70d723fd4e7aba"/> → welfare is the place where economic vulnerability and institutional treatment meet.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Statistics &amp; empirical findings</summary>
		- Larsen 2008 (WVS, 16 countries): 39% of US respondents blame poverty on laziness vs 16% in Sweden — institutional logic shapes deservingness perception cross-nationally. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e37844a449d80eabb70d723fd4e7aba"/>
		- De Blok & Kumlin 2022 (Norwegian three-wave panel, 2014–2017): procedural fairness mediates trust effect for losing recipients. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/3217844a449d80089c6bf3bc83e0131b"/>
		- Mettler 2011 evidence: large share of US population benefits from "submerged" welfare (mortgage interest deduction, employer-provided health insurance) without recognising it as government assistance.
		- Soss 1999 finding: AFDC vs SSDI recipients show divergent civic engagement despite similar material profiles — design difference, not benefit difference.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Objections to pre-empt</summary>
		- *Many institutions shape identity (media, religion, class) — why isolate welfare?* → Pre-empted in paper §III.B: only welfare allocates resources AND renders judgement at the point of maximum dependence.
		- *The mechanism only applies to direct recipients — most workers never claim benefits.* → Three scaling channels: anticipated dependency, public discourse mirroring, RTI workers concentrated in caseloads (paper §III.B).
		- *Mettler's submerged-state argument is US-specific.* → Anticipated; can flag that the channel is cleaner in dual welfare states but holds wherever there is class-asymmetric visibility of state assistance.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Open questions</summary>
		- What is the magnitude of the anticipatory channel relative to direct-recipient channel? No empirical estimate yet.
		- Is the submerged-state mechanism detectable in European data, or is it a US idiosyncrasy?
		- Are media frames partly endogenous to welfare design (Wagner's mirroring), or independent?
	</details>

## §III.C — The Damage Cascade {toggle="true"}
	<details>
	<summary>Direct quotes</summary>
		- "When voters abandon their class identity and redefine themselves in terms of their moral or cultural values, these cultural factors become more important in explaining their beliefs across multiple domains, including economic policy preferences." — Bonomi, Gennaioli & Tabellini 2021. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e37844a449d804ab8a8ffe739156395"/> → identity-switching engine.
		- "Workers can shore up their status by 'maintaining their social distance' from other groups… perceived as occupying a lower status in the social order." — Im & Komp-Leukkunen 2021, p. 458. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/3217844a449d80679bcac2d39f2b44a9"/> → status as positional good; defensive othering.
		- "Workers at higher automation risk oppose immigration but show no different technology preferences." — Wu 2022 (paper §III.C). → misattribution is empirically clean.
		- "Their own deservingness (to be included, to be treated better) by blaming (even) weaker groups… 'exclude them — rather than me'. In so doing, they apply the same deservingness criteria the welfare system applied to them." — Wagner 2022. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e27844a449d80e2b4cdce2a5e7c7b83"/> → defensive othering as institutional learning.
		- "Stigmatization damages self-esteem, hinders employment transitions, and deepens social divisions… benefit claimants assert their own deservingness while reinforcing negative stereotypes about other benefit recipients." — Patrick 2016. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1f57844a449d8028823ecc67231e49d5"/> → recipient-level evidence for kicking-down.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Statistics &amp; empirical findings</summary>
		- Ballard-Rosa, Jensen & Scheve 2022 (ISQ): economic decline activates social-identity processes producing authoritarian values via group-status threat.
		- Im 2023 (Finnish panel): expected status decline (not material hardship) drives radical-right shift among routine workers. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1ec7844a449d802fa859fd28805dd7ca"/>
		- Wu 2022: high-automation-risk workers oppose immigration but do NOT differ on technology policy → discriminating evidence for misattribution.
		- Alesina & Angeletos 2005: conditional welfare systems produce individual rather than structural attributions of poverty.
		- Patrick 2016 (UK longitudinal interviews): claimants use the system's own deservingness criteria when distancing from peers — institutional learning is documented at recipient level. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1f57844a449d8028823ecc67231e49d5"/>
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Objections to pre-empt</summary>
		- *Identity switching could run the other way (cultural identity → class abandonment) — direction is not established.* → Bonomi et al. model is symmetric; institutional welfare design is the upstream switch theorised here.
		- *Misattribution is observed in cross-section; you cannot rule out reverse causation (anti-immigration views → automation perception).* → Wu 2022's discriminating finding (no technology-preference shift) cuts against reverse causation.
		- *"Defensive othering" is anecdotal in Patrick — qualitative, small N.* → Triangulated with Wagner's quantitative survey evidence and Im & Komp-Leukkunen on workfare support.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Open questions</summary>
		- The cascade is theorised in three steps (identity → misattribution → othering) — within-individual ordering not yet established.
		- Are there contexts (e.g., strong unions) that interrupt step 1 even under stigmatising welfare? Mixed evidence in Nordic chauvinism literature. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/3217844a449d80e4905ff51eed0bdae7"/>
		- Does Bonomi-Gennaioli-Tabellini's model accommodate the welfare-design upstream variable, or does it require modification?
	</details>

## §III.D — The Recursive Loop {toggle="true"}
	<details>
	<summary>Direct quotes</summary>
		- "Poor welfare performance reduces political trust, and reduced trust makes future evaluations more negative — a 'downbound spiral'." — Haugsgjerd & Kumlin 2020 paraphrase (paper §III.D). → self-sustaining damage dynamic.
		- "In reaction, many tend to harshen their views on entitlements for those who are seen as even less deserving than themselves in what we could call a 'downward spiral' of solidarity erosion." — Wagner 2022, p. 2. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e27844a449d80e2b4cdce2a5e7c7b83"/> → recipient-level expression of the loop.
		- "The most vulnerable simultaneously support redistribution in principle and distrust the welfare state in practice." — Van Hootegem, Abts & Meuleman 2021. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/3217844a449d80e78c32efe844f84e81"/> → the attitudinal contradiction the loop produces; the political space the radical right occupies.
		- "Radical-right voters support a 'particularistic-authoritarian' welfare model: moderate transfers for 'deserving' recipients, strong workfare, low social investment." — Busemeyer, Rathgeb & Sahm 2023 paraphrase. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1f67844a449d8004beb1f247382d522e"/> → loop's terminal political configuration.
		- "Policy feedback running backwards — preferences that are outputs of policy appear as inputs." — paper §III.D framing. → self-concealing nature of the damage.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Statistics &amp; empirical findings</summary>
		- Haugsgjerd & Kumlin 2020 (panel): bidirectional negative feedback between welfare evaluations and trust.
		- Van Hootegem, Abts & Meuleman 2021: socio-economically vulnerable individuals hold *both* more egalitarian principles AND more negative welfare-state evaluations — paradox the loop predicts. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/3217844a449d80e78c32efe844f84e81"/>
		- Busemeyer, Rathgeb & Sahm 2023: PRRP voters show distinctive joint preference structure — pro-workfare, anti-poor, anti-social-investment — across multiple country cases. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1f67844a449d8004beb1f247382d522e"/>
		- Welfare retrenchments (Larsen 2017 etc.): activate status concerns even where universal coverage softens material impact. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/3217844a449d8099b7c0f994403a9b9a"/>
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Objections to pre-empt</summary>
		- *Pierson 1994's positive feedback was the original "new politics" — your reverse loop contradicts the canon.* → Pierson identified positive lock-in for popular programmes; the reverse loop runs through stigmatising programmes specifically. Same mechanism, opposite sign.
		- *The loop is too neat — real polities have many entry/exit points.* → §III.D names this; the loop is a tendency, not deterministic. Universal-design entry breaks it.
		- *Van Hootegem's paradox might just be ideological inconsistency, not a loop.* → Wagner's panel-style evidence on welfare-reform exposure → chauvinism increase rules out the pure-inconsistency reading.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Open questions</summary>
		- Has any country interrupted the loop empirically once initiated? Candidate cases unclear.
		- Time-scale of the recursive loop: how many electoral cycles before damage becomes legible?
		- Does the loop weaken or strengthen with social-investment expansion (Wagner's enabling-policy hypothesis)?
	</details>

## §III.E — Why the Mirror Image Does Not Exist {toggle="true"}
	<details>
	<summary>Direct quotes</summary>
		- "Losses loom larger than equivalent gains." — Kahneman & Tversky 1979, *Econometrica* 47(2), p. 279 (paper §III.E). → loss aversion underwrites the asymmetry on the affect side.
		- "What right-populist voters want is recognition in the relational sense, not redistribution in the material sense, and relational goods cannot be redistributed without losses to the currently-recognised." — paper §III.E paraphrase of Gidron & Hall 2017. → status is a positional good.
		- "Voters whose expectations are unmet show large political shifts, while voters whose expectations are exceeded show much weaker ones." — Kurer & van Staalduinen 2022 paraphrase. → independent confirmation of asymmetric expectations response.
		- "Conservative-authoritarian voters as being 'a few rungs up' from the bottom… last-place-aversion making them prone to emphasizing and legitimizing hierarchies." — Häusermann, Kurer & Zollinger 2023. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1f47844a449d8006a0f5e44c0decedb1"/> → why dignity-preserving welfare cannot, by symmetric operation, produce solidarity.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Statistics &amp; empirical findings</summary>
		- Kurer & van Staalduinen 2022 (APSR): unmet-expectation voters show large rightward shifts; exceeded-expectation voters show weak leftward ones — empirical asymmetry independent of this paper.
		- Gidron & Hall 2017: subjective social status declines for non-college men 1987–2014 across most OECD; redistribution expansion does not reverse the decline trend. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e37844a449d80e3ae49e2eea1f3cfcf"/>
		- Ciccolini 2025 (Perspectives on Politics): economic status loss predicts PRRP voting independently of financial loss — supports positional-status reading. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e37844a449d8042b8bbc92f3b7fc0af"/>
		- Häusermann 2024 review: extensive universal provision conveys recognition beyond material security but does not autonomously construct active solidarity. (See <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e37844a449d806caddacd103d3711d9"/> for adjacent review.)
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Objections to pre-empt</summary>
		- *Loss aversion is borrowed from individual decision-making — applying it to status is metaphor, not mechanism.* → Conceded openly in §IV. Kurer & van Staalduinen provides the closest empirical analogue.
		- *Status is not strictly positional — collective status increases (e.g., postwar working-class dignity) are historically observable.* → Those increases were achieved by political work (unions, parties), not by welfare design alone — consistent with the asymmetric account.
		- *Defensive othering can be reversed (e.g., by intergroup contact).* → Yes, but at higher political cost than maintenance of un-othered states; reversibility is not symmetry.
	</details>
	<details>
	<summary>Open questions</summary>
		- Are there any historical natural experiments of welfare-design *expansion* producing measurable solidarity uplift independent of party mobilisation? Garritzmann/Häusermann/Palier 2022 hints at coalition mechanisms but does not isolate design effects. <mention-page url="https://www.notion.so/1e37844a449d806caddacd103d3711d9"/>
		- Could a registry-based panel detect the loss-aversion claim within-individual?
		- Is the irreversibility of defensive identity investments testable, or definitionally circular?
	</details>

## §III.F — What the Asymmetric Theory Predicts {toggle="true"}
	<details>
