## The gap in §III.E that this fills

The seminar paper's §III.E currently explains why no symmetric solidarity-side mechanism exists through three pillars: loss aversion (Kahneman-Tversky 1979), positional status (Gidron-Hall 2017), and irreversibility of defensive othering (extending Pierson 1994). The third pillar then transitions to: *"Active solidarity requires political work that welfare design alone cannot do (Iversen and Soskice 2001; Rothstein 2001; Larsen 2008). Welfare provides permission, not propulsion."*

This is correct as far as it goes. It is also the weakest passage in §III.E because it gestures at a missing mechanism (*what kind* of political work, *why* welfare design specifically cannot do it) without naming it. Iversen-Soskice / Rothstein / Larsen are loaded into a parenthetical citation that does the rhetorical labour the prose itself does not.

A sharper framing exists inside Ben's curatorial corpus and is available to do that labour.

## The framing

**[grounded — direct quote-evidence from `docs/A Mind in Formation with part 6.md`]** Ben has saved hooks's distinction in *All About Love* between *cathexis* (preoccupation with the object of attachment), *care* (the action of providing for someone's wellbeing), and *love* (which contains both but is not reducible to either). The line is: *"cathexis is not love; care is a dimension of love but caregiving alone is not loving."* Translated structurally: care without connection is not love. Provision without relation is not relationship.

The same structural distinction maps onto welfare-design-and-solidarity. Decommodification — the capacity of welfare to enable workers to maintain living standards without market employment — is *care* in hooks's sense: the institution provides for material wellbeing. Solidarity, as a political achievement, is *connection*: it requires the relational thickness of shared workplaces, shared organising, shared deliberation, shared ritual. Decommodification can clear the ground for connection but cannot construct it. *Welfare states that succeed at decommodification have set their architecture for transaction, not relation.*

This is not a hedge on the asymmetric claim. It is a sharpened version of it. The asymmetric mechanism's solidarity-side null is not "welfare design *might* produce solidarity but our power is too low to detect it." It is *"welfare design as currently constituted across the 15 Western European countries observed delivers material care without delivering the relational thickness that solidarity-as-political-achievement requires, and the absence of solidarity-uplift in the data is therefore mechanism-consistent rather than mechanism-failing."*

## What this changes in the §III.E argument

The three pillars stay. The third pillar (irreversibility of defensive othering) becomes more specific about *what* is irreversible: identity investments — the relational and self-conceptual choices a worker makes when committed to defensive othering — cannot be undone by changes in transfer levels because the work that built them was relational, not transactional. Welfare reform that increases generosity changes the transfer; it does not unbuild the relational architecture the prior welfare design helped construct.

The closing of §III.E — *"Welfare provides permission, not propulsion"* — gains a mechanism for why. Permission is a property of welfare design (decommodification clears the ground); propulsion would require relational construction (the political work of shared organising, deliberation, ritual) that welfare design alone cannot do.

This is consistent with the saved Iversen-Soskice / Rothstein / Larsen citations — Iversen-Soskice's asset theory predicts that *interest-based* coalitions form around welfare design but does not predict identity-thick solidarity from it; Rothstein's universal-welfare-and-trust argument is precisely about how relational practices around universal programmes (queueing for the same services, sharing the same provision) build trust, NOT about how universal generosity per se produces solidarity; Larsen's institutional logic argument is about deservingness narratives, which are relational artefacts of welfare design's communicative practices.

All three cited authors are saying something the current §III.E gestures at but does not name: welfare design's solidarity effect, where it exists, operates through relational practices that welfare design enables but does not constitute. The hooks frame sharpens the gesture into a claim.

## Candidate one-sentence integration

For the §III.E paragraph immediately preceding *"Three asymmetries, none with a mirror image..."*:

**Current:**
> *"Dignity-preserving welfare removes an obstacle to inclusive solidarity; it does not, by itself, construct the inclusion. Active solidarity requires political work that welfare design alone cannot do (Iversen and Soskice 2001; Rothstein 2001; Larsen 2008). Welfare provides permission, not propulsion."*

**Candidate revision:**
> *"Dignity-preserving welfare removes an obstacle to inclusive solidarity; it does not, by itself, construct the inclusion. The distinction is between care and connection: welfare design as currently constituted delivers material care across the Esping-Andersen regimes (decommodification, in Esping-Andersen's terms), but solidarity is a connection-thick political achievement — it requires the shared workplaces, organising work, and relational practices that decommodification can clear the ground for but cannot construct (Iversen and Soskice 2001; Rothstein 2001; Larsen 2008). Welfare provides permission, not propulsion."*

This is ~50 words added. Voice-compatible (Ben's register; no em-dashes; sentence-coordinated). It does substantive work: names *what* is missing on the solidarity side and *why* welfare design is structurally suited to provide care but not connection.

**Cost:** 5 minutes during the §III.E retype pass tomorrow. Net effect: §III.E's third pillar becomes legible rather than gestural; the asymmetric mechanism's solidarity-side argument carries more weight.

## What this generates for the thesis

The care/connection distinction is testable. If Y3 of the PhD is the mechanism-interrogation paper (per `projects/msc_thesis/STATUS.md`), this framing specifies what to test:

**Hypothesis:** Welfare programmes that combine care (material provision) with connection (peer interaction, deliberative practice, shared ritual) produce stronger solidarity dispositions than programmes delivering equivalent care without connection.

**Two-arm test design (survey-experimental, complementary to register linkage):**

- **Arm 1 — Transactional welfare condition:** Respondents read a vignette describing a means-tested cash-transfer programme. Peer interaction = none; provision = direct bureaucratic.
- **Arm 2 — Relational welfare condition:** Respondents read a vignette describing a programme with identical material support delivered through a participatory structure (e.g., community job centre with peer organising, employee-led training cooperative, Danish "fagforening"-mediated UI).
- **Outcome:** Standardised solidarity scale + redistributive-preference scale + radical-right vote-intent.

**Prediction:** Arm 2 shows stronger solidarity disposition + weaker exclusionary disposition than Arm 1, with effect size comparable to the cross-national CWED moderation in the seminar paper.

**[adjacent on feasibility]** This is the standard Stantcheva-style elicitation design ported to a welfare-architecture manipulation. Should run on a single ESS-wave or Pew-style sample (N ≈ 2,000).

**Register-linked complement:** Danish welfare programmes vary on the relational-vs-transactional dimension. *Dagpenge* (UI) varies by union membership (members get UI via their fagforening, which has organising activity attached; non-members get UI via direct A-kasse, which is more transactional). Identify members vs non-members in the BEF register, link to political-behaviour outcomes (Folketing electoral register), test whether the moderation by welfare context interacts with relational-vs-transactional receipt mode.

**Causal-identification path:** Mass layoffs around the 2010 dagpengereform → newly eligible UI claimants. The relational-vs-transactional channel is observable in who joins which A-kasse. Restrict to involuntarily-separated workers (Y1 thesis sample); test attitudinal path-dependence by A-kasse type. If care-without-connection is right, transactional A-kasse claimants should show weaker solidarity uplift than fagforening-channelled claimants, controlling for transfer amount.

## How this connects to the two-channel theory

The care-without-connection memo is a sub-claim within the two-channel framing. Specifically: the environment channel of welfare design can build solidarity-supportive *vocabularies* (Rothstein's trust effect, Larsen's inclusive deservingness narratives), but solidarity *behaviour* requires the encounter channel to operate through *relational* programme designs, not just generous ones. The seminar paper's cross-national finding measures the environment channel's vocabulary effect on exclusion (large) and on solidarity (null). The thesis's within-individual register-linked design could test whether the encounter channel's relational sub-architecture produces solidarity differently than its transactional sub-architecture.

This is the same intellectual move as the seminar paper made for exclusion (decommodification, not spending, is the dimension), one level deeper: within decommodification, relational provision, not transactional provision, is the sub-dimension along which solidarity construction is plausible.
