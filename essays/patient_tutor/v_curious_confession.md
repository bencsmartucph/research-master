# Curious

*Version: short lyrical confession. ~750 words. Best for Substack short-form or as a teaser piece. Fragmented form, short paragraphs, voice over information density. The shortest version. Anchors marked [RETYPE].*

---

[RETYPE — opening from memory]

I have been afraid of the wrong thing.

For about a year now I have been carrying around a low-grade unease about the way I use AI in my master's. The unease has the shape of a question I never quite ask out loud: am I cheating? The question is the wrong question, but it is the question available to me, because it is the question the discourse asks. I have been answering it the way you answer a question you do not believe in but cannot get rid of. Yes. No. A little. Probably not. It depends on what you mean.

Two weeks ago, on a Tuesday morning, an AI tutor I had been quietly worrying about caught a methodological error in my paper. The kind of error a careful supervisor would have caught. The kind I had assumed, for fourteen months, that no one was going to catch.

I do not know what to do with this exactly. I want to register that I do not know.

The thing I had been describing, in my own head, as cheating turned out to be the thing keeping me honest. The thing I had been describing, in my paper, as a methodology turned out to be a different methodology than the one I was actually using. The shame I had been low-grade carrying about the first turned out to be misdirected. The pride I had been low-grade carrying about the second turned out to be premature. Both feelings were real. Neither was correctly aimed.

I have spent some time, since the discovery, trying to figure out which of my prior beliefs about my own work were actually true and which were stories I had been telling myself to feel a certain way about what I was doing. The list is longer than I expected.

I am, it turns out, more curious than the version of me that wrote the paper. The tools have not made me less curious. They have, in some way I am still figuring out, given the curious version of me more room to operate.

Most of the writing I have read about AI in education is about a different student than the one I am. The student in those essays is impatient, transactional, looking for the answer with the lowest effort cost. I am sure that student exists. I have been that student, on assignments I did not care about, on subjects I had not chosen. I am not that student on the subjects I love. I do not think I am the only person in this position.

There is a kind of writing about AI that wants to be either a warning or a sales pitch. I do not have a warning to issue and I am not selling anything. What I have, I think, is a small confession: I have been afraid of the wrong thing, and the thing I should have been paying attention to was happening underneath the thing I was afraid of.

What was happening underneath: I have been getting more curious. Not because the model is making me curious — the model does not make anyone anything — but because the cost of being curious has fallen, and the curiosity I always had now has somewhere to go.

This is not a triumphant story. I have not had a breakthrough, exactly. I have not become a better academic, exactly. What has happened is more boring than that, and probably more durable. I have stopped feeling guilty about a thing I am no longer sure was wrong. I have started paying more attention to a thing I had been ignoring. The thing I had been ignoring is what curiosity actually feels like when it is not rationed by time.

It feels, mostly, like a Tuesday morning in late April. There is a model on one screen and the data on another. There is a problem you have been chasing for a week. There is a question the model asks that you cannot answer. You go look. You come back. The work is not finished, but the work has direction now, which it did not have an hour ago.

[RETYPE — closing paragraph from memory]

A friend asked me recently whether I think the discourse about AI in education is broadly right or broadly wrong. I have been chewing on the question for a few weeks. The honest answer is that I think the discourse is right about a kind of student I am not, and is missing a kind of student I am, and that the second kind of student is more numerous than the first kind of writing seems to assume. We are quiet. We are not the people writing the essays. We have other things to do. But we are here, and we are using these tools the way the discourse assumes we are not, and the work we are doing is the most curious we have been in years. I do not know what to call this. I do not know if it needs a name. It is, for the moment, what I have.
