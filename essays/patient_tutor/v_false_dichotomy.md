# The False Dichotomy

*Version: contested-thesis essay. ~1450 words. Best for LinkedIn or a Substack first post. Audience: anyone wrestling with the cheating-vs-productivity discourse around AI tools. Anchors marked [RETYPE].*

---

[RETYPE — opening from memory]

Why does using AI to write my paper feel like cheating, while using AI to understand my paper feels like the most honest thing I have done all year? I have been carrying this question around for a few months, and an answer arrived recently in an unexpected form. The same tool I had been worrying might be hollowing out my work turned out, two weeks ago, to have caught a methodological error in it. The paper described the analysis in §V.D one way; the analysis had actually been done a different way; the gap between the two had sat in the paper for fourteen months and nobody had noticed. The tutor's question — *are you sure that's the method?* — was the question a careful supervisor would have asked. I have not had a careful supervisor on this paper. I have had a Tuesday session with Claude.

I should probably feel weirder about this than I do. There is a version of the AI-and-learning conversation in which the writer admits to using the tools and then either apologises (the cheating frame) or evangelises (the productivity frame). I am going to do neither, partly because I find both narratives boring and partly because neither of them describes what has actually happened to me. What has actually happened to me is closer to this: a year ago I built an embarrassingly elaborate set of infrastructure around an AI model — a few markdown files, some skills calibrated to my own writing, a habit of refusing answers I had not earned — and the infrastructure has slowly turned the tool into an instrument for being more curious, not less. The shame I am supposed to feel about using AI keeps failing to arrive on schedule. The pride I am supposed to feel about doing the work the old way keeps showing up at the wrong moments. Both feelings are correct. They are also, I have come to believe, two halves of a false dichotomy that this essay is going to try to dismantle.

The dichotomy is dominant enough that it needs naming. On one side is the cheating frame: students use AI to bypass the work, the work is what produces learning, therefore AI use erodes learning. On the other side is the productivity frame: AI removes drudgery, drudgery was never the point, therefore AI use frees the student to focus on higher-order cognition. Both frames assume that the relationship between effort and learning is roughly linear — that more effort produces more learning, and that AI either subtracts effort (cheating) or substitutes effort (productivity). Both frames treat the student as a machine for converting effort into knowledge, with AI as the variable that adjusts the conversion rate.

The lived experience of using these tools well is not described by either frame. Effort does not have a fixed relationship to learning. Some kinds of effort produce learning at high rates; other kinds produce almost nothing. The best supervisors and the best teachers know this and structure the work accordingly; the worst ones grind their students through volume and call the resulting fatigue rigour. What AI tools do, when configured by someone who knows what they are doing, is shift the *kind* of effort the student is asked to put in — away from the kinds that produce little learning per hour (re-reading, copying, summarising) and toward the kinds that produce a lot (defending a choice, predicting a result before computing it, writing an explanation a non-specialist could follow). This is not a productivity gain in any honest sense. The total time a curious student spends on a hard subject does not fall when they start using AI tools well; it tends to rise, because the tools make it cheaper to do the things the curious student wanted to do anyway and would not otherwise have had the budget for.

That is the move the dichotomy misses. It assumes the student's time budget is fixed and that AI either lowers their effort floor (cheating) or raises their output ceiling (productivity). The actual effect is to lower the cost of curiosity. If the student wants to read 100 papers' methodology sections instead of 5, they can. If they want to understand a specification from the ground up instead of treating it as a black box, they can. If they want to catch the discrepancy between what their paper says and what their analysis does, they can. The tools do not make the student faster at being incurious; they make the student cheaper at being curious. Most of the AI-and-learning discourse is about the first kind of student. This essay is about the second.

Three short admissions, in the spirit of not letting the dichotomy off the hook by stacking my evidence too neatly.

I have used Claude to help design parts of my paper's empirical methodology. Specifically, I asked it to think through whether a random-slopes specification was identifiable in my data and what alternatives I should consider. Some version of the cheating critique applies here. If I had been doing this entirely on my own, I would have made worse choices and produced a worse paper, and the worse paper would have been more authentically mine. I do not think this trade is straightforward and I am not sure the version of academic authenticity that requires a worse paper is one I want to defend.

I have also built, over the course of a year, a research repository that I am genuinely proud of: 150 pages of methodology notes on 100 papers in my literature, 50 replication datasets re-run from scratch on my own machine, a metadata graph linking what was studied in each paper to the theoretical questions I care about. None of this work would have been done without the AI tools. None of it would have been done with the AI tools alone, either. The tools made it cheap enough to be worth doing; the curiosity made it worth doing in the first place. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient.

And one more, because the supervisor implication is the line I am most worried about being misread. The fact that an AI tutor caught a methodological error in my paper is not a claim that AI tutors are better than human supervisors. It is a claim that an AI tutor, configured well and used regularly, is functionally better than the supervision a graduate student in my position can realistically expect to receive. My supervisor is not a worse academic than the AI; my supervisor is a busier one. The AI tutor had time to ask me whether I was sure about the slope-extraction method. My supervisor, if they had been asked the same question by me, would have given the same answer. They were not asked. They were asked twenty other questions instead, by twenty other students, and the result is that the question that mattered did not get asked by anyone except, eventually, the model.

The pedagogical literature has a name for what these tools, deployed well, are doing: they are activating practices the literature has known about for decades but has rarely been able to deliver on demand. Roediger and Karpicke (2006) on retrieval practice. Sweller and Renkl on worked-example fading. Bjork on desirable difficulties. None of these techniques are intrinsic to AI; all of them are difficult to access without it. The interesting story is not that AI invented new pedagogy. The interesting story is that AI has made old pedagogy affordable for the first time, and the people who notice will gain disproportionately from the people who do not.

The honest limits matter. The tutor sometimes gets things wrong; my own session caught it twice in one afternoon, once when it misidentified which code path produced the published correlation, and once when a convergence error made it report an infinite log-likelihood instead of the actual test statistic. Both errors were caught by the verification process the workflow requires; neither would have been caught without it. The model also confidently agrees with mistakes more often than it should, which is why every claim it makes that you intend to use needs the explicit follow-up: *what is the strongest reason this answer might be wrong?* And one more limit, the one I am least comfortable with but think is true: the practice presumes resources — time, prior education, the kind of self-knowledge that lets you write down what works for you — that are not equally distributed. The tools that turn into a tutor for me are turning into a worksheet generator for someone else. The asymmetry is not a property of the model. It is a property of who learns how to prompt it.

[RETYPE — closing paragraph from memory]

Both feelings are still correct. I am going to keep being uneasy about parts of how I have used these tools. I am also going to keep being proud of the work I have done with them, because that work is real and the curiosity that drove it is mine. The dichotomy I started with is false in the specific way that most dichotomies are false: the choice it asks you to make is not the choice you actually have. The choice you actually have is whether to use these tools to be more curious or to be less curious, faster. If you make the second choice, the discourse about cheating is correct, and you should listen to it. If you make the first choice, the discourse is correct about a kind of student you are not, and you should keep going. The supervision available to graduate students is what it is. The tools available to graduate students have changed. The first will catch up to the second eventually. In the meantime, I have a Tuesday session with Claude.
