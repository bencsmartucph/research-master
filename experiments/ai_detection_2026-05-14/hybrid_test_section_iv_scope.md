The absence of damage is necessary but not sufficient for solidarity. Universal programmes build social trust (Rothstein 2001) and produce inclusive deservingness narratives (Larsen 2008); wide universal provision conveys recognition beyond mere material security (Häusermann 2024); and the Nordic welfare states keep showing lower populist support even as globalisation pressures press down on them (Norris and Inglehart 2019). All of this holds. Yet on the asymmetric reading, what these findings record is the *baseline* dignity that welfare keeps in place, not the upward uplift the buffering model takes for granted. The political work that turns baseline dignity into active solidarity (Bornschier, Haffert, and Häusermann 2024; Jeffrey 2020) lies beyond what I tackle here. Welfare design lets that work happen; it does not itself produce it.

Welfare institutions also do not land on a blank moral slate. Pelc's (2025) finding that the Protestant work ethic predicts compensation resistance independently of material incentives hints at attitudes that exist prior to institutional contact. The welfare state shapes moral frameworks. It also inherits ones already there.

The loss aversion claim in §III.E borrows from behavioural economics by analogy. Supporting empirical work (Kurer and van Staalduinen 2022; Häusermann 2024) fits the claim well; still, it does not put the claim to a direct test. The within-individual test belongs to the registry-based follow-up. The cross-sectional finding here is large, steady, and predicted by the theory; it remains provisional in a way the asymmetric framing already acknowledges.
