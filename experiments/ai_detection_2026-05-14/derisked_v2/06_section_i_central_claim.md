This produces a theoretical claim sharper than the symmetric compensatory account it replaces. More forceful; less forgiving. We argue that the political effects of welfare design are asymmetric. Welfare institutions can fail politically. They do so through a specific, cumulative process. They damage the self-concept of vulnerable workers; this triggers a damage cascade of identity switching (Bonomi, Gennaioli & Tabellini 2021), misattribution (Gallego & Kurer 2022), and defensive othering (Wagner 2022; Patrick 2016). They cannot succeed through symmetric operation. Not even close. Variation in how routine workers convert disruption into exclusion proves robust across every specification in our data. Variation in how those same workers convert identical disruption into redistributive solidarity? Small. Non-significant. Not detectably moderated by welfare context. Dignity-preserving welfare clears ground for solidarity; it does not, by itself, produce solidarity.

Our central claim is twofold. First: welfare institutions moderate the conversion of automation exposure into exclusionary attitudes through their decommodifying quality; spending level does not drive this moderation. Second: this moderation is asymmetric. It operates reliably toward damage. Toward repair? On the data we develop here, not detectably. Both halves permit testing. We can evaluate each. The second proves harder to sustain; most of §III is devoted to defending it.
