The cascade feeds itself, and it does so with a kind of grim steadiness. Conditional welfare erodes how individuals perceive themselves. These diminished self-perceptions breed othering; othering, in turn, shapes the public sentiments that demand yet more conditions to satisfy. Political entrepreneurs respond readily with welfare chauvinism. The tightened rules then deepen the harm all over again, round and round. Paradoxically, the loop culminates in a citizenry that has forgotten the very stance it now holds was constructed for it by the mechanisms it claims to reject.

Haugsgjerd and Kumlin (2020) lay bare this pattern through panel data. Weak welfare outcomes erode political trust; that diminished trust renders later assessments bleaker still, what they term a "downbound spiral." Van Hootegem, Abts, and Meuleman (2021) provide a name for the attitudinal contradiction the loop produces. Those most adversely affected endorse the principle of redistributing wealth, yet they distrust the welfare state that would enact such redistribution. Here, precisely here, lies the gap the radical right fills. On my reading, this opening is wrought by the loop itself; it is not encountered by happy chance.

The loop conceals its own handiwork, which is perhaps its most effective trick. Because the welfare state generates the attitudes that underwrite its own erosion, each iteration feels more natural than the last. Choices that flow from policy appear to have preceded it. This is policy feedback operating in reverse. It is not Pierson's (1994) positive feedback building supportive coalitions, but its mirror image; coalitions turned against the very instruments that would address their everyday needs. The cascade lands, fittingly, in the space Busemeyer, Rathgeb, and Sahm (2023) name with precision: the "particularistic-authoritarian" welfare stance, pro-workfare, set against the poor, indifferent toward social advancement, the manifesto of the radical right, shaped in part by the welfare institutions it claims to oppose.
