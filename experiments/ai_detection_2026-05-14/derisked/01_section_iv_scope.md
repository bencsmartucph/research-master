The lack of harm is needed, but it is not enough for fellow-feeling. Universal schemes build shared trust (Rothstein 2001) and bring about open, welcoming tales of who ought to get help (Larsen 2008); wide universal provision gives something beyond mere material safety, a kind of being-seen (Häusermann 2024); and the Nordic welfare states keep showing lower backing for populist movements even as worldwide market shifts press down on them (Norris and Inglehart 2019). All of this holds true. Yet on the lopsided reading, what these findings pick up is the *floor*, the bare dignity that welfare keeps in place, not the upward push the buffering model takes for granted. The political work that turns baseline worth into something livelier, real solidarity (Bornschier, Haffert, and Häusermann 2024; Jeffrey 2020), lies beyond what I tackle here. Welfare design lets that work happen; it does not itself do the doing.

Welfare bodies do not land on a blank moral sheet, either. Pelc's (2025) finding that a Protestant work ethic foretells pushback against payouts, free of material drives, hints at stances that come before any touch with the state. The welfare state moulds moral frames. It also steps into ones already there.

Now, the loss-wariness claim in §III.E borrows from behavioural thought by way of likeness. Backing fieldwork (Kurer and van Staalduinen 2022; Häusermann 2024) fits the claim well; still, it does not put the claim to a straight test. The within-person check belongs to the registry-linked follow-up. The cross-section finding here is big, steady, and foretold by the framework; it stays open to question in a way the lopsided framing already owns up to.
