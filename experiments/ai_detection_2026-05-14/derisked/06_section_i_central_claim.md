This yields a stronger claim, frankly, than the neat symmetrical story it sets aside. The political workings of welfare design, I would argue, are lopsided. Welfare setups can fail politically in a step-by-step, building way; they wound the self-understanding of workers at risk and set off a chain of identity switching (Bonomi, Gennaioli & Tabellini 2021), misreading of causes (Gallego & Kurer 2022), and defensive othering (Wagner 2022; Patrick 2016). They cannot, through the same workings in reverse, win. The spread in how everyday workers turn hardship into shutting others out holds steady across every model this paper runs; meanwhile, the spread in how those same workers turn the same hardship into fellow-feeling for redistribution is slight, not meaningful, and shows no clear link to welfare setting. Dignity-keeping welfare clears ground for solidarity. It does not, on its own, bring solidarity about.

The heart of the paper's claim, then, is twofold. First, welfare setups shape how automation exposure becomes exclusionary feeling through their decommodifying character rather than their spending height. Second, this shaping is one-sided; it works steadily toward harm but not, in the findings built here, noticeably toward mending. Both halves can be tested. The second is the tougher one to hold up, and most of §III is given over to defending it.
