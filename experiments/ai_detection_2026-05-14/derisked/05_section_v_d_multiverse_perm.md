Denmark makes for a tricky case. High CWED generosity notwithstanding, the country shows a fairly steep RTI→exclusion slope (β=0.50); steeper, indeed, than Finland, Sweden, or Norway. Danish "flexicurity" yokes together handsome benefits with loose hiring-and-firing rules and stiff job-search duties. The upshot is a setup that gives freely in transfers yet asks much in activation. On the asymmetric reading, this is no oddity but rather one more bit of backing: oversight and strings attached eat away at self-worth even when the money flows thick. The 2003, 2006, and 2013 reforms that brought in activation are just the sort of within-land jolt the asymmetric framework holds should leave harm-marks while decommodification stays put.

The land-level finding holds up under every one-land drop. Leave out the United Kingdom (lowest CWED) and you get r=−0.802; leave out Norway (highest CWED) and you get r=−0.794; drop both at once and you still find r=−0.717 (p=0.006). Across all 105 two-land BLUP jackknife pairings, the link runs from r=−0.700 to r=−0.897, not one pair switches sign, and every last one of the 105 keeps its footing at p<0.05. No single case drives this; no pair does either. A land-label shuffle test (10,000 rounds) hands back a two-sided p=0.0001, which lines up with the parametric p<0.001.

The published BLUPs guess of r=−0.848 sits at the strong end of a sound spread. Take three estimators (bivariate land-by-land OLS at r=−0.625, BLUPs at r=−0.855, inverse-variance-weighted at r=−0.753) and run them through all 105 leave-two-out cuts; not one setup yields a sign flip. The leave-two-out band spans r ∈ [−0.922, −0.335]. Shrinkage makes the BLUPs estimator give the tightest link, as one would guess; bivariate OLS gives the loosest; inverse-variance-weighted falls between. The tie-up is sturdy across estimator picks. The size of it, well, less so.
