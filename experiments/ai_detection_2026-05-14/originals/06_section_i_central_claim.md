This generates a stronger theoretical claim than the symmetric compensatory account it replaces. The political effects of welfare design, I contend, are asymmetric. Welfare institutions can fail politically in a specific, cumulative way; they damage the self-concept of vulnerable workers and trigger a cascade of identity switching (Bonomi, Gennaioli & Tabellini 2021), misattribution (Gallego & Kurer 2022), and defensive othering (Wagner 2022; Patrick 2016). They cannot, by symmetric operation, succeed. Indeed, variation in how routine workers convert disruption into exclusion is robust across every specification of this paper's data, while the variation in how those same workers convert the same disruption into redistributive solidarity is small, non-significant, and not detectably moderated by welfare context. Dignity-preserving welfare clears the ground for solidarity, but it does not, by itself, produce it.

The central claim of the paper, therefore, is twofold. Firstly, welfare institutions moderate the conversion of automation exposure into exclusionary attitudes through their decommodifying quality rather than their spending level. Secondly, this moderation is asymmetric: it operates reliably toward damage but not, on the data developed here, detectably toward repair. Both halves are testable. The second is the harder one to sustain, and most of §III is given to defending it.
