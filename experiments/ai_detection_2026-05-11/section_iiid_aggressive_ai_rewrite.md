Indeed, the cascade documented in §III.C does not stop where it begins. Once conditional welfare has damaged a worker's self-concept, the damage radiates outward in the same direction the institutional architecture pointed it; damaged self-concepts produce othering, and othering generates the public attitudes that license further conditionality. Political entrepreneurs respond to those attitudes with welfare chauvinism. The further conditionality deepens the damage. Through this perspective, the loop runs into a population that has, over time, forgotten that the position it now defends so vigorously was something the institution itself helped construct for it.

Haugsgjerd and Kumlin (2020) document one signature of the dynamic in their Norwegian panel data: poor welfare performance reduces political trust; reduced trust then makes future welfare evaluations more negative still; the authors call this a "downbound spiral". Van Hootegem, Abts, and Meuleman (2021) name a connected attitudinal pattern: the most economically vulnerable citizens simultaneously support redistribution as a principle and distrust the welfare state as an institution. The gap between principled and operational support is the political space radical right parties occupy. On my reading, that space is constructed by the loop rather than merely discovered by political entrepreneurs.

Furthermore, the loop has a curious self-concealing property. Because the welfare state is producing the very attitudes that then legitimate its own retrenchment, each iteration of the loop appears, to the actors involved, more natural than the last. Preferences that are outputs of the policy appear as inputs to it. Effectively, this is policy feedback running in reverse. Pierson (1994) describes positive feedback, in which generous welfare programmes construct supportive constituencies who go on to defend them. The damage cascade does the opposite: it generates constituencies arrayed against the mechanisms that would best serve their material interests. The cascade ends, as Busemeyer, Rathgeb, and Sahm (2023) put it, in a "particularistic-authoritarian" welfare preference — pro-workfare, anti-poor, anti-social-investment, which is to say, the welfare programme of the radical right, partly generated by the welfare institutions it claims to oppose.
