The whole thing feeds itself. Conditional welfare chips away at how people see themselves; once that self-image cracks, othering follows. And othering breeds the very public mood that backs more conditions still. Political actors catch on quickly, riding the wave with welfare chauvinism. More conditions, more harm. Round and round it goes, until a whole population forgets that the stance it now holds was built for it, not by it.

Haugsgjerd and Kumlin (2020) lay this bare through panel data. Poor welfare outcomes wear down political trust; worn-down trust, in turn, sours how people interpret the next round of outcomes. A "downbound spiral," they call it. Here is the odd finding Van Hootegem, Abts, and Meuleman (2021) flag: those hit hardest support redistribution in the abstract yet mistrust the welfare state when it shows up in practice. That gap is where the radical right sets up shop. On my reading, the loop builds this opening; it does not stumble upon it.

The harm hides itself. Each turn of the wheel looks more like common sense than the one before, since the welfare state keeps spawning the attitudes that greenlight its own shrinking. What comes out of policy starts looking like what went in. This is policy feedback flipped on its head; not Pierson's (1994) positive feedback, which knits together coalitions that fight for a programme, but its dark twin. Constituencies form against the very tools that would serve their material interests. Busemeyer, Rathgeb, and Sahm (2023) give the endpoint a sharp name: the "particularistic-authoritarian" welfare preference. Pro-workfare, anti-poor, hostile to social investment. The radical right's playbook, grown in no small part by the welfare machinery it claims to oppose.
