Methodology Appendix

Interactive supplements for Dignity Is a Baseline: Welfare Institutions and the Asymmetric Politics of Economic Disruption
Welfare State Seminar — University of Copenhagen, 5 May 2026 — Ben Smart

This page collects the three interactive visualisations and eight static figures that underwrite the empirical analysis in §V of the paper. It is intended for the seminar Q&A and for any reader who wants to interrogate the methodological choices directly.

Three methodological decisions in particular are worth seeing rather than reading about, and each has its own interactive below: the cross-level interaction $\beta_3$, the choice of slope estimator for the §V.D country-level scatter (BLUPs vs bivariate OLS), and the cluster-robust standard error correction.

Toggle between bivariate and BLUP per-country slopes

Switch slope methodology and watch the correlation with CWED move from $r=-0.625$ (bivariate per-country OLS) to $r=-0.855$ (BLUPs from a random-slopes mixed model with controls). The published number is the BLUPs version. Hovering any country shows both estimates and the shrinkage gap. The "Both" view connects each country's two estimates with a dashed line, making the shrinkage-plus-partial-out visible country by country.

Same 15 countries, four methodologies: bivariate OLS $r=-0.625$  |  OLS with controls $r=-0.786$  |  BLUPs $r=-0.855$ ← published  |  country-wave averaged $r=-0.702$. Choice of slope methodology moves the headline correlation by 23 percentage points.

Interactive 3 — Cluster-robust SE inflation

Concept 4 — cluster-robust standard errors

How wrong is the naive standard error?

Heatmap of SE inflation factor across (intra-cluster correlation $\rho$, cluster size $\bar n$). Your paper's actual values appear as a red star. Hover any combination to see "naive SE is X× too small at this combo". Reference cases (classroom data, mild ICC, strong ICC) are marked for context.

Your country-wave clustering: $\bar n \approx 1{,}400$, $\rho \approx 0.05$. Design effect $= 1 + (\bar n - 1)\rho = 71$. SE inflation factor $= \sqrt{71} \approx 8.4\times$. Naive SE is roughly 8 times too small. Same point estimate either way; only the inference changes.

Conceptual anchor — why averages lie

Same mean, different slopes

Two synthetic countries with very different RTI-to-attitudes slopes (0.55 vs 0.18). A slider controls the gap between their mean attitudes. Drag the slider to zero — the bar chart confirms the means become identical, but the regression lines on the scatter barely move. The slope difference is preserved. Pedagogical point: the welfare-state moderation question is about slopes, not means; the multilevel model's job is to isolate the slope variation that naive comparison invisibly washes away.

Conceptual anchor — partial pooling and BLUPs

Three estimators on the same data

Twelve synthetic countries with varying sample sizes and noise levels. Three panels show what each estimator gives you: pure pooling (one global slope), no pooling (separate OLS per country, each weighted equally), and partial pooling (multilevel — small-N countries get shrunk toward the grand mean). A scenario toggle switches between low-noise/large-samples, realistic, and high-noise/small-samples conditions. Watch what happens to the no-pooling lines as data quality drops.

Why partial pooling wins under noise: RMSE versus the true slopes. Low-noise scenario: no pooling 0.009, partial 0.008 (essentially identical). Realistic: 0.035 vs 0.033. High-noise: 0.200 vs 0.152 — partial pooling 24% better. Partial pooling shines exactly when data is poor; that is the entire reason BLUPs are the principled choice for Figure 6.
