## SLIDE 0 — Title (20 s)

[Two slow breaths before you start. Look at one face in the back of the room.]

Good afternoon. The thirty-second version of what I'm about to argue: workers exposed to automation are systematically more anti-immigration than their material interests would predict. The gap is bigger in some welfare states than others. The conventional explanation — that welfare buffers the political backlash — is wrong about the mechanism. The mechanism is asymmetric. I'll show you why.

## SLIDE 1 — The puzzle (90 s)

The robust empirical regularity, established now across a dozen comparative-political-economy papers — Gingrich, Kurer, Im and colleagues, Autor, Gallego and Kurer — is that workers in routine-task-intensive occupations across Europe disproportionately support populist radical right parties. Routine-task-intensive, or RTI: occupations where the bulk of the work is repetitive cognitive or manual tasks, the ones automation hits hardest. Those workers vote populist right at higher rates than their incomes alone would predict.

There is also a second pattern, established by Vlandas and Halikiopoulou, by Caselli and colleagues. The same automation exposure converts into anti-immigration sentiment more readily in some welfare states than others. Same exposure, different political response. That's the variation this paper is about.

The standard reading of these two patterns is the buffering account: welfare buffers the backlash. Spend more, get less populism. Embedded liberalism — democratic governments compensate the dislocated, and compensation dampens political resentment.

My claim is that the buffering account has the right intuition that welfare design matters politically, but the wrong specification of *how*. The political effects of welfare design are asymmetric: detectable on damage, not detectable on repair. And they travel along *what welfare communicates*, not how much it spends.

That's the ninety-second version. The rest of the talk is the argument and the evidence.

[Click.]

## SLIDE 2 — The literature is split (90 s)

Two literatures have been speaking past each other on this question.

On one side, the buffering account. Theoretical anchor: Ruggie's embedded liberalism bargain. Compensation as quantity dampens political insecurity. Modern applications by Vlandas, Halikiopoulou, Ennser-Jedenastik. Burgoon and Schakel find welfare generosity reduces anti-globalisation rhetoric in party platforms — that's a real finding. The operative variable in this account is quantity. More spending, less populism.

But three streams of evidence push back. Gingrich's cross-national: generous welfare states show no weaker automation-to-populism effect. Stutzmann on the German coal phase-out: communities received substantial compensation; abstention rose anyway. Pelc: workers refuse compensation that fully replaces their income. Three different designs, same direction. Material compensation is doing less of the political work than the buffering account assumes.

On the other side, the recognition literature has been pointing at what's missing. Kurer: traditional welfare may be insufficient when relative decline drives resentment. Kurer and Palier: routine workers demand economic *and* cultural protection — the second cannot be supplied through transfers. Gidron and Hall: right-populist voters care about recognition as much as redistribution.

Both literatures see real patterns. Neither captures the asymmetry. My contribution reconciles them by identifying *which dimension of welfare design* the political effects actually travel along: not quantity, but quality.

[Click.]

## SLIDE 3 — The argument (75 s)

Welfare institutions are *asymmetric* in their political effects.

[Point to the left red callout.]

They can fail politically by degrading the self-concept of workers in vulnerable positions, conditioning a documented chain of psychological responses to engage in sequence. I'll show you the chain in a moment — it's the core of the argument.

[Point to the right green callout.]

They cannot, by symmetric operation, succeed. Producing solidarity requires constructive political work that welfare design alone cannot do. The mirror-image mechanism — dignifying welfare produces solidarity directly — does not exist. I'll give you three reasons in a moment, and then the empirical evidence.

[Pause. Point to the centre thesis.]

Dignity is a baseline good. Its absence damages. Its presence clears the ground for solidarity without producing it.

That sentence is the paper's title in compressed form. It's also the move that distinguishes this paper from the buffering literature it's responding to.

[Click.]

## SLIDE 4 — Why welfare and not something else (60 s)

A reasonable objection at this point: many institutions shape identity. Media construct group boundaries. Religious traditions supply moral narratives. Class structures generate interest coalitions. Why isolate welfare?

The answer is that welfare is the single state domain where economic vulnerability and institutional treatment meet at the same moment. When a worker encounters the welfare state, the institution allocates resources *and* renders judgement about the worker's claim to those resources, in the same act.

[Point to the table.]

Courts judge but rarely allocate. Markets allocate without judgement, or pretend to. Religious institutions judge but cannot compel. Only welfare does both, at the point of maximum material dependence. That combination is what makes welfare uniquely identity-forming.

[Click.]

## SLIDE 5 — The damage cascade (105 s)

Three responses follow when welfare institutions damage the self-concept. Each builds on, and is harder to reverse than, the one before.

[Trace left to right with your finger or the laser.]

A stigmatising welfare encounter degrades class identity directly. Soss documents this empirically in US welfare programmes; Wagner traces the dignity-injury mechanism explicitly.

When class identity is damaged, cultural identity activates. Bonomi, Gennaioli, and Tabellini model this exactly: individuals shift from class to cultural identification when class identity is degraded by economic disruption. Ballard-Rosa and colleagues show the downstream consequence — economic decline activates social identity processes producing authoritarian values.

Once cultural identity is in charge, grievances misattribute. Wu shows this empirically: workers at higher automation risk oppose immigration but show no different technology preferences. The grievance attaches to the cognitively available scapegoat, not to the actual source.

The third move is defensive othering. Patrick documents UK benefit claimants shoring up their own deservingness through critique of those below them, using the welfare system's own criteria. Wagner calls this *kicking down*.

The cascade ends in what Busemeyer, Rathgeb, and Sahm call the *particularistic-authoritarian* welfare preference: pro-workfare, anti-poor, anti-social-investment. The political programme of a self-concept the welfare state has helped construct.

[Pause. Look up from the slide.]

Each step has its own published literature. What's mine is the *connection* — the claim that these mechanisms link in sequence, that welfare design is the institutional condition under which they engage together, and that no symmetric protective mechanism exists with comparable evidence.

[Click.]

## SLIDE 6 — Why no symmetric mirror image (75 s)

The symmetric account would predict that dignifying welfare produces solidarity through a mirror-image cascade: stable identity, structural attribution, inclusive deservingness. The empirical null on the solidarity side, which I'll show you in a moment, says otherwise. And on my reading, the null is what the mechanism actually predicts. Three reasons.

[Point to the first column.]

First, loss aversion. Kahneman and Tversky: losses loom larger than equivalent gains. A stigmatising welfare encounter registers as a status loss. A dignity-preserving encounter does not register as a status *gain* of equivalent magnitude. Damage mobilises; the absence of damage tends not to.

[Second column.]

Second, status is positional. Gidron and Hall make the point exactly: what right-populist voters want is recognition in the relational sense, not redistribution in the material sense. Relational goods cannot be redistributed without losses to the currently-recognised. Dignity-preserving welfare removes an obstacle to inclusive solidarity; it does not, by itself, construct the inclusion.

[Third column.]

Third, defensive othering, once committed to, is costly to reverse. A worker who has built their own deservingness on critique of those below them has made an identity investment, and identity investments don't unwind by changing the institutional environment alone.

Three asymmetries, none with a mirror image. That's the prediction the empirical work tests.

[Click.]

## SLIDE 7 — Empirical setup (60 s)

European Social Survey, rounds 6 through 9, fieldwork 2012 to 2018. Thirty-four countries, N equals 188,764. The headline analysis restricts to the fifteen Western European countries with CWED welfare-quality data — that's the sample with both individual-level outcomes and country-level decommodification scores.

The estimator is a cross-level interaction with random slopes at the country-wave level, country-wave fixed effects, and cluster-robust standard errors as a parallel robustness check. The design is cross-sectional, so the claim throughout is *consistency* with the asymmetric mechanism, not causation.

Two competing welfare measures. ALMP — active labour market policy spending as a percentage of GDP. The measure the buffering literature typically uses. Quantity. And CWED — Esping-Andersen's decommodification index, capturing how effectively the welfare system enables a worker to maintain a livelihood without market dependence. Quality.

The asymmetric mechanism makes a sharp prediction: ALMP shouldn't predict the slope of RTI on anti-immigration; CWED should. The next several slides test that prediction.

[Click.]

## SLIDE 8 — Empirical strategy & hierarchy (75 s)

Before the headline, two framing points.

[Point to the equation on the left.]

The model. β₃ — the RTI-times-welfare interaction term, in red — is the parameter of interest. It tests whether welfare context moderates the conversion of RTI into anti-immigration. β₁ is the average RTI slope across countries. The random-slope term lets that slope vary across country-waves; the LR test against random intercepts gives a chi-squared above 90 with two degrees of freedom, p below ten to the minus twenty. Random slopes are required by the data, not optional.

[Point to the hierarchy box on the right.]

The hierarchy of evidence — and this is the framing point I want to land. The primary test is Model 3, the individual-level cross-level interaction estimated on roughly 82,000 individuals. β₃ equals minus zero point zero five nine, p equals zero point zero one five. Formally identified, conservative SE under random slopes.

The country-level scatter at r equals minus zero point eight five on N equals fifteen is the visual companion. The picture that explains *why* the interaction exists — not the primary evidence that it does. When I defend the result, I lead with Model 3.

OLS plus country-wave fixed effects plus clustered SE runs as parallel robustness. Three independent methodologies, directionally identical.

What survives. Three checks.

[Point to the jackknife figure on the left.]

Single-country jackknife. r ranges from minus zero point seven nine to minus zero point eight five across all fifteen single-country exclusions. Sign-stable — never crosses zero, never loses statistical significance. The headline is not driven by a single observation.

Two-country jackknife across all 105 pairs. Worst case: UK plus Norway dropped, the two endpoints. r equals minus zero point seven one, p equals zero point zero zero six. Softer, but still substantial and still significant. Even the most aggressive removal leaves a robust correlation.

[Right side.]

Macro controls. Adding GDP growth and post-fiscal Gini, β₃ strengthens slightly to minus zero point zero six six, p below zero point zero zero one. Argues against CWED proxying for level of economic development.

Methodological convergence. Three slope estimators on the same fifteen countries. BLUPs at minus zero point eight five — the published estimate. Bivariate per-country OLS at minus zero point six three, weaker but same direction. OLS plus FE plus clustered SE: directionally identical β₃. The substantive conclusion is robust to the methodology choice.

What this doesn't address — and I'm explicit about this in section V.G — is deeper institutional confounders: social trust, union density, electoral system, ethnic heterogeneity. Macro-controls handle macroeconomic confounders, not these correlates. That's why the within-country thesis design is the next move.

## SLIDE 16 — Implications (60 s)

Four implications follow.

For welfare-state theory: the political consequences of welfare design travel along *what* welfare communicates, not *how much* it spends. ALMP and CWED are different institutional dimensions, and only one of them does the political work I've measured.

For the cultural-versus-economic debate: cultural backlash isn't a rival explanation to economic disruption. It's what economic disruption *looks like*, cross-nationally, where welfare institutions are less decommodifying. The dichotomy is wrongly posed.

For policy: dignity is necessary but not sufficient for solidarity. Active solidarity requires political work that welfare design alone cannot do — coalitional framing, electoral system affordances, mainstream-party positioning. Welfare provides permission; it does not provide propulsion.

For the thesis follow-up: Danish registry data, the 2003, 2006, and 2013 activation reforms. A within-individual test of the cascade — does conditionality introduced under constant decommodification produce damage signatures? That's the next paper.

[Click.]

## Q&A — the eight defences (read once before the talk)

These are the same defences as on the palm cards. Read them once now so they're primed; you don't need to deliver them as a script in Q&A — improvise from the structure.

### D1 — "Why mixed models with random slopes rather than country FE?"

Required by the data. The LR test rejects slope homogeneity at p below ten to the minus twenty. Ignoring slope variation would understate the SE on β₃ by a factor of about three point six. The wider SE under random slopes is the correct SE; the smaller one assumes the slopes are identical across countries, which is exactly what I'm testing against. OLS plus FE plus clustered runs as a robustness check; the substantive coefficient is directionally identical.

### D2 — "Walk me through what β₃ = −0.059 actually means."

Each one-SD increase in CWED reduces the slope of RTI on anti-immigration by 0.059 scale points. A negative interaction means more decommodifying welfare flattens the conversion. The total spread across the observed CWED range — UK to Norway — is 0.236 scale points per SD of RTI.

### D3 — "How did the LR test justify random slopes?"

The chi-squared statistic exceeds 90 with two degrees of freedom, p below ten to the minus twenty. Not a marginal call. ML rather than REML on both fits, since REML log-likelihoods aren't comparable across nested random structures. AIC and BIC overwhelmingly favour random slopes too.

### D4 — "Why cluster at country-wave? What's the inflation factor?"

Within a country-wave, observations share unobserved shocks — electoral campaigns, media, contemporaneous economy — that create residual correlation. Design effect equals one plus n-bar minus one times rho. With cluster size around 1,400 and ICC around 0.05, that's 71. SE inflation factor is the square root, about 8.4. Naive SE 8 times too small; same point estimate either way. G equals 136 clusters, well above the small-sample threshold.

### D5 — "How are the Figure 6 country-slopes computed?"

BLUPs from a random-slopes mixed model with individual-level controls. Same methodology on both ALMP and CWED panels, so the difference between r equals 0.01 and r equals minus 0.85 isn't a methodology artefact, it's a measure-content finding. BLUPs do two things: shrink toward the grand mean, and partial out within-country individual confounders. Bivariate per-country OLS gives r equals minus 0.63, weaker but same direction. The partial-out does most of the work.

### D6 — "N equals 15 is small. What's the leverage check?"

Three checks. Single-country jackknife: r ranges from minus 0.79 to minus 0.85, never crosses zero. Two-country jackknife across 105 pairs: worst case UK plus Norway, r equals minus 0.71, p equals 0.006. And the individual-level Model 3 is identified from N around 82,000 — a more powerful test of the same hypothesis. Triangulation across the country-level scatter, the formal interaction, and the leverage diagnostic.

### D7 — "Why isn't the redistribution null a measurement failure?"

Three reasons. First, the same data, same estimator, comparable spec detected the exclusion moderation cleanly. Power to detect equal-magnitude solidarity moderation exceeds 0.99. Second, the ISSP 2006 supplementary test on a different sample, different outcome, different period gives the same null. Third, the asymmetric mechanism predicts the null on three independent grounds: loss aversion, positional status, irreversibility. A theory that predicts the null and observes the null is doing what theories are supposed to do.

### D8 — "Why does the radical-right voting result go the other way?"

Attitudes are what voters are; votes are what voters do given supply-side constraints. The attitude moderation tests welfare-context shaping of vulnerability into exclusionary disposition; the answer is yes. The voting moderation reflects supply-side translation: in Liberal regimes, exclusionary attitudes can't channel into radical-right ballots because the radical right is structurally suppressed — Ireland has no significant RR party in this period; the UK channels exclusion through Brexit and mainstream Conservative voting. The opposite signs aren't a contradiction of the attitude finding; they're what supply-side filtering looks like in the data.
