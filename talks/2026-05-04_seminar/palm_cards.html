Palm Cards — Dignity Is a Baseline

Welfare State Seminar · 5 May 2026 · Ben Smart · panic-recovery format
How to use these: Print on A4. Cut between cards if you want physical cards, or just keep the page next to you on the lectern. Each card has the opener line you should start the slide with (in italic quotes), 2–4 bullet cues, and a transition. The methods cards have an "If asked" section with the 30-second defence. The first card is the panic protocol — read it now, before you start.

If you panic, do this

  1. Stop talking. Two slow breaths. The audience will read this as confidence.
  2. Look at the slide. The slide knows what comes next; you just have to read it aloud and elaborate.
  3. If you've lost the thread, say: "Let me reorient — the question I'm working on here is whether [slide title]." Then keep going.
  4. If you can't recover, jump to the next slide. "Let me move on and come back to this if there's time." Nobody minds.
  5. Q&A blank: "That's a great question — let me think for a moment." Pause for 4–5 seconds. The pause looks thoughtful, not panicked. Then answer.
  6. Q&A on methods you forgot: "Let me show you the backup slide on this." Flip to it. The slide does the explaining; you narrate.

The whole audience is rooting for you. Their second-most-likely state is "wishing this person well"; their first is "thinking about lunch". Neither hostile.

0 Title slide — the first 30 seconds
Welfare states across Europe spend wildly different amounts to compensate workers exposed to economic disruption — and yet, the political effects don't track spending. They track something else. This paper identifies what.
  • Two slow breaths before you start
  • Look at one face in the back of the room — not your laptop
  • Speak slower than feels natural for the first sentence
→ The puzzle.
1 The puzzle
Workers in routine-task occupations — the ones automation hits hardest — are systematically more anti-immigration than their material interests would predict. And the gap is wider in some welfare states than in others.
  • RTI = routine task intensity (automation exposure)
  • Same shock, different political response across regimes
  • The literature has explained this by spending. We'll see why that's wrong.
→ What buffering predicts.
2 The literature is split — and asks the wrong question
Two literatures have been speaking past each other. The buffering account says quantity — more welfare spending dampens political insecurity. The recognition literature says no, redistribution and recognition aren't fungible — and routine workers demand both.
  • Buffering side: Ruggie 1982; Vlandas & Halikiopoulou; Burgoon & Schakel 2022 (welfare ↓ anti-globalisation rhetoric in party platforms)
  • Three streams of pushback: Gingrich (no buffering), Stutzmann (compensation backfires), Pelc (compensation refused)
  • Recognition challenge: Kurer 2020 — traditional welfare insufficient when relative decline drives resentment; Kurer & Palier — economic and cultural protection; Gidron & Hall — recognition matters as much as redistribution
  • The reconciliation my paper offers: the political effects travel along what welfare communicates, not how much it spends
→ The argument.
3 The argument: welfare effects are asymmetric
Welfare institutions can fail politically by damaging the self-concept of vulnerable workers and triggering a cascade. They cannot, by symmetric operation, succeed.
  • Damage mechanism: detectable, robust across specifications
  • Protective mechanism: not detectable, by design
  • Three reasons (next slide): loss aversion, positional status, irreversibility
→ Why welfare specifically?
4 Why welfare and not something else?
Welfare is the single state domain where economic vulnerability and institutional treatment meet at the same moment. The institution allocates resources and renders a judgement about the worker's claim, in the same act.
  • Other identity-shaping institutions can't match the combination
  • Wagner: recipients "internalize" and "mirror" deservingness norms
  • Soss 1999, De Blok & Kumlin: implementation quality matters most for the most vulnerable
→ The damage cascade itself.
5 The damage cascade
Three responses follow when welfare damages self-concept, each harder to reverse than the one before: identity switching, misattribution, defensive othering.
  • Identity: Bonomi-Gennaioli-Tabellini class→cultural switch
  • Misattribution: Wu — frustration at scapegoats, not technology
  • Defensive othering: Patrick "scrounger"; Wagner "kicking down"
  • Ends at: Busemeyer's "particularistic-authoritarian" preference
→ Why no symmetric mirror image.
6 Why no mirror image — three reasons
The symmetric account would predict that welfare which preserves the self-concept produces solidarity through a mirror cascade. The empirical null on the solidarity side says otherwise — and the mechanism predicts the null on three separate grounds.
  • Loss aversion (Kahneman & Tversky): damage registers, absence of damage doesn't
  • Status is positional: relational goods can't be redistributed without losses
  • Defensive othering is irreversible: identity investments don't unwind
→ Empirical setup.
7 Empirical setup
European Social Survey rounds 6 through 9, fieldwork 2012 to 2018, 34 countries, N equals 188,764. The headline analysis restricts to the 15 Western European countries with CWED welfare-quality data.
  • Cross-level interactions, country-wave FE, cluster-robust SEs, random-slope mixed models
  • Cross-sectional design — claim is consistency, not causation
  • Two welfare measures: ALMP (effort) vs CWED (decommodification)
  • Sharp prediction: ALMP shouldn't predict the slope; CWED should.
If asked about random slopes: LR test χ² > 90, p < 10⁻²⁰. Required by the data, not optional. Backup slide has the spec comparison.
→ Empirical strategy & the hierarchy of evidence.
8 Empirical strategy & methodology hierarchy
The model is a cross-level interaction with random slopes. β₃ — the RTI-times-welfare interaction — is the parameter of interest. But which test is the strongest? That's the framing point.
  • Primary test (formally identified): Model 3 individual-level. N ≈ 82,000, β₃ = −0.059, p = 0.015
  • Visual companion (illustration): Country-level scatter. N = 15, r = −0.85
  • Robustness: OLS + country-wave FE + clustered SE. Directionally identical.
  • Lead with Model 3. Treat the scatter as the picture that explains why the interaction exists, not the primary evidence that it does.
If asked which is your headline: Model 3, individual-level. The country-level r=−0.85 is striking but rests on N=15. The hierarchy matters, and three independent methodologies converge.
→ What β₃ is asking, visually.
9 What β₃ tests — the cross-level fan
Before the data — what the cross-level interaction is asking. β₃ is the rate at which the slope of RTI on anti-immigration changes per one standard deviation of CWED.
  • Each country sits on a point of the line
  • UK (z=−2): predicted slope 0.333
  • Average: 0.215
  • Norway (z=+2): 0.097
  • Spread: 0.236 scale points across observed CWED range
→ The cross-national pattern by regime.
10 Cross-national pattern: 5 regimes
Each panel is a welfare regime. The line in each panel is RTI predicting anti-immigration. Liberal steepest at 0.512. Nordic flattest at 0.413. Same routine exposure, different conversion.
  • Liberal > Southern > Continental > Nordic > Eastern
  • Same RTI, different political response
  • Buffering predicts: more spending → flatter slope. Tested directly next.
→ Marginal effects in scale points.
11 Marginal effects: the gap, in scale points
Translated into substantive scale points. A one-standard-deviation increase in RTI gets you 0.32 additional anti-immigration points in Liberal regimes versus 0.20 in Nordic regimes. Liberal slope is 60 percent steeper at the same exposure.
  • Liberal: +0.32 / Nordic: +0.20 / gap: +0.127
  • Significant: p = 0.003
  • Survives every robustness check
→ The empirical headline.
12 The empirical headline: ALMP vs CWED
The paper's most important empirical contrast. Same 15 Western European countries, two ways of measuring welfare.
  • ALMP: r = +0.01, p = 0.97 — essentially zero
  • CWED: r = −0.85, p < 0.001 — 72% of cross-national variation
  • UK lowest; Norway highest. Predicted by the asymmetric mechanism.
  • ALMP captures effort; CWED captures decommodification. Dignity travels along the second.
If asked how the slopes were computed: BLUPs from a random-slopes mixed model with controls — same methodology on both sides. Backup slide has the four-methodology comparison. Bivariate gives r=−0.62; controls partial out within-country confounders.
→ Decomposing CWED.
13 Decomposing CWED
CWED has three sub-components — unemployment, sickness, pensions. The asymmetric mechanism predicts the signal should run strongest through unemployment, where automation-exposed routine workers actually meet the welfare state. Pensions decoupled from working-age dynamics.
  • Predicted ordering: UE > SK > PEN
  • Individual-level: UE β = −0.053, SK = −0.037, PEN = −0.019
  • Confirmed. Cascade fires through the point of vulnerability.
→ The asymmetric confirmation.
14 Same data, opposite outcome
Same data tested two ways. RTI predicts anti-immigration more strongly in Liberal regimes than Nordic — interaction beta point one two seven, p equals point zero zero three. Robust everywhere.
  • Exclusion side: β = +0.127, p = 0.003 — robust
  • Solidarity side: β = +0.011, p = 0.29 — null
  • ISSP confirmation: β = +0.010, p = 0.55 — same null, different sample
  • Two readings: measurement issue, or substantive asymmetry. I take the second.
If asked about the null: Power to detect a solidarity moderation of equal magnitude exceeds 0.99. Power isn't the binding constraint. The asymmetric theory predicts the null on three independent grounds.
→ Robustness.
15 Robustness — what survives
What survives. Three checks. The pattern is robust to single-country exclusion, robust to two-country exclusion, robust to macro controls, and robust to the choice of slope methodology.
  • Single-country jackknife: r ∈ [−0.79, −0.85]. Sign-stable. Never crosses zero.
  • Two-country jackknife (105 pairs): worst case UK + NO, r = −0.71, p = 0.006.
  • Macro controls (GDP + Gini): β₃ strengthens to −0.066, p < 0.001. Argues against "CWED proxies for development".
  • Methodology convergence: BLUPs −0.85, bivariate −0.63, OLS+FE+clustered directionally identical.
If asked about deeper confounders (social trust, unions, electoral system): macro controls handle macroeconomic confounders, not these. That's why the within-country thesis design with Danish registry data is the next move. The cross-sectional claim is consistency, not identification.
→ Implications.
16 Implications + closing
Four implications follow.
  • Welfare-state theory: the dimension that matters is what welfare communicates, not how much it spends
  • Cultural-vs-economic debate: cultural backlash IS what economic disruption looks like under thin decommodification
  • Policy: dignity is necessary but not sufficient for solidarity
  • Thesis follow-up: Danish registry, the 2003/2006/2013 activation reforms — within-individual test of the cascade
→ Closing slide. Land it slowly. "Dignity is a baseline." Pause. Take questions.
Q&A — Methods defences (if asked)
D1 "Why mixed models with random slopes rather than country FE?"
Required by the data. The LR test rejects slope homogeneity at p < 10⁻²⁰. The wider SE under random slopes is the correct SE; the smaller one assumes slopes are identical, which is exactly what we're testing against.
  • SE inflation under RS vs RI: 3.6×
  • OLS+FE+clustered runs as a robustness check; substantively identical
D2 "Walk me through what β₃ = −0.059 means."
Each one-SD increase in CWED reduces the slope of RTI on anti-immigration by 0.059 scale points. A negative interaction means more decommodifying welfare flattens the conversion. The total spread across the observed CWED range is 0.236 scale points per SD of RTI.
D3 "How did the LR test justify random slopes?"
χ²(2) statistic exceeds 90, with p below 10⁻²⁰. Not a marginal call. ML rather than REML on both fits, since REML log-likelihoods aren't comparable across nested random structures. AIC and BIC overwhelmingly favour random slopes too.
D4 "Why cluster at country-wave? What's the inflation factor?"
Within a country-wave, observations share unobserved shocks — electoral campaigns, media, contemporaneous economy — that create residual correlation. Design effect = 1 + (n−1)·ρ. With n̄ = 1,400 and ρ ≈ 0.05, that's 71. SE inflation factor = √71 ≈ 8.4×.
  • Naive SE 8× too small; same point estimate either way
  • G = 136 clusters, well above the small-sample threshold
  • Paired with country-wave FE: dummies absorb levels, clustering handles residual correlation
D5 "How are the Figure 6 country-slopes computed?"
BLUPs from a random-slopes mixed model with individual-level controls. Same methodology on both ALMP and CWED panels — so the difference between r=0.01 and r=−0.85 isn't a methodology artefact, it's a measure-content finding.
  • BLUPs do two things: shrink toward grand mean, partial out within-country confounders
  • Bivariate per-country OLS gives r=−0.625 (weaker but same direction)
  • Partial-out does most of the work — RTI workers are older, less educated, lower-income, all of which independently predict anti-immigration
D6 "N=15 is small. What's the leverage check?"
Three checks. Single-country jackknife: r ranges from −0.79 to −0.85, never crosses zero. Two-country jackknife across all 105 pairs: worst case is UK plus Norway dropped, r = −0.71, p = 0.006 — softer, still substantial, still significant.
  • Individual-level Model 3 identified from N≈82,000 — more powerful test of same hypothesis
  • Triangulation: country-level scatter (visual), Model 3 (formal inference), jackknife (leverage diagnostic)
D7 "Why isn't the redistribution null a measurement failure?"
Three things. First, same data, same estimator, comparable spec detected exclusion cleanly — power to detect equal-magnitude solidarity moderation exceeds 0.99. Second, ISSP 2006 — different sample, different outcome, different period — gives the same null. Third, the asymmetric mechanism predicts the null on three independent grounds.
D8 "Why does the radical-right voting result go the other way?"
Attitudes are what voters are; votes are what voters do given supply-side constraints. Liberal regimes structurally suppress radical-right ballots — Ireland has no significant RR party; the UK channels exclusion through Brexit and mainstream Conservative voting. The voting moderation reflects supply-side translation, not contradicts the attitude finding.
  • RTI×Liberal on attitudes: +0.127, p=0.003
  • RTI×Liberal on RR voting: −0.123, p=0.032
  • Different DVs, different mechanisms; full account needs party-level data — separate paper
Q&A — Substantive questions (if asked)
S1 "What's the difference between ALMP and CWED?"
ALMP is a flow variable — Euros per GDP per year on training, job-search assistance, employment subsidies. CWED is a parametric variable — the legal architecture of entitlement: replacement rate, duration, coverage, qualifying conditions, waiting period.
  • A country can double ALMP overnight without changing CWED
  • UE benefit generosity is inside CWED already — that's why we don't compare CWED to UE spending separately
  • ALMP aggregates enabling (training) + punitive (sanctions) — political signatures cancel; that's why ALMP shows null
S2 "How is decommodification actually measured?"
Esping-Andersen's definition — the degree to which a worker can maintain a livelihood without reliance on the market. CWED operationalises it from policy parameters: replacement rate, benefit duration, coverage rate, qualifying conditions, waiting period — for unemployment, sickness, and pensions separately.
  • Read off legal entitlements, not spending flows
  • Three sub-scores (UEGEN, SKGEN, PGEN) sum to TOTGEN
  • That's why CWED ≠ ALMP — different domains
S3 "What can you actually claim?"
A consistency argument — the data are consistent with the asymmetric mechanism, and inconsistent with the symmetric buffering account, on multiple independent margins. Not a causal estimate. The thesis design with Danish registry data is where causal identification lives.
  • Can argue: robust association; methodology-symmetric ALMP-vs-CWED contrast; survives jackknife and macro-controls; asymmetric pattern replicates on independent ISSP test
  • Can't prove: causation, time-invariant institutional confounding, the loss-aversion claim directly
  • Not about: normative welfare ranking, supply-side translation, all populism, the within-country test
★ If you remember nothing else
ALMP r = +0.01. CWED r = −0.85. Same 15 countries, same slope methodology. Power isn't the problem. Asymmetric mechanism predicts the pattern observed.
