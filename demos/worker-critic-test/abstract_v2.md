## Abstract

Whether automation exposure erodes or galvanises collective labour organisation remains a central question in comparative political economy, and the existing literature offers two sharply opposing predictions. Insider-outsider theory (Rueda 2005) treats automation-exposed workers as labour-market outsiders who are systematically under-represented in unions designed around stable insiders; threat-response accounts (Olson 1965; Mosimann and Pontusson 2017) instead expect perceived economic insecurity to motivate collective action. We test these competing claims using European Social Survey rounds 7–9 (2014–2018), covering roughly 85,000 respondents across 18 Western European countries. Routine task intensity is assigned to each respondent by merging ISCO-08 occupation codes at the three-digit level to the task scores of Goos, Manning, and Salomons (2014). We estimate the conditional association between RTI exposure and union membership using multilevel logistic models with country fixed effects, standard individual controls (age, gender, education, income decile, and employment status), and a cross-level interaction with country-level sectoral bargaining coverage drawn from the Visser ICTWSS database; because RTI is not exogenously assigned, our estimates recover a conditional correlation rather than a causal treatment effect. Automation exposure raises the probability of union membership by 4.2 percentage points (β = 0.042, SE = 0.011, p < 0.001), but only in countries in the top tercile of sectoral bargaining coverage; in the bottom tercile the effect is statistically indistinguishable from zero (β = 0.003, SE = 0.014, p = 0.83). The pattern is robust to an occupational-tenure placebo, alternative RTI measures from Autor and Dorn (2013) and Acemoglu and Restrepo (2020), and exclusion of public-sector workers (β = 0.038). The association is concentrated among non-college workers. Through this perspective, the institutional infrastructure undergirding collective response determines whether automation anxiety yields solidarity or atomisation, reframing how scholars should interpret worker reactions to economic disruption.
