## Verification of the Load-Bearing Claim

The synthesis hangs on the claim that Wagner (2022) "explicitly tests both directions" of the asymmetry within a single design. **This claim is correct and faithful to the lit note.**

Verification trail:
- Wagner note line 1: "two opposing mechanisms" — kicking-down (conditionality) vs. solidarization (social investment)
- Line 13: "strong evidence for the 'kicking down' mechanism"
- Line 16: "Evidence for the 'solidarization' mechanism is limited but present in a small number of cases"

**However**, the synthesis omits a material caveat that Wagner himself flags (note line 42/50): the sample (manual workers, no immigrant-background interviewees) presents "favorable conditions for a test of the 'exclusion'-hypothesis ('kicking down'), but rather difficult conditions for a test of the 'inclusion'-hypotheses ('solidarization')." This is a sample-design asymmetry, not a population-level asymmetry. The synthesis presents the within-paper asymmetry as evidence against design heterogeneity — but Wagner explicitly says his design is *not* a clean test of solidarization. The asymmetric reading is still defensible, but the synthesis overstates Wagner's evidentiary value for it. **This is the most important substantive issue.**

## Issues Found

### 1. Coverage gap — missing closely relevant corpus papers (-10)

For an asymmetric-mechanism question, three papers in the corpus speak more directly than V&H (2022) does to the symmetric/asymmetric distinction and are not cited:

- **Ennser-Jedenastik et al. (2019)**, "Cushion or catalyst" — literally the named symmetric/asymmetric framing in the corpus
- **Halikiopoulou & Vlandas (2016)** — earlier formulation of the labour-market-protection moderator
- **Gingrich (2019)** — "Compensation does not reduce populist voting among automation-exposed." Closest existing test of the protective null in the corpus
- **Milner (2021)** — "welfare doesn't buffer" — another protective-null finding

The brief asks for 3 papers, so the worker had to choose. But a critic note on the unselected-but-directly-relevant set is warranted. The gap section in the synthesis would be stronger if it acknowledged that Gingrich (2019) and Milner (2021) already provide partial protective-null evidence — the seminar paper's contribution would then be re-positioned as paired-design + recognition mediator, not as "first to find protective null." Failure to acknowledge this risks overclaiming the seminar paper's novelty.

### 2. Theoretical framing — undercaveats Wagner (-5)

As noted above, the within-design asymmetry in Wagner is real but partially attributable to sample selection. The synthesis's sentence "it is therefore difficult to attribute to design heterogeneity" is technically true (it is one design) but materially misleading (the design is asymmetrically powered). Writer should add one clause noting the sample caveat.

### 3. Bergman characterisation — slightly overreaches (-3)

The synthesis says Bergman's catalysing finding is "most consistent with an asymmetric reading." Bergman's note frames the result as conditional on producerist alignment, skill specificity, and visibility — not as a clean asymmetry test. The synthesis acknowledges this in the next sentence, so the issue is mild, but the headline framing is slightly leading.

### 4. V&H 2022 characterisation — accurate (no deduction)

### 5. Gap identification — incomplete (-2)

The two gaps identified are real and well-argued. But a third gap is missed: **none of the three papers tests heterogeneity by RTI within a single welfare-design contrast**, which is the seminar paper's specific design innovation.
