## The Central Puzzle

Ben is animated by what the manuscripts name the **populist spending paradox**: welfare compensation in automation-exposed regions has not merely failed to reduce populist voting — in some specifications it has *increased* it (Gingrich 2019). This is not a minor anomaly. It inverts the foundational logic of embedded liberalism (Rodrik 1998; Ruggie 1982) and suggests that the standard policy toolkit — transfer payments, passive compensation, aggregate welfare generosity — is either insufficient or counterproductive when the grievance driving political behaviour is not primarily material.

This puzzle sits at the exact intersection of three literatures that Ben has woven together: comparative welfare state research, electoral sociology, and the economics of labour market change. His dissertation is an attempt to resolve it theoretically and then test the resolution empirically.

---

## The Theoretical Move

Ben's distinctive intellectual move is to treat **status** — specifically, the loss of *relative* economic position within a social hierarchy — as the proximate mechanism linking economic disruption to populist support. Material hardship is real, but it tends to produce demand for compensatory welfare (left-wing); it is *anticipated* status loss, the fear of falling, that drives radical right support (Kurer 2020; Im 2023).

This distinction generates an asymmetry with profound policy implications. Traditional welfare states are designed to respond to experienced hardship. But the politically active group — the voters most susceptible to populist mobilisation — are those who have not yet fallen but fear they will. They want status restoration, not income replacement. More welfare "in the traditional sense might not help alleviate grievances" (Kurer 2020, p. 1826).

Ben's theoretical resolution — the **Dignity-Preserving Active Assistance (DPAA)** framework — proposes that welfare institutions buffer against populist politics when they operate across three dimensions simultaneously:

1. **Status politics**: Maintaining or enhancing relative standing rather than reinforcing stigmatising distinctions between contributors and recipients
2. **Temporal security**: Proactive prevention of anticipated vulnerability rather than reactive response to experienced hardship
3. **Ontological security**: Respecting autonomy and dignity rather than imposing punitive compliance

Passive transfers may address dimension 2 reactively but fail on 1 and 3. Punitive workfare may address dimension 2 proactively but fails catastrophically on 1 and 3 by imposing status degradation and surveillance. Universal, investment-oriented, dignity-affirming programmes — the DPAA ideal-type — can potentially address all three.

## What Ben Actually Cares About

Reading the manuscripts against the theory modules and literature, several deeper intellectual commitments become visible:

**He is not primarily interested in automation as an independent variable.** The ISCO-task-score apparatus is a means to an end — measuring a kind of economic exposure. The interesting question is not "does automation cause populism?" (several papers already do this) but "through what psychological and institutional mechanisms does automation exposure translate into political behaviour, and can welfare state design interrupt those mechanisms?" He is wary of automation-determinism.

**He cares about institutions as moderators, not just outcomes.** The heterogeneous effects question — does the automation–populism link vary by welfare regime? — is the institutional argument. This is where Ben's UCL context (Economics of the Welfare State group) is formative. He is not satisfied with reduced-form results that treat institutions as controls.

**He wants to bridge the economic/cultural divide.** He explicitly rejects the framing of economic vs. cultural explanations. His framework positions status anxiety as the proximate cause of cultural expression: economic processes generate status concerns that manifest through cultural and identity politics (Bonomi, Gennaioli & Tabellini 2021). The cultural backlash (Norris & Inglehart 2019) is an *output* of mismanaged economic disruption, not an autonomous force.

**He is drawn to mechanisms and temporal distinctions.** The anticipated/experienced hardship distinction (Kurer 2020; Pitts & Winter 2024) is not a footnote — it is architecturally central. He is interested in the *sequence* by which vulnerability becomes political behaviour. This suggests he would be interested in mediation analysis and in designs that can distinguish prospective from retrospective economic perception.

**He is policy-motivated but academically rigorous.** He wants to say something useful about welfare design, but he will not sacrifice identification for policy relevance. His target journals (APSR, AJPS, CPS) require causal or near-causal identification. The policy story has to come through the mechanism, not as a free-standing addendum.

## Theoretical Commitments and Their Empirical Implications

| Theoretical commitment                         | Empirical implication                                                                       |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Status matters more than absolute income       | Need positional income change (`posit_income_change`), not just household income            |
| Anticipated hardship drives radical right      | Need forward-looking economic expectations, not just current conditions                     |
| Welfare *design* matters, not just generosity  | Need variation in ALMP spending, conditionality, benefit type — not just expenditure levels |
| Institutions moderate individual-level effects | Need multilevel design with country-level institutional variables                           |
| Temporal anticipation is the key mechanism     | Need mediation analysis or instruments for subjective expectations                          |

## What Ben Has Not Yet Resolved

Several tensions remain open in the manuscripts:

**The identification problem.** The framework generates clear hypotheses, but the causal identification strategy is not specified. Cross-sectional ESS data with country × wave FE can document correlations; it cannot cleanly isolate the causal effect of welfare design on the status anxiety → populism link. This is a legitimate first-paper limitation (especially at early ideation stage), but it must be confronted early.

**The measurement gap.** DPAA is a latent construct. There is no off-the-shelf DPAA index. Any empirical test requires operationalising the three dimensions (status, temporal, ontological security) from existing survey and administrative data — a non-trivial measurement challenge.

**The heterogeneity hypothesis.** The most tractable empirical application of the theory is a heterogeneous effects test: does the relationship between economic exposure and populist voting vary by welfare regime type? This is testable with existing data, but it requires a theory of which regime dimensions matter — not just Esping-Andersen typologies, but the specific DPAA-relevant dimensions.

**The supply vs. demand question.** Is this a story about voters (demand) or parties (supply)? The theory is primarily demand-side, but party mobilisation strategies interact with economic conditions. The Langenkamp crosswalk data enables a populism vote score on the supply side.

## Intellectual Style and Preferences

Based on the manuscripts and infrastructure choices:

- **Theoretically ambitious**: Ben is not content with purely empirical papers. He wants the theory to do work.
- **Empirically disciplined**: He uses replication data from rigorous papers (Baccini, Kurer, Cicollini, Milner) as his scaffolding. He is not cavalier about identification.
- **Cross-national by disposition**: His datasets span 20+ European countries. He thinks in welfare regimes.
- **Receptive to mediation**: The status anxiety mechanism is intermediate — it can be tested through mediation or moderated mediation designs.
- **Preference for individual-level data**: ESS is the primary unit of analysis, with contextual variables attached at the country or region level.

## Summary for Research Direction

Ben is building toward an empirical chapter that can:

1. Document that economic vulnerability (across multiple dimensions: automation, trade, austerity, positional income decline) predicts populist political behaviour in Europe
2. Show that this relationship is *mediated* by status anxiety / recognition concerns rather than material deprivation alone
3. Demonstrate that the mediated relationship is *moderated* by welfare regime features — specifically features capturing whether welfare design is dignity-preserving and investment-oriented
4. Conclude that the policy implication is redesigning welfare institutions (not just their generosity) to address the DPAA dimensions

The key empirical constraint: all three of these steps require different data strategies, and the causal claim in step 3 is the hardest to identify with available data.
