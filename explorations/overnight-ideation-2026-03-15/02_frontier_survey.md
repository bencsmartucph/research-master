## 1. Welfare State Politics and Populism: The Preference Architecture Problem

### What the frontier has established (2022–2026)

The most important development in this subfield is the maturation of a literature on the **distinctive welfare preferences of populist radical right (PRRP) voters** — and the finding that these preferences are not simply "anti-welfare" but are internally coherent around a logic of particularism and producerism.

**Rathgeb & Busemeyer (2021, WEP)** ("How to study the populist radical right and the welfare state?") established the conceptual baseline: PRRP voters simultaneously favour moderate social transfers for "deserving" recipients (the elderly, the native-born, the long-term worker) while opposing universalist redistribution and actively endorsing workfare and conditionality. This is not a confused position — it is a "particularistic-authoritarian welfare state" preference structure.

**Busemeyer, Rathgeb & Sahm (2021, WEP)** extended this empirically, finding that PRRP voters have notably low support for social investment while maintaining moderate support for passive transfers. The interpretation: social investment is coded as a middle-class cosmopolitan project, not as a benefit for "people like them." This is a crucial finding for Ben's DPAA argument — it means social investment policies may face a demand-side political barrier among the very voters they are designed to protect.

**Bergman (2024, WEP)** — "Labour market policies and support for populist radical right parties: the role of nostalgic producerism, occupational risk, and feedback effects" — is the most directly relevant piece on the frontier. It introduces three important refinements:

- **Nostalgic producerism** as the ideological frame through which PRRP voters evaluate labour market policy: policies that visibly reward productive contribution are accepted; those coded as assisting "non-contributors" are rejected.
- **Occupational risk** as a moderator: workers in objectively high-automation-risk occupations respond differently to the same policy design depending on whether it appears to offer dignified pathways (enabling ALMPs) or punitive compliance requirements (workfare).
- **Self-undermining feedback effects**: high levels of punitive activation can generate backlash, shifting public opinion against interventionism. This is directly relevant to Ben's temporal and dignity dimensions — it suggests not just that poorly designed policies fail to reduce populist support, but that they may actively generate it.

### Gaps on the frontier

The preference-architecture literature is well-developed on *what* PRRP voters prefer but weak on *why* the same policy design produces different political reactions across welfare regimes. Comparative variation in institutional mediation is underexplored. There is essentially no work asking whether DPAA-style policies — if they could be credibly signalled as dignity-preserving and production-enabling — could shift PRRP voter preferences, or whether the preference structure is sticky.

## 2. Status, Recognition, and the Radical Right

### What the frontier has established (2022–2026)

The Gidron & Hall (2017, 2018) subjective social status framework has been substantially extended and in some ways contested on the 2022–2026 frontier.

**Ballard-Rosa, Jensen & Scheve (2022, International Studies Quarterly)** — "Economic decline, social identity, and authoritarian values in the United States" — provides a causal identification advance. Using panel survey data tracking individuals through objective economic shocks, they demonstrate that economic decline activates in-group/out-group identity boundaries and shifts respondents toward authoritarian values. Crucially, the mechanism is social identity rather than material hardship per se: what predicts authoritarian value shift is the *relative* decline narrative (who is gaining at whose expense), not absolute income loss.

**Green, Hellwig & Fieldhouse (2020, BJPS)** — confirmed earlier but increasingly influential in 2022–2026 citations — introduced "Group-Based Economics": perceptions of how out-groups are faring relative to one's own group predict populist vote better than personal economic assessments. This has spawned a small but growing literature testing whether welfare state design affects zero-sum group-based economic perceptions.

The key unresolved debate: **does status anxiety operate as a proximate cause of populist support independent of economic conditions, or is it merely a mediator through which economic shocks translate into political behavior?** Gidron & Hall treat it as proximate; critics argue the causal chain runs from economic conditions → status perceptions → political behavior, making status anxiety a mechanism rather than an independent driver. This matters empirically because it changes the identification strategy and the policy implication.

**Mutz (2018, PNAS)** remains highly cited on the status threat side, but the 2022–2026 frontier has pushed back. The criticism: Mutz's cross-sectional design cannot distinguish status threat from prior political identity — Trump supporters may score higher on status threat *because* they support Trump, not the reverse. Panel designs (Ballard-Rosa et al. 2022) that track status perceptions over time are now regarded as methodologically superior.

### Gaps on the frontier

The status literature is largely focused on the US and UK. Cross-national comparative evidence on how welfare regime type *moderates* the status → populism pathway is thin. A paper testing whether social-democratic welfare states — which in principle provide more institutional recognition of workers' contributions — actually suppress the status-threat pathway relative to liberal or conservative regimes would make a genuine frontier contribution.

## 3. Temporal Dynamics: Anticipated vs. Experienced Hardship

### What the frontier has established (2022–2026)

This is the most theoretically active corner of the frontier and the one most directly relevant to Ben's DPAA framework.

**Kurer (2020, CPS)** remains the foundational piece, but its 2022–2026 reception has refined the argument considerably. The original finding — that anticipated occupational decline (surviving in a declining-middle environment) predicts radical right vote, while actual experienced hardship reduces it — has been replicated, qualified, and extended.

**Im (2023)** — "With Status Decline in Sight, Voters Turn to the Radical Right" — directly tests the anticipation mechanism in the Finnish case, where the generous Nordic welfare state should in principle insulate against material vulnerability. The finding: lower-middle-income voters *fearing* future status loss drive Finns Party support, not those who have already declined. The welfare state buffering is real for material hardship outcomes but insufficient for status anxiety. This is a critical piece because it rules out the simpler story that Nordic welfare states neutralise the populist pathway — they do not, because the pathway runs through anticipated status, not material need.

**Häusermann, Kurer & Zollinger (2023)** — "Aspiration and Apprehension: How Prospective Economic Evaluations Shape Party Support in 8 Western European Countries" — is the broadest cross-national test to date. The key finding: prospective economic evaluations (aspiration = expecting improvement; apprehension = expecting deterioration) shape party choice differently across the partisan spectrum. Those with *apprehension* about long-run economic prospects are the swing voters most susceptible to both mainstream-right and far-right mobilisation depending on framing. Aspiration voters cluster toward liberal and Green parties. The implication: welfare policies that speak to future economic security (not just current compensation) may alter the political dynamic for this apprehension group.

**Pitts & Winter (2024, Political Studies)** — "Politics and the future of work: Routine work, automation risk and redistributive preferences in the age of populism" — provides the most direct test of the automation-risk/temporal mechanism. Workers in high-routine occupations who face subjective risk of automation are the pivotal political group: coping on current income, not yet materially harmed, but facing elevated subjective risk. This group shows distinctive redistributive preferences — they favour investment and insurance over compensatory transfers — and their political direction depends on which party credibly claims to address future risk.

### The central theoretical advance

These papers together establish what might be called the **temporal trilemma for welfare states**: compensatory transfers address experienced hardship but miss the politically pivotal anticipation group; social investment may address anticipation but faces demand-side resistance from PRRP-aligned voters (Busemeyer et al. 2021); and doing nothing risks the apprehension group being captured by radical right narratives of status restoration. The welfare state is damned on multiple fronts unless it can signal something distinctive: proactive, dignity-preserving, and credibly future-oriented.

### Gaps on the frontier

The empirical tests of the anticipation mechanism are largely cross-sectional or use attitudinal proxies for anticipated hardship. **No published paper uses a pre-constructed measure of positional income change** (as Cicollini 2025 provides) to directly test whether downward positional trajectory — as distinct from absolute income level — predicts radical right vote while controlling for current income. This is a tractable gap.

## 4. Policy Feedback and Implementation Quality

### What the frontier has established (2022–2026)

Policy feedback research — how welfare policies shape political preferences, identities, and participation over time — has shifted toward a more pessimistic view of activation and conditionality.

**Bergman (2024)**, discussed above, formalises the **self-undermining feedback mechanism**: punitive activation generates experienced misrecognition, which shifts welfare recipients toward anti-welfare attitudes and then toward PRRP parties. The causal pathway: activation policy → dignity violation → welfare chauvinism → PRRP support. This is distinct from the anticipation pathway; it targets current welfare recipients, not workers fearing future decline.

**Wagner (2023, JESP)** — "Kicking down on the ladder of deservingness? Has welfare reform led to welfare chauvinism?" — tests whether welfare recipients who experience "exclusion and misrecognition" respond by endorsing stricter conditionality for those perceived as less deserving. The finding: yes — exposure to punitive activation increases welfare chauvinism among recipients, consistent with Bergman's mechanism. This is a vicious cycle: bad policy creates worse politics creates worse policy.

The institutional context matters: **Moreira & Lødemel (2014, passim)** and subsequent work has documented substantial variation in *how* activation is implemented across welfare regimes — from enabling (supporting capability development with dignity) to punitive (compliance-based sanctions). The frontier question for Ben's work is whether implementation quality variation, *within* similar formal policy designs, differentially activates the self-undermining feedback mechanism.

### Gaps on the frontier

Implementation quality is typically measured through administrator surveys or qualitative case studies. There is no large-scale cross-national individual-level evidence linking caseworker-level implementation variation to political outcomes. This is partly a data problem — individual-level welfare receipt variables are absent from ESS — but attitudinal proxies (perceptions of institutional fairness, satisfaction with government services) are available in ESS (`stfgov`, `trstprl`, `trstlgl`) and might serve as partial proxies.

## 5. Austerity and Electoral Politics

### What the frontier has established (2022–2026)

**Baccini, Pinto & Weymouth (2024)** is the most important piece in this area and represents the methodological gold standard for the dissertation programme. Using a difference-in-differences design with district-level austerity cuts as quasi-exogenous variation, they establish a causal link from austerity to populist vote share. This is the cleanest identification strategy in the welfare-state/populism literature to date.

Key findings: the effect of austerity on populist voting is heterogeneous across welfare regimes — cuts to in-kind services (health, education) have larger political effects than cuts to transfers; district-level effects are not fully captured by individual-level ESS analysis, pointing to community-level mechanisms (consistent with Bolet 2021 and Rodríguez-Pose 2018).

**Fetzer (2019, AER)** — "Did austerity cause Brexit?" — remains the key prior work, establishing the regional austerity → Brexit vote link in the UK using welfare reform as quasi-exogenous variation. The mechanism includes both material effects (reduced benefit income) and community degradation effects (service withdrawal).

The frontier question following Baccini et al. (2024): is the effect of austerity on populism **mediated by status/recognition mechanisms** or **by material hardship alone**? Their paper demonstrates reduced-form effects; the mechanism remains untested.

### Gaps on the frontier

No published paper uses the Baccini (2024) austerity data **merged with Cicollini's positional income change measure** to test whether the austerity → populism pathway runs through status-relative decline rather than absolute income decline. This is a tractable question with available data.

## 6. Social Investment: The Empirical Evidence

### What the frontier has established (2022–2026)

**Hemerijck, Ronchi & Plavgo (2023, European Political Science Review)** — "Social investment as a conceptual framework for analysing well-being returns and reforms in 21st century welfare states" — provides the most comprehensive recent synthesis of what social investment actually accomplishes. Key findings:

- Social investment policies consistently improve stock (human capital), flow (labour market transitions), and buffer (safety net adequacy) outcomes in countries that implement them consistently.
- The political problem: social investment generates diffuse long-run benefits but concentrated short-run costs, making it harder to build winning coalitions than passive transfers.
- Countries with already-high social investment spending (Nordic) show diminishing returns; those with low base-rates (Southern, Eastern Europe) show larger returns — with implications for heterogeneity analysis.

The key challenge to the social investment thesis from the 2022–2026 frontier: **Busemeyer et al. (2021)** show that the very voters who would benefit most from social investment (routine workers in automation-exposed occupations) are the least likely to demand it. The political economy is self-defeating: social investment requires sustained political investment, but those who need it most are politically aligned with parties that oppose it.

### Gaps on the frontier

The social investment literature rarely measures political consequences — it asks whether social investment improves labour market outcomes, but not whether it shapes political preferences or populist support. A paper that uses variation in social investment spending across ESS country-waves (available from CPDS via Baccini 2024 raw data) to test whether social investment reduces status anxiety or populist support would be a direct contribution to the frontier.

## 7. Welfare Conditionality

### What the frontier has established (2022–2026)

The welfare conditionality literature has increasingly turned toward political consequences, linking conditionality design to legitimacy, take-up, and political behavior.

Work by **Immervoll & Knotz (2018)** (preceding the 2022–2026 window but highly cited in it) established a comparative typology of conditionality strictness. The 2022–2026 frontier has moved toward testing the political consequences of this variation.

**Wright & Dwyer (2022)** and related UK work documents the lived experience of conditionality — Universal Credit's sanctions regime — and its effects on welfare recipients' dignity, mental health, and political engagement. The finding: punitive conditionality demobilises politically, consistent with earlier work on political participation and welfare receipt.

The conceptually important contribution in the 2022–2026 window is the distinction between **conditional reciprocity** (you receive help in exchange for active contribution — compatible with producerism and dignity) and **punitive surveillance** (you comply with bureaucratic requirements under threat of sanction — inconsistent with producerism and dignity). PRRP voters support the former, oppose the latter. Ben's DPAA theory maps onto this distinction but has not been tested empirically.

### Gaps on the frontier

Cross-national variation in conditionality design is well-documented (Immervoll, Knotz). Individual-level data on conditionality experiences is sparse. Crucially: **no paper tests whether conditionality design moderates the Kurer-style anticipation pathway** — whether workers in high-automation-risk occupations who live in high-conditionality welfare regimes show stronger or weaker radical right support than equivalent workers in low-conditionality regimes. This requires ESS individual-level data merged with country-level conditionality measures.

## Summary Assessment: Where the Frontier Stands

### What is well-established (2022–2026)

- PRRP voters have a coherent, distinctive welfare preference structure (Busemeyer/Rathgeb tradition)
- Anticipated hardship drives radical right support more than experienced hardship (Kurer → Im 2023 → Häusermann et al. 2023 → Pitts & Winter 2024)
- Punitive activation can generate self-undermining political feedback (Bergman 2024, Wagner 2023)
- Austerity causes populist voting through identifiable mechanisms (Baccini et al. 2024)
- Social investment improves outcomes but faces a demand-side political problem

### What remains genuinely contested

- Whether status anxiety is a proximate cause or a mechanism (identification problem)
- Whether cross-national welfare regime heterogeneity moderates the core pathways (limited evidence)
- Whether implementation quality — as distinct from formal policy design — matters politically

### What is genuinely missing (tractable gaps)

1. **The positional income pathway**: Does downward positional income trajectory (Cicollini's `posit_income_change`) predict radical right vote independently of current income, after controlling for standard demographics? This is directly testable with available data.

2. **Regime moderation of the status pathway**: Does the Gidron/Hall-style subjective social status → populism relationship vary systematically across Esping-Andersen welfare regime types? Available in ESS + country-level regime coding.

3. **The austerity–status mechanism test**: Does the Baccini austerity → populism effect run through individual-level positional income decline? Requires merging Baccini's district data with Cicollini's positional income data — both available.

4. **Implementation quality as political mediator**: Does `stfgov` / institutional trust moderate the automation-exposure → populist support relationship? Testable in ESS, though identification is cross-sectional.

5. **Proactive versus reactive welfare design and the apprehension group**: Does spending on ALMPs (CPDS data, available via Baccini raw files) suppress the anticipation pathway? Requires country × wave variation in ALMP spending merged with individual ESS data.
