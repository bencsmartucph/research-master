# Economic Vulnerability, Status Politics, and Welfare Institutions: A Multidimensional Framework for Understanding Populist Resilience

## 1. Introduction: Beyond the Populist Spending Paradox

Conventional political economy models predict that economic vulnerability should increase support for redistributive policies and the parties offering them. Yet empirical evidence increasingly contradicts this expectation, revealing what Gingrich (2019) identifies as a "populist spending paradox": in regions exposed to automation, welfare compensation not only failed to reduce populist voting but in some specifications actually increased it. This paradox fundamentally challenges the embedded liberalism thesis that welfare states can effectively buffer citizens against the political consequences of economic disruption (Rodrik, 1998; Ruggie, 1982).

As Kurer (2020, p. 1826) observes: "What makes this development so fascinating from a political science perspective is the difficulty of an adequate policy response. When it is not actual economic adversity that drives political resentment among relative losers of occupational change, 'more welfare' in the traditional sense might not help alleviate grievances." This suggests that something more complex than simple material deprivation drives populist support, requiring a more nuanced understanding of how welfare institutions mediate between economic vulnerability and political behavior.

This paper advances a theoretical framework explaining why standard compensatory welfare approaches inadequately address populist mobilization while certain welfare designs may prove more effective. By synthesizing insights from economic sociology, political psychology, and comparative welfare state research, I identify three critical dimensions through which welfare institutions shape political responses to economic vulnerability: status politics, temporal security, and ontological security. These dimensions collectively inform a "Dignity-Preserving Active Assistance" (DPAA) theory of institutional buffering that explains cross-national variation in populist resilience.

## 2. Economic Deprivation versus Cultural Backlash: Transcending a False Dichotomy

### 2.1 The Limitations of Economic Deprivation Explanations

Economic explanations for populist support identify various material factors underlying political discontent. Trade exposure (Autor et al., 2020; Colantone & Stanig, 2018), automation risk (Frey et al., 2018), and regional inequality (Rodríguez-Pose, 2018) have all been linked to increased support for populist parties and candidates.

However, these approaches face significant limitations. Purely economic explanations struggle to explain why populist voters often support policies contrary to their apparent economic interests or why populist support is not concentrated among the poorest segments of society but often among relatively secure middle-income voters (Gidron & Hall, 2017).

Most critically, recent research demonstrates that the relationship between economic hardship and populist voting is neither simple nor direct. As Kurer (2020, p. 1825) finds: "surviving in an environment of structural decline drives support for right-wing populist parties, whereas the actual experience of economic hardship immediately and substantively reduces this support." Similarly, Pitts and Winter (2024) distinguish between anticipated hardship (fear of future decline), which activates status concerns and leads to socially conservative politics associated with right-wing populism, and experienced hardship (actual economic disadvantage), which drives demand for economic security more typically associated with left-wing policies.

This distinction helps explain why economically secure but status-threatened individuals might support right-wing populism, while those experiencing actual economic hardship might support left-wing redistributive policies. Economically vulnerable workers who fear but have not yet experienced downward mobility may be particularly susceptible to populist appeals that promise status restoration rather than material compensation.

### 2.2 The Limitations of Cultural Backlash Explanations

Cultural explanations have gained prominence in response to the limitations of economic accounts. Norris and Inglehart (2019) argue that populism represents a cultural backlash against progressive value change, primarily among older generations and less educated segments of society who feel their traditional values threatened. Mutz (2018) finds that status threat—concerns about the declining position of traditionally dominant groups—predicts support for Trump better than economic hardship. In her experimental design, she demonstrates that it is "the dominant group's sense of its status in the hierarchy" rather than material circumstances that drove political preferences.

These cultural approaches provide valuable insights into the psychological dimensions of populist politics but often treat cultural grievances as autonomous from economic conditions. This can obscure how economic processes shape the context in which cultural identities are formed and mobilized. As Carreras et al. (2019) demonstrate in their study of Brexit voting, cultural attitudes like anti-immigrant sentiment and Euroscepticism significantly mediate the relationship between long-term economic decline and support for leaving the EU. Their research suggests that "regional economic inequalities are critical to explain the emergence of cultural grievances. People who live in districts that have suffered long periods of economic decline are more likely to adopt anti-immigrant and Eurosceptic attitudes" (Carreras et al., 2019, p. 21).

This mediating role of cultural grievances challenges the portrayal of economic and cultural explanations as competing alternatives. Instead, economic vulnerability appears to create conditions where cultural grievances flourish, often manifesting in anti-immigrant attitudes and opposition to international integration, which in turn drive support for populist political options.

### 2.3 Status Politics: The Missing Link

Recent scholarship suggests that subjective social status operates as a crucial mediating variable linking economic conditions to political behavior. Gidron and Hall (2017, p. S57) argue that support for populist right parties reflects a fundamental status anxiety: "We suggest that one way to do so is to see status anxiety as a proximate factor inducing support for populism, and economic and cultural developments as factors that combine to precipitate such anxiety."

Their conceptualization of subjective social status—"the level of social respect or esteem people believe is accorded them within the social order" (Gidron & Hall, 2017, p. S61)—provides a bridge between economic and cultural explanations. Economic changes (declining manufacturing employment, rising precarity) and cultural developments (increasing value placed on higher education, diversity, and cosmopolitanism) jointly undermine the status of less-educated men, creating fertile ground for populist appeals.

Similarly, Ballard-Rosa et al. (2022) demonstrate how economic shocks generate status concerns that manifest through cultural and identity politics. Their research shows that economic anxiety activates in-group/out-group boundaries, particularly when those boundaries align with existing social hierarchies. These activated boundaries become vehicles for expressing economic grievances in cultural terms, explaining why economic vulnerability often manifests as cultural backlash.

Mutz (2018) shows that status threat, rather than material hardship, drove support for Trump in the 2016 election. Her analysis reveals how economic competition can activate status boundaries, particularly among historically dominant groups facing perceived decline. This helps explain why economically secure individuals may still support populist candidates who promise to restore their group's perceived status.

Gest, Reny, and Mayer (2017) further develop this status perspective through their concept of "nostalgic deprivation"—the perceived discrepancy between individuals' current social status and their perceptions of their past status. Their research demonstrates that this subjective sense of status decline, rather than objective economic position, motivates support for radical right figures who promise to restore an idealized past social order.

These status-based approaches suggest that economic vulnerability matters politically primarily through its effects on subjective status perceptions rather than through direct material deprivation. As Gidron and Hall (2018, p. 2-3) observe: "If compensation comes in the form of social benefits, our analysis suggests that this may not be enough to assuage voters drawn to radical parties. Radical right voters are not especially strong supporters of redistribution. What distinguishes them from voters for mainstream parties are concerns about recognition – the feeling that they have been pushed from the mainstream to the margins of society."

## 3. The Spatial Dimension: Community Degradation and Populist Support

The spatial context of economic vulnerability significantly shapes its political consequences. Colantone and Stanig (2018) demonstrate in their analysis of Brexit voting that regions more exposed to Chinese import competition showed significantly higher support for leaving the EU. Their research reveals that trade-induced regional decline created conditions favorable to nationalist politics, with communities experiencing economic vulnerability more susceptible to anti-EU narratives.

Similarly, Autor et al. (2020) show that U.S. regions more exposed to import competition from China experienced increased political polarization, with trade-exposed districts with white majorities shifting toward conservative Republicans and those with non-white majorities shifting toward liberal Democrats. This research highlights how economic vulnerability interacts with local demographic characteristics to produce divergent political responses.

Bolet (2021) provides further evidence for this spatial dimension through her analysis of pub closures in Britain and their relationship to UKIP support. Her research demonstrates that the closure of community pubs—which serve as social hubs and embody local traditions—significantly increases support for radical right parties. This "Local Socio-Cultural Degradation Theory" suggests that the decline of communal spaces triggers both social isolation and cultural identity loss that make individuals more receptive to populist appeals promising to restore traditional communities.

As Bolet (2021, p. 7) argues, these local spaces "are not only functional enablers of social interaction; they also convey historical, geographical, and social meanings that their inhabitants internalize and re-enact in their daily interactions within such spaces." Their decline represents not just material loss but the erosion of identities and traditions that provided meaning and social integration.

Rodríguez-Pose's (2018) concept of "the revenge of the places that don't matter" similarly emphasizes how persistent territorial inequalities fuel anti-establishment voting. His research shows that populist support is particularly strong in regions experiencing long-term economic decline relative to metropolitan centers, reflecting resentment among those who feel geographically marginalized in addition to economically vulnerable.

Fetzer (2019) demonstrates how policy changes affecting local communities can trigger populist backlash. His analysis of austerity measures in the UK reveals that regions more affected by welfare cuts showed significantly higher support for Brexit. This suggests that welfare institutions operate not just at the individual level but at the community level, with retrenchment undermining social cohesion and fostering conditions favorable to populist politics.

This spatial dimension highlights how economic vulnerability manifests not just as individual hardship but as community-level degradation that undermines social integration and traditional identities. As Gidron and Hall (2018) argue, populist support represents a problem of social integration—a response to feeling "pushed from the mainstream to the margins of society." This marginalization has both economic and cultural dimensions but is fundamentally rooted in the experience of valued social roles and identities becoming peripheral to the dominant social and economic order.

## 4. Cognitive and Identity Mechanisms

### 4.1 Identity Switching and Belief Polarization

Bonomi, Gennaioli, and Tabellini (2021) provide a formal model explaining how economic insecurity triggers identity switches that shape political preferences. Their framework identifies two key mechanisms: (1) when policy conflict makes certain social divides salient, voters identify with either economic or cultural groups, and (2) this identification causes voters to slant their beliefs toward the stereotypes of their identified group.

When voters identify with their economic class, they prioritize material interests and support redistributive policies. However, when they identify with their cultural group (based on values, religion, or nationalism), they prioritize identity-based concerns that may contradict their economic interests. Economic shocks that disproportionately affect certain cultural groups can trigger identity switching from class to culture, leading economically vulnerable voters to support policies that protect cultural identities rather than material interests.

This identity-switching mechanism helps explain why economic vulnerability sometimes translates into cultural backlash rather than support for redistribution. As the authors explain, "If these shocks hurt less educated and hence more conservative voters, and benefit more educated and hence more progressive voters, they make cultural cleavages more salient and can induce a switch to cultural identity. As a result, economic losers become more socially and fiscally conservative" (Bonomi et al., 2021, p. 2375).

### 4.2 Relative Deprivation and Nostalgic Deprivation

Gest, Reny, and Mayer (2017) introduce the concept of "nostalgic deprivation" to explain support for radical right movements. This psychological phenomenon refers to the perceived discrepancy between individuals' current social, political, and economic status and their perceptions of their past status. Unlike traditional relative deprivation, which often compares oneself to others in the present, nostalgic deprivation focuses on temporal comparison between perceived past and present status.

The authors demonstrate that "supporters of the Radical Right are consumed by their perceived drop in status" (Gest et al., 2017, p. 3). This perception of declining status relative to a remembered past drives support for candidates who promise to restore previous status hierarchies—often through appeals to make America "great again" or return Britain to former glory.

Crucially, nostalgic deprivation operates across multiple dimensions: economic, political, and social. Individuals who perceive themselves as economically declining, politically underrepresented, and socially marginalized are particularly susceptible to radical right appeals that promise comprehensive status restoration across these domains.

### 4.3 Group-Based Economics and Zero-Sum Thinking

Green, Hellwig, and Fieldhouse (2020) further develop this relational understanding of economic perception through their concept of "Group-Based Economics." Their research demonstrates that perceptions about how out-groups (such as ethnic minorities or metropolitan elites) are faring economically compared to one's in-group strongly predict support for Brexit. This focus on group-based comparisons helps explain why voters often respond to relative economic gains and losses rather than absolute economic changes.

As Green and colleagues argue, "When the same groups of people are affected by both types of developments, the corresponding discontent can be especially deep" (Green et al., 2020, p. 7-8). This insight connects economic vulnerability to identity-based grievances, explaining why economically threatened groups often direct resentment toward other groups perceived as gaining at their expense.

The zero-sum quality of status comparisons makes them particularly politically potent. Unlike absolute economic resources, which can simultaneously increase for everyone, relative status inherently creates winners and losers. As Gidron and Hall (2017, p. 66) note, "Because social status is based on a rank ordering, it is somewhat like a positional good, in the sense that, when many others acquire more status, the value of one's own status may decline." This explains why status-threatened groups often resist policies that might elevate the position of outgroups, even when those policies offer material benefits.

## 5. Welfare Institutions and Political Mediation

### 5.1 From Compensation to Social Investment

Welfare institutions fundamentally shape how economic vulnerability translates into political behavior, yet traditional compensatory approaches often fail to address the status and identity dimensions driving populist support. Gingrich (2019, p. 3) identifies the core dilemma: "Automation then, simultaneously threatens the material well-being and social status of exposed workers, the former mechanism pushing voters to the mainstream left parties, while the latter pushes them to the populist right." Traditional compensatory policies may address material needs but often fail to address status concerns, limiting their effectiveness in countering populist tendencies.

Recent research suggests a shift from passive compensation to active social investment might better address these multidimensional concerns. Hemerijck, Ronchi, and Plavgo (2023, p. 485) conceptualize social investment as a bundle of complementary policy functions: (1) "raising and maintaining the 'stock' of human capital and capabilities throughout the life course"; (2) "easing the 'flow' of gendered labour market and life-course transitions"; and (3) "granting inclusive safety nets." This multifunctional approach extends beyond material compensation to address capabilities development, participation, and inclusion—dimensions directly relevant to the status concerns driving populist support.

Busemeyer and Garritzmann (2017) provide evidence for the appeal of social investment approaches. Their research demonstrates that "rather than demanding compensation for globalization-related income losses ex post, individuals in open economies are more likely to demand educational investments in order to avoid unemployment ex ante by demanding increased skill-investments" (p. 1). This suggests citizens intuitively prefer proactive capabilities development over reactive compensation when facing economic insecurity.

### 5.2 The Distinctive Welfare Preferences of Populist Voters

Understanding how welfare institutions shape populist tendencies requires examining the distinctive welfare preferences of populist voters. Busemeyer, Rathgeb, and Sahm (2021) demonstrate that populist radical right party (PRRP) voters exhibit a distinctive welfare preference profile: moderate support for social transfers, strong support for workfare, and remarkably low support for social investment. This pattern differs significantly from both traditional left and mainstream right voters, suggesting PRRPs appeal to a distinct welfare constituency.

Rathgeb and Busemeyer (2021) further elaborate that PRRP voters favor what they call a "particularistic-authoritarian welfare state" that emphasizes deservingness hierarchies rather than universal rights. This model combines moderate support for "deserving" recipients (particularly the elderly and native-born) with punitive conditions for those deemed undeserving (particularly immigrants and the unemployed).

This distinctive welfare preference profile helps explain why traditional compensatory approaches often fail to reduce populist support. As Kurer (2020, p. 1805) observes, "Voters who experience material hardship become dependent on state support and should thus have a strong interest in a generous welfare state to cushion the economic downfall and support their re-entry in the labor market. Voters who primarily suffer from status anxiety are more likely to look for propositions that promise to maintain or reinstall their place in the social hierarchy." Traditional welfare approaches may address the former but rarely the latter, limiting their effectiveness in countering populist tendencies.

### 5.3 Welfare Design and Social Integration

Welfare institutions significantly shape social integration—a critical factor in populist susceptibility. Gidron and Hall (2018, p. 2) define social integration as "the social relations that link individuals and promote their sense of being valued members of society." They identify three dimensions of social integration: the degree to which individuals see themselves as part of a shared normative order, their levels of social interaction with others, and the extent to which they feel recognized or respected by others in society.

Different welfare designs foster different patterns of social integration. Universal welfare programs that provide benefits based on citizenship rather than means-testing promote broader social inclusion and foster perceptions of shared fate. As Gidron and Hall (2018, p. 2) note, "A decent job has always been central to effective social integration and there are no symbolic substitutes for it." Welfare approaches that focus on meaningful employment opportunities and skill development directly address this core component of social integration.

By contrast, means-tested programs that sharply distinguish between contributors and recipients can reinforce social divisions and undermine perceptions of shared fate. Wagner (2023, p. 3) demonstrates how welfare recipients who experience "exclusion and misrecognition" often respond by "kicking down" against groups perceived as less deserving, creating a "downward spiral of solidarity erosion." This suggests welfare designs that emphasize status distinctions and conditionality may inadvertently reinforce divisive identity politics.

### 5.4 The Temporal Dimension: Proactive versus Reactive Approaches

Beyond program design, the temporal orientation of welfare institutions significantly shapes their political effects. Traditional compensatory approaches typically engage after economic hardship materializes, addressing experienced rather than anticipated hardship. However, as Pitts and Winter (2024) and Kurer (2020) demonstrate, anticipated hardship often drives populist support more strongly than experienced hardship.

Busemeyer and Garritzmann (2017, p. 4) elaborate this temporal distinction: "Skill-investment might be preferred as an effective insurance policy against labor market risk compared to ex-post compensation through transfer payments." This suggests proactive approaches that address anticipated vulnerability before it materializes may more effectively prevent populist reactions than reactive approaches that engage only after hardship occurs.

This temporal distinction helps explain the "populist spending paradox" identified by Gingrich (2019). Traditional compensatory welfare programs that engage after economic hardship materializes may effectively address material needs but fail to alleviate the anticipatory status anxiety driving populist support. By contrast, proactive social investment approaches that develop capabilities and enhance future security may better address the temporal dimension of economic vulnerability.

## 6. Dignity-Preserving Active Assistance: A Theoretical Synthesis

The three dimensions identified above—status politics, spatial context, and temporal security—collectively inform what I term "Dignity-Preserving Active Assistance" (DPAA) theory. This theory posits that welfare institutions can effectively buffer against populist politics when they:

1. Maintain or enhance economic status rather than reinforcing status hierarchies
2. Support community integration and place-based dignity rather than focusing solely on individual assistance
3. Provide proactive security against anticipated hardship rather than merely reacting to experienced hardship

DPAA extends beyond traditional Active Labor Market Policies (ALMPs) to encompass any social, welfare, or labor market policy designed to help individuals and communities absorb economic shocks without undermining their sense of dignity and belonging. This conceptualization distinguishes DPAA from both passive transfers (which may provide material support but lack investment in capabilities and communities) and punitive workfare (which may activate but often at the cost of dignity).

### 6.1 Status Stabilization Pathway

DPAA policies address the status dimensions of economic vulnerability in several ways. First, by focusing on capability development rather than passive assistance, they offer pathways to valued social roles that maintain status despite economic changes. As Hemerijck et al. (2023, p. 489) observe, social investment connects to Sen's capability approach with its emphasis on "human flourishing," enabling rather than merely compensating.

Second, DPAA policies can potentially reduce status anxiety by focusing on skill development and enablement rather than punitive compliance. These approaches may offer socially acceptable pathways for individuals to maintain or regain social status and sense of contribution, potentially reducing the appeal of PRR parties that mobilize around narratives of status loss. As Bergman (2024, p. 523) notes, populist right success often draws on "nostalgic producerism" that positions supporters as "virtuous, being squeezed by non-productive 'parasites'." By emphasizing productivity and contribution, DPAA policies may align with producerist values while avoiding welfare chauvinism.

Finally, DPAA approaches are typically implemented with greater emphasis on dignity and agency than traditional welfare programs. Respect for autonomy and recognition of recipients' capabilities may reduce the status degradation often associated with welfare receipt. As Wagner (2023, p. 4) suggests, social investment policies that "provide active resources enabling recipients to improve their human capital, labor market position, and generally their capacity to socially participate... can lead to experiences of social inclusion."

### 6.2 Community Integration Pathway

DPAA approaches can address the spatial dimension of economic vulnerability by focusing on community-level interventions rather than solely individual assistance. As Bolet (2021) demonstrates, the decline of communal spaces contributes significantly to populist support by undermining social cohesion and cultural identity. DPAA policies that invest in community institutions, support local businesses, and enhance public spaces may mitigate this "local socio-cultural degradation" that fuels populist sentiment.

Additionally, DPAA approaches can potentially address place-based identity concerns that manifest through populist politics. Rodríguez-Pose (2018) argues that persistent territorial inequalities have created "places that don't matter"—regions that feel economically and culturally marginalized within national economies. DPAA policies that recognize the distinctive needs and identities of these regions may reduce their susceptibility to populist appeals that promise to restore their importance.

Colantone and Stanig (2018, p. 951) emphasize the importance of this place-based approach: "As globalization threatens the success and survival of entire industrial districts, the affected communities seem to respond with their voting behavior sociotropically." This suggests welfare institutions must address community-level economic resilience, not just individual economic security, to effectively mitigate populist tendencies.

### 6.3 Temporal Security Pathway

DPAA approaches address the temporal dimension of economic vulnerability through their proactive orientation. By investing in capabilities development before hardship materializes, they potentially reduce the anticipatory anxiety that drives populist support. As Pitts and Winter (2024, p. 12) note, "The central social group in the swing to the radical right, on this reading, are those who are coping on their current income but for whom the subjective risk of automation is increasing." Traditional compensatory approaches typically ignore this group because they have not yet experienced material hardship, while DPAA policies can proactively address their future-oriented concerns.

This proactive orientation aligns with Busemeyer and Garritzmann's (2017, p. 1) finding that "individuals in open economies are more likely to demand educational investments in order to avoid unemployment ex ante by demanding increased skill-investments." By investing in capabilities enhancement before vulnerabilities materialize, DPAA policies address the anticipated hardship driving populist support rather than waiting until vulnerabilities translate into material hardship.

Additionally, DPAA approaches may increase temporal security by providing more predictable pathways through economic transitions. Their focus on skills development and capabilities enhancement creates forward-looking narratives that contrast with the nostalgic orientation of populist politics. Rather than promising to restore an idealized past (a core feature of populist appeal identified by Gest et al., 2017), DPAA approaches potentially offer constructive pathways toward a secure future.

## 7. Implications and Directions for Future Research

The DPAA framework has several implications for understanding welfare institutions' role in mediating between economic vulnerability and populist politics.

First, it suggests welfare institutions' effects on populist tendencies depend not only on their generosity but on their qualitative design—how they treat status, community, and temporal concerns. Two welfare states with similar spending levels but different approaches may have opposite effects on populist mobilization. A generous welfare state emphasizing means-tested benefits and punitive activation might exacerbate status anxiety despite its material generosity, while a welfare state with moderate spending focused on dignity-preserving active assistance might more effectively mitigate populist sentiment.

Second, the framework highlights the importance of implementation quality in determining welfare policies' political effects. As Bergman (2024, p. 526) argues, some labor market policies create "self-undermining feedback effects, whereby high levels of intervention might lead to public opinion (and voting behaviour) to decrease interventionism and vice versa." This occurs when policies are implemented in ways that undermine dignity and agency, reinforcing rather than alleviating the status anxiety and existential uncertainty driving populist support.

Third, the DPAA framework suggests welfare institutions' effects on populist tendencies may vary depending on existing inequality patterns and political contexts. In highly unequal societies where status hierarchies are already pronounced, welfare institutions may need to more explicitly address status concerns to effectively buffer against populist politics. By contrast, in more egalitarian societies with weaker status hierarchies, the material dimensions of welfare provision may remain more politically salient.

Future research should examine how different welfare regimes shape status perceptions, community integration, and temporal security across diverse economic and political contexts. Particularly valuable would be longitudinal studies tracking how welfare reforms affect populist tendencies over time, allowing for identification of causal mechanisms linking institutional changes to political outcomes. Additionally, comparative research examining how similar economic shocks produce different political responses across welfare regimes would enhance understanding of institutional mediation effects.

The DPAA framework offers a promising foundation for developing welfare approaches that address both the material and psychological dimensions of economic vulnerability. By focusing on dignity, community, and security rather than merely material compensation, these approaches may enhance democratic resilience in an era of rapid economic change.
