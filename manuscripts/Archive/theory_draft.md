### Ben Smart
### University of Copenhagen
### Welfare State Seminar — Draft

## I. Introduction

A growing body of research documents that the economic losers of automation, trade exposure, and deindustrialisation disproportionately support populist radical right parties — parties whose policy programmes typically involve welfare retrenchment, immigration restriction, and opposition to the very redistributive mechanisms that would most directly address their material situation. The empirical pattern is robust across countries, time periods, and measurement strategies. Workers in high routine-task-intensity occupations, residents of communities exposed to import competition, and individuals experiencing subjective status decline all show elevated support for exclusionary politics (Gingrich 2019; Colantone and Stanig 2018; Kurer 2020; Autor et al. 2020).

The question this paper addresses is not *whether* this pattern exists — that question has been thoroughly studied — but *why it takes the form it does*. Why does economic disruption produce exclusionary politics rather than demand for redistribution? Why do people experiencing genuine material vulnerability turn against the welfare state rather than toward it? Why, in short, does economic disruption so often produce the wrong politics — political responses that systematically fail to serve the interests of the people experiencing the disruption?

The conventional answer from comparative political economy treats the welfare state as a buffer that moderates the intensity of political backlash. More generous welfare spending, in this framework, reduces the economic insecurity that fuels populist support. Less generous spending leaves that insecurity unaddressed, producing stronger backlash. The operative variable is the quantity of compensation. The policy implication is straightforward: spend more, get less populism.

This paper argues that the buffering framework is fundamentally incomplete. It cannot account for a growing body of evidence showing that compensation frequently fails to reduce backlash, sometimes actively increases it, and is often resisted by the very people it is designed to help. Instead, this paper proposes that welfare institutions function not as buffers but as *sorting mechanisms* — institutional configurations that determine the *direction* of political response to economic disruption, not merely its intensity. The same economic shock, filtered through dignity-preserving welfare institutions, can produce demand for solidarity and structural reform. The same shock, filtered through stigmatising, conditional institutions, produces othering, scapegoating, and what Busemeyer et al. (2023) term the "particularistic-authoritarian" turn. The welfare state does not simply stand between economic disruption and political backlash. It actively shapes what the political response *is*.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section II establishes the empirical failures of the buffering model. Section III develops the theoretical argument: the sorting mechanism, operating through welfare institutions' effects on the self-concept, determines whether economic disruption activates a cascade of identity switching, misattribution, and defensive othering — or whether it is processed through class identity and solidarity. Section IV traces how punitive welfare design manufactures the attitudinal raw materials for its own delegitimation through a recursive feedback loop. Section V considers the counterfactual: how dignity-preserving design prevents the cascade from activating. Section VI discusses the visibility asymmetry of modern welfare states as a compounding factor. Section VII concludes.

## II. The Limits of the Buffering Model

The theoretical foundation for treating the welfare state as a buffer against political extremism derives from the "embedded liberalism" bargain described by Ruggie (1982). The core logic is that democratic governments, recognising that economic openness produces losers as well as winners, provide social protection as compensation in exchange for continued public support for open markets. When this bargain holds, losers accept openness because they are materially cushioned against its worst effects. When it breaks down — through welfare retrenchment, austerity, or insufficient compensation — the losers revolt.

Contemporary applications of this framework test whether welfare generosity reduces support for populist or radical right parties. Vlandas and Halikiopoulou (2022) argue that the effect of social risk exposure on radical right voting depends on the generosity of social policies targeting those specific risks. Their framework implies that governments can shape political outcomes through the policies they implement. In this telling, the welfare state is a policy lever that responsible governments can pull to reduce the appeal of radical politics.

Three categories of evidence suggest this framework is missing something important.

**Compensation that doesn't work.** Gallego and Kurer (2022) identify what they call a "concerning finding" from the only careful comparative study of whether public policies can prevent the political backlash of automation losers. Gingrich's (2019) analysis finds that workers highly exposed to automation are not less likely to vote for populist parties in countries with more generous early retirement policies, more in-kind spending, or more protective labour market regulation. None of the standard compensatory instruments reduce the political turn to protest voting. If the buffering model were correct — if the operative variable were the generosity of compensation — countries with more generous welfare states should show weaker automation-to-populism effects. They do not.

**Compensation that backfires.** Stutzmann (2025) examines the political consequences of Germany's coal phase-out, a case where affected communities received substantial compensatory investment. Despite maintained material conditions, affected municipalities showed higher abstention rates, lower support for the issue-owning party, and signs of perceived economic deprivation. Bergman (2023) theorises "self-undermining" policy feedback effects, where visible welfare interventions favouring groups that potential radical right voters view unfavourably — immigrants, labour market outsiders, the unemployed — actually catalyse greater radical right support. Fetzer's (2019) influential analysis of Brexit and austerity shows that welfare cuts predicted Leave support, but the mechanism was not material deprivation per se — it was the erosion of political trust, efficacy, and the sense of being heard by government.

**Compensation that is resisted.** Perhaps most puzzling for the buffering model, Pelc (2025) demonstrates that a sizeable portion of workers resist being compensated out of work even when the compensation fully replaces their income. Workers prefer reduced hours over full unemployment at the same pay. They prefer compensation from former employers over identical compensation from government. And Protestant religious affiliation predicts resistance to non-work compensation even after a windfall. These findings cannot be reconciled with a model in which welfare generosity is the operative variable. Something about the *form, source, and meaning* of compensation matters independently of its material value.

What these disparate findings share is an implication: the political effects of welfare institutions cannot be reduced to their spending levels. Something about how welfare operates — what it communicates, whose identity it affirms or degrades, what moral frameworks it installs — determines whether economic disruption is metabolised as solidarity or backlash. As Guriev (2024) observes, compensating the losers of economic transformation has proven much harder than economists assumed — not because of insufficient resources, but because of political and institutional constraints that the buffering framework does not theorise.

## III. The Sorting Mechanism: Welfare, the Self-Concept, and Political Direction

### A. The Core Argument

The central theoretical claim of this paper is that welfare institutions mediate between economic disruption and political response by shaping the *self-concept* of economically vulnerable individuals — their understanding of their own worth, their position in the social hierarchy, and their relationship to other groups in society. This self-concept determines which of several possible political responses to disruption gets activated.

The formulation is borrowed from Banerjee and Duflo, who argue that the goal of social policy in times of economic change is to help people absorb shocks without allowing those shocks to affect their sense of themselves. This paper takes that formulation literally as a causal hypothesis. When welfare institutions preserve the self-concept — when they communicate to disrupted individuals that their situation is a structural problem rather than a personal failing, that they remain valued members of the political community, and that the appropriate response is collective — disruption can be processed through class identity and solidarity. When welfare institutions damage the self-concept — when they communicate that recipients are objects of suspicion, that their economic position reflects moral failure, and that they must compete against other claimants for scarce legitimacy — disruption activates a cascade of psychological and political mechanisms that produces exclusionary politics.

This is a different claim from saying that welfare institutions affect political attitudes. That claim is well-established. The specific contribution here is to identify the self-concept as the pivotal mechanism, and to show that previously isolated findings about identity switching, misattribution, and defensive othering are not independent phenomena — they are sequential stages of a single cascade, and the welfare state is the switching variable that determines whether the cascade fires.

### B. Two Channels: Implementation and Visibility

Welfare institutions shape the self-concept through two distinct channels.

**The implementation channel** operates through direct recipient experience. When individuals interact with welfare systems, those interactions teach them things about their relationship to the state and to other citizens. Soss (1999) demonstrates this through field research showing that different welfare programmes create different "learning environments." Programmes with respectful implementation and universal eligibility foster civic engagement and institutional trust. Programmes characterised by surveillance, conditionality, and suspicion generate alienation and political withdrawal. The encounter with the welfare state is not merely a material transaction — it is a formative political experience.

Wagner's (2022, 2023) research on welfare recipients' attitude formation provides the most direct evidence for this implementation channel. His core finding is that welfare recipients learn the recognition norms enacted on them by the state and subsequently mirror those norms in their attitudes toward other groups. Recipients of conditional, sanctioning welfare tend to adopt narrower conceptions of deservingness — they become more likely to draw sharp boundaries between themselves and those they perceive as less deserving. Recipients of enabling, social-investment-oriented welfare show the inverse pattern — positive experiences generate what Wagner terms more inclusive welfare attitudes, including toward outgroups. The welfare state, in short, teaches its recipients how to think about who deserves what. And what it teaches depends on how it treats them.

De Blok and Kumlin (2022) add an important nuance. Their research on procedural fairness in welfare delivery shows that fair procedures matter more for those who receive unfavourable outcomes — that procedural justice "softens the blow" for policy losers. When the process feels fair, people accept adverse outcomes without generalised institutional distrust. When the process feels arbitrary, degrading, or punitive, adverse outcomes generate broad disaffection. This asymmetry means that implementation quality matters most precisely for the people the welfare state is supposed to help — those who are already economically vulnerable and already receiving bad news about their material prospects.

**The visibility channel** operates more diffusely, shaping not just recipients' self-concepts but the broader public understanding of who receives welfare and why. Mettler (2011) identifies what she terms the "submerged state" — the extensive system of tax expenditures, loan guarantees, and indirect benefits that primarily serve middle and upper-income groups but remain invisible as government assistance. Mortgage interest deductions, employer-provided health insurance subsidies, and retirement savings tax advantages are all forms of welfare, but they are not experienced or politically processed as such. Meanwhile, direct transfers to lower-income groups — unemployment benefits, social assistance, housing allowances — remain highly visible, politically salient, and stigmatised.

This visibility asymmetry shapes attributional frameworks across the income distribution. Recipients of submerged welfare attribute their economic security to personal merit and market success. Recipients of visible welfare are marked — by themselves and others — as dependents on government assistance. The political consequence is that a large share of the population that benefits from the welfare state does not recognise itself as doing so, and develops beliefs about economic life — meritocratic beliefs, individual responsibility attributions, scepticism of "handouts" — that are structurally at odds with solidarity. Hochschild's (2016) finding that many American welfare recipients opposed government assistance is not a paradox once the visibility asymmetry is understood. These recipients have been taught, by the institutional design of the welfare state itself, to view their situation through the moral framework that visible welfare invites.

This channel is particularly relevant for understanding the political economy of welfare states that combine substantial hidden benefits for the middle class with stigmatised, means-tested benefits for the poor — a structure the author has previously analysed as a "dual welfare system" in the Australian context. The dual structure creates differentiated citizenship: one tier of citizens who experience the state as a facilitator of their market success, and another tier who experience the state as a monitor and disciplinarian. These differentiated experiences produce differentiated political identities with profound consequences for welfare politics.

## IV. The Damage Cascade

When welfare institutions damage the self-concept — through stigmatising implementation, punitive conditionality, or visibility asymmetry that marks recipients as dependents — they create the psychological preconditions for a cascade of mechanisms that converts economic disruption into exclusionary politics. This section traces that cascade through three stages.

### A. Stage One: Identity Switching

The starting point is Bonomi, Gennaioli, and Tabellini's (2021) model of identity switching. Their formal framework demonstrates that individuals possess multiple potential social identities — class-based, cultural, national, ethnic — and that which identity becomes politically salient depends on context. When economic disruption occurs, individuals whose class identity has been degraded are more likely to switch to cultural or national identity as an alternative source of self-worth and social positioning. Under cultural identity, preferences shift: redistribution demand decreases, cultural conservatism intensifies, and the individual's political behaviour comes to reflect their cultural group's stereotypical positions rather than their material interests.

Gallego and Kurer (2022) describe a closely related process: when occupation-related identities become less valuable due to economic decline, individuals become more likely to adopt identities that provide higher prestige and self-esteem, such as national identities. Kurer (2020) documents how routine workers threatened by automation but still clinging to their jobs are drawn to right-wing populist parties that offer remedies against status anxiety — not economic compensation, but the promise of restored social worth in a nostalgic vision of society where routine work was valued.

The welfare state enters this process at a specific point. Class identity is not degraded solely by economic disruption. It is degraded, or preserved, by the institutional context in which disruption is experienced. A worker who loses their manufacturing job in Denmark enters a system that treats retraining and income support as citizenship rights — the experience, while painful, does not fundamentally challenge their status as a valued member of the community. A worker who loses a similar job in a system organised around means-testing, conditionality, and behavioural surveillance enters an institutional experience that actively communicates reduced worth. The Danish worker's class identity may survive the economic shock. The other worker's class identity has been doubly damaged — first by the job loss itself, and then by the institutional response to that loss.

The implication is that the welfare state doesn't merely fail to prevent identity switching. In its conditional, stigmatising form, it *accelerates* the switch by degrading precisely the identity (class, occupational) that might otherwise anchor a solidaristic political response. This is the sorting mechanism at work: institutional design determines whether economic disruption is processed through the identity that produces demand for redistribution, or through the identity that produces cultural backlash.

### B. Stage Two: Misattribution

Once individuals have switched from class to cultural identity, the second stage of the cascade activates. Gallego and Kurer (2022) provide the most developed account of the misattribution mechanism: workers experiencing economic decline from automation tend to blame immigration and trade rather than technological change itself. Their analysis identifies three mutually reinforcing reasons for this pattern. First, it is psychologically more gratifying to attribute economic decline to identifiable outgroups than to impersonal structural change — immigration and trade offer clear adversaries, while machines do not. Second, political entrepreneurs supply anti-immigrant and anti-trade narratives that connect economic grievances to politically actionable targets. Third, automation operates gradually and invisibly compared to the more noticeable shocks of immigration or trade competition.

Wu (2022) demonstrates empirically that workers at higher automation risk feel less secure and are more likely to oppose free trade and immigration, but do not have different preferences about technology spending. The grievance is real; the attribution is wrong. And as Gallego and Kurer note, this misattribution is not merely an intellectual error — it leads to support for policies that do not address the actual source of the problem.

The welfare state's role in this stage operates through attributional frameworks. Alesina and Angeletos (2005) show that welfare regimes create self-reinforcing beliefs about the causes of economic inequality. Generous, universal systems tend to produce structural attributions — the belief that economic outcomes are shaped by forces beyond individual control, including luck, institutional context, and market structure. Residual, conditional systems tend to produce individual attributions — the belief that economic outcomes reflect effort, character, and personal choices. When people who hold individual attributions face economic disruption, they are predisposed to look for someone who is getting something they don't deserve, rather than for structural explanations. The misattribution of automation effects to immigration is made more likely by an attributional environment in which individual behaviour, not structural forces, is understood to drive economic outcomes.

This connection between welfare design and attributional frameworks is underappreciated in the misattribution literature. Gallego and Kurer treat misattribution as primarily a function of cognitive availability and political supply. This paper suggests it is also a function of institutional environment. People are more likely to blame outgroups for economic disruption when their welfare state has already taught them that economic outcomes are a matter of individual deservingness.

### C. Stage Three: Defensive Othering

The third stage completes the cascade. Individuals who have switched to cultural identity, attributed their economic difficulties to outgroups, and internalised individual-responsibility frameworks engage in what Wagner (2022) terms "kicking down" — the assertion of their own deservingness through the denigration of groups perceived as less deserving.

The empirical evidence for this mechanism is striking. Patrick (2016) documents how out-of-work benefit claimants in the UK engaged in extensive "othering" — distinguishing themselves from other claimants they perceived as less deserving, less genuinely in need, or less morally worthy. Importantly, the criteria they used to draw these boundaries were the same criteria the welfare system had applied to them: willingness to work, gratitude, compliance, and the distinction between "choosing" benefits and being forced onto them by circumstances beyond one's control. The welfare experience did not simply fail to generate solidarity. It provided the moral vocabulary — the deservingness criteria — that recipients then deployed against those below them.

Patrick interprets this as a defensive form of citizenship engagement. Claimants facing stigma from above deployed the dominant narrative as a discursive resource to shore up their own position. But the consequence is that the "scrounger" narrative gets recirculated and extended by the very people it targets. Each act of defensive othering embeds and strengthens the framing consensus, reducing the space for solidaristic alternatives. The "othered" also "other," and the political implications compound.

Van Hootegem (2023, 2024) describes the same process through the lens of self-group distancing — the tendency for individuals to differentiate themselves from groups of similar status whom they perceive as undeserving. This distancing serves an identity-maintenance function: when your own status is threatened, asserting a boundary between yourself and those below you is a way of claiming that you are not like them, that your position in the hierarchy — however precarious — is morally earned.

Im and Komp-Leukkunen (2021) connect this process directly to automation and welfare chauvinism. Routine workers facing status threats from automation may find economic concerns less salient than status concerns — many manage to avoid unemployment even as their occupational prestige declines. These workers may support workfare and strict conditionality not out of material self-interest but because punishing the unemployed allows them to maintain status distance from the group most visibly below them in the hierarchy. Supporting strict welfare conditions for others is a way of saying: I work, they don't, and that distinction matters.

## V. The Recursive Loop

The three stages of the damage cascade do not operate in a single pass. They form a recursive system — a feedback loop in which the outputs of the cascade become the inputs for its next iteration.

The logic runs as follows. Conditional, stigmatising welfare design damages the self-concept of economically vulnerable recipients. That damage activates identity switching, misattribution, and defensive othering. The othering produces public attitudes that support further conditionality and welfare retrenchment — because the recipients themselves are now advocating for narrower deservingness boundaries, stricter conditions, and harsher treatment of those they perceive as undeserving. Political entrepreneurs respond to these attitudes by offering programmes of welfare chauvinism and exclusionary nationalism. If enacted, these programmes further degrade the institutional environment, producing more stigma, more identity damage, and more othering in the next iteration.

Haugsgjerd and Kumlin (2020) provide empirical evidence for part of this loop. Their panel analysis shows that welfare state evaluations and political trust operate bidirectionally: poor welfare performance reduces political trust, and reduced trust makes future welfare evaluations more negative. They describe this as a "downbound spiral" in which malperformance sets in motion a broader process than past research implied. The spiral is self-sustaining: once trust erodes, it becomes harder for the welfare state to regain legitimacy, even if performance improves.

Van Hootegem, Abts, and Meuleman (2021) identify a related paradox: individuals in the most vulnerable socio-economic positions are simultaneously the most supportive of redistribution and the most critical of the welfare state's capacity to achieve just outcomes. This paradox resolves once the recursive mechanism is understood. Vulnerable individuals support the *principle* of redistribution because they need it. But their direct experience of welfare implementation has taught them that the welfare state does not treat people fairly, does not operate impartially, and does not produce just outcomes. They support redistribution in theory and distrust the institution responsible for it in practice. This gap between principle and trust is precisely the space that the radical right occupies — offering an alternative politics that validates the intuition that "the system is broken" while redirecting the blame toward outgroups.

The recursive loop has a further implication: it makes the damage self-concealing. Because the welfare state produces the attitudes that legitimate its own retrenchment, each iteration of the loop makes the next iteration appear more natural, more democratically demanded, and less institutionally determined. When a majority of citizens in a conditional welfare regime support strict conditionality, this appears to validate the design rather than revealing it as the product of prior institutional socialisation. The preferences look like inputs to policy; they are, in significant part, outputs of it.

This is what "policy feedback running backwards" means. The standard policy feedback story (Pierson 1994; Mettler 2002) is about how policies create constituencies that support them — positive feedback that makes welfare states durable. The reverse process creates constituencies that *oppose* the very welfare mechanisms that would serve their interests. It is the production of the wrong politics at the institutional level.

## VI. The Counterfactual: Why the Cascade Doesn't Always Fire

If conditional, stigmatising welfare design produces the damage cascade, what does dignity-preserving design produce?

The evidence is less extensive but consistent. Wagner (2023) documents what he terms an "upward spiral" of solidarity formation among recipients of social investment policies. In a small number of cases, recipients who had positive experiences with enabling, capability-enhancing programmes expressed more inclusive welfare attitudes and explicitly connected their positive experiences to support for others receiving similar opportunities. The mechanism is the mirror image of the damage cascade: positive institutional experiences preserve the self-concept, which maintains the capacity for solidaristic identification, which produces support for inclusive welfare provision.

Rothstein (2001) provides institutional-level evidence: universal welfare programmes generate higher social trust than means-tested alternatives. Universal provision avoids the stigma that comes with proving need, and it creates shared institutional experiences across class lines — experiences that foster perceptions of common fate rather than competitive deservingness. Larsen (2008) extends this analysis, showing that universal welfare states produce more inclusive deservingness narratives that emphasise citizenship over moral sorting.

The Nordic welfare states serve as the primary empirical reference point for the protective mechanism. Norris and Inglehart (2019) find consistently lower populist support in Nordic countries despite exposure to the same globalisation pressures affecting other advanced economies. This resilience is typically attributed to welfare generosity, but the sorting mechanism framework suggests a more specific explanation: Nordic welfare states preserve the self-concept through universal provision, dignity-affirming implementation, and institutional designs that communicate shared citizenship rather than hierarchical deservingness. The political returns to this design — what this paper terms the "dignity dividend" — operate not primarily through material compensation but through the maintenance of identity structures that channel economic disruption into solidarity rather than scapegoating.

Häusermann (2024) has recently argued that extensive and universal public good provision — not merely in welfare benefits but in opportunities and life chances connected to education, housing, employment, and culture — conveys social recognition beyond material security. Such provision is likely to diminish zero-sum status perceptions because it addresses not just what people have but what they feel they are worth. This formulation is close to the core argument of this paper: what welfare communicates about worth determines how economic disruption gets politically processed.

A note of caution is warranted. The comparison between Nordic and liberal welfare states is complicated by the many things that differ across these contexts beyond welfare design — labour market structures, immigration histories, cultural traditions, party systems, and the historical depth of social democratic governance. The claim here is not that welfare design is the sole determinant of political response to economic disruption. It is that welfare design is a significant and underappreciated determinant — one that the dominant buffering framework has missed because it focuses on the wrong dimension (generosity rather than institutional communication).

## VII. The Submerged Dimension: Visibility and the Politics of Non-Recognition

A final theoretical dimension deserves attention because it compounds the sorting mechanism in ways that operate independently of implementation quality.

The modern welfare state does not distribute benefits in a uniform, visible manner. As Mettler (2011) has documented, many of the most substantial transfers in advanced economies — tax expenditures for home ownership, employer-based retirement savings, private health insurance subsidies — are structurally hidden from political view. Their recipients do not experience them as government assistance. They experience them as the natural rewards of responsible behaviour, market participation, and personal merit.

This visibility asymmetry matters for the sorting mechanism because it shapes the *political identity* of the middle class in relation to the welfare state. In a fully universal system, the middle class experiences itself as sharing the welfare state with everyone else — using the same public services, drawing on the same social insurance, subject to the same institutional logic. In a dual system — where the middle class receives substantial but invisible welfare while the poor receive smaller but visible welfare — the middle class experiences itself as self-reliant and views visible welfare recipients as fundamentally different from themselves.

The consequence is a fracturing of the political coalition that the welfare state requires for its own sustainability. Middle-class voters who don't recognise their tax concessions as welfare are less likely to support visible welfare spending for others. They may support retrenchment of visible programmes while their own, hidden benefits remain politically protected. Korpi and Palme (1998) identified this dynamic through their "paradox of redistribution": the more a welfare state targets benefits at the poor exclusively, the less redistribution it ultimately achieves, because targeted benefits lack the broad constituency that universal benefits create. The visibility channel explains part of this paradox: when your welfare is hidden, you have no reason to identify politically with those whose welfare is visible.

In the Australian case that originally motivated this line of inquiry, the dual structure is particularly pronounced. Superannuation tax concessions, private health insurance rebates, and capital gains tax discounts constitute an enormous hidden welfare state for the middle class and wealthy, while Jobseeker and other visible transfers are politically contested, means-tested, and deeply stigmatised. The political result is predictable from the sorting mechanism: those receiving visible welfare experience institutional degradation of their self-concept, producing defensive othering and exclusionary attitudes; those receiving invisible welfare experience themselves as independent market actors, producing meritocratic beliefs that further legitimate the stigmatisation of visible recipients. The dual system sorts the population into two political identities with opposing relationships to the welfare state — and both identities, for different reasons, tend to support retrenchment of the visible tier.

## VIII. Conclusion: Toward a Political Theory of Welfare Design

This paper has argued that welfare institutions function as sorting mechanisms that determine the political direction — not merely the intensity — of response to economic disruption. When welfare design preserves the self-concept of economically vulnerable individuals — through universal provision, dignity-affirming implementation, and institutional communications that frame economic disruption as a structural challenge warranting collective response — disruption can be processed through class identity and solidarity. When welfare design damages the self-concept — through stigmatising conditionality, surveillance-based implementation, and institutional communications that frame economic need as moral failure — disruption activates a cascade of identity switching, misattribution, and defensive othering that produces the "wrong politics": exclusionary, chauvinistic, and self-defeating.

The argument synthesises and connects previously isolated findings from several research traditions. Bonomi, Gennaioli, and Tabellini's identity switching model explains why economic disruption can produce cultural rather than economic political responses. Gallego and Kurer's misattribution framework explains why automation's effects get attributed to immigration. Wagner's analysis of welfare recipient attitudes explains how stigmatising welfare generates the deservingness boundaries that fuel welfare chauvinism. This paper's contribution is to show that these are not independent phenomena but sequential stages of a single cascade — and that the welfare state is the institutional variable that determines whether the cascade fires.

The recursive dimension of this argument has implications that extend beyond the specific mechanisms discussed. If welfare institutions produce the political attitudes that legitimate further welfare retrenchment, then the relationship between welfare design and political preferences is endogenous in a way that cross-sectional analysis struggles to capture. Citizens' welfare attitudes are not simply inputs to the policy process; they are, in significant measure, outputs of prior institutional exposure. This endogeneity suggests that welfare states may be more path-dependent than commonly recognised — not because of the interest-group lock-in that Pierson (1994) emphasised, but because institutional designs shape the very preferences that determine their political sustainability.

The analysis also has implications for the left's strategic challenge in responding to radical right populism. The standard response — more redistribution, better compensation, stronger safety nets — addresses the wrong dimension of the problem. As Gidron and Hall (2017, 2018) have argued, economically displaced individuals often want recognition more than redistribution. But the argument here goes further: the *form* of redistribution matters because it shapes whether recipients experience the welfare state as recognising their worth or as confirming their failure. More spending through stigmatising, conditional channels may actively worsen the political problem it aims to solve. What Kurer (2020) identified as the "strategic disadvantage" of responsible centre parties — their inability to compete with the radical right's identity-based appeals using "mundane, precise, and politically feasible policy reactions" — may be partly a consequence of welfare designs that undermine the political identity structures on which solidaristic politics depends.

The "dignity dividend" — the political returns to welfare design that preserves recipients' sense of themselves — is not captured by standard measures of welfare generosity, replacement rates, or spending levels. It is a property of how welfare is experienced: whether the encounter with the welfare state leaves individuals feeling like citizens navigating a shared challenge, or like supplicants demonstrating worthiness before a suspicious authority. The research agenda that follows from this paper involves developing measures of this experiential dimension of welfare provision and testing whether it moderates the political consequences of economic disruption at the individual level — a task the author intends to pursue using Danish registry data in subsequent work.

As Mijs (2021) has written, breaking the loop between institutional design and exclusionary attitudes requires taking seriously people's beliefs and lived experiences, and designing social policy to bring the two into closer alignment. Until then, there is nothing surprising about the fact that people processed through degrading institutional encounters approach politics from the defensive, exclusionary vantage point of those encounters. The welfare state is not merely a response to the politics of economic disruption. It is one of the forces producing that politics. Whether it produces solidarity or scapegoating is a design choice — and the evidence suggests that many advanced democracies have been making the wrong one.
