# The Wrong Politics: How Welfare Institutions Sort Economic Disruption into Solidarity or Scapegoating

**Ben Smart**

University of Copenhagen — Department of Economics

Welfare State Seminar — April 2026

---

## Abstract

Workers exposed to routine-task automation are more anti-immigration than their material interests would predict, and the gap is systematically larger in some countries than others. This paper argues that welfare state design shapes the political direction of economic disruption — and that the dimension that matters is decommodification rather than spending effort. What welfare institutions communicate to vulnerable workers does most of the political work: whether economic vulnerability reads as a structural challenge warranting collective response, or as a personal failure warranting suspicion.

Using the European Social Survey (rounds 6–9, 34 countries), I find that automation exposure converts into anti-immigration sentiment significantly more strongly in Liberal welfare regimes than in Nordic ones. Spending measures miss the pattern entirely: active labour market policy expenditure is uncorrelated with the cross-national variation (r=0.01, N=15, matched sample). Decommodification reveals it. CWED generosity accounts for 72 per cent of the cross-national variation in the RTI→exclusion slope (r=−0.848, p<0.001), and the pattern holds at the individual level (β=−0.059, p=0.015, N=81,885). Identity switching (Bonomi et al. 2021), misattribution (Gallego and Kurer 2022), and defensive othering (Wagner 2022) form a single institutional chain; decommodification is the condition under which the chain fires.

## I. Introduction

Since the seminal contributions of Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003) on the task-based framework and the subsequent empirical literature on routine-biased technological change, a robust finding has emerged across comparative political economy: workers in routine-task-intensive occupations disproportionately support populist radical right parties (Gingrich 2019; Kurer 2020; Im et al. 2019; Autor et al. 2020; Gallego and Kurer 2022). This pattern holds across countries, time periods, and measurement strategies, confirmed by analyses using panel data, cross-sectional surveys, and regional-level indicators of robotisation (Caselli et al. 2021).

The question this paper addresses is why the pattern takes the form it does. People experiencing material vulnerability turn against the welfare state rather than toward it, channelling grievance into anti-immigrant politics rather than redistributive demand. The puzzle is why economic disruption so often produces the *wrong politics* — responses that systematically fail the people experiencing the disruption.

The conventional answer treats the welfare state as a buffer that moderates the *intensity* of political backlash. Building on Ruggie's (1982) framework of embedded liberalism, this literature conceptualises welfare spending as a compensatory mechanism: generous provision reduces the insecurity that fuels populist support; insufficient compensation leaves insecurity unaddressed (Vlandas and Halikiopoulou 2022; Colantone and Stanig 2018). The operative variable is quantity. Spend more, get less populism.

In contrast to this framework, I argue that welfare institutions function as *sorting mechanisms* rather than buffers — institutional configurations associated with the *direction* of political response to economic disruption, not merely its intensity. The same economic shock, filtered through dignity-preserving welfare institutions, can produce demand for solidarity and structural reform. Filtered through stigmatising, conditional institutions, the same shock produces othering, scapegoating, and what Busemeyer, Rathgeb, and Sahm (2023) term the "particularistic-authoritarian" turn. The cross-national patterns documented below are consistent with welfare design shaping what the political response *is*, though cross-sectional evidence establishes association rather than causation; a causally identified test awaits designs exploiting policy changes over time.

The mechanism, I argue, is the *self-concept* of economically vulnerable individuals — their understanding of their own worth, their citizenship status, and their position relative to other groups. When welfare design preserves the self-concept through universal provision and dignity-affirming implementation, disruption can be processed through class identity, producing demand for redistribution. When it damages the self-concept through stigmatising conditionality and institutional communications that frame economic need as moral failure, disruption activates a cascade — identity switching (Bonomi, Gennaioli, and Tabellini 2021), misattribution (Gallego and Kurer 2022), and defensive othering (Wagner 2022; Patrick 2016) — that produces exclusionary politics.

The paper's contribution is twofold. It connects these previously isolated micro-mechanisms into a single theoretical chain, with the welfare state as the institutional condition under which the chain activates. And it provides cross-national evidence that the moderation disappears under spending measures and emerges clearly under decommodification — a finding that directly challenges the buffering model's reliance on generosity levels as the operative variable.

## II. The Limits of the Buffering Model

The dominant framework treats the welfare state as a buffer against political extremism. Its theoretical foundation is Ruggie's (1982) embedded liberalism bargain: democratic governments, recognising that economic openness creates losers, provide social protection as compensation, on the expectation that compensation dampens extremism. Contemporary applications test whether welfare generosity reduces radical right support (Vlandas and Halikiopoulou 2022). The operative variable is quantity. Spend more, get less populism.

But the evidence is harder on the framework than this account allows.

**Compensation that does not work.** Gallego and Kurer (2022) identify what they call a "concerning finding" from Gingrich's (2019) comparative study — the only careful cross-national analysis assessing whether public policies aimed at alleviating labour market risks for automation-exposed workers can prevent the political turn to protest voting. Gingrich finds that workers highly exposed to automation are not less likely to vote for populist parties in countries with more generous early retirement policies, more in-kind spending, or more protective labour market regulation. If the buffering model were correct, more generous welfare states should show weaker automation-to-populism effects. They do not.

**Compensation that backfires.** Stutzmann (2025) examines Germany's coal phase-out, a case where affected communities received substantial compensatory investment and where both compensation and interest group representation were fulfilled. Material conditions held; affected municipalities nonetheless showed higher abstention rates and lower support for the issue-owning party. Bergman (2023) theorises "self-undermining" policy feedback effects, whereby welfare interventions visibly benefiting groups that potential radical right voters view unfavourably can catalyse greater support for the radical right. Fetzer's (2019) analysis of Brexit makes the channel explicit: welfare cuts predicted Leave support through the erosion of political trust and efficacy, not through material deprivation. The institutional channel is doing the political work.

**Compensation that is resisted.** Pelc (2025) demonstrates that a sizeable portion of workers resist being compensated out of work even when the compensation fully replaces their income — preferring reduced hours over unemployment at the same pay, and compensation from employers over identical compensation from government. The form, source, and meaning of compensation carry political weight independently of its material content. As Guriev (2024) observes, compensating the losers of economic transformation has proven much harder than economists assumed — not because of insufficient resources, but because of political and institutional constraints the buffering framework does not theorise.

The buffering model is not entirely wrong. Albanese et al. (2021) find that EU structural fund disbursements reduce populist voting in recipient regions, suggesting that visible, tangible local investment can work where individualised transfers cannot. The finding cuts the same way as the argument developed below: what the spending says about recipients, and about their relationship to the political community, is what does the work. Spending effort alone is too coarse.

## III. The Sorting Mechanism

### A. The Self-Concept as Pivot

Welfare institutions mediate between economic disruption and political response by shaping the *self-concept* of economically vulnerable individuals — their understanding of their own worth, their position in the social hierarchy, and their relationship to other groups. The self-concept, I argue, is the pivot: it determines which political response to disruption fires.

The formulation draws on Banerjee and Duflo's (2019) argument that the goal of social policy in times of economic change is to help people absorb shocks without allowing those shocks to affect their sense of themselves. I take this literally as a causal hypothesis: when welfare institutions preserve the self-concept — communicating that economic disruption reflects a structural challenge warranting collective response — disruption can be processed through class identity and solidarity. When they damage the self-concept — communicating that recipients are objects of suspicion whose economic position reflects moral standing — disruption triggers the cascade described below.

Welfare institutions shape the self-concept through two channels. The **implementation channel** operates through direct recipient experience. Soss (1999) demonstrates that different welfare programmes create fundamentally different "learning environments": programmes with respectful implementation foster civic engagement, while programmes characterised by surveillance and conditionality generate alienation. Wagner's (2022) research provides the most direct evidence: welfare recipients learn and mirror the recognition norms the state enacts on them. Recipients of conditional welfare adopt narrower deservingness conceptions; recipients of enabling welfare show more inclusive attitudes. De Blok and Kumlin (2022) add that procedural fairness matters most for "losers" — fair treatment preserves institutional trust even when outcomes are unfavourable, meaning implementation quality is most consequential precisely for those already economically vulnerable.

The **visibility channel** operates through the "submerged state" (Mettler 2011) — tax expenditures and indirect benefits serving middle and upper-income groups that remain invisible as government assistance. Recipients of submerged welfare attribute their security to personal merit. Recipients of visible welfare are marked as dependents. This asymmetry produces a political identity fracture: a large share of the population benefits from the welfare state without recognising it, developing meritocratic beliefs structurally at odds with solidarity.

Both channels operate through direct recipient experience, but the cross-national patterns documented below require the mechanism to scale beyond direct recipients. Three transmission pathways do this work. Workers aware of their exposure to economic disruption factor anticipated future dependency into present self-understanding, and welfare institutions are the most legible signal of what that dependency will look like. How the welfare state treats its recipients shapes how welfare is talked about in political speech, in journalism, and at the dinner table — and that discourse reaches well beyond the recipient caseload, mirroring at the population level the recognition norms Wagner (2022) documents at the recipient level. And routine-task-intensive workers are disproportionately represented in the populations most likely to have encountered, or to anticipate encountering, visible welfare systems. The aggregation from recipient experience to population-level attitudes is neither instantaneous nor lossless, but it is also not speculative: it is what Wagner and Soss (1999) mean when they describe welfare as a site of political socialisation that reaches beyond the encounter itself.

### B. The Damage Cascade

Damaged self-concepts trigger a cascade of responses, each building on the last.

First, identity switches. Bonomi, Gennaioli, and Tabellini (2021) model how individuals move from class to cultural identity when class identity is degraded by economic disruption, and under cultural identity redistribution demand decreases while cultural conservatism intensifies. The welfare state enters at a specific point: stigmatising implementation degrades class identity directly, accelerating the switch. A worker entering a system that treats retraining as a citizenship right retains class identity; one entering a system of means-testing and surveillance has that identity doubly damaged. Ballard-Rosa, Jensen, and Scheve (2022) supply the downstream evidence: economic decline activates social identity processes producing authoritarian values through group status threat. Welfare institutional mediation, I argue, is the missing upstream variable — it determines whether the pathway they document actually fires.

Once cultural identity is in charge, grievances misattribute. Economic frustration gets pointed at cognitively available scapegoats. Gallego and Kurer (2022) identify three factors: psychological gratification from blaming outgroups, political supply of anti-immigrant narratives, and automation's gradual invisibility. Wu (2022) confirms empirically that workers at higher automation risk oppose immigration but show no different technology preferences. Alesina and Angeletos (2005) add that conditional systems produce individual rather than structural attributions — predisposing people to search for someone getting something undeserved before they search for structural explanations.

The final move is defensive: othering. Patrick (2016) documents UK benefit claimants shoring up their own deservingness through critique of those below them, using the same criteria the welfare system applied to them. Wagner (2022) calls this "kicking down" and documents its institutional origins. Im and Komp-Leukkunen (2021) connect it to automation: routine workers support strict conditionality because it maintains status distance from groups below them, not because strict conditionality is in their material interest. Häusermann, Kurer, and Zollinger (2023) find that conservative-authoritarian voters are typically "a few rungs up" from the bottom — close enough to feel the proximity as a threat.

### C. Why Welfare, and Not Something Else

A reasonable objection at this point is that many institutions shape identity. Media environments construct group boundaries; religious traditions supply moral narratives; class structures generate interest coalitions. Why isolate welfare as the mediator that converts economic disruption into exclusionary politics?

Welfare is the single state domain where economic vulnerability and institutional treatment meet. Other institutions shape identity, but welfare is the one whose verdict has material and symbolic force simultaneously. When a worker encounters the welfare state, the institution is allocating resources *and* rendering a judgement about the worker's claim to those resources. That joint operation is unusual. Courts render judgement but rarely allocate resources to the individual judged; markets allocate without judgement; religious institutions judge but cannot compel. Only welfare does both, and it does them to people at the point of their maximum material dependence.

This joint operation is what makes welfare uniquely load-bearing as a communicator of citizen worth. Wagner's (2022) finding that recipients learn and mirror the recognition norms the state enacts on them is intelligible only under these conditions: because the encounter is materially consequential and judgement-laden, recipients cannot dismiss the signal as one view among many. They have to metabolise it. Soss (1999) documents the specific learning that follows. What the welfare state says about you, when you encounter it, is part of what you know about yourself. The implication for the empirical analysis is precise: if welfare institutions shape the self-concept of vulnerable workers, they should exert their strongest influence where vulnerability is highest and institutional contact most concrete. Routine-task-intensive workers satisfy both conditions simultaneously — their material position is eroding, and they are disproportionately represented in the caseloads of visible welfare systems. The cross-national pattern is visible in ESS data because the sample contains the population where the mechanism should have the most purchase.

## IV. The Recursive Loop

The cascade forms a self-sustaining system. Conditional welfare damages self-concepts. Damaged self-concepts produce othering. Othering generates public attitudes supporting further conditionality. Political entrepreneurs respond with welfare chauvinism. Further conditionality deepens the damage.

Haugsgjerd and Kumlin (2020) demonstrate this with panel data: poor welfare performance reduces political trust, and reduced trust makes future evaluations more negative — a "downbound spiral." Van Hootegem, Abts, and Meuleman (2021) identify the paradox: the most vulnerable simultaneously support redistribution in principle and distrust the welfare state in practice. This gap is the space the radical right occupies.

The loop makes the damage self-concealing. Because the welfare state produces the attitudes that legitimate its retrenchment, each iteration appears more natural. Preferences that are outputs of policy appear as inputs. This is policy feedback running backwards — not Pierson's (1994) positive feedback creating supportive constituencies, but the reverse: creating constituencies opposing the mechanisms that would serve their interests.

The cascade produces Busemeyer et al.'s (2023) "particularistic-authoritarian" welfare preference: pro-workfare, anti-poor, anti-social-investment — the programme of the radical right, generated in part by the welfare institutions it claims to oppose.

---

## V. The Counterfactual and Its Complications

The protective side of the mechanism runs the other way. Universal programmes build social trust (Rothstein 2001) and produce inclusive deservingness narratives (Larsen 2008). Extensive universal provision conveys recognition beyond material security, diminishing zero-sum status perceptions (Häusermann 2024). Nordic welfare states show consistently lower populist support despite globalisation pressures (Norris and Inglehart 2019).

The sorting mechanism does not operate on a blank slate. Pelc's (2025) finding that Protestant work ethic predicts compensation resistance independently of material incentives suggests some attitudes exist prior to institutional contact. The welfare state shapes moral frameworks, but it also inherits them.

## VI. Empirical Analysis

### A. Data and Measurement

The analysis uses the European Social Survey (ESS), rounds 6–9 (fieldwork 2012–2018), covering 34 countries and 188,764 observations.

**Automation exposure** is measured by routine task intensity (RTI) scores mapped to three-digit ISCO-08 occupation codes following Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003). Match rate: 87.8% (N=165,667).

**Exclusionary attitudes** are measured by a three-item anti-immigration index (whether immigrants make the country worse, undermine cultural life, or are bad for the economy; reverse-coded 0–10; Cronbach's α=0.864).

**Welfare institutional context** is operationalised in three ways. Countries are classified into welfare regimes following Esping-Andersen (1990) and Ferrera (1996): Nordic (DK, SE, NO, FI, IS), Continental (DE, FR, AT, BE, NL, CH), Liberal (GB, IE), Southern (ES, IT, PT, CY), and Eastern (CZ, PL, HU, SK, SI, EE, LT, LV, BG, HR). Country-level ALMP spending (% of GDP) is drawn from the CPDS (22 countries). Total welfare generosity from the Comparative Welfare Entitlements Dataset (CWED), measured as the mean decommodification score across unemployment, sickness, and pension programmes for 2005–2011 (15 Western European countries).

**Controls** include age, age squared, gender, education (ES-ISCED), household income decile, and urban/rural residence.

### B. Empirical Strategy

The core test is a cross-level interaction:

*Y*_ij = β₀ + β₁(RTI_i) + β₂(Welfare_j) + β₃(RTI_i × Welfare_j) + γ(**X**_i) + u_j + ε_ij

where β₃ tests whether the conversion of automation exposure into exclusionary attitudes depends on welfare institutional context. I estimate mixed models with country-wave random slopes for RTI — `(1 + RTI | country-wave)` — confirmed over random intercepts alone by likelihood ratio test. OLS with country-wave fixed effects and clustered standard errors serves as a robustness check; results are directionally identical. The cross-sectional design cannot establish causality — I test whether the pattern is consistent with the sorting mechanism and inconsistent with the simpler "more spending = less backlash" alternative.

### C. Descriptive Results

Figure 2 presents the central descriptive finding. Across all welfare regimes, higher RTI is associated with more anti-immigration attitudes — the relationship is positive and significant everywhere. The slope is steepest in Liberal regimes (β=0.512), followed by Southern (β=0.462), Continental (β=0.443), Nordic (β=0.413), and Eastern (β=0.263). The marginal effects plot (Figure 3) makes the comparison precise: a one-standard-deviation increase in RTI is associated with approximately 0.32 additional scale points of anti-immigration sentiment in Liberal regimes, compared to 0.20 in Nordic regimes. The gap is statistically significant and substantively meaningful.

### D. The CWED Finding: Decommodification as the Operative Variable

Comparing two welfare measures produces the paper's most important finding. When individual countries' RTI→anti-immigration slopes are plotted against their ALMP spending levels — restricting to the same 15 Western European countries used in the CWED analysis — the correlation is near-zero (r=0.01, p=0.97, N=15). Countries spending more on active labour market policies show no systematically flatter or steeper vulnerability-to-exclusion slopes. Spending effort, holding sample constant, is unrelated to the sorting pattern.

The picture changes dramatically when welfare is measured by its decommodifying quality. Figure 6 plots the same country-level slopes against CWED total generosity — the composite score capturing how effectively welfare systems enable workers to sustain living standards without employment. The correlation is strongly negative: r=−0.848, p<0.001, N=15 countries. Welfare generosity measured as decommodification is associated with 72 per cent of the cross-country variation in how strongly automation exposure co-varies with exclusionary attitudes. The United Kingdom — lowest in CWED generosity — shows the steepest slope. Norway — highest in generosity — shows the flattest.

[**Figure 6 here:** CWED generosity vs. country-level RTI→anti-immigration slopes.]

At the individual level, Model 3 confirms this with the cross-level interaction:

| Variable | β | SE | p |
|---|---|---|---|
| RTI | 0.215 | 0.025 | <0.001 |
| CWED generosity | -0.069 | 0.064 | 0.281 |
| **RTI × CWED generosity** | **-0.059** | **0.024** | **0.015** |

The interaction is negative and significant (β=−0.059, p=0.015, N=81,885): a one-standard-deviation increase in welfare generosity reduces the marginal effect of RTI on anti-immigration attitudes by 0.059 scale points. The larger standard error relative to earlier specifications reflects the conservative random-slopes estimator, which absorbs cross-national slope heterogeneity. The finding strengthens with country-level GDP growth and Gini controls (β=−0.066, p<0.001), arguing against the concern that decommodification proxies for broader economic conditions.

The contrast between the ALMP and CWED results is not a measurement nuisance — it is the paper's central empirical argument. ALMP spending captures *effort*: governments can spend heavily on punitive workfare that communicates suspicion. CWED generosity captures *decommodification*: the degree to which the welfare state enables citizens to sustain themselves without dependence on market employment. Following Bonoli's (2013) typology, ALMP aggregates across enabling programmes (upskilling) and punitive ones (benefit sanctions). CWED measures what the welfare state *provides* rather than what it *demands*. The theoretical mechanism I propose — that welfare institutions are associated with different political outcomes through what they communicate about citizens' worth — predicts exactly this: spending levels are unrelated; decommodification matters.

Denmark presents an instructive complication. Despite high CWED generosity, Denmark shows a relatively steep RTI→exclusion slope (β=0.50) — higher than Finland, Sweden, or Norway. Danish "flexicurity" combines generous benefits with high labour market flexibility and active job search requirements, creating a system generous in transfers but demanding in activation. This may indicate that the *quality* of generosity matters beyond its level. The country-level finding is robust to all single-country exclusions: excluding the United Kingdom (lowest CWED) gives r=−0.802; excluding Norway (highest CWED) gives r=−0.794; excluding both simultaneously gives r=−0.717 (p=0.006). The result does not depend on any single case or the two highest-leverage observations jointly.

### F. Redistribution, Radical Right Voting, and the Solidarity Asymmetry

The redistribution models tell a different story. RTI predicts slightly more redistribution support across all regimes (β=0.044, p<0.001), but the cross-regime variation does not match theoretical predictions — the RTI × Liberal coefficient is small and non-significant (β=0.011, p=0.285). The institutional moderation of the vulnerability→solidarity pathway is not detectable in this data. The asymmetry matters: welfare context more clearly attenuates the conversion of vulnerability into exclusion than into solidarity. Whether this reflects genuine theoretical asymmetry — that it may be easier to push people toward exclusion than to pull them toward solidarity — or measurement limitations in a single redistribution item remains an open question (see Appendix C).

The radical right voting models (Model 6) require honest treatment. RTI predicts radical right voting in Nordic countries (β=0.220, p<0.001), but the RTI × Liberal interaction is *negative* and significant (β=−0.123, p=0.032) — the opposite direction to the attitudinal finding. The most plausible explanation is institutional rather than substantive: Ireland has no electorally significant radical right party, and the UK's first-past-the-post system suppresses radical right seat conversion despite substantial UKIP vote shares (15–27% in the ESS waves 7–8 period). High-RTI voters in Liberal regimes may have channelled exclusionary preferences into Brexit support rather than party votes. The behavioural models should be treated as descriptive of electoral systems' conversion of attitudes rather than tests of the sorting mechanism per se.

### G. Limitations

Several limits matter for how to read these findings. The cross-sectional design cannot establish temporal ordering. Country-level welfare indicators are confounded with other institutional differences: Nordic countries with high CWED generosity also tend to have higher social trust, stronger unions, proportional electoral systems, and lower ethnic heterogeneity, and the macro-controls robustness check (GDP growth, Gini) addresses macroeconomic confounders but not these deeper institutional correlates. The CWED finding, while striking (r=−0.848), rests on 15 country-level observations. The individual-level Model 3 interaction provides a more defensible test, but full identification requires within-country variation in welfare generosity over time — exploiting CWED changes following benefit reforms, a design I intend to pursue with Danish registry data in subsequent work.

## VII. Discussion and Conclusion

The evidence supports a clear conclusion: welfare design is associated with the *direction* of automation's political effects, not merely their intensity. Disruption processed through welfare institutions that preserve the self-concept co-occurs with redistributive solidarity; the same disruption processed through institutions that damage it co-occurs with exclusionary politics. The RTI→exclusion slope is significantly steeper in Liberal than Nordic regimes across all specifications. The moderation disappears under spending measures and emerges clearly under decommodification. And the ALMP null on the same 15-country sample rules out welfare-state size as the operative variable.

The contrast between ALMP and CWED results speaks directly to the theoretical argument. The buffering model asks: *how much* does the welfare state spend? The sorting mechanism asks: *what does the welfare state communicate?* A country can spend heavily on active labour market policies that are punitive, conditional, and dignity-stripping — and that spending will register as "generous" while communicating suspicion to recipients. Decommodification captures something different: the degree to which the welfare state enables citizens to maintain their living standards and, crucially, their sense of themselves without dependence on market employment. This is the institutional quality that Banerjee and Duflo identify as the goal of social policy — absorbing shocks without allowing them to affect the self-concept.

These findings bear on the debate about whether radical right support has "economic" or "cultural" roots. Ciccolini (2025) argues that the distinction is flawed because voters give a cultural reading to economic phenomena. This paper pushes that argument in an institutional direction: welfare design is part of what shapes whether economic disruption receives a cultural reading in the first place. The cultural backlash is not a rival explanation to the economic one — it is what economic disruption looks like, cross-nationally, where welfare institutions are less decommodifying.

This paper is about attitudinal sorting, not vote translation. The behavioural results in §VI.F — RTI predicts radical right voting in Nordic countries, the RTI × Liberal interaction is negative — are better read as confirmation of that distinction than as a puzzle to be explained away. Attitudes sort. Supply-side institutions then determine whether attitudes become votes: party systems, electoral rules, and the available radical right options vary substantially across the welfare regimes compared here. The UK and Ireland in the ESS sample lack a conventional radical right channel for exclusionary preferences in the waves studied, and the Brexit referendum provided an unusual offramp for Liberal-regime exclusionary sentiment that conventional party-choice models cannot register. A full account of how sorted attitudes become exclusionary politics requires bringing supply-side variables into the analysis and, ideally, extending the dependent variable beyond conventional radical right vote share. That is a separate paper. The claim developed here is more specific: welfare institutional context shapes the conversion of economic disruption into exclusionary attitudes. Whether those attitudes find an electoral channel is a question about party supply and electoral rules, not about the sorting mechanism itself.

The exclusion–solidarity asymmetry is worth pausing on. The damage mechanism produces clear empirical patterns. The protective mechanism — dignity-preserving welfare converting vulnerability into solidarity — produces weaker ones. The asymmetry may be real rather than measurement-driven: preventing the damage cascade may be more achievable than actively producing solidarity, and failure to prevent it compounds through the recursive loop.

The "dignity dividend" — the political returns to welfare design that preserves recipients' sense of themselves — is not captured by standard measures of generosity. It is a property of how welfare is *experienced*: whether the encounter leaves individuals feeling like citizens navigating a shared challenge, or like supplicants demonstrating worthiness. Developing measures of this experiential dimension, and testing whether it moderates the political consequences of disruption with causally identified designs, is a task I intend to pursue using Danish registry data in subsequent work.

The welfare state is more than a response to the politics of economic disruption. The cross-national patterns documented here are consistent with welfare design shaping the political direction of that disruption — a claim the present evidence supports associatively, and that designs exploiting policy changes over time will need to establish causally. What the evidence establishes is narrower and firmer: decommodification, not spending, is the dimension of welfare design along which the sorting pattern is visible.

## Appendix A: Robustness Checks

Figure A1 presents a coefficient stability plot for the RTI × Liberal interaction across specifications. The coefficient ranges from 0.073 (jackknife excluding GB) to 0.161 (jackknife excluding IE), with a jackknife mean of 0.117 and standard deviation of 0.013. The result survives exclusion of Eastern European countries (β=0.128), removal of the income control (β=0.132), and inclusion of left-right self-placement (β=0.116). The 95% confidence interval never includes zero in any specification.

The CWED×RTI interaction is similarly robust. Adding country-level GDP growth and post-fiscal Gini as macro controls — sourced from the Comparative Political Data Set over 2012–2018 — leaves the buffering coefficient essentially unchanged (β=−0.066, p<0.001), arguing against the concern that decommodification proxies for broader economic conditions rather than welfare quality per se.

## Appendix B: Education Moderation

The descriptive analysis reveals that the RTI→anti-immigration relationship is steeper for non-college workers in all regimes. In Liberal regimes, the bivariate slope drops from 0.280 (non-college) to 0.128 (college) — a 54 per cent reduction. This pattern is consistent with the theoretical prediction that education provides alternative identity resources buffering the switch from class to cultural identity.

[**Figure B1 here:** RTI→anti-immigration slopes by education and welfare regime.]

The formal three-way interaction (Model 4: RTI × regime × non-college) shows a positive but non-significant coefficient for the RTI × Liberal × non-college term (β=0.084, p=0.179). The formal test does not reach conventional significance thresholds. The descriptive pattern is consistent with the theoretical prediction — and parallels the finding of Ballard-Rosa, Jensen, and Scheve (2022) that education buffers the economic decline → authoritarian values pathway — but should be treated as exploratory pending a design with adequate statistical power for three-way interactions.

## Appendix C: The Solidarity Pathway

Model 5 (redistribution support, *gincdif* reverse-coded 1–5) shows RTI predicting slightly more redistribution support everywhere (β=0.044, p<0.001), but the cross-regime interaction does not match predictions: the RTI × Liberal coefficient is small, non-significant, and in the wrong direction (β=0.011, p=0.285). The institutional moderation of the vulnerability→solidarity pathway is not detectable in the ESS data.

A supplementary test using the ISSP 2006 Role of Government module (N=10,216, 12 countries) finds no interaction between automation exposure and welfare generosity on unemployment spending support (β=+0.010, SE=0.016, p=0.55) — consistent with the Model 5 null. That this precedes the period in which automation risk entered broader public discourse by several years suggests a scope condition: the solidarity pathway may require automation exposure to register as a salient class-level threat, not merely as individual occupational risk.

The redistribution asymmetry — clear exclusion effects, no solidarity effects — may reflect genuine theoretical asymmetry (damage cascades are easier to trigger than solidarity spirals), temporal mismatch (the ISSP data predates automation salience), or measurement limitations in single-item redistribution scales. Distinguishing these interpretations requires panel data tracking individuals across periods of welfare reform — a design I intend to pursue in subsequent work.
