## Argumentation moves (multi-sentence patterns)

### Move 1: The "Through this perspective" pivot + citation

**Pattern:** establish a viewpoint → use *Through this perspective,* / *Through such perspectives,* → cite an author who supports it.

> *"**Through this perspective**, Ezrow, Tavits and Homola (2014 p.1560) contend political actors competing in polarized electorates may receive an electoral benefit when adopting distinctly non-centrist and populist policy positions."* (Global Media)

### Move 2: Citation-then-paraphrase scaffold

**Pattern:** make a claim → next sentence opens with *Supporting this theory,* / *Corroborating this idea,* / *This view is consistent with* → cite + paraphrase.

> *"...polarisation is inherent to the architecture of online networks. Indeed, the relative anonymity and reduced social cues of interaction within online echo chambers can minimise perceived differences between members and thus foster in-group identification (Lee 2006 p.423). For Wojcieszak (2010 p.637), such characteristics increase the susceptibility to normative influence..."* (Global Media)

### Move 3: The "yet" mid-sentence concessive

**Pattern:** long compound clause → comma + *yet* + counter-clause → final implication.

> *"The open, decentralised technology of the internet was once promised as the foundation of deliberative democracy..., **yet** digital technologies have instead contributed to a society facing increasing political divides in public beliefs and media content..."* (Global Media)

### Move 4: The "Whilst X, Y" concessive

**Pattern:** sentence-initial *Whilst* + author's claim → main-clause counter or extension.

> *"**Whilst** Daniels (2009 p.6) suggests radical mobilisation and widespread recruitment in these factions are unlikely, she raises concerns over how these groups and cloaked websites may challenge the epistemological basis of racial equality..."* (Global Media)

### Move 5: Triplet conclusion ("Firstly... Secondly... Thirdly...")

**Pattern:** in a closing paragraph, summarise the argument as three points using inline (not bolded, not numbered) *Firstly* / *Secondly* / *Thirdly*.

> *"**Firstly**, the prominence of ideological echo chambers supports Wojcieszak's (2010) theory... **Secondly**, opinion and informational climates within networks means members are more likely to accept others' arguments... **Thirdly**, a media industry constrained by economic pressures is more likely to draw attention to the competitive and polarising aspects of politics..."* (Global Media)

### Move 6: The "Essentially, X" wrap-up

**Pattern:** at the end of a paragraph that built an argument, restate the gist using *Essentially,*. 14 occurrences across the corpus — this is your second-most-common transition.

> *"**Essentially**, although social media may carry the risk of encouraging superficial political activity or 'slacktivism', SNS still allows for the connection of individuals through various platforms..."* (Social Media and Democracy)

### Move 7: Block quote → single-sentence commentary → next claim

**Pattern:** for theory-heavy sections, set up a direct quote from a primary source (with page number), follow with one sentence of commentary that names what the quote does, then return to your argument. The quote-mosaic pattern. Page numbers always.

## How to use this lexicon

**Before drafting:**
- Read the top-30 quick reference. Pick 3-4 transitions and 3-4 verbs from the list as anchors for the section.
- For theory-heavy sections, pre-select 2-3 nouns from the discourse/structure column that you want to land in the section.

**While drafting:**
- When stuck on a transition, scan "Transitions, by purpose" and grab one that matches your move (opening / pivoting / citing / closing).
- When you find yourself reaching for a generic word like "shows" or "important", check whether a corpus-verified verb/adjective fits.

**After drafting (auditing):**
- Run `/voice-audit path/to/draft.md` for a deterministic check.
- Goal: 5+ Ben transitions per 1000 words; ≥3 distinctive verbs from the lists; at least one signature noun-phrase pattern (architecture-of-X or undergirding) per theory section.

**Quarterly maintenance:**
- When you write new prose under your name (signed essays, accepted papers, theses), add the file to `manuscripts/Writing Samples/Pre-AI/` (or a `Confirmed-Ben/` sibling folder) and re-run the corpus extraction. This keeps the lexicon current as your voice evolves.
