## K. Distinctive multi-clause sentence patterns

Beyond single openers, these *sentence shapes* recur:

- **"X, where Y, allowing Z"** — long compound with where + gerund: "...creating a virtual public sphere that incites collective decision-making on the basis of unconstrained argumentation among citizens."
- **"X, [comma + relative clause], [comma + main verb]..."** — your default long-compound shape; 25-67 word sentences are standard.
- **"X, but Y, ultimately Z-ing"** — concession that pivots to the larger frame.
- **"Whilst X may Y, X still Z"** — concessive that defends a position. ("Whilst Habermas' theory is perhaps meaningful... the theory fails to take into account...")
- **"These critiques are correct, yet are not central to..."** — granting then narrowing.
- **"X may Y; however, Z."** — semicolon-bridged contrast.

# Argumentation moves (multi-sentence patterns)

### Move 1: The "Through this perspective" pivot + citation

**Pattern:** establish a viewpoint → use *Through this perspective,* / *Through such perspectives,* → cite an author who supports it.

> *"**Through this perspective**, Ezrow, Tavits and Homola (2014 p.1560) contend political actors competing in polarized electorates may receive an electoral benefit when adopting distinctly non-centrist and populist policy positions."* (Global Media)

### Move 2: Citation-then-paraphrase scaffold

**Pattern:** make a claim → next sentence opens with *Supporting this theory,* / *Corroborating this idea,* / *This view is consistent with* → cite + paraphrase.

> *"...polarisation is inherent to the architecture of online networks. Indeed, the relative anonymity and reduced social cues of interaction within online echo chambers can minimise perceived differences between members and thus foster in-group identification (Lee 2006 p.423). For Wojcieszak (2010 p.637), such characteristics increase the susceptibility to normative influence..."* (Global Media)

### Move 3: The "yet" mid-sentence concessive

**Pattern:** long compound clause → comma + *yet* + counter-clause → final implication.

> *"The open, decentralised technology of the internet was once promised as the foundation of deliberative democracy..., **yet** digital technologies have instead contributed to a society facing increasing political divides..."* (Global Media)

### Move 4: The "Whilst X, Y" concessive

**Pattern:** sentence-initial *Whilst* + author's claim → main-clause counter or extension.

> *"**Whilst** Daniels (2009 p.6) suggests radical mobilisation and widespread recruitment in these factions are unlikely, she raises concerns over how these groups and cloaked websites may challenge the epistemological basis of racial equality..."* (Global Media)

### Move 5: Triplet conclusion ("Firstly... Secondly... Thirdly...")

**Pattern:** in a closing paragraph, summarise the argument as three points using inline (not bolded, not numbered) *Firstly* / *Secondly* / *Thirdly*.

> *"**Firstly**, the prominence of ideological echo chambers supports Wojcieszak's (2010) theory... **Secondly**, opinion and informational climates within networks means members are more likely to accept others' arguments... **Thirdly**, a media industry constrained by economic pressures..."* (Global Media)

### Move 6: The "Essentially, X" wrap-up

**Pattern:** at the end of a paragraph that built an argument, restate the gist using *Essentially,*. 14 occurrences across the corpus — your second-most-common transition.

> *"**Essentially**, although social media may carry the risk of encouraging superficial political activity or 'slacktivism', SNS still allows for the connection of individuals through various platforms..."* (Social Media and Democracy)

### Move 7: The "For [Author]," topical-attribution pivot

**Pattern:** sentence-opener using *For [Author],* + claim to economically attribute a sub-thesis to a thinker.

> *"**For Wojcieszak** (2010 p.637), such characteristics increase the susceptibility to normative influence by encouraging members to adjust their opinions..."* (Global Media)

> *"**For Hesmondhalgh and Pratt** (2005), the implications of these changing paradigms go beyond policy, it is the commercialisation of cultural production..."* (Culture Policy)

### Move 8: The granted-then-narrowed structure ("These critiques are correct, yet are not central to...")

**Pattern:** acknowledge an opposing view in the strongest form → mid-sentence "yet" → narrow into your distinct claim.

> *"**These critiques are correct, yet are not central to** the capability of social media to augment democratic possibility; although superficially committed actors may not meaningfully instigate democratic change, they still have the potential to be mobilised through passive or monitorial use of social media."* (Social Media and Democracy)

### Move 9: Block quote → single-sentence commentary → next claim

**Pattern:** for theory-heavy sections, set up a direct quote from a primary source (with page number), follow with one sentence of commentary that names what the quote does, then return to your argument. Page numbers always.

### Move 10: Stacked parenthetical citations

**Pattern:** a single claim is supported with *three* inline citations stacked in parentheses. This is your *signature* citation density.

> *"...where unregulated and often homophilous networks ultimately undermine deliberative values such as compromise and consensus by increasing political polarization and intergroup hostility (Sunstein 2007 p.19; Van Aelst et. Al 2017 p.13; Wojcieszak 2010 p.636)."* (Global Media)

### Move 11: The "Although X is essential, much of Y..." opener

**Pattern:** concede that one frame matters, but pivot the paper to a different frame.

> *"**Although the scholarly dispute over the democratic opportunities of these new communication technologies is essential**, much of the literature surrounding politicians and Twitter also focuses on the means and motivations behind politicians' usage of social media."* (Politicians and Twitter)

### Move 12: "Indeed, [strong supporting move]" as paragraph-internal amplification

**Pattern:** mid-paragraph, after a claim, an *Indeed,*-led sentence that intensifies or specifies. This is your most-frequent transition (28 times) and is *not* a paragraph-opener — it threads through paragraphs.
